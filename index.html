The Sponsorship Playbook

How to Get Sponsorships: a complete operating system for winning corporate sponsorships

Synthesized from a large corpus of sponsorship-sales content (hundreds of articles, talks, and guides). This is the actionable distillation. The exhaustive, deduplicated reference lives in digests/merged_A–D.md.

This is a synthesis of modern sponsorship-sales best practices — a methodology used to close deals from five-figure activations to multi-million-dollar naming rights.

Executive Summary

Sponsorship is marketing, not philanthropy. Companies don't buy logos, your enthusiasm, or your cause — they buy access to your audience and measurable marketing outcomes. So the entire job, in order, is: (1) deeply understand your audience with data, (2) build and value an inventory of assets/activations against what those outcomes are worth on the open market, (3) build a prospect list from your audience data, (4) do direct outreach asking only for a short discovery meeting (never pitching, never attaching a proposal), (5) run discovery meetings that diagnose the sponsor's goals, (6) co-create a custom offer (never gold/silver/bronze tiers), (7) close, (8) over-deliver and prove it with a fulfillment report, and (9) renew/upgrade for more money. Most people fail because they skip to step 6 (a generic package) and blast it out — "spaghetti at the wall." The work that actually closes deals is direct outreach to a specific named human at a specific company, and almost nobody does it.

1. The core operating philosophy (the reframes that drive everything)

These principles recur across virtually every post and video. Internalizing them is described as the single hardest and most important step ("change your mindset").

  1. Sponsorship is marketing, NOT philanthropy / a donation / charity / major gifts / an investment. It's a two-way B2B commercial exchange. "It has as much in common with philanthropy as baseball does with spaghetti." If you want a donation, ask a rich person; if you want an investment, ask an angel. Don't call those sponsorship.
  2. They sponsor your AUDIENCE, not you and not your cause. "Your cause attracts an audience and your audience attracts the sponsor." "A sponsor does not care about your cause." Your audience is your single most valuable asset — your "Fabergé egg," "golden goose." No audience, no sponsorship. The end.
  3. Sponsorship is a verb, not a noun. "There's no such thing as sponsors" — only companies using sponsorship as one marketing/sales tool among many to hit goals. Ask "What are your marketing and sales goals?" not "What do sponsors want?"
  4. YOU are the sales tool — not the package. "The sponsorship proposal does not make the sale. You do." (This line ends nearly every video.) A proposal is a key to nothing; people buy from people.
  5. Quality of audience beats quantity. "1,000 university students" is worth far less than "25 people who control corporate budgets over $10 million." The riches are in the niches. Some of the biggest deals come from the smallest audiences.
  6. Small is an advantage. A small property that knows its audience and over-services can out-compete juggernauts for $5K–$100K sponsors — full customization, decision-makers in every meeting, no bureaucracy.
  7. Diagnose before you prescribe. Sending a package before discovery is "the cardinal sin" — like buying a birthday gift for a stranger, or a doctor prescribing before examining. "Fall in love with the sponsor's problem, not your package."
  8. Front-load the work; the ask is ~5%. ~95% of the job is prep (audience data, inventory, valuation, activations, discovery, customization). "30 hours of prep makes the actual ask incredibly simple."
  9. Rejection is the job. Hunt for "no." "He who fails the most wins" (Seth Godin). "If you can remember the name of the person who told you no, you're not prospecting enough." A fast, clear "no" is a gift; the enemy is "maybe" and silence.
  10. There are no shortcuts and no silver bullet. "Sponsors are hard to find because they want to be hard to find." Treat it as a discipline with its own rules, like accounting or law. Expect a 3–5 year horizon — "you don't propose marriage on the first date."

Supporting mindsets: be in asking mode, not telling mode ("there is no better research tool than the question mark"); speak the sponsor's language (marketing outcomes, not your mission); present as an equal, not a beggar ("if you're begging, you've already lost"); effort is non-linear ("90% effort does not equal 90% results — it may equal $0").

2. The end-to-end system (the canonical sequence)

Doing these out of order is the #1 cause of failure. The most common fatal error is building a package (step 6) before doing audience research (step 1) and discovery (step 5).

  1. Audience research / survey FIRST — build hyper-segmented data.
  2. Build the asset inventory (everything sellable) and value it.
  3. Prospect — build the list from your audience data (Concentric Circles).
  4. Find the named decision-maker + a warm "in."
  5. Direct outreach — short email/call; the only goal is to book a 15-min discovery meeting. No deck, no pitch, no proposal.
  6. Discovery meeting(s) — ask 5–10 questions, listen, diagnose, qualify. Bring nothing.
  7. Create/customize assets & activations — only after discovery, tailored to what you learned.
  8. Proposal — only if/when the sponsor asks; review it together (don't just send it).
  9. Negotiate & close — money comes up here, not before. Lawyer-reviewed contract (a contract is not a proposal).
  10. Deliver / activate — track and quantify everything, live, during the event.
  11. Fulfillment report — fast, post-event; proves ROI and opens renewal.
  12. Renew / upgrade — meet to review the report; re-discover, re-value, sign for more money and/or multiple years.

Memorable shorthand: "Research → discovery call → meeting → meeting → meeting → draft proposal → update proposal → success."

The named frameworks all map to this spine: the 5-Step Direct-Outreach System (audience data → prospect selection → contact ID → direct outreach → discovery-led proposal); a six-part lifecycle framework (Clarify → Differentiate → Launch → Amplify → Renew → Scale); the 9-Step Sponsorship Magnet; the Five Stages of Sponsorship Sales. They differ only in emphasis.

3. The funnel math (the numbers that define the system)

This is the most load-bearing, most-repeated quantitative model in the entire corpus. Track activity volume, not revenue — "when done properly, revenue comes in almost on its own." Build the pipeline backwards from a revenue goal.

The canonical "$250K year" funnel

200 qualified contacts → 45 discovery meetings → 15 proposals → 5 sponsors @ $50K = $250,000.

  • 200 contacts across 50 working weeks = just 4 direct outreaches per week (~3–4 hours of real work).
  • "Most sponsorship teams do not make four direct contacts a week. They make zero." The hours exist — they get spent on proposal-polishing, event prep, content, and calendar admin.
  • Implied conversion: ~1 in 4 contacts → meeting; ~1 in 3 meetings → proposal; ~1 in 3 proposals → close.

The "$350K / Know Your Numbers" backwards model

Revenue goal ÷ average deal = # sponsors needed. e.g. $350K ÷ $10K = 35 sponsors. At ~50% conversion per stage: 70 proposals, 140 discovery meetings, 280 cold outreaches over 12 weeks (≈ 6 proposals / 12 meetings / 24 calls per week). Higher-volume variant: to close 10 sales you may need ~900 prospects (stacked 20% reply → 30% meeting → ~33% close → 50% proposal-close).

The diagnostic

  • A 2-sentence email asking for 15 minutes should book ≥1 discovery meeting per 4 contacts. Below 1 in 20, your list / email / targeting is broken.
  • Count only named-person, specific-reason, specific-meeting-ask contacts. "If it's zero, the pipeline didn't move because you didn't do the work. If it's 1–3, you started and stopped. If it's 5+, you ran a real sponsorship week — regardless of replies."

4. Stage 1 — Audience research & data (the heart of everything)

"No audience data kills sponsorship programs dead." Do this first — prospecting and packaging both fail without it.

  • Who is your "audience"? Everyone who engages: attendees, listeners, members, donors, volunteers, board, program users, social followers, email subscribers. For nonprofits, your audience is your donors/volunteers/board/supporters — NOT the people you serve. (A food bank's audience is its donors, not its recipients.)
  • Niche down relentlessly (the "Russian nesting doll" / "sieve" / "onion"). Neighborhood, not city. Job title + income bracket, not "occupation." Banned descriptors: "general public," "families," "the middle class," "professionals," "women 30–50." "Everybody is the target market of nobody."
  • Build a minimum of 3 named avatars on top of the macro audience, each with ~25 data points. Personify them (e.g., "Financial Frank," a Brooklyn tax specialist, $75K/yr, two kids, time-poor, likes tech brands).
  • Good vs. bad segment (verbatim contrast): "40-year-old high-net-worth moms in suburban Chicago with 2+ kids who play baseball" (good) vs. "women 30–50" (useless).
  • Data points to collect: age (by year), income (by $5K bracket), geography (by neighborhood), education, job title, decision-making authority / budget control / # of reports, customer longevity, annual/quarterly/monthly spend, brands they buy and love, brands that advertise to them, intended purchases (car, house, banking, insurance, retirement — and when), and psychographics (beliefs, motivations, what they love/hate about you).
  • Survey mechanics (Chris's exact method): SurveyMonkey Pro (~$350/yr); send 2–3 e-blasts (Tue/Wed/Thu, 10am–1pm); incentive = chance to win one of three $25 gift cards; keep it <10 minutes, multiple-choice; give 2–4 weeks. Yields 30+ data points at 95% confidence. Resurvey every 6 months (and after any audience jump). Data older than 6 months "is not current enough."
  • New property with no audience? Build projected data from ticket buyers, and research the interest audience (e.g., running magazines/clubs for a new race), then refine after the first event.

5. Stage 2 — Properties, inventory & assets

  • Property = a sponsorship opportunity / rights-holder: a collection of related assets serving a specific audience (an event, festival, podcast, athlete, team, conference, gala, race series, naming right, program). One organization can have many properties.
  • Asset = a single sellable unit (a logo placement, booth, speaking slot, e-blast, sampling right, naming right, VIP access, tickets). Alone it's "little more than advertising" until it's packaged under a property, valued, and activated.
  • Inventory-building brainstorm (30 min, with board/leadership/comms AND program staff): spend only 5 minutes on "what can we put logos on?" — then ban the word "logo" for the rest of the session. Then ask: what can we sell? what speaking/product-placement opportunities are we missing? Bundle into categories. Target ~100 assets.
  • Asset categories: naming rights (spaces & programs), signage, product giveaways/samples, exhibiting, speaking, webpage, social, newsletters, pass-through benefits, use of venue, paid/traditional media, employee benefits.
  • The value ladder — map every asset to the sponsor's funnel: Awareness → Lead gen → Conversion → Sales. The deeper it reaches, the more it's worth.
  • Low value (awareness only): logos, social mentions, signage, press-release inclusion, CPM ad reads.
  • High value: speaking slots, media partnerships, tailored content, broadcast rights.
  • Premium (unbuyable elsewhere): new-customer acquisition, naming rights, direct lead capture, pass-through benefits, sampling rights.
  • Logos are the LEAST valuable asset (recurring, emphatic) — ~⅓¢ per view, lost in clutter. Challenge: list 50 assets that need no logo. Logos genuinely work in only a few places: fashion-show runway branding, race-car/suit/helmet livery, Twitch/stream overlays.
  • Sampling rights and lead-gen are among the MOST valuable ("a license to print money").
  • Don't sell what you don't control (e.g., naming rights to a venue you don't own — the deal collapses at delivery).
  • Define exclusivity carefully (category vs. direct-competitor) and have a lawyer vet it.

6. Stage 3 — Valuation & pricing

Valuation is mandatory — "you can't talk money until you know your value." Required for any $50K+ ask; sponsors increasingly demand a third-party-style valuation before negotiating.

  • The one-line method: "Where else / how could my sponsor get this exact same outcome WITHOUT me?" — then price against that market rate. Compare to ad rate cards, PPC/Google Ads, social-ad CPMs, InMail, a radio host's interview fee, etc. Cost per view × your reach.
  • Value each asset individually, one at a time ("how do you eat an elephant? one bite at a time"). Value each prospect's property separately — 5 sponsors = 5 valuations.
  • Quick valuation hack: open a Facebook/LinkedIn/Google ad targeting your exact audience and read the platform's quoted CPM/CPC right before you'd pay. That's what a brand pays to reach your people. Charge less = leaving money on the table.
  • Brand value (intangible uplift): the additional benefit of reaching your audience through you vs. raw media. Score yourself (a %) on factors sponsors value — audience knowledge, loyalty, reputation/trust, low competition, customized activations, exclusivity, sponsor summit — typically +10–30% each. Sum, convert to cents, add to each asset's hard value. Worked example: a $1.00/view logo at 92% brand value → $1.92/view (nearly doubles revenue). Ceiling: never charge >3× hard value (7× exists but "is not normal").
  • Digital comparables: CPM $5–10/1,000 impressions = logo (cheapest); CPC $2–10/click; CPS ~$0.80–1/send; CPL $50–2,000+/lead (most valuable).
  • Pricing tactics:
  • Never put a price in the proposal. A written number reads as set-in-stone; you'll undersell or get a "take it or leave it." Discuss money verbally, after valuation, during negotiation.
  • People undervalue far more often than they overvalue.
  • A sponsor may pay more than your number if they want your audience badly — another reason not to hard-code price.
  • Be willing to flex a few hundred/thousand; walk away from radical discounting.
  • Document your math ("show your work like math class") — sponsors ask "how did you come up with this?" and can smell a guess.
  • Never guess, price off your costs, copy someone's valuation, or "tack on zeroes."

7. Stage 4 — Activation

"Activations are what you sell to sponsors, not logos." An activation is an experiential-marketing opportunity that produces a measurable sponsor outcome.

  • Asset vs. activation: putting samples on a table = asset; letting the sponsor come, sending e-blasts, running a contest, giving a speaking slot = activation. Collecting emails at the gate = asset; running a raffle for an iPad in exchange for a ballot = activation.
  • The golden rule: a great activation satisfies one audience need AND one sponsor need simultaneously — it lives in the Venn overlap of (sponsor goals) ∩ (audience goals), within the marketing budget.
  • Output vs. outcome: the photo booth is the output; the 5,000 email signups is the outcome. Sell and measure the outcome, on the sponsor's definition of success (not yours).
  • You lead the ideas, but co-create — bring loose ideas to a brainstorm right after signing (book an "activation brainstorming session," not an invoice — your first post-signature touch). "Who knows a sponsor's pain points better than the sponsor?"
  • Substance over flash. Low-cost (even free) activations routinely beat sponsor expectations; add "pizzazz" only after the goal is met.
  • Worked example (recurring): sponsor needs an email list + audience wants interactive moments → run a contest where the prize is the sponsor's product and entry requires an email signup. Sponsor gets the list + product trial; audience gets engagement.
  • Budget: plan it up front; if you run 5–10% over package value, eat it — beyond that, the sponsor pays (especially if they proposed the pricier idea). An "activation credit" is a spend-account line item the sponsor can draw from.

8. Stage 5 — Prospecting & pipeline building

Always prospect FROM your audience data — never from "biggest brands in the industry" or by budget. A brand your audience doesn't care about won't get engagement, so you can't deliver (breach risk).

Concentric Circles (the core method) — warmest center → coldest edge

  1. Circle 1 (hottest): brands your audience named buying/loving in your survey (best: brands they intend to buy in the next 18 months — yields 20–30+ alone).
  2. Circle 2: brands that already advertise to your audience (ask what media they consume; consume it; note the advertisers).
  3. Circle 3: brands that should advertise to them but don't yet (your research). "This is where most people start AND end — which is why they struggle."
  4. Circle 4: direct competitors of every brand in circles 1–3 (add 2–3 each). - Yields 60–100+ prospects; cap working lists at 200–300 (bigger can't be qualified). Then research each one before outreach.

The Nine Paths to a Sponsor (companies are made of people)

Reach, at C-level / VP / Director / Manager: Marketing (primary), Sales, Sponsorship, Communications, Brand, Product Development, HR/Employee Engagement, Customer Retention, and (nonprofits) CSR/Community Investment — but only to ask them for a warm intro to the marketing/sales people.

Rules

  • Don't chase the big fish. Coca-Cola/Nike/Red Bull get thousands of requests a day, are the coldest leads, and would under-monetize a non-aligned audience. ~99% of big-brand asks fail.
  • Respect "small" sponsors ($500–$10K) — highest-likelihood upgrades; "small to you may be huge to them."
  • Look locally first; prove value, then climb the brand's corporate ladder to regional/national.
  • Current sponsors and exhibitors are your HOTTEST prospects — far easier to grow than to find new.
  • No "magic database" of decision-makers exists. It's old-fashioned research + LinkedIn.

Named free-pipeline plays ("394 warm prospects/year")

  • The Business Breakfast: borrow an office boardroom (≤20 people), coffee + muffins only, one hour before the workday; you + 3 others each bring 3 people; host introduces you; in ≤3 minutes state who you are / what you do / your project / how they can help — and the only two ways to help are (1) buy you a coffee about it in two weeks, or (2) host the same event next month. (~144/yr)
  • The Friday Five: every Friday ~1pm, connect 5 prospects to your referral network with a forwardable intro email. (~250/yr)

9. Stage 6 — Outreach (cold email/call + cadences)

The only goal of outreach is to book the discovery meeting. Not to pitch. Not to attach a proposal. "If you email expecting a purchase, you're not prospecting, you're spamming."

The cold email

  • 2 sentences (3 max). "Anything over three sentences, you could just delete the email yourself."
  • The first sentence states that YOUR AUDIENCE is interested in THEIR product (the shared-audience overlap) — not that you'd like their money.
  • Name something specific about them (recent news, a new hire).
  • Ask for 15 minutes, and propose a specific time yourself ("Tuesday or Thursday?") — don't ask "what works for you?"
  • No attachments. No emojis. Never use the word "sponsorship" (or "partnership"). Use a real personal email, never info@/sponsorship@.
  • Add: "If I've reached the wrong person, could you point me to the best contact?" — a "wrong contact" reply is a useful answer.

Find the right human + a warm "in"

Search LinkedIn for titles with Brand, Marketing, Business Development, Communications, Sponsorship, Product, Sales. Always seek a warm introduction first (a "friend of a friend" is enough) — it's the single most-repeated outreach tactic.

Follow-up cadence

  • 7-day cadence: Day 1 email, Day 2 email, Day 3 email, Day 4 call, Day 5 email, Day 6 email, Day 7 call — on one contact at a time. No response after 3 contacts (~3 weeks) → move on. Silence is a "no."
  • Voicemail works when email doesn't — a 30-second voicemail naming the same audience overlap + same 15-min ask "proves the property is real."
  • The 7×7 method: 7 touches across 7 departments (Marketing, Sales, Sponsorship, Comms, Brand, Product, HR), ~3 contacts each ≈ 21 contacts/company. Call line: "Yep, that's me — I sent you three emails; the 7×7 method says I have to phone now."
  • "Pleasant but Persistent": a "no" this year ≠ a "no" next year. One client courted an org 8–10 years before they became a stage sponsor. Each cycle: "What I proposed didn't work — what else are you looking for?"
  • When a sponsor says no, immediately call their competitor. One rep grew a competitor to 10× the lost sponsor's spend; when the original wanted back in — "No."

10. Stage 7 — Discovery meetings

Discovery is NOT a sales meeting — it's a fact-finding mission, "interview like a journalist," "sniffing each other out." The real goal of the first meeting is the second meeting (plus the intel to build a tailored offer).

  • Bring NOTHING. No proposal, package, one-pager, pricing, or leave-behind. In the strictest version, not even a pen (writing things down the instant they speak makes prospects "feel preyed upon" — dictate notes into your phone afterward). Softer version: a blank notebook + a short question list. "How do I know what to give them when I don't know their goals?"
  • Ask 5–7 questions (10 max) from your ~37-question bank — never all of them ("a meeting, not an interrogation"). Tailor and go off-script. Talk ~20% of the time; ~90% of what you say ends in a question mark.
  • Sequence: start in the audience space → then their business goals → then their success metrics. Walk in already knowing the Googleable basics.
  • The single most powerful question: "What do you mean by that?" — even when the answer seems obvious. "The first-level answer is almost never the real answer, in sales and in marriage." Peel the onion: "Can you tell me more?" → "sales fell" becomes "sales dropped 32%, down 15% YoY."
  • Ask budget directly and early: "What's your budget?" (not "Do you have a budget?") — then go silent (the game of chicken). If they truly have none: "Great — that means we can do some really cool stuff, as long as we can show ROI."
  • Record (with permission) to harvest the sponsor's exact words for the proposal. Confirm the avatar back to them. Summarize the diagnosis before offering any solution.
  • No elevator pitch — ever. Don't say "sponsorship." Use a work-appropriate icebreaker (a sports team or hit show beats the weather; never "so how's that [team]?").
  • End with BAMFAM (book the next meeting before leaving) + the fit/permission close (§17).

11. Stage 8 — Proposals & business cases

  • Two distinct documents:
  • Business case = sent before a meeting, no pricing/grids, ≥50% audience data + activation ideas + case studies. Its only job is to earn a discovery meeting.
  • Proposal = built after discovery, has values. "If there are dollar figures in it, it's a proposal."
  • Skip the proposal unless asked. Chris has sold "tens of millions with no proposal" (discovery → contract → invoice); the $1.8M/yr × 10-yr deal used none. A proposal asked for early (after 5 min / 1–2 emails) is a brush-off; after several meetings it's genuine.
  • Never attach a proposal to a cold email / never mass-blast unsolicited proposals — straight to trash, and you get blocked. "Like throwing limp spaghetti at a wall." Marketing directors get ~10,000 packages a month (a team of 3 just to decline them).
  • Deliver a draft live in the second meeting and shape it together ("the Hybrid Proposal" — standardize the audience-data parts, customize the rest).

The 6-page proposal (the "50% Rule")

~6 pages. NEVER write "sponsorship proposal/package" anywhere (incl. the title). ≥50% must be audience data. 1. Title/cover: event name, tagline, date, a great photo, website. Keep it simple. ("The more you add, the more likely they delete it.") 2. Audience (before money): current data, niched demographics + psychographics, charts not paragraphs, a photo of a packed event. 3. Opportunity (≤3 short paragraphs): one on your org (no sob story), one on the event, one on attendees/market research. 4. Menu (NOT "levels"): title it by the objective ("Engage Leaders in Industry X"), list items by what they help with. Never end with "Don't see something you like? Call us and we'll customize" — the single worst line. 5. Sample activations (3+ types: contest, sampling, email capture, social) — they prove you know the game and drive conversation. 6. Contact + CTA: a real personal email; invite them to say what's missing. No pricing, no order form, no payment cutout.

Use the prospect's exact discovery language in the proposal — recognition accelerates closing.

12. Stage 9 — Negotiation & closing

  • Money comes up only after valuation, during negotiation — never upfront.
  • BAMFAM — Book A Meeting From A Meeting (signature tactic). Schedule the next step before you part: "You want a proposal? Absolutely — how does Wednesday at 2 look?" If they won't book a next step (or won't shape the proposal, or won't connect you to decision-makers), you have "happy ears" — a hidden objection. Without a booked next step, momentum dies "like a slippery fish."
  • Hunt for "no." Treat everything other than "where do I put my card" as a "no" to handle. "All prospects are liars" — ~50–70% who say "send me a proposal" never buy. No-response is worse than "no" ("the kiss of death").
  • Invite objections in the room: "Why might this not work? What will your decision-maker say? If in 6 months it didn't work out, what went wrong?" — better there than as ghosting later.
  • Complaint ≠ objection. "That's expensive," "I'm too busy" are complaints; respond with "What do you mean by that?" "You can't overcome an objection you believe — so stop believing it."
  • Negotiate by adjusting the package, not the number arbitrarily: too expensive → remove assets; want more outcome → add assets and raise price.
  • Pin down a hidden budget: "If I put a $500K proposal in front of you, could you say yes? No? Stop me — $400K? $300K? $50K?"
  • Find other decision-makers without asking "who decides": "So I invite the right people on my end — who on your end should be part of this so we get it right?"
  • Tiered options are acceptable ONLY live, after discovery, anchored to their stated problem: #1 solves it at budget; #2 "twice as much for 60% more"; #3 "sign 3 years, get 50% off."
  • The "do-nothing" close (million-dollar case): "What would we do with a $1M cheque? Nothing. We'd lock it in the safe and book a meeting — until we know what your customers value and what your brand stands for, we don't have the right to spend a penny. If we can't do what you need, we give it back." Silence is your most important tool.
  • The "is it fair?" close: "It's really hard for people to say something isn't fair… unless they're being unfair."
  • A literal "no" is final — walk away (and don't burn the bridge). A brush-off, "not right now," or "no budget" is not final.
  • Always lawyer the contract. A contract ≠ a proposal. Only sign deliverables you can fulfill.

13. Stage 10 — Fulfillment & reporting

The fulfillment report is "the most valuable tool every sponsorship property has" — proof you delivered, and the on-ramp to renewal. Always produce one, for a $500 sponsor or a $1M sponsor.

  • Track everything you promised, live, during the event: email opens/clicks, downloads, coupon use, social engagement, attendance, web traffic + pages, booth visits, activity participation.
  • Contents (~6 pages): event overview; a two-column chart: "promised + its value" vs. "delivered + its actual value" (proves over-delivery/ROI); a photo of every single thing you promised (esp. the sponsor's activation in action); web/social screenshots (in case posts get deleted); attendance + metrics; ROI per deliverable; thank-you page. Be honest about misses — the sponsor was there.
  • Measure what's in YOUR control, not their conversion failures (you delivered 1,000 leads; their weak landing page converting only 506 is not your miss).
  • Timing: 24–72 hrs ideal; ≤1–2 weeks — fast, before they forget and reallocate next period's budget. The two-week post-event window is the most important timeframe in the calendar.
  • Reality check: getting the cash is ~20% of the work; activation + delivery + fulfillment is the other ~80% (you'll do 10×–100× more work after the cheque).
  • Make-it-right rules (from the horror stories): something will eventually go wrong (that's OK); do everything to fix it; always have an activation strategy; always deliver the report; handle delivery with YOUR team — never trust the sponsor's rep or a third party. (Cautionary tale: a sponsor's own rep hid behind the booth texting — so order booths with no chairs.)

14. Stage 11 — Renewals & upgrades

Renewal is the cheapest revenue there is — a fulfillment-report meeting can open a fiscal year with 60–80% of revenue already booked. Renewals usually earn more.

  • Renew immediately after the event, while it's fresh and before budgets reallocate. "Shiny-object syndrome is lethal."
  • The renewal loop: fulfillment report → book a feedback meeting (not a pitch) → listen, gather feedback → implement it before pitching → make the renewal offer → ask for a referral.
  • The "magic question" (after over-delivering + presenting the report): "What would we have to do to get you to give us twice as much money next year and make it the best marketing investment you've made in five years?" When they agree to 2×, charge 1.5× in exchange for a flat-priced 2-year deal.
  • Re-run discovery and re-value EVERYTHING every year (new and reused assets) — never resell identical assets; prices should rise (you're better + inflation). Even +$200 × 10 assets = +$1,000.
  • If they say no: ask "why not?", always ask for a referral, and make the intro yourself the same day. Don't torch the bridge — stay cordial; they may return or refer.
  • Invest in the sponsorship ASSET, not just the personal relationship — marketing staff turn over and may be gone at renewal.
  • Upgrade exhibitors → sponsors: a sponsor is ~200× an exhibitor's value (~90% margin). Run a discovery session with every exhibitor — they're your hottest prospects.
  • Podcasts/ongoing: report quarterly or monthly, not annually (keeps the sponsor reassured). For multi-year/naming deals, never wait until the end to report.

15. Scaling, team & operations

  • Sponsorship is not a part-time or solo job. (One CEO insisted on doing it alone and raised $0 over 2 years.) Even one helper + a daily review beats going solo.
  • Don't cut steps to save time — ask "what can I remove from my schedule?" not "what can I skip?" Then delegate.
  • Set small daily goals — the "3-a-day" method: every day, move 3 people at each stage (research 3 leads, qualify 3, send 3 intro emails, call 3, run 3 discovery sessions, draft 3 proposals, follow up 3). "Do this every day and you will make budget, then exceed it."
  • Use a sales tracker with ≤7 stages; track activity volume, not revenue.
  • Don't scale mediocrity (Keith Cunningham: "scaling mediocrity only leads to bigger piles of mediocrity"). Fix the leaky bucket before adding hours.
  • Build internal sponsorship culture: invite colleagues to discovery + fulfillment meetings; host an internal Sponsor Summit; limit valuation participants ("too many cooks"); find a protégé so the culture outlasts you.
  • Get the board on board: their job is introductions, not cold-calling or carrying proposals. Give them a strong Case for Support, do the legwork (scripts, drafts, scheduling), and reconnect them to the cause every meeting.
  • Hiring: require a clear opportunity + current audience data + avatars first; pay market rate; never hire for a borrowed Rolodex (it leaves with them — "you paid them to build contacts for your competitors"); ask about their first-90-days plan; track activity not just closes; never publicly shame numbers.
  • Brokerage verdict: avoid full-cycle brokers. "The ask is ~5% of the process," yet brokers take ~20–25% (often evergreen). The "magic Rolodex" is a myth (they got those contacts from their last client — and will sell yours to your competitor). A ~$50K goal can't support a broker; a ~$500K goal funds a full-time in-house hire with money to spare. If you must outsource: defined scope, flat fees, no ownership of sponsor relationships, full transparency.

16. The anti-patterns (what NOT to do)

  1. Gold/Silver/Bronze tiers — the cardinal sin (condemned in ~12+ posts). ~95–97% of sponsors reject them; some refuse to read tiered decks. They force sponsors to buy junk to get the one asset they want, breeding resentment that kills renewals. Big brands succeed in spite of tiers (other leverage); small orgs get ghosted. It's the rigidity, not the name — renaming tiers doesn't fix it. (Note: a minority of sources do recommend tiers — these are clear outliers against the dominant, relentless anti-tier stance.)
  2. Going in proposal-first. "Send me a proposal" = code for "no thanks." It shortens the sales cycle straight to a no.
  3. Mass-blasting unsolicited proposals / attaching a proposal to a cold email. Trash + spam-block.
  4. Leading with logos. The lowest-value asset; ~⅓¢/view.
  5. Selling your cause/mission instead of your audience. A sponsor doesn't care about your cause.
  6. Vague audiences ("general public," "families," "professionals").
  7. Pricing in the proposal, or guessing/copying valuations.
  8. No fulfillment report → you re-prospect from scratch every year and forfeit easy renewals.
  9. Treating sponsorship as philanthropy / a part-time job / a solo gig.
  10. "Motion vs. action": internal busywork (building packages, polishing tiers, posting on LinkedIn) feels productive but only sponsor-facing steps (discovery, surveys, outreach, fulfillment) move deals. "The hours were never the constraint — the willingness to be told no by a specific person was."
  11. In-kind without valuation — giving a product slot away "free" permanently anchors your audience's worth at $0 to that sponsor; instead buy the product yourself and fold its cost into the valuation.
  12. Sponsorship-marketplace dependency — paying a fee for "promised meetings" while doing no direct outreach. "The property paid to have someone else not do it."

17. Scripts & templates (the verbatim library)

The most complete verbatim collection is in digests/merged_A–D.md §16. Highlights:

Cold email (shared-audience formula, ≤3 sentences):

"Hi [Name] — I run [property], and our audience is [specific niche overlap with their customer]. I noticed [specific recent thing about their brand]. Do you have 15 minutes Tuesday or Thursday for me to learn about your marketing goals and see if there's a fit?"

The "wrong contact" add-on: "If I've reached the wrong person, could you point me toward whoever owns [marketing/brand] partnerships?"

The advice/soft-open (warming a cold contact): "I'd love your thoughts on [event/program]. We'd like to work with [company] in some way and I'd appreciate your advice on the best approach." (Goal: 5 minutes of advice, not a sale.)

The 7×7 phone open: "Yep, that's me — I sent you three emails. The 7×7 method says I have to phone now."

Discovery — the universal probe: "What do you mean by that?" / "Can you tell me more about that?" / "How would you know if we solved it?"

Budget ask: "What's your budget to solve this?" → silence. If none: "Perfect — no budget means we can try some really cool stuff, as long as we can prove ROI."

Decision-makers: "Who else on your end should be part of this so we really get it right?"

The permission/fit close: "It sounds like we might be a fit — would you agree? Is this a good time for me to put together a draft proposal for your feedback?"

BAMFAM: "You'd like a proposal? Great — how does Wednesday at 2 look so we can review a draft together?"

The "just send me a proposal" refusal: "I could, but I'd just be guessing at what matters to you. Give me 15 minutes first so whatever I send is actually built around your goals."

The "do-nothing" / $1M-cheque close: "We'd do nothing with it — lock it in the safe and book a meeting. Until we know what your customers value, we don't have the right to spend a penny. If we can't deliver, you get the cheque back."

The renewal magic question: "What would we have to do to get you to give us twice as much next year — and make it the best marketing investment you've made in five years?"

The "is it fair?" close: "Does that feel fair to you?"

Objection-decoder (treat these as a polite "no" and re-open with questions): "Just send me a proposal," "Let me share this internally," "Let me think about it," "We don't do sponsorship," "No budget," "We only do in-kind," "I'm too busy right now."

18. Glossary of named concepts

  • BAMFAM — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting." Always schedule the next step before the current meeting ends. The #1 anti-ghosting tactic.
  • Property — a sponsorship opportunity / rights-holder; a collection of assets serving a specific audience.
  • Asset — a single sellable unit (logo, booth, e-blast, naming right).
  • Activation — an experiential opportunity producing a measurable sponsor outcome (the thing you actually sell).
  • Inventory — the full list of sellable assets (target ~100).
  • Avatar — a named, niched buyer persona (≥3) with ~25 data points.
  • Brand value — the intangible % uplift from reaching an audience through you vs. raw media (+10–30%/factor).
  • Business case — a no-price pre-meeting document whose job is to earn a discovery meeting.
  • Fulfillment report — post-event proof-of-delivery doc; the top renewal + prospecting tool.
  • Concentric Circles — the 4-ring prospecting method built from audience data.
  • Happy ears — believing a prospect's polite brush-off is genuine interest.
  • The 50% Rule — ≥half of any proposal must be audience data.
  • Moves management / 3-a-day — track pipeline by activity volume; move 3 prospects per stage daily.
  • The 7×7 method — 7 touches across 7 departments to warm a company.
  • Motion vs. action — busywork that feels productive vs. the sponsor-facing steps that actually close.

20. Industry-specific playbooks

  • Festivals & events: audience data + tailored activations + strategic timing; sell experiences (sampling, contests, VIP, charging stations), not logo soup; secure media sponsors first, then cash sponsors.
  • Nonprofits/charities: sponsorship is marketing, not fundraising — your audience is your donors/volunteers/supporters, not the people you serve; seek audience alignment, not mission alignment; commission-based selling risks charitable status (AFP/CRA/IRS scrutiny).
  • Sports/athletes & motorsport: car/suit/helmet livery is the rare place logos hold value; "the sky is the limit" on prospects (almost any non-conflicting brand); sell the fanbase's data.
  • Podcasts/influencers: value via CPM/CPA + (downloads ÷ spend) × 100, then add assets/activations; report monthly/quarterly; pivot to who your audience is (a martial-arts podcast pivoted to recruiters/HR firms → six figures).
  • Conferences/associations: B2B audiences with budget authority are gold; upgrade exhibitors into sponsors (200× value); decision-maker access is the premium asset.
  • Schools/municipalities: naming rights, programs, facilities; conservative stakeholders need the "marketing not begging" reframe.

21. Applying this to BuilderShip (a tailored starting plan)

BuilderShip is the Nebius/Oakland boat hackathon. Translating the system to a tech hackathon:

  1. Define the audience as data, not "developers." Survey/segment registrants: roles (founders, ML engineers, devrel), seniority, employers, what cloud/AI/dev tools they currently pay for and intend to buy, budget authority, follower counts, what they build. Aim for 25+ data points and 3 avatars (e.g., "open-weights tinkerer building agents," "seed-stage founder choosing infra").
  2. Build the inventory beyond logos: API credits redemption, a sponsor's model/tool as the "official" stack, judging slots, workshop/talk slots, a challenge track named for the sponsor, a recruiting/lead-capture booth, the after-party, the boat itself (naming), data on what tools teams chose.
  3. Value each asset against what that outcome costs the sponsor elsewhere (a dev-conf booth, a paid devrel campaign, CPL for qualified dev leads — which are expensive).
  4. Prospect from the audience data: the dev-tool, AI-infra, and cloud brands your builders already use → who advertises to them → who should → competitors. (Nebius is the host; the list is sponsors adjacent to the audience, not Nebius's competitors necessarily.)
  5. Direct outreach, 4/week, to named DevRel/marketing/partnerships leads — "our audience is [N open-weights agent builders who deploy on GPU cloud]; 15 minutes Tuesday or Thursday?" No deck.
  6. Discovery → custom activation (a sponsor wants qualified ML-engineer leads + brand love with the open-source crowd → sponsor the agent track + run a "build on our API" challenge with a prize).
  7. No tiers. Custom offers anchored to each sponsor's recruiting / awareness / developer-adoption goals.
  8. Fulfillment report within a week: leads captured, demos built on their tool, social reach, photos of their activation, talent funnel — then renew for next year at the fulfillment meeting.
