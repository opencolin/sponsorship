## 1. Frameworks & Processes

### The Sponsorship Sales Process (canonical step order — recurs across nearly every post)
1. Audience research (survey first — this is the foundation)
2. Prospecting (build list from audience data)
3. Outreach (email/call to get a MEETING, never to send a proposal)
4. Discovery session (NOT a sales meeting — diagnose problems)
5. Proposal — only when the prospect asks for it (usually 2nd–4th meeting)
6. Negotiation
7. Close / sponsorship dollars
8. Activation
9. Fulfillment (+ fulfillment report)
10. Follow-up meeting → renewal

> "Imagine if you were doing an oil change on your car or baking a cake and you followed two steps and then skipped right to the end. Your car probably wouldn't even start." Skipping steps (esp. cold-emailing the proposal) is the #1 error.

### The "Sponsors on Demand" Cadence Framework (leads daily, meetings weekly, sponsors monthly)
- **Leads every day:** know audience (20+ data points/segment) → mine survey for brands → prospect via 3 sources (brands audience uses; brands that advertise to audience; endemic brands) → add 3–4 competitors per brand. Nets "hundreds and hundreds," sometimes thousands of prospects.
- **Meetings every week:** reach out to get a meeting (not send proposal). Goal benchmark: 1–2 meetings/week. **Need ~40 meetings on average to hit most sponsorship sales goals.** Easier to do 1–2/week than cram 40 right before the event.
- **Sponsors every month:** flows naturally from leads + meetings + discovery + BAMFAM.

### The 7×7 Outreach Method
- Take ONE contact in ONE division at a sponsor company.
- Send 3 emails, then 1 phone call, then 2 more emails, then another phone call = 7 contact attempts.
- "7×7" = 7 contact attempts for 7 access points (departments).
- Only reach out once per business day. Space it out.
- If no response, move to next contact, then next division, then cross the company off.

### Email Outreach Cadence (the explicit 7-day sequence — recurs in Festivals + Emails posts)
`Email #1 (Day 1) → Email #2 (Day 2) → Email #3 (Day 3) → Phone Call #1 (Day 4) → Email #4 (Day 5) → Email #5 (Day 6) → Phone Call #2 (Day 7)`
- Applies to one person; if silence, repeat full cadence with next contact, then third contact.
- If the third person doesn't reply, review your emails for the faux pas (below).
- Clients following this cadence get ~30% email OPEN rate.

### Seven Access Points to Sponsorship Dollars (departments to target inside a company)
Sales, Sponsorship, Marketing, Branding, Communications, Product Development, HR.
- Get contact info for 1–3 people per department → ~21 people per company to potentially meet.

### The Prospecting Formula
`A1 + I(A2 × V) = P`
- A1 = Audience; I = Inventory; A2 = sponsorship Assets; V = market Value; P = Prospect.
- Full form: **Audience + Inventory (Sponsorship Assets × Market Value) = Prospects.**

### The Four Prospect Circles / Groups (warmest → coolest; recurs in Geography + Festivals)
1. **Group 1 (warmest):** brands the audience said they use/consume/spend on (from survey).
2. **Group 2 (warm):** brands that advertise/market TO your audience.
3. **Group 3 (cooler):** brands that SHOULD be interested based on audience data.
4. **Group 4 (coolest):** competitors of every brand in Groups 1–3 (add 3–4 each). Easily 60–100+, sometimes thousands of prospects.

### Festival Sponsorship — 5 Steps
Asset/Inventory building → Valuation → Audience segmenting → Sponsorship proposal → Fulfillment.
- **Inventory building:** brainstorm the MOST complete asset list possible (don't judge value yet). Visit the venue, photograph it, note logo spots, traffic flow, interactive opportunities. Analogy: grab every garment that might fit before trying any on.
- **Valuation:** cut the list down to highest-value assets (the "dressing room" — keep what fits). Value varies by asset type, geography, audience, and individual sponsor. Ask the sponsor which assets they care about.

### Activation — 3 Simple Steps (Activation Made Easy)
1. Survey your audience (find what they value, dislike, want more of).
2. Talk to your sponsor about the survey results (→ app sponsorships, contests, co-created content).
3. Discuss measurable outcomes with the sponsor (leads, awareness lift, engagement) → design activation around BOTH audience value and sponsor goals.

### Crisis Decision Framework (Cancel / Postpone / Move Forward)
- Three options: Cancel, Postpone, Move Forward. EVERY option (including "change nothing") is a decision with consequences and costs money.
- MUST consult lawyer, accountant, and venue BEFORE deciding.
- MUST talk to sponsors — "Talk now and talk often." Hearing your plans first via social media = "a very serious error."
- The 4 questions used to decide (and chose Postpone):
  1. What is best for the sector and the industry?
  2. What is best for the attendees?
  3. If it were us, or those we cared about, attending, how would we want to be treated?
  4. In five years, which choice will we look back on as the best possible choice?

## 2. Tactics & Techniques

### Prospecting
- Choose prospects by **audience alignment, NOT depth of pockets / name recognition.** "The more money they have, the harder it will be to get them to give you some."
- Mine your audience survey for brands mentioned; then consume the media your audience consumes and collect the brands advertising there.
- **Endemic sponsors** (products align with your property, e.g. food brands at a food festival) have value but are NOT your warmest prospects — "should not be your bread and butter."
- Categorize prospects into A/B/C lists. If no A-listers want to meet, the trip/effort isn't worth it — "You don't really have a sponsorship pipeline anyway."

### Outreach
- The ENTIRE point of outreach is to book a MEETING, not to pitch/send a proposal.
- **YOU propose the specific date/time** (e.g. "Does Thursday at 4:15 work?"). Don't ask "when are you free?" — causes email ping-pong. Then make yourself available.
- Never use the word "sponsorship" in outreach. Frame as "pick your brain about a project," "see if there's common ground."
- Personalize every email; use the contact's name; never "to whom it may concern."

### Discovery Meetings
- Discovery = a "getting-to-know-you" meeting to determine (a) the prospect's pain points and (b) whether you can help. NOT a sales meeting.
- Build rapport with small talk first (weather, sports, pets — keep surface-level; avoid sex/religion/politics/anything too personal).
- Ask 5 questions max (per "9 Things"); narrow to 5–10 of the 37 discovery questions; insert them casually, NOT back-to-back ("not a good-cop/bad-cop interrogation").
- Discovery flow: rapport → audience → marketing goals → challenges/roadblocks → diagnose → recommend tailored assets/activations.
- Ask permission to take notes / record at the start.
- Don't bring the proposal. Don't give an elevator pitch. Don't use sales jargon.
- Starting with audience questions "makes me look like I know what I'm doing because I'm starting with the important stuff."
- End EVERY discovery session by booking the next meeting (BAMFAM).

### Proposals
- Lead with audience data and assets/activations — NOT your mission/cause.
- Only ONE paragraph on ONE page for your cause/mission (per the proposal template).
- Send a proposal only AFTER it's requested. Make it custom, post-discovery.
- For far-away/international prospects: send a **business case**, not a proposal (see §5).

### Negotiation & Closing
- Don't try to close in the discovery session — but if the sponsor offers (rare), take it (Andrea closed $250K mid-discovery).
- You do NOT have to close in person, even on a travel trip. Realistic in-person goals: have the discovery session, hand over proposal copies (bring several), plan activations.
- Believe every objection literally (see §3, objection handling).

### Activation & Fulfillment
- Activation = "switching on" the sponsorship; making sure the sponsor uses EVERY asset they paid for, AND achieving their outcomes (not just delivering outputs).
- Build an **activation strategy/plan**: use the package as a guide; outline key dates, deadlines, and the team member accountable for each piece. Sets up the fulfillment report.
- Brainstorm activations WITH the sponsor (set questions, see §3).
- Capture social content from every activation; design activations to be shareable (use event + sponsor hashtags).

### Renewal
- Begin renewal "as soon as possible after your event" — waiting risks the sponsor filling their schedule.
- Renewal = treat like a NEW opportunity: new discovery-like meeting → new audience data → new custom assets/activations → re-value (the renewed opp is usually MORE valuable; don't reuse the old value). You CANNOT reuse old assets — the sponsor has new needs.
- Deliver fulfillment report → hold follow-up meeting → use intel to build a better proposal for next year. "There is no better way to increase your retention rate and move sponsors up the ladder."

## 3. Scripts, Templates & Exact Language

### Cold-email faux pas to AVOID (verbatim list)
- Sending emails and expecting instant money.
- Writing really long emails (5,000-word emails are "always the wrong approach").
- Attaching photos (triggers spam filters; prospect doesn't want them yet).
- Skipping a subject line (blank = "reeks of spam").
- Mass-emailing your entire contacts list (recipients see all others; reads as spam; and if 30 say yes to "2:30 Tuesday," you can only take one).
- Attaching your sponsorship proposal (the biggest faux pas).
- Emojis in subject line → "Straight to the trash."

### Email length benchmark
Average email body should be **50–125 words** (cites Campaign Monitor). Not 5,000, not 1,000, not even 500.

### Email Template #1 — When you don't know who to contact
> _"Hey Ray, Any chance you can suggest the best contact for those in the product X side at your company? Thanks, [your name]"_

Typical reply: _"Hey Chris, Yes, you would contact Mike. He is the product X marketing manager. I've copied him on this email."_

Then to Mike:
> _"Hi Mike, Just following up on Ray's note. I would love to set up a quick call to discuss some of your areas of focus and see if there is some common ground between your priorities and our plans for the year. How does your schedule look on Wednesday? Does 3:00 work for a phone call?"_

(Note: never mentions "sponsorship." These emails led to an **$85,000** deal.)

### Email Template #2 — Cold email
> _"Hey [name], I saw [on social media, website, etc.] that you are involved in related products/projects. I would love to connect and ask your thoughts about a cool project I'm working on. Are you free tomorrow at 3:00? [your name]"_

(Feeds the recipient's ego by asking advice; focused on THEM.)

### Email Template #3 — Noteworthy company from audience research
> _"Hi [name], I saw on [social media site, website, etc.] that you are involved in the marketing side of [their company's name]. I recently surveyed our database and [company name] had a strong showing. I'm wondering if you have a couple of minutes to talk about what we found and whether our audience is one of your priorities? Any chance you're free this week for a quick call? [your name]"_

### Small-shop first-email content rule (Three-Headed Hydra)
The email should contain "as little as possible: No details about the event, no gold-silver-bronze levels, no titles in your email signature, no asks of any kind, simply a request to pick their brain about a project you're working on and a proposed date and time for a meeting."

### Athlete cold-email rules
- Address with a personal touch (use their name).
- Introduce yourself with credibility hooks ("five-time winner of X marathon," "podcast host," "athlete with 100,000 followers").
- Keep to ~3 sentences: who you are, what you want, why they'd be interested.
- Do NOT ask for money — offer a solution ("I love your products and would like to introduce them to my fans").
- Suggest a time for a call. NEVER send a proposal/sales letter/attachment. (Email signature with social links is fine.)

### The 4 Objections + exact responses (Sponsorship Follow-Up — verbatim)
Core rule: **"No matter what a prospect tells you…believe them."** "In the world of sales, only one word means no…and that's 'NO.'" Hearing "no" is GOOD; hearing "maybe" is bad.
1. **"We don't have the budget right now"** → not a no. Find out when they do their budget; offer to split payments across their fiscal if they come on board now; or ask what budget they're working with. "They will either tell you or give you a firm no. Either way, it's a win!"
2. **"I'm really busy and don't have time"** → not a no. "Tell them that you're sorry for interrupting a busy time and ask them when you should plan to get back in touch." OR offer a 5-minute call — if they don't like it, promise never to call again and they can hang up at the 5-minute mark.
3. **"Just send me something by email"** → sounds like yes but isn't. Don't send a deck. Reply that everything you do is custom and ask about their goals.
4. **"Let me think about it"** → your signal to ask what they need to make a decision; ask them to bring their decision-makers to the table and you'll do the same.

> "The question [how many objections to overcome] isn't relevant… Find solutions for your prospects until there are no more solutions to be found, at which point wish them luck, thank them for their time…and then ask for a referral."

### The "what do you mean by that?" technique (Tech Support)
- Sponsors talk in double-speak. The single best question to bust through BS: **"What do you mean by that?"**
- Forces a more truthful answer. Use SPARINGLY (asking it after every reply is "a lot weird" and derails the conversation).

### Brainstorming-session questions to ask sponsors (Activation Defined — verbatim list)
- Considering the theme of the event, what will our audience like to see?
- What have you done successfully at past events or promotions?
- What will be of no interest to our audience based on your experience?
- Is there anything you can see in our packages our audience might hate?
- Which experiences could you offer that will help offer solutions and solve problems for attendees?
- Do you see any potential challenges with what we are proposing?
- Is there anything you would add to our package?

### Discovery questions — by category (Discovery Questions post)
**Audience:**
- Tell me more about your ideal customer.
- I'm very curious whether we share common goals and an audience. Could I briefly tell you about our audience?
- What kind of feedback do your customers give you that you think we could possibly help with?
- What kind of audience data matters most to you?

**Business Goals:**
- How do your leads and customers travel through the sales funnel?
- How have you moved customers through the sales funnel with past sponsorship properties?
- What is a successful partner goal outcome to you?
- Where are you having the hardest time connecting with ideal customers?

**Success Measures & ROI:**
- What kind of advertising are you using? Are you pleased with the outcomes?
- How have you worked with sponsorship properties? What's worked and what hasn't?
- How much does networking matter to you? Who do you want to meet at a networking event?
- Is brand awareness a major advertising goal? Why or why not?

**Tech-support post additions:** ask about sales goals (next year/quarter); their marketing plan & budget; their target audience; the average customer's decision-making process (independent vs. needs nurturing/discount); whether they must build awareness or pay for marketing, and how much. (These can't be Googled — that's the point.)

**Athlete sales-meeting questions:** Who is your target audience? How do you normally engage in sponsorship? What does your target market value? Sales goals for the coming year? Most important elements of sponsorship?

### Follow-up meeting questions (Activation/Fulfillment/Renewal — verbatim)
- What did you think of the fulfillment report?
- How did the event or campaign go in your opinion?
- Did you see the reaction you hoped for from your target demographic? How can we improve that next year?
- We would love to have you back next year, what can I change or improve to make this a must-do campaign/event again?

### Membership-survey questions (Associations)
- What are your greatest business challenges?
- Where do you feel you can offer more to your clients/patients?
- Where do you feel your practice/company needs to invest more?
- Main business objectives for the next year? Next five years?
- Main challenge in retaining talent?

### Contract language — Weak vs. Strong (Contract Red Flags — verbatim)
**Deliverables:**
- Weak: "Sponsor will receive social media promotion and brand visibility."
- Strong: "Sponsor will receive: Four dedicated Instagram posts; Logo placement on the event homepage; Inclusion in two attendee email campaigns; One on-site activation booth; Category exclusivity within financial services."

**Payment:**
- Weak: "Payment due prior to the event."
- Strong: "50% payment due upon signing. Remaining balance due 30 days before the event date."

**Exclusivity:**
- Strong: "Exclusive within payroll software providers." (narrow, not "technology").

### Words/phrases to AVOID
- "Sponsorship" / "sponsor" with prospects — reframe (see §7). Avoid in proposals, emails, title pages.
- "Just send me a proposal" — "five words you never want to hear."
- Sales jargon (gatekeeper, MQL, puppy dog close, sandbagging, tire-kickers) — prospects aren't impressed.
- Calling a deal a "win win" — "isn't going to cut it" without provable ROI.
- "Always be closing" — replace with "always be opening."

## 4. Numbers & Benchmarks

### Funnel / pipeline math
- **~40 meetings on average** to meet most sponsorship sales goals.
- 1–2 meetings/week benchmark.
- 7×7 outreach = 7 attempts × 7 access points; ~21 contacts per company (1–3 per dept).
- Prospect list: 60–100+ realistic; "hundreds and hundreds," sometimes thousands.
- Email open rate following the cadence: **~30%.**

### Conversion / close
- Andrea closed a **$250,000** deal ~40 minutes into a discovery session.
- Conversion rates in fundraising/sponsorship are "tiny" and competition is "fierce."

### Pricing / value benchmarks
- **Logo value: ~1/3 of a penny per view ("if you're lucky")**; elsewhere "a few pennies up to a few dollars for 1,000 views"; "three cents per view at most." 150–200 views × 3¢ = trivial.
- Re-value renewals — example opportunity went from $35,000 first deal and should be higher on renewal.

### Real dollar figures (case proof)
- A small shop: $0 budget → **$130,000** unrestricted, renewable revenue in ~1 year, working ≤15 hrs/week.
- Breakfast summit: **$1,200** breakfast → rewrote strategy → **+$150,000** revenue.
- Upgraded a sponsor by **$10,000** just to grant sponsor-summit access.
- Email Template #1 sequence → **$85,000** deal.
- Custom re-offer sold for **25% more** than the sponsor's original requested option.
- FOCUS case (Morgan): ~$350K avg (cap $800K) → **$2.1M within 3 years.**
- Memphis in May: ~**$2M+/yr** cash sponsorship (plus in-kind); >$149M generated for Memphis economy.
- Small-audience deals: **$80,000–$150,000** individual deals for an audience of ~150 people; sold dozens. Both a 100s-of-thousands property and a 100s-of-people property each landed **million-dollar** deals.
- A first-year conference: 350 attendees, yet won out on total sponsorship revenue.

### Sponsor expectation stats (Festivals — what sponsors anticipate receiving)
- Reinforce image: 76%
- Increase brand loyalty: 72%
- Boost awareness/visibility: 70%
- Show social responsibility: 43%
- Push product usage/sales: 42%
- Generate more traffic: 39%
- Introduce a service/product: 36%
- Get more databases: 31%

### Audience-data benchmarks
- Need **20 data points per audience segment** (Sponsors on Demand); **25 data points** (Memphis in May); "dozens" for small audiences.
- Re-survey audience at least every **6 months** (data older than 6 months is stale).
- Wait ~2 weeks for survey responses to come in.

### Activation cost facts
- Activation can cost **$500 to $50,000 — or be free**; cost doesn't matter if it solves ≥1 sponsor need + ≥1 audience need.
- Activation spend often **doubles or triples** the sponsorship fee itself (esp. sports/festivals/experiential).
- Golf example: don't take free $500 wine — pay for the wine AND sell a **$3,000** wine sponsorship.

### Audience / market data points cited
- LinkedIn US users (2022): 66.8M.
- Metaverse: ~400M people; up to 80% under age 16.
- Conference example phrasing: "375 event professionals. Up to 30% are VPs and C-suite, 50% mid-level, 20% consultants."
- Endurance athletes: ~79% college-educated; ~73% household income >$75K; spend >$90 on running shoes; near-even gender split; core ages 25–54.
- Golf (PGA): C-suite, lawyers, top management, high income.
- Memorable charity example: 100 attendees, all young professionals looking to buy **$500,000** homes → went to real estate, lawyers, accountants.

### Crisis refund rules
- Cancel → expect to refund sponsors. Stock gold/silver/bronze (logos only) package → chance of partial refund "near zero" (most value delivered AT the cancelled event; no valuation done).
- With a real valuation + known sponsor goals → can compute % of contract delivered; if most value was pre-event, sponsor may accept a partial refund.
- Alternatives to cash refund: credit toward next year / other properties serving the same audience.

### Currency caution (International)
- 1,000,000 yen ≈ only ~$6,600 USD. Always convert with free currency calculators.

### Virtual-event platform pricing (as published)
- Eventbrite: free tickets = no charge; paid = $1.79 + 3.5%/ticket, no max.
- Vimeo: Plus $7/mo (5GB/wk, 250GB/yr); Pro $20/mo; Business $50/mo; Premium $75/mo.
- Brandlive: ~$2,000/mo (unlimited).

## 5. Named Concepts & Terminology

- **BAMFAM** — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting." Always leave a meeting with the next one scheduled. The single biggest desired outcome of a discovery session.
- **Property** — the thing you're selling sponsorship for (event, conference, festival, podcast, organization). "The one making the sale."
- **Asset** — a thing a sponsor can "buy" (logo placement, speaking slot, booth, social posts, naming rights, audience access). Audience = "the crown jewel of your sponsorship assets."
- **Activation** — intentional design of experiences/touchpoints that achieve the sponsor's OUTCOMES while delivering real value to the audience; "switching on" the sponsorship; a form of experiential marketing. "Sponsorship = activation. Period." Logos are "missing 90% of the sponsorship world."
- **Fulfillment** — making sure sponsors take advantage of every asset they paid for; delivering the promised outcomes.
- **Fulfillment Report (a.k.a. post-event report)** — formal report proving you did everything you said. Built as a chart of all promised benefits, marked delivered/not, + stats (attendees, social, web traffic, ROI) + photos of assets/logo/signage/booths/product placement/speaking/action shots. Packaged as a portfolio/digital product. Used to invite the sponsor to a follow-up meeting (reviewed beforehand). Gives sponsors something to "circulate internally to justify the investment again next year."
- **Leverage** — using/activating all elements of the sponsorship to achieve outcomes (distinct from passive exposure).
- **Avatars / Personas** — fictional profiles representing each niched audience segment.
- **Brand value (intangible value)** — your "earning power compared to your regular rate of return"; factors: events you participate in, exclusivity, reputation, offer uniqueness, direct-sales opportunities, activation opportunities, audience loyalty & size.
- **Endemic sponsor** — a sponsor whose products/services naturally align with your property (food brand at a food festival). Has value but not warmest.
- **In-kind / contra sponsorship** — sponsor gives free goods/services instead of cash. "An in-kind donation is NOT a sponsorship." Value gets lost; only worthwhile if it amplifies value (e.g. a gas grill as a contest prize, NOT 1,000 pens).
- **Pass-through benefits** — an asset category for inventory (benefits passed to the audience).
- **Audience segments (3 tiers, ranked):** B2B audience (highest — make business decisions, access business budgets) > VIP audience (upper-middle-class, wealthier, donate/buy generously) > General Population (lowest — unqualified leads; goal is to make this segment "your smallest… even nonexistent").
- **Discovery session / Sponsor summit** — summit is "a discovery session on steroids" (informational, two-way; NEVER a place to close).
- **CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility)** — corporate-giving program that "operate[s] much like foundation grants"; one stream of corporate fundraising. Have a CSR strategy but not to the exclusion of sponsorship (which is unrestricted revenue).
- **Cause marketing** — distinct third stream beyond CSR and sponsorship.
- **Business case** — a low-pressure, informal alternative to a proposal for far-away prospects. Contains NO pricing/levels/exhibiting. Focuses on: **Where** the audience comes from, **Who** they are, **What** they earn, **Why** they're important, **What** they buy.
- **ABO (Always Be Opening)** — Greg Hickman's mindset; replaces ABC. "Which relationships can I open today?"
- **Local champion** — an accessible insider (e.g. a store manager) who becomes your foot-in-the-door, vs. trying to reach the unreachable CEO.
- **RFID** — tech used to gate VIP areas / limit participation at events.
- **Naming rights** — temporary (occasionally permanent) moniker bestowed on a sponsor (e.g. NASCAR Coca-Cola 600; name a festival stage).

## 6. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

### The proposal / package mistakes
- **The sponsorship package is an OUTCOME, not a starting point** — "the sponsorship package doesn't sell your sponsors… YOU DO!" (recurs heavily).
- Writing/rewriting 15–20 drafts of a package by committee — "very few prospects will even read your proposal."
- Leading with a proposal, one-pager, or a giant mission/vision email hoping to sell without meeting.
- Bringing the proposal to the first meeting / discovery session.
- Cold-emailing the proposal (the biggest single faux pas).
- Gold/silver/bronze tiers — sponsors "don't like being forced into paying for a bunch of expensive assets just to get a couple of assets they're interested in." (Hybrid model is "second-best" if you can't fully customize.)

### The "money, money, money" / ATM mistakes
- Treating sponsors as walking ATMs / cash cows. "Even if you're curing children's cancer… the sponsor is not obligated to give you money."
- Selecting prospects because "they have lots of money" / household name recognition. Big brands are oversaturated with requests → low response odds; everyone else wants them too.
- Asking for too little ("a few hundred dollars") — same work as a big deal; not worth the time; doesn't elevate your brand. "Go big or go home" (start higher than a few thousand).

### Discovery / sales-mindset mistakes
- Treating discovery as a sales meeting; giving an elevator pitch; using sales jargon.
- Walking in with no discovery questions prepared (looks like you're not serious).
- Pelting the prospect with too many questions (5 max in first meeting).
- Jumping to a quick/simple solution too fast (Tech Support lesson) — kills trust even when you're right; "you earn the right to offer up a quick and easy solution" only after listening.
- Pulling on heartstrings / guilt (Car Sales) — the salesperson talking about their kids' college / Disney trip instead of answering the customer. Empathy plays backfire.

### Audience mistakes
- No audience research / stale data (>6 months).
- Bragging about raw audience size instead of niched quality. "A sponsor would rather have 50 event attendees who fit their target market than 6,000 people who don't."
- Believing a small audience disqualifies you (self-fulfilling prophecy; broadcasts lack of confidence).

### Logo / vanity mistakes
- Over-relying on logo placement (worth ~1/3¢ per view). Only achieves brand awareness, which many sponsors already have or don't want.
- Focusing on vanity metrics instead of outcomes.

### Follow-up / objection mistakes
- Not following up (out of fear). Follow up after ~1 week of silence; follow up again a few days later; then call it.
- Interpreting objections as a "no" (a career mistake that lost a longtime sponsor to a competitor). Only "NO" means no.
- Pushing AFTER a firm no = actually being a pushy salesperson.

### Fulfillment / renewal mistakes
- Skipping the fulfillment report → "terribly low sponsor renegotiation rates."
- Letting sponsors go at the end of a deal instead of renegotiating (far easier to renew than find new).
- Reusing old assets/activations on renewal (sponsor's needs changed).
- Fudging/lying about metrics in the report — "Your sponsor will find out."

### "Jack of all trades" mistake (Donuts)
- Offering to do everything (logos, speaking, booths) when you're only mediocre at most → can't deliver → no renewal. Pick your "donut" (your strongest asset) and be the best at it. "The niches are in the riches" applies to your own services too. Sponsors want outcomes, not a specific set of assets.

### Skipping valuation
- "Skipping valuation means leaving money on the table" — often thousands to tens of thousands. It's YOUR job to state the baseline value, not the sponsor's. Don't copy online valuation numbers.

### Board-of-directors anti-patterns (verbatim themes)
- "You know who you should contact?" — assuming sponsorship is intuitive (it isn't); the biggest bank/employer has been thought of by every charity.
- Confusing sponsorship with fundraising/philanthropy/mentorship/donations.
- Thinking board members need a package or must make the sale (they don't — their job is intros, one new intro/month).
- Choosing the deepest pockets; ignoring audience.
- Asking for sponsorship two weeks before the event.
- Failing to value; failing to renegotiate.

### Contract red flags (Contract Red Flags)
- Vague deliverables (biggest red flag).
- Unclear payment terms (cash-flow risk).
- Broad exclusivity (e.g. "technology" blocks software/telecom/hardware/IT/AI) — keep narrowly scoped + define duration, geography, online/on-site.
- One-sided cancellation clauses.
- Unclear reporting expectations (define metrics, timelines, data ownership BEFORE signing).
- Overlooked usage rights / content ownership (esp. creators/podcasts — define owner, duration, platforms, paid-ad use, approval).
- Automatic renewals (budget surprises; review notice periods, pricing adjustments, opt-out).

### Activation mistakes (Activation Made Easy)
1. Focusing too much on activation size & visual appeal.
2. Not customizing activations.
3. Failing to value the activation properly.
4. Relying too heavily on automation (QR-only, no human interaction → fails to convert).
5. Ignoring social content capture (un-shareable = value left on the table).
6. Reusing activations.

### Cause-marketing / charity mistakes (verbatim set)
Seeing companies as cash cows; focusing only on CSR/philanthropy; thinking "we have no audience" (you do — it's about WHO, not how many); focusing on logo placement; focusing on the proposal/one-pager; "leaving it to the big shops"; not exploring the full spectrum (CSR + sponsorship + cause marketing).

### Activation that backfired (cautionary tale)
Charity race scheduled multiple long sponsor speeches before the start → delayed the race, ignored competitive runners' mindset → runners left early and complained publicly → sponsors associated with negative sentiment. Lessons: timing matters as much as content; not every segment wants the same thing; activation should enhance, not interrupt.

## 7. Mindset & Principles

### Memorable lines (verbatim)
- "The sponsorship package is not the starting point, but it's an outcome." / "The sponsorship package doesn't sell your sponsors… YOU DO!"
- "The riches are in the niches." (recurs constantly — applies to audience AND your own services.)
- "Sponsorship = activation. Period."
- "The best way to make your sponsors happy is to make your audience happy."
- "No matter what a prospect tells you…believe them." / "In the world of sales, only one word means no…and that's 'NO.'"
- "The second you make a sale, you just lost your warmest prospect."
- "Always be opening" (ABO) > "always be closing" (ABC).
- "It's not about the size of the dog in the fight but how strong its bite is." / "A small audience isn't a weakness but a superpower."
- "Sponsors want outcomes." They don't care if you use assets A–Y or just asset A, as long as you hit their objectives (and don't blow the budget).
- "If your bucket is leaky, it doesn't matter how much water you pour into it." (Sponsorship Alchemy — fix the weak ingredient, don't just send more cold emails.)
- "Pumpkins will not become carriages no matter how much we wish they could." (No magic — low-quality ingredients = low-quality result. Work smarter, not harder.)
- "Sponsors lie" — usually not malicious; they say "send me a proposal / let's meet again" to avoid conflict. Learn to sniff out double-speak.
- On a small audience: "At the end of the day, a sponsor would rather have 50 event attendees who fit their target market than 6,000 people who don't."

### Core philosophies
- **Sponsorship is MARKETING, not fundraising/philanthropy/donations/investment/charity.** "It's marketing!" Speak the sponsor's language (marketing). Prospects "rarely blur the lines between business and philanthropy."
- **Sponsors are CUSTOMERS** (better than "partners," which is too vague). Internally reframe sponsors → customers to prioritize their needs, put dollars on the back-burner, and clarify "what are you selling?" Don't ask a customer for money "right out of the gate."
- **Switch from Asking Mode to Listening Mode.** "Just listen. And maybe drop your agenda too." Solve the prospect's problems, not yours.
- **Think from the sponsor's perspective** — "If you want to catch anything, then you have to think like the fish." Would YOU say yes to your own proposal?
- **You are an equal, not a beggar.** "Talk to your sponsor like a person." Groveling/begging "comes across as very substandard." Believe your opportunity is worthy.
- **Coming up with the right answer is only half the battle** — proving you HEAR and UNDERSTAND the prospect is the other half (Tech Support).
- **Quality of audience > quantity.** Niche down relentlessly. Specific data ("26 male audience members from Chicago, mid-40s, $100K, in finance") beats "15,000 male audience members."
- **Treat ALL sponsors like VIPs / gold** — even small ones; send fulfillment reports to anyone who could become a $100K sponsor ("which could be all of them"). Retention is cheaper than acquisition.
- **Be "pleasantly persistent"** (Kevin Grothe) — pursue until the deal progresses or stalls; silence counts as an answer.
- **Get good at the basics first** — "The best bakers… made basic birthday cakes" before five-tier wedding cakes; "the most masterful mixologists… started with rum and Coke." Build on experience.
- **Real partnerships are built in a crisis through generosity** — one organizer offered NEW digital activations to postponed-event sponsors for FREE: "We want our partners to look back next year and see this as the most valuable marketing investment they made all year, even in a time of crisis. If we expect our sponsors to be flexible and understanding, then we must do the same."
- **A legal solution may not equal a customer-service solution** — "thinking long term about a sponsor might mean some short term sacrifice." Don't take a transactional approach that costs the relationship.
- **You can't over-communicate** in a crisis — "giving your sponsors more information is better than waiting and hoping the problem will go away."
- **There's a place for everyone in sponsorship** (FOCUS) — faith-based, nonprofit, niche, small — "hard doesn't mean impossible." Don't impose a glass ceiling on yourself.
- **Growth comes from working as a team** (FOCUS) — share successes/failures, build a culture of sponsorship.

## 8. Industry-Specific Nuances

### Small shops / charities / nonprofits
- "Small shop" = under $500K/yr revenue (Hydra) OR under $2M revenue / <3 revenue-generation staff (Charity Edition).
- Don't need a fancy package or company list — just an email asking to pick a brain.
- Don't see companies as cash cows; keep CSR separate from sponsorship (sponsorship = unrestricted revenue).
- Small/local companies are LESS inundated with requests, activate more meaningfully, and stick around — organizers report easier meetings in a smaller city; partners "are still people I consider friends many years later."
- Even 100 attendees can be a goldmine if niched (young professionals buying $500K homes → real estate/lawyers/accountants).

### Associations & membership orgs
- Audience is "already segmented" → a "sitting duck" marketing opportunity → prospecting is easier; you can charge a PREMIUM for member access.
- Sell far beyond events: conferences, webinars, contests, social/digital, networking events, lunch-and-learns, newsletters, educational opportunities, product launches, tournaments, discount programs (e.g. insurance), members-only library.
- Price assets by benchmarking industry publications (full-page ad, digital ad rates) and industry conferences (booth/package/level pricing, attendance).
- Make sponsorship LESS intrusive to members (they don't want to feel overexposed to marketing as the price of membership).
- Run an annual sponsor summit; can even send a sponsor's survey to members.

### Boards of directors
- Their role = make 1 new introduction per month (NOT cold-call, NOT make the sale, NOT bring a package). Trust the professional fundraiser. Think audience, not cause.

### Sponsor summits (3 formats — the names are informal)
- **The Beast (full-on):** invite-only, keynote(s), off-site swanky, multi-day, organizer pays ALL costs incl. travel. For: sports teams, major national charities, big facilities; selling six/seven-figure packages.
- **The Mid-Sized ("good enough"):** 2–4 hrs, one industry-known speaker, food + networking + group work + Q&A + thought leadership. Tack onto a major event you already run (you pay event costs, NOT travel). For: five/six-figure sellers; first-timers wanting low risk.
- **The Summit Over Easy (small breakfast):** focus groups of 5 up to 30 people; ~$1,200; can run several across key geographies. A "$1,200 breakfast" rewrote a strategy → +$150K. **Everyone should do these.**
- Universal rules: hold at least once a year, every year, no exceptions; NEVER a sales event (recognition, access, information gathering only); never close a deal there; use the invite list as a bargaining tool (invite top 5–20%); offer top sponsors a private closed-door session with senior leadership/thought leaders (no competitors). Fund it by dividing cost across all sponsorship opportunities and billing sponsors.

### Festivals
- Types: music, sports, tech, art, holiday, film, food/beer.
- Reasons to sponsor: publicity, credibility, funding.
- Need LOTS of sponsors → build a big prospect list (100+); expect non-responses.
- Activation ideas: creative/ugly-statue contest (auto-enroll), bathroom branding, phone-charging stations (or branded portable chargers), branded food/cups.
- Send fulfillment report ~1 week after the event.

### Sports / athletes
- Why brands sponsor athletes: brand image, sales, community/PR, product testing, personal touch/face.
- Sponsorship types for athletes: Financial, Product/In-kind, Service, Affiliate, Ambassador.
- "Sponsorship is part of the marketing budget" — even mom-and-pop businesses have one.
- Build brand (pick a persona) → build audience (social, blog, YouTube, podcast, newsletter) → become a thought leader → then prospect.
- Athlete proposal structure (6 pages): Title/letter; Audience; Opportunity (brand+opportunity+audience); Menu & pricing; Sample activations (menu tied to their goals); Contact page + CTA.
- Start with lower-value sponsorships, climb the ladder; winning creates a snowball effect.
- Secure a media partner if you can't get recognition alone.

### Golf tournaments
- Affluent, educated, influential audience (PGA: C-suite, lawyers, top management) → target LUXURY brands + sophisticated activations.
- Standard assets: title; hole/tee/green sponsors; cart; food/drink; ticket/table/invitation/prize branding; speaking; custom emails; signage.
- 6 tips: (1) multiple sponsors per hole (tee-off AND green) — low-level but adds up; be transparent, avoid conflicts. (2) In-kind ≠ sponsorship (pay $500 for wine, then sell a $3,000 wine sponsorship). (3) Get the right to add high-end gifts to the prize table (iPads, trips). (4) Make sponsorship a requirement to play (sponsors theme a hole + enter teams) — no scramble to sell foursomes. (5) Sell a VIP pass at registration (pre-pay all on-course games via credit card + first raffle round). (6) Be strategic about logo placement — a "name-tag sponsor" once got mistaken for the title sponsor.
- Profit, not philanthropy: even charity golf sponsors want to reinforce image with the right audience.

### Marathons / triathlons / endurance
- Audience: ~79% college-educated, ~73% income >$75K, near-even gender split, ages 25–54, year-round training, high charity overlap.
- Go beyond athletic-wear brands → luxury, social-consciousness, recovery tech, health-tech wearables, plant-based/functional nutrition, hydration science, mental-health/meditation apps, telehealth.
- Pain points now: recovery quality, sleep, hydration, burnout, longevity.
- Modern assets: GPS app integrations, livestream branding, sponsored performance data; experience upgrades like digital pacer boards, AR course previews, personalized post-race data, app challenges tied to sponsor rewards.

### Municipalities (cities/townships)
- Funding for capital projects (rec complexes, infrastructure) beyond tax revenue.
- Sponsorship vs. advertising: sponsorship = recognition/marketing partnership; advertising = sale of a specific public space.
- Properties/assets: facility naming rights, arenas, pools, fields, museums, libraries, community centers, marinas, parks/trails, tourist routes/attractions, vehicles, equipment, programs, services, publications, websites, social.
- Segment by asset (a playground attracts parents/kids; a rink hosting other towns attracts a broader audience). More specific = more value.
- Price via trade-mag ads, Google AdWords, PPC, local paper/digital/radio/TV rates.
- Use exclusivity carefully: one presenting sponsor + official category sponsors (drink/snack/sunglasses); also a negotiating tool to retain a major sponsor.
- Multi-year agreements reduce prospecting and suit naming rights (consistent recognition); set a threshold above which a multi-year deal is required.
- **Municipal sponsorship policy is mandatory** — define acceptable sponsors (no tobacco naming a kids' playground), execution/activation, roles/responsibilities, revenue types, approvals (council involvement, # members present), legal implications, funding allotment (not toward salaries / personal gain), third-party rules. Protects councillors; consult a lawyer.
- City/town reps get less-threatening responses than event reps ("less clear you are trying to sell them something").

### Creators / personal brands (newsletters, Substack, LinkedIn, podcasts, coaches)
- Shift: brands now invest in niche, highly-engaged, trusted creators over celebrities.
- Sponsors evaluate: audience relevance, engagement consistency, demographics, credibility, content quality, brand safety, community trust, posting consistency, conversion potential — NOT just follower count.
- Niche > scale: a cybersecurity consultant with 8,000 engaged subscribers can beat a general creator with 250,000 passive followers; a B2B SaaS sponsor prefers 5,000 engaged ops leaders over 100,000 weak followers.
- Package by outcomes/integration, not rigid deliverable lists: newsletter placements, sponsored podcast segments, LinkedIn content partnerships, webinars, community sponsorships, branded series, dedicated emails, product integrations.
- Report honestly: open rates, CTR, engagement, downloads, referral traffic, lead quality. Don't overpromise ROI you can't measure.
- Smaller creators are more sponsor-ready than they think — target companies already serving their audience.

### Podcasts
- Host-read ads are valued because listeners trust the creator; long-term integrations; social posts as an activation; sit-down interviews as an activation.

### Conferences / virtual events
- Virtual tactics ≈ physical, with caveats: confirm the sponsor is still on board; be upfront about changed funding needs (could be more OR less); platforms — Eventbrite, Vimeo, Brandlive, Cramer, Jack Morton.
- If a sponsor already paid and you cancel/go virtual → refund or credit; keeping money + not delivering invites lawsuits and reputational damage; give the sponsor an out.
- Virtual perks: wider audience (no travel/hotel), per-session metrics reveal popular topics.
- In crises, silence from a prospect = a "no" (the sponsor may be uninterested, not taking partnerships, or out of business). Don't wait for a formal rejection.

### Metaverse
- Platforms: Roblox, The Sandbox, Decentraland (Adidas, Domino's, Coca-Cola, Nike, Samsung); NFL Zone (Fortnite), Warner's Rhythm City (Roblox), Decentraland Metaverse Fashion Week.
- Treat it like EVENT sponsorship — still need prospecting, discovery, valuation, budget, metrics.
- Activation ideas: custom skins (vet what you brand — it's permanent), virtual storefronts (e.g. Pacsun's Pacworld), digital concerts/events, virtual apparel (Fortnite × Balenciaga).
- Benefits: cheaper (no venue), more visibility (~400M users), younger audience (~80% under 16), convenience (no physical assets).
- Metaverse KPIs: consumption behavior, attention tracking, path tracking, participation, conversions, sales, email signups, social followers. May be paid in crypto.

### International / geography
- Sharpen value proposition (more risk for an international sponsor). Vet prospects — is a national/international sponsor really worth the strife vs. a local one?
- Send a **business case**, not a proposal (Where/Who/What-earn/Why/What-buy).
- "Go to where your sponsors are located." YOU bend on time zones (you're the one asking). Use tech to avoid travel (sold a deal to an Australian company for a US event from Canada — zero trips, just middle-of-the-night calls).
- Use trips to create DEADLINES ("I'm in town 3 days") — serious prospects step up, non-serious vanish.
- Stack/regiment travel meetings: A-listers first, breakfast for A-listers only, 1 hr per meeting, ≤30 min between, make sponsors come to you (set up at a café). Sample 3-day haul: 2 breakfast + 3 lunch + 3 dinner + 1 networking event + 15 coffee + 1 business breakfast = **35 prospects**. Anchor travel around a networking event.
- Build international following on social (open contests globally); learn the language / hire a HUMAN translator; build on one successful partnership; create case studies; get clear on currency.
- National events that change location yearly: a feature, not a bug — don't exhaust local sponsors; if attendees travel, prospects aren't geography-specific.

### Cause-marketing ethics (interview w/ Mena Gainpaulsingh)
- Every org needs an **Acceptance of Funds / ethical policy** — "Reputation is everything for a charity."
- Most at risk: orgs advocating for a beneficiary group/social outcome (e.g. human-rights charity taking arms-trade money; labor-rights charity working with a discriminatory employer).
- Policy can cover: acceptable company types, acceptable support types (donation/sponsorship/in-kind), terms that could over-burden the charity, and the review process.
- Watch for: reputational risk, perception the company seeks to influence the charity, requests for database access (privacy/data-protection law).
- Ensure ethical sponsorship by: always research sponsors; have unbreakable guidelines; think about consequences; have an ethical policy. Challenges: gray areas, balancing sponsors vs. consumers, transparency, immediate vs. long-term impact.
- Ethical sponsorship = transparency + honesty (rise of ethical consumerism); source companies with CSR policies.

### Audience-data hack (LinkedIn Insight Tag — free)
- Step 1: create a free LinkedIn Ads account (no ads needed) → Campaign Manager.
- Step 2: install the LinkedIn Insight Tag (JS pixel) after the `<body>` tag in your global footer. No personal data; IPs hashed/truncated; identifiers replaced with pseudonyms after a week, deleted after 180 days; doesn't slow the site.
- Step 3: wait several weeks.
- Yields: job function, job title, industry, seniority, location (state/city) — all charted most→least prevalent. Lets you say "30% of my audience works in finance at a manager level." Still recommends a real audience survey (captures brand usage too).
