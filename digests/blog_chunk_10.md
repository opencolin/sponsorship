## 1. Frameworks & Processes

### The Direct Outreach Sequence (the "TSC method," 5 steps)
The system that produces discovery meetings. Order: **(1) Audience data → (2) Prospect selection → (3) Contact identification → (4) Direct outreach → (5) Discovery-led proposal.** Direct outreach is NOT "send a proposal to a list," NOT "post on LinkedIn that you're looking for sponsors," NOT "attend the right networking event." It's a disciplined sequence. "The properties that close deals run this sequence. The properties that do not run it almost always blame timing or the market. The market is not usually the deciding difference. The sequence is."

### The 4-Call Discovery Process (Dave Stewart / Wet Fly Swing podcast)
Discovery is a *sequence*, not one meeting. Runs 3–4 conversations across 2–3 weeks:
- **Call 1 — Discovery:** goals, audience, budget cycle, "what would have to be true."
- **Call 2 — Business case:** walk through what was learned, how the property's audience + inventory line up.
- **Call 3 — Discovery-led proposal review:** draft on the screen, adjusted live, approved or rejected in the room.
- **Call 4 — Negotiation (when needed):** adjustments based on what the prospect needs to take to their CMO/CFO.
Most properties skip calls 2 and 3 — they treat call 1 as the pitch, run out of conversation, default to "I'll send the proposal," the proposal goes quiet. "The slow process is the fast process." Properties that book the follow-up before leaving the discovery meeting close ~2× as often as those who send the proposal cold and wait.

### The Six-Step Event Sponsorship Checklist
1. Determine the kind of event you want to host.
2. Choose the right type of sponsor (media vs. financial vs. in-kind vs. promotional).
3. Create an asset list and valuation for your sponsorship package.
4. Make your sponsorship proposal.
5. Find the right contact and send them your proposal (i.e., secure a meeting first — do NOT cold-attach the proposal).
6. Follow up if you don't hear back.

### The Three Components of a Winning Sponsorship Program — "The 3 Ds"
The program boils down to only three things (NOT the proposal):
- **Data** — audience data (demographic/psychographic/geographic), media footprint (past media partnerships → case studies), and marketing strategy (you must still market the event yourself).
- **Discovery** — a discovery meeting is NOT a sales meeting; learn the prospect's target audience, why they've failed to reach it, business goals, and success measures. Goal: determine whether you can fix their problems with your solutions.
- **Delivery** — offer assets/activations valued by market research; deliver on the contract; produce a fulfillment report (one page = chart listing every asset/activation + whether delivered; note why if not).
"You need data, discovery, and delivery for a sponsorship program to successfully launch."

### The Ultimate Sponsorship Prospecting Formula
**A1 + I (A2 × V) = P**
- **A1** = your Audience (everyone who engages with your activations: attendees, alumni, volunteers, donors).
- **I** = your Inventory of sellable things.
- **A2 × V** = your Assets × their market Value (= the inventory). Intangible assets without an established market value require finding precedence.
- **P** = your Prospect.
"When you take your audience and add your inventory… your sponsorship prospect will present itself." When clients can't find prospects, send them back to the asset-building stage. Worked examples:
- Heart surgeons + (branding, speaking ops, education events, product giveaways) = Pharma companies, professional insurance companies, BMW/Lexus dealers.
- Future tradespeople (apprenticeship students) + Inventory = Trade unions seeking talent, post-secondary schools, tool manufacturers, safety gear/training, small-business loans/financial institutions.

### The Sponsorship Marketing Funnel (assets mapped to funnel stages)
Same 4 stages as a normal funnel, but you apply *assets* to move the audience through. Asset value increases down the funnel:
- **Awareness** (lowest value): logo placement, social posts, thank-you from the stage. "Like that fast food craving… you spend $5… it sufficed" but isn't worth much.
- **Interest:** product/beverage/perfume samples, free or limited trials, redirecting audience to sponsor's site.
- **Desire:** free test drive, inspiring them to visit the lot/store. "Directly inspiring them to make a purchasing decision."
- **Action** (highest value — called "Action" not "Purchase" because the outcome can be a donation, signup, etc.): the actual conversion. Drives ROI for the sponsor → more ROI for you → renewals.
Key: a sponsor wants a *mix* of all four stages (drives multiple outcomes), not all action-assets. The right mix is only determined via a discovery session.

### The Valuation Process (core method, recurs across many posts)
1. Brainstorm with stakeholders → list EVERY possible asset (50, 100, even 250 — "you want as many assets in the early stages").
2. Research the market value for each asset/activation (what a professional would charge for the same).
3. Do a "gut check" — is your service equal to, better than, or worse than market value? Price accordingly. (e.g., "we're not as experienced in social media as a marketing agency, so maybe we shouldn't charge $3,000 for social media posts.")
4. Discard/set aside low-value assets; keep ~a dozen strong ones.
5. NEVER value based on your expenses, never make up numbers, never directly copy someone else's valuation found online.
The audience is the exception — the *sponsor* sets the value of your audience, so be flexible/omit pricing on it.

### Advanced Valuation Analyses ("Unlocking Your Value")
Layer these on top of basic market research to justify higher value:
- **Brand Equity Analysis:** measure the difference between branded vs. unbranded product/service sales (e.g., why people attend *your* festival vs. any other).
- **Bang-for-Buck Analysis:** for multiple properties under one umbrella (e.g., racing teams/racers). Spreadsheet each property; input annual fee, brand, reach, media partnerships, social; compute weighted average + percentages → pie chart.
- **Financial Analysis** (Vertical, Horizontal/base-year, Technical, Fundamental/intrinsic-value).
- **ROI Analysis:** compare sponsor spend since starting vs. cash-flow advantage you provide. Example: sponsor spent $10K on failed campaigns, pays you $20K, generates $40K → $20K profit → renews. Augments case-study data; essential for renegotiation.
- **Stakeholder Behavioral Analysis (stakeholder mapping):** identifies primary/secondary/key stakeholders, who influences the deal positively/negatively. Stakeholder interests usually reduce to: mental health, security, safety, physical health, environment, time, work, social change, or economics.
- **Business Valuation Analysis:** reviews customer-behavior changes, sponsorship costs, top-line revenue.

### The Audience Survey Process (recurs constantly — the foundational tool)
1. **Talk to your current sponsors first** to learn what audience data they need; add it to the agenda at your next sponsor meeting ("tell them you're working to improve the way you report").
2. Build the survey in sections (see Scripts section for the question bank).
3. Distribute by email and/or physical mail; announce in advance.
4. Incentivize (giveaway entry, discount code, early-bird tickets, free parking, "money can't buy" experience).
5. Wait ~2–3 weeks (one post: wait a month for stragglers).
6. Tabulate → segment into 3–5 niches → niche down further (25+ data points per segment).
7. Use segments to build prospect lists, value audience segments, and feed fulfillment reports.
Re-survey **every 6 months** (then maybe annually once you know your audience). Re-survey again even when renewing with an existing sponsor — treat renewal as a brand-new opportunity.

## 2. Tactics & Techniques

### Prospecting (the audience-led method, the dominant theme)
- **Start with your audience, not a list of big companies.** "Your audience determines the sponsors you work with." Prospecting is NOT pulling names from hats or filling out Fortune-500 sponsorship forms.
- From survey results, **tally the brands your audience mentions** using/wearing/consuming → rank by frequency → top mentions = your **hottest prospects** ("served up on a silver platter").
- **Expand the list outward (the funnel of warmth):**
  1. Hot: brands your audience directly names.
  2. Warm: brands that advertise TO your audience (read the papers/watch the shows your audience consumes for a week; note the advertisers).
  3. Cooler: brands that *should* advertise to that audience but don't.
  4. Coolest: 2–5 direct competitors of every brand already on the list (this is how you reach ~100 prospects).
- **Also mine competitors:** review sponsors of competing events over the past 2–3 years for inspiration (NOT to poach — if they're in multiyear deals you can't get them anyway).
- **Target list size: 50–60 (one post says 100, another says "two hundred companies whose target customer overlaps with your audience").** A long list is "protection" against ghosting/rejection/dropouts. "Bigger is better."
- A data-built list "tends to be shorter and stranger" — includes off-category brands (luxury at a running event, financial services at a music festival, gaming at a nonprofit gala) "because the audience data shows the overlap. The off-category brands are often the most interested."
- **Vetting process** (apply consistently): research website → most-active social platforms → press releases/news from the last several years → older news if needed. Check: what they produce/sell, whether they have sponsorship available, brand values, partner types, controversies/scandals, location (overseas = time-zone + travel costs). Cut mismatches and any sponsor with a *recent* controversy (past-and-resolved is okay).
- **Omnichannel prospecting:** SEO (track brands ranking for your audience's keywords), social search/groups, content marketing, email.
- **Document your prospecting process** (Google Doc / Word / voice-to-text) so you can repeat it next year and get the same outcomes.

### Contact Identification (from "Direct Outreach")
- Find a **specific human** in the marketing function with budget authority — NOT "info@" / "marketing@" / website forms.
- Right titles: **Director of Marketing, Brand Partnerships Manager, Head of Sponsorships, VP Marketing.**
- Find via LinkedIn, the company leadership page, or prospecting-tool directories. Verify by reading their feed/recent press to confirm they own the budget.
- "This step takes longer than most properties expect… A perfect email to the wrong person is identical to a bad email to the right person. Both produce silence."
- Also look for roles in: sales, product development, communications, business development, sponsorship, marketing, branding.
- **Always prefer a warm intro:** ask around your org/partners/vendors for a mutual connection; mention them in the first contact ("like the other person vouching for you"). This avoids the "dreaded cold call."

### Discovery Meeting Tactics
- Informal; can be in-office, Zoom, or phone.
- Purpose: extract info you "can't find on LinkedIn or in press releases" — their real challenges, what they've tried that failed, how they define brand awareness.
- **Ask only 5–7 questions (10 absolute max)** — don't make it feel like an interrogation.
- Ask questions NOT answerable by a Google search (don't ask what products they sell).
- Take notes. Do NOT show a deck. Do NOT quote a price. Do NOT try to close. Leave any sponsorship materials "at the office."
- Cover: their target audience (geo/demo/psychographics — compare to yours), ongoing & recent marketing campaigns (what they targeted, where it fell short), ongoing & recent advertising campaigns, most-prioritized goals (now / next 6 months / next 1–2 years), and their budget.
- If you discover you can't solve their problems → be upfront, thank them, move to the next prospect.
- **BAMFAM**: always book the follow-up meeting before leaving (see Concepts).

### Proposal Tactics
- **Never go "proposal first."** Write the proposal ONLY after the discovery session AND only when the prospect asks for it. Many deals close with no proposal at all.
- Proposal ≈ **6 pages** (yours may be slightly longer/shorter).
- It's an information tool (esp. for sharing rich audience data), NOT a sales tool. "The sponsorship proposal doesn't make the deal — you do."
- **No grids, no pricing, no gold/silver/bronze** in the proposal.
- When a prospect/boss/volunteer demands one, you can build it (see "3 scenarios"), but strip sales language + prices and load it with CTAs.

### Negotiation & Closing
- Move conversations off email/phone into meetings "sooner than later."
- **Wait for the verbal yes** before celebrating; expect several meetings/calls (sponsor must review budget + consult internal stakeholders).
- When a prospect says "just send me a proposal," respond by asking **what they want in the proposal**, and **book a follow-up meeting** to review it together.
- Negotiate asset pricing live, when the time comes — not via a price list.
- "Nobody buys a product called 'sponsorship.' They buy an outcome."

### Activation Tactics (the "secrets")
1. You MUST have a discovery session first (can't build a good activation blind).
2. You MUST know your audience deeply (25+ data points/segment).
3. Budget isn't as important as you think — "You can spend $50 on an activation, but if your audience is happy and so is your sponsor, it did its job." Don't blow your whole budget on one activation.
4. Borrow/be inspired by existing activations you like — but ADD something new (never lift wholesale).
5. **Involve your sponsors in the planning** — bring loose ideas, ask their opinions. "A sponsor understands their challenges best, so they will have the best activation ideas." Don't have activations "set in stone" — rigidity ("my way or the highway") turns sponsors away.
6. Simple is okay — complexity correlates with cost; don't tack on lasers/holograms unnecessarily. Ask "does it really need to be this complex?"
7. Photos and videos are a must — capture footage *during* the event with the audience interacting (hire photographers/videographers, station them throughout). Proves enjoyment to a sponsor rep who may not have attended.
8. Track results / KPIs (attendance, email-list growth before/after, social followers before/after, new leads before/after, conversions, sales) → feed the fulfillment report.

### Fulfillment & Renewal Tactics
- Deliver the **fulfillment report** 1–2 weeks after the event (for title/multiyear: quarterly preferred, or at least annually/semi-annually).
- One page = chart of every promised asset/activation + delivered (yes / over-delivered / no + why).
- Include photos, attendance data, ticket-sales data, ROI metrics, charts/graphs.
- Write the report even if you don't want to renew ("like showing your work").
- Use the report meeting to gather feedback AND open renegotiation. "Book a meeting" to walk through it.
- **Renew at the post-event report meeting** — don't wait (sponsor availability fills up). Treat the renewal as a NEW opportunity: re-survey, re-discover, re-value, create new assets.

## 3. Scripts, Templates & Exact Language

### Direct-Outreach Cold Email (2–4 sentences, structure: specific reason → useful offer → concrete ask)
Working version, quoted verbatim:
> "I am reaching out because we just finished an audience survey for our event, and a meaningful percentage of our attendees identified your category as a top consideration this year. We have audience data your team would probably find useful regardless of whether sponsorship turns out to be a fit. Open to a fifteen-minute call this Tuesday or Wednesday?"

The ask is a **15-minute discovery meeting** — "It does not pitch. It does not include a deck. It does not summarize the sponsorship opportunity."

### Micro-Influencer Outreach DM/Email Template (verbatim)
> _Hi [influencer name],_
> _I hope you're doing fantastic. I'm [name], and [describe who you are a bit professionally]. My brand [explain what your company or organization does briefly]._
> _I've followed your content for a while, and I think our values and interests align well. I feel like your audience would like our [product]. Would you like to collaborate on a YouTube video where you unbox and try our products?_
> _What are your thoughts on this opportunity? I'd love to hear them when you have a minute. Thanks for your time!_
> _Sincerely, [Your name]_

Rules with it: don't dwell on who you are — dedicate most words to mutual benefit; keep it short; be direct (ask for the opportunity); include a link to your media kit + socials; inject personality; **don't send attachments** (spam/suspicious flag).

### Strong Call-to-Action (replaces "send a cheque"/"check a box") — verbatim
> _"If you want to get in front of 40 year old, high net worth moms, with 3+ kids, who plan to buy a new house in the next 18 months, let's talk. Everything we do is custom, so you only pay for the benefits you want. Give us a call let's find something that fits your budget and your goals together. It will only take five minutes, guaranteed."_

Variant: "If your goal is to get in front of 40-year-old, high-network moms who live in the suburbs with three or more children, we want to hear from you." / "Everything we do is custom, so tell us what you're trying to achieve, and we'll provide it!"

### Reframing Script for "We Don't Do Sponsorship" (Joan's script, verbatim)
> "What if we could put you in front of a room of 30 of your ideal buyers, all of whom are in a position to make a purchase within the next 18 months? And what if you were the only one in the room from your industry? And rather than sponsoring the event, you simply paid for the whole thing and gave people a chance to network in a casual, low pressure environment where they could relax and you could get to know them in a more direct way?"

Result: "Oh, well THAT we have budget for! … I have a business development budget of $75K that I could tap into."
General reframe: "what if I could put 30 of your ideal clients in a room… give you special access to them for networking… give them a really specific experience that this audience asked for in a survey? Why don't we build something where you're working directly with your target audience?" — Notice you avoid the word "sponsorship," avoid logos, avoid salesy language. When a sponsor says "we don't do sponsorship," reply: **"neither do we."**

### Audience-Data Objection-Handler Script (verbatim)
> "My property is a conference with 375 event professionals. Of that group, 30 percent are VPs and C-suite, 50 percent are mid-level professionals, and 20 percent are consultants. Which of these audience segments appeals to you the most?"

### The One Discovery Question to Always Ask
> "Our audience is X, Y, Z. Is this an audience segment you typically try to engage?"
(If the sponsor isn't interested in your audience, you aren't a match — and that's okay.)

### Renegotiation Script (from "What Do Your Sponsors Actually Want")
The friend asked sponsors to cross out the assets they didn't care for, asked why they bought them ("to get an asset not in the package"), then asked: if she included **2× more of Asset A, would they pay 2× more?** — **30% of prospects said yes.**

### The Audience Survey Question Bank (verbatim, from "Use Audience Data")
**Geographic & Demographic:** Age; Gender; Annual pre-tax income; Number of children; Highest level of education completed; Employment sector.
**Motivations & Opinions:** What motivates you to participate with this organization? Why did you choose to attend this event? What are your favorite parts of participating? What could use improvement? What holds you back from participating further?
**Interests & Usage:** What events/programs have you attended in the last 12 months? Best part of the event experience? What could use improvement?
**Brand Preference & Loyalty categories:** Automotive; Financial Services; Insurance; Credit Card Provider; Retail; Telecom; Travel.
**B2B bonus questions:** How long in their sector? What industry association? Annual budget they control? Types of business purchases? Number of staff managed? Products purchased in last two years? How much do they travel for work? Team national or local? Industry trends/concerns? What they spend most on in their department? What they wish they could spend more on? Org publicly traded / privately owned / non-profit?

### Sample Niched Audience Segments (verbatim examples to aim for)
- "40-year-old, high net worth moms, who live in suburban Chicago, with two or more kids who play baseball and Bank with TD but are not satisfied with their service."
- "55-year-old, partner track, corporate lawyers, responsible for bringing in new business and making purchasing decisions for their law firm, who own two cars and looking to switch from BMW to Mercedes."
- "University of X grads, who studied X program, work on Bay St in Toronto, making X salary, who buy houses in York Region and commute 2 hours daily and whose children attend your summer camp programs."

### Survey Open-Rate / Response Tactics (verbatim)
- "Test multiple subject lines and measure what has the best response rate"
- "Appeal to the benefit they will receive"; "Let them know the survey will be completely anonymous"; "Use intriguing subject lines and make sure they don't look like spam."
- "Tell them how long it will take at the beginning"; use multiple-choice; eliminate all but the most important questions.

### Six-Page Proposal Structure (verbatim breakdown)
- **Page 1 (Title):** org/business name, logo, event/program name. Avoid the words "sponsorship package."
- **Page 2:** your audience (succinct yet engaging).
- **Page 3:** ¶1 = your org, history, causes (ONE paragraph — "Write, rewrite, and edit"); ¶2 = your event (what/when/why).
- **Page 4:** introduce your sponsorship package with selective language — refrain from "sponsorship packages" AND "sponsorship opportunities." May call it a **menu**; note it's "always customizable per the sponsor's recommendations."
- **Page 5:** list the packages (without using that exact language).
- **Page 6:** call to action + contact info front and center.

### Festival Proposal Structure (variant)
Title page (name/logo/tagline; optionally dates/location/website) → Your Opportunity (≤2 paragraphs: org, then festival + 1 lead-in paragraph on audience) → Audience Data (charts/tables, appealing colors, crowd photos if available — the section to spend the MOST time on) → Activation Ideas (yours + the prospect's from discovery) → Media Exposure (clip relevant press; don't include negative press; don't overload positive press) → Case Studies & Testimonials (attendance, harmony with past sponsors, audience experience, raw numbers, booth visits) → Contact Page + CTA. **Explicitly NO grids, NO pricing.**

### Follow-Up Email/Call (event checklist)
"Mention who you are, why you're getting in touch, and that you sent your sponsorship proposal on X date. Then ask if the sponsor has had a chance to look things over and whether they have any questions." Offer to walk through it and implement their suggestions.

### Micro-Influencer Follow-Up Cadence
Initial DM/email → wait ~1 week → follow-up (shorter than the original; reintroduce, state the date you reached out, restate the offer, ask if still interested) → wait another week → one final attempt (after which a response is unlikely).

### Stonyfield Social Contest Copy
Asked Vertical Challenge followers to name the inflatable cow with something "udderly fantastic." Cow named **MooMoo** (chosen by Stonyfield because it's easy for kids to say, memorable, and won't offend). Tagged the sponsor + each resort at every tour stop to generate buzz.

## 4. Numbers & Benchmarks

### Outreach Funnel Math (from "Direct Outreach" — the most concrete numbers in the chunk)
- **Prospect list: 200 companies** whose target customer overlaps with your audience.
- **7 touches per prospect**, spread across **2–3 weeks**: **5 emails + 2 phone calls.** Each email a different angle (audience-data, activation-idea, case-study, why-now, final-check-in). After 7 touches with no response → move on ("the contact is dormant. Try again next year.").
- The phone call is "the touch most properties skip and the touch that disproportionately moves replies." Voicemail proves the property is real + the request specific.
- **Discovery process: 3–4 conversations across 2–3 weeks.**
- Booking the follow-up before leaving the discovery meeting → closes **roughly twice as often** as sending the proposal cold.
- **A working week:** 4–8 new outreaches/week; 3–5 active prospect conversations; 2–4 discovery meetings/week; 1–2 discovery-led proposals/month.
- **That property closes ~5 sponsors a year.**
- **Diagnostic test:** pick 4 prospects, find named decision-maker, send 2-sentence email asking for 15 min, schedule a follow-up phone call for non-responders 5 business days later, count discovery meetings. **Meeting rate ≥ 1 in 4 = working; below 1 in 20 = list/email/contact is wrong.**

### Survey Benchmarks
- Response rate: don't expect 100%; **25% is enough** ("if half got back to you, excellent; a quarter, still okay").
- **At least 25 data points per audience segment**; **3–5 audience niches** to start.
- Survey length: **≤15 questions** (one post); some surveys recommend 2–3 weeks to complete.
- Best email send window: **10 a.m.–1 p.m. on Tuesdays, Wednesdays, Thursdays.**
- Survey email cadence: **3 emails max** (announce → distribute → reminder a few days later).

### Pricing / Valuation Anchors
- Logo is worth "a few hundred bucks at most"; "Don't spend more than five minutes on logos."
- A coffee-station activation can be valued at "several thousand dollars."
- "If you only price your assets at $5,000… your sponsor will still only pay $5,000" even if worth 2–3×. Custom-agreed value of $15,000 → sponsor pays $15,000.
- Deal sizes referenced: $1,000 to $10 million; five-, six-, seven-figure deals; "$75K business development budget"; "$30,000," "$40,000."

### Title Sponsorship Duration
- Multiyear is mandatory; **3–5 years is a good starting point**; **10 years is best**; some arena deals run 20+ years. Deeper naming changes (e.g., renaming a street) only make sense at 10 years.
- Fulfillment reports for title deals: **quarterly preferred** (at minimum annually/semi-annually).

### Micro-Influencer Tiers & Pricing (from Influencer Marketing Hub)
- **Nano:** 1,000–10,000 followers. **Micro:** 10,000–100,000. **Macro:** 100,000–1M. **Celebrity:** millions.
- Micro-influencer rates: **$250–$1,250 per Facebook post; $20–$100 per Twitter/X tweet; $25–$125 per TikTok video; $200–$1,000 per YouTube video; $100–$500 per Instagram post.**
- Macro-influencers: **$10,000+ per post.** Survey response: even 25% is workable.

### Industry / Market Figures
- Insurance sports-sponsorship spend now **> $1B globally**; influx started ~mid-2000s (NBA + State Farm).
- State Farm: ranked among top insurance sponsors 2024–25, **$46M annual return.**
- **Formula 1: > $2B sponsorship revenue in 2024** (trails only NFL).
- NBA 2025 US fan age breakdown: 13–17 = 7.2%; 18–24 = 10.21%; 25–34 = 18.5%; 35–49 = 26.2%; 50–64 = 22.3%; 65+ = 15.6%. The **25–64 bracket is the insurance sweet spot.**
- Podcast listeners: 464.7M (2023) → projected 504.9M (2024). Social media users: ~5B (2023–2024).
- Motorsport: Busch + Kevin Harvick "driving billboard" → Busch sales **+10.1%** early 2020; **Busch Light +17.7%**.
- Coca-Cola Racing Family videos: 5 of 6 videos **>15M views, >10% engagement rate.**
- Audience-data example phrasing: "20% of your audience earns $85,000–$110,000 and has attended five events in your industry within the last 12 months"; "54% of my audience uses your products"; "2000 Honda Drivers with 24% looking to buy a new vehicle within 12 months"; "250 senior-level executives."

### Timing Benchmarks
- Sponsor response to a proposal: "upwards of a week or more"; follow up after "several weeks" of silence.
- Festival planning starts **9–12 months out** (best time = immediately after the prior event ends); secure sponsors by ~6 months; finalize by 3 months.
- Fulfillment report: deliver **1–2 weeks** after the event.
- Instagram Live ideal length: **10 minutes** (max 60).
- A multiyear deal: "same amount each year for three or five years," or shorter-term for more money.

## 5. Named Concepts & Terminology

- **BAMFAM** — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting." Always schedule the next meeting while still in the current one ("take five minutes to prioritize it"). Keeps momentum, shortens the path to a signed contract.
- **Property** — your sponsorable entity (event, festival, team, podcast, organization, conference). Also: "sponsorship property."
- **Asset** — a tangible or intangible thing you sell to a sponsor (logo, speaking op, naming rights, social posts, VIP experiences, signage, sampling rights, your audience). "Your audience is an asset and arguably your biggest asset."
- **Activation** — "a customized experiential marketing opportunity that fulfills one need of your audience and one need of your sponsor simultaneously." The antidote to gold/silver/bronze tiers. Examples: misting station, free back massage, photo booth, coffee/sampling bar, branded lounge, biergarten, phone-recharge station.
- **Inventory** — the full set of sellable assets (Assets × Value).
- **Discovery session/meeting** — informal info-gathering meeting (NOT a sales meeting) to learn a prospect's challenges, goals, and what they've tried.
- **Fulfillment report (a.k.a. post-event report / wrap-up report)** — document proving you delivered each promised asset/activation; basis for feedback + renewal; legal cover against breach-of-contract.
- **Avatar (customer avatar)** — a characterization/amalgamation of an audience niche given a name (e.g., "Busy Bobby" = audience with too much to do + too little time). "It's a lot easier to sell to one Busy Bobby than 500 people." Make 3 avatars from 3 niches.
- **Brand awareness** — the lowest-value, top-of-funnel outcome; what logos deliver. Sponsors usually already have it and don't want it.
- **Brand value / brand equity** — value a brand adds beyond a generic equivalent (iPhone vs. any phone).
- **Name in wordmark** — the sponsor's name *enmeshed into* the event name (e.g., "Blue Enterprises Art Festival 2023"), NOT "Art Festival by Blue Enterprises." If you won't do name-in-wordmark, you aren't offering title sponsorship.
- **Title sponsorship / naming rights / presenting rights** — selling visible property naming (podcast/festival/building/street). Among the most expensive, most-benefit-laden assets. Requires: name in wordmark, multiyear deal, exclusivity, regular fulfillment reports, activations, sometimes adopting the sponsor's products/services, VIP/hospitality, tickets/passes/giveaways/contests, sampling rights.
- **Category exclusivity** vs. **competitor exclusivity** — two flavors of title-sponsor exclusivity (e.g., no other automotive sponsor at all vs. only no direct competitor). Must be defined in the contract — "you can't assume the sponsor shares your definition."
- **In-kind / contra sponsorship** — sponsor provides products/services (t-shirts, hotel rooms, food/drink, merch) instead of cash. The provider still pays to obtain the items.
- **Media sponsor / promotional partner** — provides publicity/coverage (TV, radio, newspapers, online) or attracts a celebrity/public figure; often heavily brands the event in return. Media partnership "doesn't always have to cost money."
- **Financial sponsor** — gives cash in exchange for promotion/branding/advertising.
- **Motion vs. Action** — Motion = going in circles (endless meetings, perfecting the proposal, re-editing) that *feels* productive but isn't. Action = moving straight ahead (calling reps, booking follow-ups). Programs need both; don't let nerves trap you in motion.
- **Psychographics** — audience behaviors, motivations, purchasing drivers, opinions, brand loyalties (vs. demographics/geographics).
- **Stakeholder mapping** — see Stakeholder Behavioral Analysis above.
- **Sponsorship menu** — preferred euphemism for the sponsorship package; signals customization.
- **The Baseball / Moneyball Framing** — heavy hitters (one-shot home runs = the giant sponsor / one magic call) strike out under pressure; **base hitters** (consistent small wins) win the long game. Bases = response (1st), proposal interest (2nd), verbal yes (3rd), signed deal (home). Your audience = your base hitters.
- **"The riches are in the niches"** — recurring mantra: the more segmented the audience, the more valuable to a sponsor.

## 6. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

### The Cardinal Sins (recur across many posts)
- **Going "proposal first"** — cold-attaching/cold-mailing the proposal. "The iron is ice cold. Freezing cold, even." Sponsors trash unsolicited decks. Hold the proposal until they ask (2nd or 3rd meeting+).
- **Gold/Silver/Bronze tiers** (and any renamed equivalent — first/second/third, exec/mid/junior, blue/yellow/purple). The *rigidity* is the problem, not the name. Forces the sponsor to buy A,B,C,D,E when they only want B → they overpay begrudgingly or walk → resentment → no renewal. "Leaves money on the table, frustrates sponsors, and makes the sponsorship seeker look like an amateur."
- **Including a price list / pricing grid in the deck/proposal.** "If you haven't talked to your sponsor yet… how can you possibly guess how much to charge them?" When they ask for the price list, say "I would never dream of charging a sponsor for a bunch of assets they don't want" → use it to open a budget/goals conversation. Budgets "magically increase" when sponsors see custom options.
- **Overpricing assets** ("'cause I said so" pricing) above market value → every sponsor (even deep-pocketed ones) sees through it and says no. And the killer follow-up: "How did you come up with these numbers?" — you can't say "I made them up."
- **Underpricing / not valuing at all** — leaves thousands–tens of thousands on the table; sponsors happily pay low and never correct you ("you're the only one who ends up burned").
- **Copying someone else's valuation** found online — can't explain provenance, often overinflated or undercut, not based on your service quality.
- **Vague audience data** — "middle-class families," "20-somethings," "general public," "people who care about [topic]." Equivalent to a bakery menu that just says "cake for people who like desserts." If you tell sponsors your audience is the "general public" you are "leaving money on the table while frustrating them."
  - A single *paragraph* of audience data is NOT enough — needs **3+ pages / at least a full page**, in charts/graphs.
- **Selling/relying on logos & social blasts as primary assets** — they deliver only brand awareness (no leads, conversions, or sales). "Don't spend more than five minutes on logos."
- **Treating the discovery session like a sales meeting** — pitching, dumping your org's mission/cause/event story, trying to close. "Until they tell you what that goal is, you are not in a position to ask for the sale."
- **Saying "we customize" without actually customizing** — "merely lip service." If you really would customize, you'd do it.
- **Assuming you know what the sponsor wants** (Drucker: "The customer rarely buys what the company thinks it sells him"). Pushing Asset A because *you* make Asset A while the sponsor needs Asset B → you under-deliver → no renewal, possible breach of contract.

### Process / Behavior Anti-Patterns
- **Chasing only the biggest fish** ("sponsor everything" Fortune-500s) — they're inundated with "thousands of unsolicited sponsorship packages every single week." Like playing the lottery. Start local/small, build a track record. Also: a startup/nonprofit usually can't solve a Fortune-500's problems → no fit.
- **"Just send me a proposal" syndrome** = symptom of trying to close on the first call, going in cold, and targeting only the biggest companies. Move such prospects to "declined" and use the decline email to build a relationship for next year.
- **Skipping research under time pressure** — like a doctor doing an untrained procedure or a stylist who skipped the hair-dyeing lesson. Signals you'll be careless as a partner.
- **Spending too long on any one step** (perfecting the proposal/assets forever) — comfort masquerading as progress. **Start before you feel ready** (a client who finally started was "negotiating a five-figure deal in a matter of weeks").
- **Waiting forever for a "no"** — a flat "no" is clear; don't try to decode it. Follow up once or twice; if silent after a week or two, take it as a no. (But DO decode soft replies — "let me think about it," "I don't have time," "budget's thin" — by asking what they need / when they'll be available / parking them for a later event.)
- **Funneling too much budget into one activation** — leaves other sponsors/areas underfunded; can't count on 100% of the audience gravitating to one sponsor.
- **Using the word "sponsorship"** with prospects who associate it with logos/tiers — avoid the S-word; but DON'T just swap in a buzzword like "partnership"/"experiential marketing." Focus on the *outcome*.

### "Surefire Ways to Sabotage" (the explicit anti-pattern list)
Sending the proposal too soon; gold/silver/bronze tiers; overestimating asset value; relying on assumptions / no research about the sponsor's audience; **making major event changes (theme, venue, date) without telling the sponsor** (their themed signage/booth becomes useless — "they look stupid because you didn't keep them in the loop"); pairing with a controversial/scandal-ridden partner (other sponsors + audience may pull out); **trash-talking the sponsor (especially on social media)** — "torching a bridge you may again have to cross"; **failing to deliver promised goals** (the single biggest relationship-killer; better to be honest up-front / work with a smaller sponsor than to over-promise and burn a big one).

### "Worst Advice" (anti-patterns framed as bad advice to ignore)
"Sponsorship is easy"; "cold-send your proposal"; "skip research under time constraints"; "overprice assets to get more money"; "money is the only thing a sponsor offers"; **"any sponsor is better than no sponsor"** (e.g., a vegan/animal-rights org unknowingly taking a Tyson/Hillshire-affiliated sponsor → audience-trust destroyed + bad press). A sponsor "should never" fund 100% of an event — desperation breeds skipped vetting and bad decisions.

### Podcast-specific mistakes
"One of the biggest podcast sponsorship errors is only offering prospective sponsors commercials" — go beyond ad reads (contests, giveaways, interviews with the sponsor).

## 7. Mindset & Principles

- **"Sponsorship is a verb, not a noun"** — "Sponsors simply don't exist." "Asking what 'sponsors' want is like asking 'what does running want?' It doesn't want anything! It's an activity used to achieve a particular goal." A director of marketing doesn't *become* "a sponsor" by using sponsorship — they're still measured on leads/meetings/sales. The real question is *"what do those who want to reach our audience want, and what tools can we use to help them achieve those goals?"*
- **"Nobody buys a product called 'sponsorship.' They buy an outcome."**
- **"Sponsorship is marketing, not philanthropy."** "A sponsor will never hand over cash out of the goodness of their hearts. That's a donation." If you want straight cash, "take out a loan, seek investors, or ask for donations." Sponsors pay for ROI.
- **"Companies don't want to sponsor you — they want to sponsor your audience."** "The more you can get used to putting yourself second, third, even fourth, and the sponsor and your audience first, the faster your sponsorship program will move."
- **"The sponsorship proposal doesn't make the deal — you do."** The proposal is a small part of the program; relationships close deals.
- **Sales is a hallway of doors, each hiding a bomb** (Greg Hickman / AltAgency): "You open the door by asking the right questions. Then… you have to diffuse the bomb." The moment you share pricing, hidden objections detonate ("let me think about it," "I need to talk to so-and-so").
- **Failure is a teacher, not a verdict.** "He who fails the most…" Expect to be "rejected dozens of times." Like learning to ride a bike — you don't quit after one fall. "Failure can only be a teacher if you let it be."
- **Treat sponsors as people, not ATMs.** "A partner, you're invested in. A sponsor, to many, is a walking ATM." Be friendly (not necessarily friends) — but a warm attitude can NEVER compensate for a weak program.
- **Audience data is the heart of the program.** "If your sponsorship program is a living being, your audience data is the heart. Once the heart is gone, your sponsorship program is lifeless."
- **Simpler is better** (the "three-ingredient cake" metaphor): a great program needs only Data, Discovery, Delivery — don't over-engineer.
- **Be honest in valuation** ("your own moral compass"): raise prices on what you're excellent at, keep prices lower on what you're not. Don't be the restaurant that charges more for the same dish.
- **Show your math.** Sponsors will ask where the numbers came from; have an overview ready (builds goodwill, repeatable next time).
- **Authenticity wins activations** (motorsport lessons): the Ty Dillon/Death Wish tweet and Coca-Cola Racing Family worked because they "felt authentic" and didn't "reek of corporate kissing-up."
- **The best activation isn't the biggest one.** Repeated across activation posts: "the best activations fulfill your audience and your sponsor. You don't need giant inflatables or screens or 3D elements." (Stonyfield's $-modest inflatable cow beat Mountain Dew/Chevrolet for buzz.)
- **Stop trying to sell anything — including sponsorship.** "Don't even bring [a package] to the meeting… get to know your prospect and their goals."
- **Knowing your own value is your job alone.** "You're the only one who knows your sponsorship opportunity's value. Your sponsor doesn't know it. Your competitors don't know it." Guessing/copying leaves money on the table.

## 8. Industry-Specific Nuances

### Sports (Insurance sponsors)
Insurers chase sports for affluent/loyal older-skewing audiences (25–64 sweet spot), brand trust borrowed from excitement, risk/performance alignment, and increasingly **first-party data capture + lead gen** via digital activations, gated content, app integrations. Top insurance sponsors: **State Farm** (NBA, college athletics, esports — LoL World Championship/MSI/All-Star), **Allianz** (Olympics, soccer, motorsport; Allianz Arena/Parque), **Allstate** (college football, Canadian soccer, Big Ten women's), **Geico** (NASCAR, MLB, NHL, NCAA). For teams approaching insurers: **start LOCAL** (Google "[your state] insurance companies"), lead with audience data, design digital-first/measurable activations, emphasize local-within-national, report ROI not impressions. Match: "most people in their 30s are starting to think about insuring more than their car" → 32-year-old married homeowners earning $50k are a perfect insurance match.

### Micro-Influencers / Podcasters
Micro = 10K–100K followers; more attainable + affordable + higher engagement than macro/celebs; "the riches are in the niches." Process differs: reach out **directly via DM/email** (not a mutual-connection cold call); DMs from non-followers land in a side folder. Activations are **all social** (product placement, unboxing, livestream tests, contests, giveaways, brand ambassadorship, account takeovers, sponsored posts/Q&As, affiliate). Influencer partnerships now beat celebrity endorsements (audiences are "desensitized" to celebs). Brands that use micros: Nivea, La Croix, Coca-Cola, HelloFresh, Glossier, Target, Adidas, Starbucks, Airbnb, Nike, Sephora, etc. Podcast sponsors want listenership, downloads, social engagement — and more than ad reads.

### Festivals
- **Planning timeline:** 9–12 months out (theme, goals, attendance projection, budget, venue, ticket pricing, staff/vendors/performers, prospect list + outreach, advertising) → 6 months (permits, safety plan, secure sponsors, cement activations, tickets on sale) → 3 months (finalize vendors/sponsors/performers, performance schedule) → 1 month (test app, print maps, check-in/payment incl. RFID, ramp marketing, backup/rain plan) → 1 week (watch weather, cleanup plan, rundown, setup) → day-of (final walk-through, station staff, parking/booths) → after (fulfillment report, thank staff, renegotiate). Full festival staff list provided (site/production/stage/security/VIP/hospitality/sponsorship/box-office/artist-relations/etc.).
- **Permits often needed:** premises license, temporary-use/structure, event permit, seller's permit, building permits, business license, health permit, fireworks/fire permit, alcohol license, noise permit.
- **7 must-have festival activities:** Contests/Giveaways, Competitions, Merch/Shopping, Food & Beverage, Games/Play Zones, Lounge/Recharge, Photo/Social-Share — each with Purpose / Examples / How-to-Plan / How-to-Measure (entries, QR scans, leads, dwell time, revenue/attendee, vendor sales, RFID tap-ins, hashtag usage, UGC volume). Modern trends: sustainability-led (refill/recycling), immersive tech (AR scavenger hunts), QR/app/wearable check-ins, wellness/cooling lounges, creator/UGC-friendly layouts.
- **Festival social media assets (low→high value):** promotional posts (low — just awareness) < teaser videos < live streams < Instagram Lives (Live Rooms, ~10 min) < user-generated content (UGC) < social contests < giveaways. Value of co-streaming hinges on YOUR following eclipsing the sponsor's. Create an event hashtag; optimize each platform's bio/links beforehand; skip LinkedIn as a festival; small YouTube presence beats none.
- **What festival sponsors want (6):** lots of research/audience data, a proactive approach, consideration as people, productive discovery sessions, detailed fulfillment reports, customized opportunities.

### Title / Naming-Rights Sponsorship
See concepts above — distinct because it bundles the most benefits, demands name-in-wordmark + multiyear (3–10+ yrs) + exclusivity + frequent (quarterly) fulfillment reports + possible product/service adoption + VIP/hospitality + sampling rights. Always lawyer-reviewed. Still requires full discovery + valuation (don't give activations away free just because there's a title sponsor).

### Motorsport (NASCAR / F1 / Formula E)
Standard = logos on car/uniform, but elevate it: Ty Dillon/Death Wish authentic tweet; Coca-Cola Racing Family video series; Busch's ever-changing "driving billboard" car (→ measurable sales lift); Heineken Neon House (themed hospitality, airbrushed helmets as décor — "anything but a photo wall"); Heineken Greener Bar (sustainability matched to Formula E's eco audience); American Express Fan Experience (tiered access by card → FOMO + exclusivity). Lesson: themed zones, green initiatives, and "make a standard activation better" beat reinventing the wheel; the right activation fulfills the sponsor's + audience's needs.

### Activations at Scale (July 2024 / SDCC examples)
LIV Golf Fan Village (160over90; 70-ft guitar putting green), eBay Live Storefront, NEST Grapefruit Squeeze pop-up truck (FOMO + freebies), IMDboat (passport/stamp mechanic), CIROC Limonata Villa, Paramount+ The Lodge (franchise zones + freebies), The Penguin Iceberg Lounge (Samsung screens), Adult Swim Pirate Parrrty, #FXSDCC (The Bear restaurant replica), Hulu Animayhem (Futurama Slurm Factory). Recurring success drivers: immersive/themed, freebies, photo-ops, social buzz, FOMO/limited-time. Repeated caveat: size/budget is NOT the point.

### Nonprofits / Causes
"Sponsorship is not philanthropy" applies doubly. Sponsorship deck should spend 3+ pages on audience (demographics + connection-to-cause questions + brand preference). Cause case studies focus on attendance, harmony with past sponsors, audience experience — not traditional business case studies.

### B2B Audiences (hidden value)
Often overlooked but immensely valuable — "look at how much it costs to advertise to senior-level executives on LinkedIn." If you identify, say, 250 senior execs who attend a specific networking event, that's a high-value B2B sponsorship opportunity. Ask B2B-specific survey questions (budget controlled, purchases made, staff managed, etc.).

### Case Study: Stonyfield × Vertical Challenge (model activation)
30-year multi-generational ski/snowboard race series; discovery revealed Stonyfield targets families + wants brand awareness + products in hands. Audience was loyal + receptive + wanted healthier sponsors (vs. past high-sugar sponsors whose samples parents refused). Solution: an inflatable arch-shaped cow (start gate) + reps handing samples + a social naming contest ("name that cow," tagging sponsor + each resort) → UGC explosion → cow named MooMoo → became the unofficial tour mascot; resorts and radio DJs reposted unprompted. Lessons: unique + simple + low-cost; a great activation lifts the whole event (benefiting even big co-sponsors Mountain Dew & Chevrolet).
