## 1. FRAMEWORKS & PROCESSES (named, step-by-step)

### The 11 Stages of the Sponsorship Journey
The full documented methodology (so you don't restart from zero each prospect):
1. **Identify Properties** — define what you're offering (event/gala/expo/program). Don't skip, but spend little time on it.
2. **Identify Your Audience** — "your audience is the greatest asset you can sell to a sponsor." Send an audience survey (demographics, geographics, psychographics).
3. **Segment Your Audience** — niche down hard ("the riches are in the niches"). Endpoint example: "35-year-old female accountants in Thornton Park in Orlando making $124,000 a year."
4. **Build Your Inventory of Assets** — brainstorm every relevant asset; only those you can realistically produce. A logo is "about the weakest asset you can sell."
5. **Get Internal Buy-In** — sit with senior staff/board; address pushback (list shrinks here).
6. **Complete a Valuation** — price every asset at adjusted market value; no emotional attachment; sort most→least valuable, then trim.
7. **Research Prospects** — use the concentric circle method; aim for 60–70, up to 100+.
8. **Get the Meeting (and Ask Questions)** — book the discovery session; ask ≤10 of the 37 discovery questions.
9. **Build a Custom Package** — co-create with the prospect ("I found X asset that I think could help Y problem. What do you think?").
10. **Close the Deal** — sign paperwork; hire a lawyer; reread the contract.
11. **Fulfill the Deal** — deliver everything legally promised; track record attracts more sponsors.

### The Five Stages of Sponsorship Sales
1. **Inventory Building & Valuation** — start here; "No assets, no sales." Your inventory tells you who to talk to and sets your budget. Avoid the "shoulder shrug" valuation method.
2. **Prospecting** — start from your inventory. Going to a prospect because they "have a lot of money" won't sell; telling them you have "their target market in your database or at your event" will.
3. **Getting the Meeting** — bring nothing (no proposal/one-pager). Only goal: gather info + get the second meeting. Only ask questions.
4. **The Sponsorship Proposal** — max 6 pages; always custom (never say it's custom); never gold/silver/bronze. (As one expert put it: "I'm not all that convinced that you even need a sponsorship proposal at all!")
5. **Activation, Fulfillment & Renewal** — deliver everything, build a fulfillment report, ask for the renewal while they're still excited.

### The Anatomy of a Great Sponsorship Program (6 parts)
1. Audience Research (the "heart and soul")
2. Defined Goals
3. Asset Valuation ("second most important part")
4. Activation Opportunities
5. Sponsorship Package (custom, NOT tiered)
6. Sponsorship Proposal (≤6 pages; follow template; produced only after discovery + multiple meetings)

### Sponsorship Strategy Foundation (Complete Guide to Sponsorship Strategy)
Foundation requires: **A Defined Property → Inventory Building → Audience Identification → Custom Activations → Asset Valuation → Identify the Market → Sales Pipeline → Sales Process → Fulfillment/ROI Report.**

### Sales Pipeline Stages (Sponsorship Strategy)
Cold prospects → Cold calls → Discovery meetings → Sponsorship proposals → Deals closed. Set per-stage weekly goals (cold calls/week, discovery sessions/week, proposals/week). Feed more prospects in as the pipeline narrows.

### The $10M Big-Ticket Process (Path to Big Ticket Sponsorship Sales)
Every six/seven-figure negotiation over two years required these stages:
1. Several well-defined audience segments (≥30 data points each)
2. Clearly defined goals for each segment
3. Agreed-upon measurements for those goals
4. Usually a formal media sponsorship
5. Complex, highly interactive activations connecting sponsor to audience
6. A third-party valuation proving market value
7. Quarterly reporting on benchmarks

### Concentric Circle Prospecting Method (4 circles, warmest→coldest)
1. **Circle 1 (warmest):** brands your audience named directly in the survey (brand loyalty/preference questions). Order most→least mentioned.
2. **Circle 2:** companies that advertise/market to your audience.
3. **Circle 3:** brands that *should* have interest in your audience but currently don't (e.g., audience likes Gibson guitars → add Fender, ESP, Rickenbacker, PRS).
4. **Circle 4 (coldest):** 3–4 direct competitors for every prospect already on the list. Research-heavy; cap the time spent.

### 5-Step Prospecting (Art of Sponsorship Prospecting)
1. Survey your audience (focus on brand loyalty/preference questions).
2. Add the brands your audience mentioned (order most→least recurring; typically 30–40).
3. Continue building (advertisers of each brand → who *should* advertise but doesn't → direct competitors). Grows list 2x–4x, up to ~100.
4. Research each prospect (website, LinkedIn, press releases) — the "sniff test."
5. Eliminate irrelevant prospects (separate, don't delete — opportunity may return).

### 5 Steps to Securing Corporate Philanthropy (nonprofits)
1. Set goals + mission statement (3 things: why you exist, who you help, how you help).
2. Understand your value proposition.
3. List your assets (in-kind, pro bono, volunteer hours, etc.).
4. Be smart about partners (align with mission; scale matters — aim too high and big corps won't have time). Approach asking for *advice on a project*, not a donation.
5. Present your assets (after a discovery meeting; show recognition options).

### Influencer Sponsorship Process (9 steps)
Understand your brand → Know your audience → Choose companies in your niche → Build a media kit → Write a great pitch → Price services fairly → Connect with sponsors → Discovery session → Follow up (always BAMFAM: "When can we meet again?").

## 2. TACTICS & TECHNIQUES

### Prospecting
- Two WRONG selection criteria sponsorship seekers use: **name value** and **money**. "Your audience doesn't know how much a sponsor paid you, and they don't care."
- "Coca-Cola coming to your health conference tells your audience not to bother attending." Biggest-name brand can't overcome a needs/values mismatch.
- The most controversial sponsorships are those with the biggest disconnect between sponsor and audience (e.g., meat company at a vegetarian conference, soda maker at a health expo).
- Focus survey brand questions on: travel, retail, insurance, automotive, telecom, credit card, food, financial services — "covers pretty much every basis of consumerism."
- Refresh audience data regularly (general advice: every 6 months; influencers every 3 months).

### Getting the Meeting / Outreach
- **Never lead with the proposal.** The only goal of first contact is to schedule the discovery session.
- Use warm connections — "it's always better if you know someone who knows someone." Don't play "seven degrees of separation"; flimsy connections (colleague's sister's mother-in-law's dog) are off-putting.
- Steward board/committee members "like they are title sponsors" — meet one-on-one for coffee, don't e-blast asking for "contacts." Bring them a specific list of people you know they're connected to and ask for intros.
- **The advice visit:** ask contacts to meet and ask them for advice on who else to meet and what assets you're missing. Be specific.
- Ask for referrals from: current sponsors, volunteers, committees, investors, board, business contacts, service providers, your own referral network.
- Call a personal work line (not the main office number) to avoid phone-tag/gatekeeping.
- If a manager/admin assistant refers you up to a VP who'd been unresponsive, that warm internal intro makes the VP responsive.

### Discovery Session (the most important tactic)
- It is **NOT a sales meeting** — it's an informational/qualifying conversation. Walk in "with the mindset of an investigative journalist or detective."
- Bring nothing but a pen and paper. Don't try to tell them anything about you unless asked.
- Ask 5–10 questions max (never all 37). Tweak questions to fit your property.
- "Peel the onion" — ask follow-ups when answers are vague. Even "tell me more" works.
- Let conversation flow naturally; don't interrogate. Have a strong icebreaker prepared, but avoid being *too* friendly (don't trade pet photos / weekend stories at the expense of the program).

### Proposals
- **The 50-Percent Rule**: 50% of the proposal must be audience data. The other 50% = intro, assets, activations, contact info. NOT more cause talk.
- Intro = 3 sentences max about company/cause/mission.
- Proposal = **max 6 pages.** Six pages is "just enough to pique interest."
- Include case studies/examples of past sponsor wins (growing database, leads, conversions, sales) with copy, data, photos.
- Don't include pricing in the package (podcast guide) — pricing gives only "take it or leave it," and you undercut yourself when a sponsor would've paid more.
- The proposal "proves you were listening at the discovery session" / "proving that you listened in class when you sit down to do your homework."
- "The sponsorship proposal cannot make the sale. That's up to you!"

### Negotiation (Festival deals do's/don'ts)
- DO have targeted audience data ready (≥20, ideally ~25 datapoints/segment).
- DON'T negotiate before a discovery session.
- DO know your value (valuate before you meet).
- DON'T forget to customize assets/activations.
- DO be realistic; DON'T highball ("Playing hardball with the sponsor often results in playing the game by yourself").
- DO be flexible — **use a range, not a single dollar figure**, walking into negotiation.
- DON'T get taken for a ride (back out if continually lowballed).
- DO have a lawyer review the contract.
- DON'T promise what you can't deliver.
- Once negotiated, dollar amounts shouldn't change beyond negotiation, *especially not* once in a binding contract.

### Closing
- "Can I close a sponsorship sale on the first meeting?" — **No, and that shouldn't be the goal.**
- Don't go into the initial meeting trying to hit a sales quota.

### Activation & Fulfillment
- Activation = "making sure you deliver everything you said you would and making sure your sponsor takes full advantage of the assets you've sold them."
- Activation often **costs money** (ad design, product placement) — educate the sponsor about costs and **budget activation costs into the price** of the sponsorship.
- ASK the sponsor for help on activations — "sponsors want to help. They're glad to." Combine your audience knowledge + their challenge knowledge.

### Renewal
- The most valuable window is **after the event, once you've delivered** — not early in the program.
- Deliver the fulfillment report, then ask the magic renewal question (see §3).
- Ask while they're "still excited about the event."

### Communication cadence with sponsors (frequency scales toward the event)
Months out: ~once/week → within several months: ~twice/week → days before: daily or several times/day. Method doesn't matter ("You could send smoke signals... Call, email, DM, FaceTime").

## 3. SCRIPTS, TEMPLATES & EXACT LANGUAGE (verbatim)

### Cold email template (a go-to — "works 10% of the time")
> "Hi Dave,
>
> I noticed on LinkedIn that you are involved in the sponsorship program at Company X, focused on high net worth moms who live in the suburbs.
>
> We did some research and found that around 30% of our attendees match this demographic and thought you would be interested in a conversation.
>
> Are you around on Tuesday at 3:00 for a 15-minute discovery call?
>
> Best, Chris"

### Subject-line formulas (quick, concise, personal)
- "Met you at _______, let's connect!"
- "_______ recommended I reach out."
- "Had an idea for _________."

### Email best practices
- "Come in hot" — make the first sentence count (mobile preview); skip "Hope this finds you well."
- Don't ask for their sponsorship yet in a follow-up — premature, can backfire.
- Wrong-contact line (cold email): "apologies if I am reaching out to the wrong person, but if you could advise me who I should speak to it would be most appreciated."

### The Best Question to Ask a Sponsor (renewal — verbatim)
> "What would we have to do as the property or rights holders to get you to agree to give us twice as much money next year and for it to be the best marketing investment you've made in the last five years?"

Softer alternative:
> "I don't want to suggest that you should give us more money or not. I just want to know, what would it take for us to be your preferred partner, for us to double our engagement, and for it to be the best thing you've done in five years?"

Setup: preface with "I have a rather bold question," then ask permission before asking. Keep tone lighthearted/noncommittal.

### Discovery starter questions (Five Stages)
- "Who is your target audience?"
- "How do you normally engage in sponsorship?"
- "What does your target market value?"
- "What can you tell me about your sales goals for the coming year?"
- "What would you consider to be the most important elements of a sponsorship package?"

### Curiosity / elaboration questions (Power of Curiosity)
- "What do you mean by that?" (ask repeatedly to get 100% clarity — e.g., on "brand awareness")
- "Why do you think that is?" (forces reflection; surfaces what worked/failed)
- "What have you tried that has worked?"
- "What assumptions are you making about your sales goals?"

### Multiyear renewal questions (when sponsor says "maybe")
- "When do you think you'll know for sure?"
- "Who else needs to be involved to help you make the decision?"
- If still waffling after follow-up → it's a no.

### Influencer pitch template (verbatim)
> "Hello [name of contact at company],
> My name is [your name] and I'm an influencer in [niche]. I've been a big fan of your [product/service] for a long time and I personally use it regularly. I was wondering if you might be interested in a deal where I can promote your [product/service] on [social media platform of choice]?
> My [social media platform] account is [account name]. You'll see that I talk a lot about [niche]. My page attracts a big following of [number of followers] who love to check out my thoughts and recommendations.
> It's my goal to provide my audience with [products/services] they'll love, and I think we'd be a great fit! I have collaboration experience from working with [past partnerships].
> I'm happy to send my media kit if you want to check it out. You can also visit my website [web address] for more information.
> Thanks so much for your time!"

### Custom-package co-creation line
> "Hey, I found X asset that I think could really help with Y problem. What do you think of that?"

### Phrases / words to AVOID
- **"Just send me the proposal"** (when YOU hear it after 1 meeting = polite "no, not interested"; a brush-off / lie). Response: tell them "no" — you won't send a proposal until you know their marketing outcomes.
- In a proposal, never say "we don't have enough money" or "we're having a hard time" — reads as a red flag, not sympathy.
- Don't write "if you don't see something you like, just let us know — we're willing to customize anything" (signals a stock package).
- Don't say the package "is custom" — let the contents prove it.
- Don't call tiers gold/silver/bronze — "calling them by any other name doesn't help."

## 4. NUMBERS & BENCHMARKS

### Prospect pipeline
- Target **60–70 prospects, up to 100+.** Minimum "more than 10." Brands named directly by audience typically 30–40; list grows 2x–4x via advertisers/competitors.
- Eliminating on research cuts the list only slightly (5–10 prospects, "very surprised if it was more").

### Audience data depth
- **≥20 data points per segment, ideally ~25** (festival/podcast). Big-ticket: **≥30 data points.**
- Proposal should contain "at least 25 data points on your audience segments."

### Survey
- Response rate: 100% never happens; "If a quarter of your audience answers, that's decent." Allow 2–3 weeks; send periodic reminders; motivate with coupon/discount/contest prize.

### Outreach cadence
- **Reach out once per day, alternating phone and email, over 7 days, one contact at a time** (Five Stages). Daily-but-once = not harassment.
- Festival version (explicit): Day1 email, Day2 email, Day3 email, Day4 call, Day5 email, Day6 email, Day7 call = **2 calls + 5 emails over 7 days.** If no reply in a week (barring illness/long vacation), move on.
- Nine Paths version: up to 3 emails (spaced) → phone → 2 more emails → another phone → move down the org.
- Influencers: 1–2 follow-ups acceptable, max 3.

### Proposal/document specs
- Proposal max 6 pages; intro 3 sentences; 1 paragraph (1 page) on company/cause; 50% audience data.

### Email stats cited
- 40%+ of salespeople say sales emails are the hardest part of sales (HubSpot).
- Mobile = 41.9% of email opens, Q1 2019 (Litmus).
- 70% of salespeople give up after the first attempt (Yesware).
- 64% of recipients open based on subject line alone; personalized subject lines ~22% higher open rate (sports fan engagement).

### Pricing / valuation rules
- Price every asset one at a time: "How do you complete a valuation on an inventory of 50, 100, or 1,000 assets? One asset at a time."
- Market value = baseline/litmus test; then determine if yours is worth more or less.
- Exceed market value when: very unique services, deep expertise, long record of happy clients, proven results.
- Marketing/digital prospecting budget: most companies dedicate **≥10%** of income to marketing, up to **15%** in some industries.

### Renewal pricing trick (Best Question)
- Sponsor agrees to "twice as much" → **charge 1½× instead** in exchange for a **2-year agreement** at a flat price. Small financial hit, but more revenue than last year + locked second year.

### Multiyear
- **Start at a 3-year term** ("enough time for both parties to reap consistency without feeling locked in"). Don't ask for 10 years after one good year.
- Produce the fulfillment report **within 2 days to 1 week** after the event; meet to renegotiate ASAP.
- Negotiate money year-by-year (rate should rise if value rises; plateau = flat/slight increase).

### Influencer pricing
- Industry baseline: **$10 per 1,000 likes** on a sponsored post (HubSpot). Negotiate up only with tens of thousands+ followers; a million → more still.
- Influencer tiers (Digital Marketing.org): nano 1,000+, micro up to 100k, macro 100k+, mega up to ~1M.
- Always state exactly what's delivered (e.g., "$200 = three IG posts + two IG Stories").
- Don't promise 50,000 likes and deliver 8,500 — "better to promise a small outcome and then achieve that exact outcome."

### Market-size figures cited
- Sponsorship is a "$60 billion industry" (Path to Failure).
- US sponsorship spend: ~$180.8M (2023, Statista), →$220M by 2027 (CAGR 5.03%); separately "$194 million" in 2024 (CAGR 3.94% 2024–28). Canada: $11.9B USD in 2023 (CAGR 8.51%). (Note: figures as quoted in posts; units inconsistent in source.)
- Global sports sponsorship: $97.35M (2022) → $105.47B (2023) → projected $189.54B by 2030; CAGR 9.2% (2024–32). Top sports assets: signage, digital activations, club/venue activations.
- Music-festival sponsorship rose $574M → $1.17B/yr (2003–2010, LA Weekly). Music-festival profit margin 5–25% (Coachella 38% in 2019).
- Sports betting partnerships valued ~$6.1M; renewals ~$3.4M (≈half don't re-up). US online sports betting revenue projected $9.65B (2024), CAGR 13.03% → $15.75B by 2028.
- Women's sports sponsorships +22% (2023→24), 5,300+ active deals. LPGA 1,100+ (+25% YoY), WTA 1,050+ (+34%), WNBA 500 (+7%).
- Mega-deal examples: Pepsi/NFL $2B/10yr; Jordan/Nike since 1984 ~$1.5B; LeBron/Nike lifetime $1B; Adidas/MLS $700M (since 2018); Budweiser/MLB ~$20M/yr.
- Super Bowl 2020 ad: record $5.6M / 30 seconds. 26 of 32 NFL stadiums branded.
- Avg sports fan spent $725/yr (2020). Social media engagement rate benchmark: average 1–5% (sports orgs often surpass 5%).
- RFID costs: active tags $15–20 each (2,000 attendees = $40,000); basic passive ~$0.10/tag; metal passive $1.50/tag. Active reader (≤3,000 ft) $1,250–1,500; fixed-position reader $10,000–20,000 (incl. install); handheld passive reader ~$3,000.

## 5. NAMED CONCEPTS & TERMINOLOGY

- **Property** — what you offer the sponsor; "a distinct entity that caters to a specific audience" (event/gala/marathon/conference/concert/competition; naming rights; programs).
- **Asset** — a tangible or intangible item you sell that helps the sponsor connect with your audience. "Marketing tactics" (advertising, branded content, sampling, speaking ops, social, naming rights, etc.).
- **Activation** — applying/executing a sold asset at the event; experiential marketing connecting sponsor to audience. A *winning* activation simultaneously fills an audience need AND a sponsor need.
- **Tangible benefits** — "quantity assets," measurable (impressions, interactions, leads, products handed out, logo placement, booths, demos, speaking, live social feeds). Metrics: Lead Generation, ROI.
- **Intangible benefits** — no marketplace proxy value; where naming rights, brand alignment, storytelling, cause marketing derive value. Factors affecting value: audience knowledge, activation strength, fulfillment/reporting practices, marketing plan/budget, media partnerships, org capacity, org reputation, audience buying power.
- **Audience survey** — multi-question sheet covering demographics, geographics, psychographics (behaviors/motivations). The starting point for prospecting + segmentation.
- **Psychographics** — lifestyle, interests, attitudes, opinions, values, personality, usage habits, motivations. "The real meat of the audience survey."
- **Discovery session** — informational/qualifying first meeting; ask questions only; not a sale.
- **Sponsorship package** — a menu of valued assets the sponsor selects from. Best version is fully custom, NOT tiered.
- **BAMFAM** — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting." Set the next meeting while the sponsor is in front of you; combats lost time/momentum.
- **Fulfillment report** — post-event report; chart of every promised asset + whether you delivered/over/under/didn't, with photos and metrics (logo placement, speaking, product placement, samples, web traffic, social stats). Proves ROI; sets up renewal/upsell. (Quarterly for major deals; don't wait until after for big agreements.)
- **Naming rights** — name affixed to a physical thing (building/stadium/library/hospital wing); broad reach, exclusivity, long/perpetual terms. Traffic can outweigh audience fit.
- **Title sponsorship** — brand name in the wordmark, e.g., "The Company XYZ Wellness Expo"; usually exclusive; more community/targeted.
- **Presenting rights** — name appears *within* the title but not in the wordmark, e.g., "The Napa Auto Parts 200 presented by Dodge"; most cost-effective; may or may not be exclusive; can be shared.
- **In-kind donation / contra** — sponsor gives expertise, time, services, goods, or free equipment/staff instead of (or alongside) cash. Don't reflexively refuse it.
- **Pass-through benefits** — a listed asset category (benefits passed through to the sponsor/audience).
- **Leverage / "exploit" the sponsorship** — an added menu (added value, self-liquidators, team recognition, co-sponsor cross-promotions) letting sponsors get more ROI from the relationship.
- **Self-liquidators** — discounted merch offers (e.g., highlight reel, official jersey at a low price) at the game or sponsor location.
- **Happy ears** — taking a prospect at face value too early; assuming brush-offs are genuine interest. "Top cause of budget failure."
- **Concentric circle method** — the 4-circle prospecting model (warm→cold).
- **Avatars/segments** — niched-down audience groups (e.g., "40-year-old fathers of two in Greektown, Detroit who work as accountants and make $69k a year").
- **Category exclusivity** — granting a sponsor sole rights in their product category.
- **Sponsor summit** — annual event to thank sponsors, network, and collect improvement ideas (private reps time for top sponsors).
- **Media kit / press kit** — influencer's equivalent of a proposal: bio, testimonials, past collaborations/sponsorships, case studies, social stats (growth over time, viral posts).
- **RFID** — radio-frequency identification; wristbands/wearables enabling cashless, hands-free purchases + attendee data capture (attendance, ROI, dwell, access control, safety). UHF (300MHz–3GHz, ~40 ft), HF (3–30MHz, payments/ticketing), LF (30–300kHz, ~0.33 ft).

## 6. MISTAKES & ANTI-PATTERNS

- **#1 biggest problem: incorrectly pricing your assets** (guessing/assuming). Even a perfect package/proposal fails with wrong prices. Fix via cost method, market value, competitor reference, geography. (Geography example: "A snowblower is worth a premium in Minnesota but all but worthless in California.")
- **Gold/silver/bronze (stock/tiered) packages** — overdone (sponsors have seen them 100s of times), not a show of good faith, forces sponsors to overspend to get the assets they want (assets split across tiers). Big companies "succeed in spite of it, not because of it" — they have other leverage; small orgs can't get away with it. Zero room for customization = zero collaboration.
- **Leading with the proposal / sending it unsolicited.** "Like film studios don't want unsolicited scripts, sponsors don't want unsolicited proposals." Thousands arrive monthly, "all go in the trash."
- **Treating the discovery meeting as a sales meeting** — you'll glean nothing and get ghosted.
- **Logo placement as the main focus** — "the only goal outcome of 'logo placement' is 'awareness' and very few sponsors have an exclusive goal of increased brand awareness." Logos don't generate leads/media/sales.
- **Transactional thinking / treating the sponsor as an ATM or investor.** Sponsors aren't investors (no profit cut). Treating them as investors = "essentially stealing their money."
- **Overpromising / underdelivering** — jeopardizes the long-term relationship; you get the one check but nothing more. Breach of contract risk.
- **Having "happy ears."** Be a little suspicious of early-stage prospects.
- **Measuring ROI backward** — "they'll spend money once they've made it" (you must spend to make money in sponsorship).
- **Thinking like a technician, not a marketer/business owner** — seeing only your own side of the deal.
- **Being indecisive / too slow** — taking 6–12 weeks (or a month+) to decide. "Sponsors want quick decisions." Sometimes "better to ask for forgiveness than permission" vs. red tape.
- **Thinking smaller is easy** — small "is boring." No need to go small in a $60B industry.
- **Thinking you're uniquely different** — "saying you're different is code for saying that you don't know what you're doing." Usually means no valuation/audience research. Hard to market "unique."
- **Vague audience segments** — "the middle class," "families," "everyone," "working people in their 40s" are "nebulous descriptors," not audiences. None will trigger an investment.
- **Emotional attachment to assets** — sometimes your favorite is low-value; sometimes a sleeper is worth more.
- **Confusing the sponsorship package** — get a colleague outside the project to test for clarity; list benefits next to each asset.
- **Proposal too long / too much company history** — eyes glaze over; gets deleted/trashed.
- **Changing asset pricing after presenting** without telling the sponsor = "a huge bait and switch."
- **Festival-specific:** not going for the decision-maker; bypassing smaller sponsors entirely (need a mix — 4–5 mid/large + smaller fillers); spamming proposals; not taking "no" for an answer; erasing "not right now" refusals (it's a no for this quarter/year, not forever — re-approach at fiscal year-end/early Q1); not asking the sponsor's help on activations; communication breakdowns after signing.
- **Podcast-specific:** pitching before valuing; including pricing in the proposal; deferring to everything the sponsor says (act like the expert); being too salesy (no 30-sec elevator pitch); high-value assets are NOT commercials/ads (only name recognition).
- **Multiyear-specific:** assuming a deal is multiyear (must be in the contract); not negotiating for more money; not producing a fulfillment report.
- **Don't chase the biggest brands first** (Nike, Coca-Cola) as a beginner — they get hundreds of requests/day and ignore most. Start local (breweries, distilleries, local insurers/dealerships).
- **Don't use digital tools to mask weak fundamentals** — "Digital Measures Can't Disguise Misguided or Lazy Sponsorship."

## 7. MINDSET & PRINCIPLES (verbatim where memorable)

- "Your audience is the greatest asset you can sell to a sponsor." (repeats across many posts — the central thesis)
- "The riches are in the niches."
- Sponsorship "is really just a marketing discipline" — "those sponsorship seekers who treat it like a marketing discipline have the most success."
- Sponsorship is "about giving and receiving... not a one-sided relationship where you mooch off the sponsor and give them nothing in return."
- "A sponsor is not a walking, talking ATM."
- "Sponsorship is not about how convincing you can be or how much you can beg for cash. Rather, it's about understanding the challenges of another company and then applying solutions to those problems."
- "Companies are full of people, and you want to connect with people."
- On curiosity (Einstein quote used): "The important thing is not to stop questioning." Approaching with curiosity (not selling) "does make you a better sponsorship salesperson simply because you're not trying to sell." The "perfume salesperson" analogy — push too early and the sponsor gives you "a wide berth."
- "Assuming anything in sponsorship is a mistake."
- "You cannot overcome an objection you believe." (sales adage)
- On value (Path to Failure): not knowing your value = selling a $20,000 ring for $200.
- "When in doubt, give your audience more of what they want using sponsorship, and everyone walks away happy."
- "Over delivery equals renewals year after year."
- "The sponsorship proposal cannot make the sale. That's up to you!"
- Three prerequisites for six/seven-figure deals: **audience data + knowing your value + sales skills** (plus high-value offers).
- Action vs. motion: "Both can seem like forward progress, but ultimately, only one pushes you toward your goal, and that's action. Motion is spinning your wheels."
- Sponsors lie (to avoid confrontation): silence is usually a brush-off; "just send me the proposal" is usually a polite no.
- Let the sponsor set the pace on when to see the proposal (could be after 1 meeting, 4 meetings, or never).
- "There is no 'best' way" to sell sponsorship — but the stages, when followed, bring in more dollars.
- Don't be too friendly with prospects — friendship "at the expense of your sponsorship program... is deleterious."
- The human touch reigns even in the AI age; beware technology fatigue.

## 8. INDUSTRY-SPECIFIC NUANCES

### Sports
- Audience segments: viewers at home, attendees, dedicated vs. casual fans, annual pass holders, corporate ticket holders — each gets *different* benefits (don't give general attendees season-ticket perks).
- Sponsor decision criteria they evaluate: buying habits, audience intimacy with property, reach, lifestyle/brand alignment, whether paying for too-broad an audience (reduces ROI), activation image fit, whether team/sport "is bigger than the brand" (hard to break through noise), other co-sponsors, logistical/staffing feasibility, past activation performance.
- Deloitte: fans want (1) safety/comfort/cleanliness, (2) a good view, (3) high-quality game, (4) exciting atmosphere. "If you get #4 right, you can generate funds that improve #1 through 3."
- Different fan bases differ: NHL/NFL fans care little about pre/post-game entertainment; NASCAR fans want unique entertainment; NFL highest on "exciting stadium atmosphere."
- 7 best sports activations: RFID wristband contests; free fantasy sports betting; playing games (e.g., Goodyear tire Plinko/"Throwdown" at NASCAR; Puma cornhole); branded photo walls/props (Dove greenscreen; Veuve Clicquot golden frame); exclusive fan experiences (AmEx US Open); sports memorabilia giveaways (McDonald's inflatable thundersticks); pop-up bars (Heineken). Activations must tie a sponsor's goal to an audience segment.
- Sports betting only viable for audiences 21+; pop-up bars/alcohol need majority legal drinking age.
- Activation strategy phases: Sponsor Onboarding → Clarify Agreements (checklist) → Confirm Execution (sign-offs, on-site reps, photos, screen captures, social proof).
- Annual sponsor summit recommended for goodwill + improvement input.
- Top sports companies to pursue: Pepsi, Red Bull, Coca-Cola, Optimum Nutrition, Reebok, Dick's Sporting Goods, Puma, State Farm, Budweiser, Castrol (mix of heavy-hitters + smaller names; smaller teams target Optimum Nutrition, Reebok, etc.).

### Alcohol in Sport
- **Pros:** appeals to a major adult segment (sports fans skew 35–49, then 18–34); alcohol sponsorship rising (~$106M new deals recently across NFL/MLB/NBA/NHL/MLS/college); very profitable (global alcohol market ~$2.4B 2024 → ~$3.8B 2032 [as quoted]); many targets to pursue.
- **Cons:** off-limits for under-21 / youth-heavy audiences; clashes with kid-friendly image (e.g., "Jose Cuervo Park"); concerns it normalizes/glorifies drinking; big-name brands often unreceptive to small orgs.
- Modern alcohol sponsorship = brand alignment + premium experiences + responsible-drinking programs (Anheuser-Busch + TEAM Coalition; age-gating, moderation messaging, education). WHO SAFER guidelines recommend limits. ~Half of Americans plan to drink less in 2025 (+44% vs 2023); sober-curious trend. Non-alc options (Athletic Brewing, Heineken 0.0, Budweiser Zero, Guinness 0.0) broaden appeal.
- Start local (breweries/distilleries) for first alcohol sponsorship.

### Festivals
- Audience is the primary asset (sponsors want access to it, not the event). Use only *current* data (5-year-old data ≠ today's audience). ≥20–25 datapoints/segment; present in graphs/tables/charts.
- Festival assets/activations are inherently more exciting than a business conference — leverage that.
- Need a *mix* of sponsors: 4–5 mid/large + smaller fillers. Avoid going too niche (large-only) or empty-looking (small-only).
- Re-approach "not right now" sponsors at fiscal year-end or early Q1.
- 2023 festival activation case studies (why they worked = audience need + sponsor need): About-Face pop-up beauty shop at Lollapalooza (heat-proof makeup, mirror with 25%-off text code); L'Oréal golden boombox/cassettes at Essence Fest; AmEx Centurion Lounge at ACL (card-gated VIP by main stage); Vodafone 5G GiveVision headsets for visually impaired at Wimbledon; Neutrogena sunscreen dispensers (1,700+ fl oz) at Coachella; Adobe Art Garden at Lollapalooza (local artists + Express templates); Beats Cleats & Eats (Coca-Cola + DoorDash/Goldfish/Acura, 4,500 samples) at MLS All-Star; Pinterest Manifestival at Cannes Lions.
- May 2024 activations: SHEIN rest station at VELD; IHEARTCOMIX × Universal "Girl Dinner" for *Abigail* (150 creators, 430M+ social reach — exclusivity/FOMO); M&M's Sweet Tour (traveling); Netflix/Shondaland *Bridgerton* Promenade (single-day + exclusive merch); SailGP projection preview; Porsche Electric Garden at Met Gala; Glow Recipe at Disney Springs (Sephora-purchase express line). Lesson: it's not about budget — satisfy audience need (FOMO, exclusives, rest, photos).
- Best festival sponsor types: Coca-Cola (king), Red Bull, Tito's, Monster, Pepsi — but "almost any company" fits given variety of festivals.
- Festival benefits for brands (sponsor side): big business/growing; brand visibility (esp. naming rights); new audience; strengthen current audience; competitive advantage; lead conversion (capture forms, contests/giveaways → email list → qualified leads); industry insights; increased sales (direct + indirect).
- RFID for festivals: cashless/hands-free payment, accurate attendance, ROI tracking, dwell/activation analytics, access control, gamification (contests, treasure hunts, drink tokens/order points), personalized check-in messages, cash-free VIP lounge. Incorporate sponsors when their goal = email growth/leads/conversion; collaborate on an RFID activation.
- 27 festival-planning apps grouped by job: event management (EventMobi, Bizzabo, Attendify, Eventzilla, Boomset, Odoo Events), ticketing (Eventbrite, Brown Paper Tickets, Yapsody, Setster), POS (Square), team/comms (Slack, Asana, Blossom, HeyTell, Planning Pod), notes/files (Evernote, Dropbox, 24me), audio (AudioTools, Decibel Ultra), weather (The Weather Channel), layout (MagicPlan), photos (Capsule), social walls (Everwall, LiveTweetApp), waivers (Smartwaiver).

### Podcasts / Influencers
- High-value assets are NOT commercials/ad reads (only name recognition). Use interviews on the show, product placement, giveaways/contests with the product as prize.
- Influencers need a **media kit** (not a proposal): bio (short), testimonials, past collabs, case studies (optional), social stats focused on *growth over time / viral posts* (not just current follower count).
- Influencer audiences differ by platform (IG/FB 25–34; Twitter/Pinterest 30–49; LinkedIn 46–55; TikTok 18–24; Snapchat 13–34; YouTube 15–25) — segment by platform + age + income.
- Influencer marketing platform reach (2021): Instagram 93%, IG Stories 83%, TikTok 68%, Facebook 63%, YouTube 48%, IG Reels 36%, Pinterest 35%, Twitter 32%, Snapchat 26%.
- Influencer tips: post often + consistently; awesome content (prospects dig into old posts — delete weak old content); 3 hashtags in post + up to ~9 sweet spot (vet meaning, e.g., #susanalbumparty cautionary tale); use geotags; make contact info easy; have a real website; use social automation.
- Match the sponsor's preferred communication channel (omnichannel; meet them where they're active). Phone calls still part of outreach.

### Nonprofits / Causes / Corporate Philanthropy
- Reframe: you're not just a receiver — your audience (qualified leads) is what the sponsor wants. Several corporate objectives tie to audience growth (lead gen, brand awareness, reach, sales).
- A great cause "is never going to motivate a sponsor company to hand over a check with six or seven zeroes." Cause is "irrelevant" to winning the deal.
- **Corporate philanthropy/giving ≠ sponsorship.** Foundation/CI/philanthropy leaders will say "we don't do sponsorship." Use them to ask who handles marketing/communications/sales goals — *that's* the road to sponsorship.
- Don't include "Mission, vision, impact measures or corporate philanthropy" in big-ticket sponsorship sells.
- Corporate giving types: employee giving, corporate foundations, matching gifts, skilled volunteering, employee/corporate grants, pro bono/in-kind, internal events, days of giving.
- Partner-fit example fails: child-health org × candy company; good fits: Habitat for Humanity × Lowe's/Valspar.

### Title / Naming / Presenting Rights (big-ticket)
- Single title sponsor (larger, exclusive fee) + several presenting sponsors = perfect balance; leverage high-profile brands as a selling feature to attract others.
- For naming rights, *traffic can outweigh audience* (any major corp for a stadium); for a hospital wing/college library, be more audience-specific.
- Ask a signed sponsor for their competitor list to avoid conflicts; avoid pairing culturally adverse brands.
- Negotiation moves: Determine their motivator (philanthropy? brand awareness? marketing?), Switch up the offer, Meet their needs, Go customized (exclusive stage branding, host a cocktail party/café, dedicated time slot), do a proper valuation (eyeballs can be tens/hundreds of millions — don't guess).
- 25+ "sweeten the pot" ideas to tempt prospects: named awards, product endorsements, venue-location input, backlit/larger signage, upgraded hospitality, VIP hosting, dedicated event website, hosted live social feeds/chats, branded contests, content re-shares, professional videography for their YouTube, blog guest features, DM insert samples, sponsoring a special room (arcade/escape room), celeb appearances at their booth, contra (equipment/staff/services), charity collection drives.

### The Nine Paths to a Sponsor (departments to target inside a company)
1. **Marketing** (C-level/VP/manager/director/coordinator/admin) — sponsorship is a marketing discipline.
2. **Sales** (director/VP/coordinator/manager) — get hard numbers (last quarter, predictions) to craft gap-filling solutions.
3. **Sponsorship Department** (manager, then committee members) — clearest understanding, but not all companies have one.
4. **Communications** (≠ marketing; spreads brand awareness) — director/VP/PR roles.
5. **Branding** — brand manager/strategist/director of brand marketing/strategy.
6. **Product Development** — APM up to CPO.
7. **HR** — not for dollars, but for warm introductions.
8. **Customer Retention** — knows the target audience (CX specialist, customer success advisor).
9. **Community Investment/Philanthropy/Foundation** (for nonprofits) — ask them who handles marketing/comms/sales (don't say "sponsorship").
- Use LinkedIn to map the hierarchy (names + titles + tenure) but **don't message via LinkedIn** (hidden messages; not part of the cadence) — write names down and log off.

### Future / Trends (2024+)
- Growth across all regions; more companies seeking sponsorship.
- Sports betting big but ~half don't renew; VR/AR valuable (360° viewing, 3D player cards, AR scavenger hunts); women's sports surging; AI integrating (largely untapped — content ideas for Gen Z, e.g., Kellanova/Pop-Tarts Bowl) but "AI lies" — triple-check; use AI for email drafts, image creation, report outlines, segmentation, data culling, activation ideation only — useless for negotiation/discovery.
- Naming rights rebounded in 2024 after a 10-year-low in 2023 — re-valuate.
- TikTok/Instagram/shorts important if you offer social assets; ~50% of Gen Z search on social, not Google.
- "Old-school" approaches stand out (e.g., mail a physical survey card).
- Pickleball = fastest-growing sport (8.9M US players 2023) — opportunity, but still must segment (not "everybody").
- Digital prospecting tools: CRM (contact/lead management), survey builders, email automation (automate the cadence), marketing software (segmentation — split by $5k–$10k income brackets / specific job titles), event management software (registration, reporting for case studies). Tips: don't jump in over your head, determine goals first, stay within budget (10–15% of income), check automation workflows, use tech as needed (human touch wins).

### Fan Engagement (sports)
- 6 ideas: connect on social (interactive polls with real stakes, challenges), host contests (photo/design contests where fans create showcaseable content; prizes can be merch/tickets/meet-greets), email marketing (personalized subject lines, segmentation, exclusives), work with great sponsors (survey-matched), experiential marketing/activations, VIP experiences (lounges, loyalty/themed days, special parking/access).
- Measure via: post-event satisfaction surveys (issue 1–2 days after), social engagement *rate* (interactions ÷ followers × 100; avg 1–5%), app downloads + *usage behavior* (retention beats raw download count).
