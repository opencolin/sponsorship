# Sponsorship Playbook — Consolidated Knowledge Digest (merged_A)

Consolidated from a large corpus of sponsorship-sales content on how to GET / sell / price / activate / renew corporate sponsorships. Deduplicated; "recurring" flags heavily-emphasized points.

---

## 1. Core Philosophy & Mindset

- **Sponsorship is marketing, NOT philanthropy / charity / a donation / major gifts / an investment.** It is a two-way, mutually beneficial B2B business arrangement. (The single most repeated principle.) "Sponsors aren't giving at all. They're paying for a service that benefits their marketing goals." "It has as much in common with philanthropy as baseball does with spaghetti." If you want donations ask a rich person; an investment, ask an angel — don't call it sponsorship.
- **Sponsorship is a verb / an activity, not a noun.** "There's no such thing as sponsors" — only companies using sponsorship as one tool among sales/marketing/advertising to hit goals. Ask "What are your marketing/sales goals?" not "What do sponsors want?"
- **It's about your AUDIENCE, not your cause/you.** "Your cause attracts an audience and your audience attracts the sponsor." "Companies want to sponsor your audience, not you." "Sponsors don't sponsor properties, they sponsor audiences." Don't take it personally — it means you own something valuable.
- **Your audience is your single most valuable asset.** Analogies (recurring): your audience is your "Fabergé egg," your "golden goose," "a necklace worth millions you didn't know about." "No audience, no sponsorship. The end."
- **You are the prize / it's an even playing field.** You are not a beggar; you hold the power because you own the audience the brand wants. "There's only one thing brands will spend money into debt to get — customers (a fancy word for audience)." "If you're begging, you've already lost." Reframe "asking for money" as a professional exchange. (Dermot the gym owner: "I'm auditioning *you*… we're not beggars.")
- **People sell sponsorship, not the package.** "The sponsorship proposal/package does NOT make the sale — you do." (Signature line; ends nearly every video.) "People buy from people."
- **Quality of audience > quantity / size.** "1000 university students" is worth far less than "25 people who control corporate budgets in excess of $10 million." Some of the biggest deals come from the smallest audiences. "More is not better; better is better."
- **The riches are in the niches.** Smaller, hyper-specific segments are worth more.
- **Small is an advantage.** A small shop that knows its audience and delivers can out-service juggernauts and "wrestle sponsors away" — especially for $5K–$100K sponsors. Sell your size: full customization, decision-makers at every meeting, no bureaucracy. "Working in a small shop is only a disadvantage if you decide it is." "Nobody cares more about your sales, brand, and sponsors than you and your staff."
- **Fall in love with the problem you solve, not your solution.** Plus Keith Cunningham: "Shoes that don't fit are not a bargain at any price." Rigid packages = jamming a size-7 shoe onto every sponsor's foot.
- **Logos are not value; experiences/outcomes are.** "Brand awareness is not enough anymore… and likely never was."
- **There are no shortcuts / no silver bullet.** "Sponsors are hard to find because they want to be hard to find." Treat sponsorship as a discipline with its own rules ("like accounting, like law"); a "full-contact sport, done face to face." Prepare for a 3–5 year commitment ("you don't propose marriage on the first date").
- **Start before you're ready / build the plane as you fly.** "The best time to plant a tree was 20 years ago; the second best is right now." Waiting is "the most expensive choice." Amelia Earhart: "Always think with your stick forward." James Russell Lowell: "All the beautiful sentiments in the world weigh less than a single lovely action." "Fortune favors the bold."
- **Rejection is normal and impersonal.** Seth Godin: "He who fails the most wins." "If you can remember the name of the person who told you no, you're not doing enough prospecting." "This is business… it has nothing to do with you as an individual" — use losses to refine.
- **Anxiety/fear is the bridge, and a barometer.** Reinterpret anxiety as excitement ("I'm not stressed, I'm amped"). "Before the cold call the answer was already no — you've only shifted a guaranteed no to a maybe yes." Fear also diagnoses gaps: don't know what to say → you lack audience data; can't offer ideas → weak activations; can't prove ROI → do valuation.
- **"The only thing worse than prospecting not working is when it works."** The scariest moment is "Sure, how can I help you?" Prospecting is "like the gym" — getting started is the scariest part; it gets easier with reps but never goes away.
- **Get paid to show up, not to close** (Emanuel's reframe): compute $/hour from total revenue ÷ selling hours; you earn it whether or not a given call closes. Shift from "selling people" to "serving people."
- **Listen more than you speak; be in asking mode, not telling mode.** "There is no better research tool than the question mark." "Listen to discover, not listen to respond." "The only person who knows exactly what your sponsor wants is your sponsor — so ask them."
- **Efficiency ≠ effectiveness** (Drucker: "Efficiency is doing things right; effectiveness is doing the right thing"). The industry obsesses over efficiency and isn't effective. (See Motion vs. Action, §17.)
- **Don't talk about yourself / speak the sponsor's language (marketing outcomes).** Translate: donor count ≈ leads; grant ≈ investment; impact ≈ fulfillment. The one-page request gets funded over the fist-thick binder.
- **Relationships are nonlinear and long-term** (Nazareth/Hughes): "Nothing moves in straight lines." "It's the small time invested and not the great big moves that net the biggest results." Make friends before you need them. "Even the largest companies are run by people."
- **Be choosy / take the pressure off.** Disqualify value-mismatched sponsors early; be comfortable that your offering may not fit, and that's OK (keeps dialogue alive after a "no").
- **Don't sell out, but don't assume your audience will revolt.** If your audience would genuinely resent corporate money, you have a *double obligation* not to take it — but usually the worry is a false assumption, and refusing just hands the sponsor to a competitor or to ambush marketing.
- **Sponsorship is hard and not a cure-all.** Sponsors are demanding; if they don't get ROI they'll ask for a refund. Sometimes sponsors create more problems than they solve.

## 2. The End-to-End Process (overall sequence/funnel)

### The canonical sponsorship sales sequence (recurs in nearly every source; doing it OUT of order is the #1 cause of failure)
1. **Audience research / survey FIRST** — build hyper-segmented data before anything else.
2. **Prospect** — build a prospect list FROM the audience data (concentric circles, §7).
3. **Research each prospect** — public info: brand values, products, partners, recent news/hires.
4. **Outreach** — short email/DM/call whose ONLY goal is to book a discovery session (NOT to sell).
5. **Discovery session(s)** — ask 5–10 questions, listen, qualify; bring nothing. (Often several meetings.)
6. **Create/customize assets & activations** — only AFTER discovery, tailored to what you learned.
7. **Valuation** — value each asset/activation one at a time against market value; adjust up/down.
8. **Negotiate** — money comes up here, not before. Proposal only if/when asked.
9. **Contract** — lawyer-reviewed; only sign deliverables you can fulfill. (A contract ≠ a proposal; always have a contract.)
10. **Deliver / activate** — track and quantify everything meticulously during the event.
11. **Fulfillment (wrap-up) report** — delivered fast post-event; opens the renewal conversation.
12. **Renew / upgrade** — meet to review the report, renegotiate (usually for MORE money; multi-year).

Verbatim shorthand: *"Research, discovery call, meeting, meeting, meeting, draft proposal, update proposal, success (or more edits)."* Key dictum: "Find a sponsor first, then create assets tailored to their needs based on what you learned during discovery." Doing assets first is "backward."

### Anatomy of a sponsorship program (the prep stack)
Define goals (revenue? publicity? new customers?) → create assets list → evaluate/value assets → develop activation opportunities → add to package → create proposal → pitch → close.

### The Moves-Management Sales Funnel (track activity, not revenue)
"Don't track revenue as your primary metric; track activity volume at each stage. When done properly revenue comes in almost on its own." Build the pipeline *backwards* from a revenue goal. Stages (in Excel): **Cold prospects → Cold outreach → Discovery meeting → Invited to submit proposal → Yes/No (closed).** (See worked funnel math, §15.) "The secret to good prospecting is volume, knowing your numbers, and every single day moving through your moves-management cycle."

### Named variant frameworks
- **5-Step Sponsorship System** ("Direct Outreach"): (1) Audience data — 25 pts + 3 avatars ("you cannot pitch what you cannot describe"); (2) Prospect selection (~200 companies overlapping your audience); (3) Contact identification (named human in marketing with budget authority); (4) Direct outreach (email/call asking for a 15-min meeting; no deck/pitch/proposal); (5) Discovery-led proposal (built collaboratively; closes because it solves their stated problem). Steps 1–3 prep, 4 = revenue, 5 = closing.
- **9-Step "Sponsorship Magnet"**: (1) Stop chasing; (2) Stop copying everyone; (3) Know your audience (niche down); (4) Grow your audience (lead magnets, opt-ins, mobile site, nurture funnel); (5) Use case studies (numbers > grids); (6) Find media partners (biggest multiplier); (7) Do what you say; (8) Produce a fulfillment report; (9) Use social media wisely (post assets/activations/data, never "we need money").
- **5 Steps to Selling Sponsorship** (webinar): (1) Change your mindset (hardest/only step); (2) Know your value; (3) Always provide a fulfillment report; (4) Stop using the package to make the sale; (5) Know your audience.
- **The Sponsorship Blueprint** ("My Opportunity is Different"): Define offering → Stand out (value, bundling, readable sales material) → Prospect off your audience → Launch & renew.
- **7 Ways to Offer Real Value**: detailed audience research → learn needs in discovery → customize assets → co-create activations → skip the proposal unless asked → track data during the event → fulfillment report.

## 3. Audience Research & Data

- **Audience research is "the heart" of the program — do it first.** Without it, prospecting and packaging both fail. "No audience data kills sponsorship programs dead."
- **Survey your audience.** Audience = whoever engages: attendees, listeners, members, donors, volunteers, board, program users, investors, partners. For nonprofits, audience = donors/volunteers/board/those who care about the cause — NOT the people you serve (a food bank's audience is its donors/volunteers, not its food recipients).
- **Niche down relentlessly** ("Russian nesting doll" / "sieve" analogy) until you can't split further. Neighborhood/borough not city; job title + income bracket not "occupation." Avoid vague descriptors: "general public," "families," "the middle class," "40-somethings from Philly." Targeting "the general public" (~570M in North America) earns a form-letter "no."
- **Avatars / buyer personas:** create a minimum of **3** distinct niched personas on top of the macro audience, each with its own ~25 data points. Name and personify them (e.g., "Financial Frank," a Brooklyn tax specialist, $75k/yr, two kids, time-poor, likes tech brands). An avatar is an amalgamation, not a real person. "You cannot pitch what you cannot describe."
- **Vanity metrics ≠ audience data.** "1,000 people come to our conference," follower counts, "60% female" are shallow. Sponsors want buying habits, interests, employment, education, decision-making authority — e.g., "of the 25-yr-old women, how many are from Detroit, make $110k, work in pharma?" "People are more than just one thing."
- **Data points to gather (beyond age/gender/geography/marital status/occupation/income):** customer longevity, spend per year/quarter/month, brands consumed/loved, brands that advertise to them, # of your events attended, decision-making capacity / budget control / staff reporting to them, education level, intended purchases (car, house, banking, insurance, retirement).
- **Refresh cadence:** resurvey **every 6 months** (and immediately after any big audience/listenership jump). Data is "current" only if collected within the last **3–6 months**; older than 6 months "is not current enough."
- **New property with no audience:** build *projected* data from ticket sales; for a brand-new event, research the **interest audience** (e.g., running magazines/orgs for a race; sponsorship pros for a sponsorship conference). Refine after the event.
- **Survey mechanics** (Chris's exact method): Pro SurveyMonkey ≈ $350/yr; send 2 e-blasts (e.g., Tue 10am + Thu 10am); incentive = chance to win one of three $25 gift cards; give ~1 month to respond with a few reminders. Yields 30+ data points at 95% confidence / 5% margin of error. (See survey-size math, §15.)

## 4. Properties & Inventory (Assets)

- **Property** = any sponsorship opportunity / rights-holder (event, festival, podcast, athlete, team, conference, building, esport, nonprofit) — "not a physical property." Also defined as a *collection of related assets serving a specific audience in a specific way*; a rights-holder can have many properties (gala, golf tournament, race series, naming rights, networking series).
- **Asset** = a single sellable unit (one tweet, one e-blast, a logo placement, a booth, a speaking slot, signage, tickets, sampling rights, naming, VIP/hospitality). Alone it's "little more than advertising unless packaged under a property, valued, and activated."
- **Inventory** = the full list of everything you're willing to sell. **Inventory development** = canvassing your event/venue to list all asset opportunities.
- **Inventory-building brainstorm** (30-min session with committee, board, leadership, comms AND program staff). Spend only **5 minutes** on "what can we put logos on?" then **ban the word "logo."** Ask: What can we sell? What speaking opportunities are we missing? What product-placement opportunities are we missing? What were the 3 coolest sponsorship/cause-marketing things you saw in the last 3 months? Bundle results into categories.
- **Asset categories to check:** naming rights of physical spaces; naming rights of programs; signage; product giveaways/samples; exhibiting; speaking opportunities; webpage; social media; newsletters/mailings; pass-through benefits; use of venue; paid media; traditional media; employee benefits.
- **Whittle to superstars:** prefer ~3 superstar assets per property over 7 mediocre ones. "If your prospect list is too small, your asset inventory is too small" — build more assets to drive prospecting. Seekers can generate "close to 100 assets."
- **Asset value tiers** (from "Finding High-Value Assets"):
  - **Low-value (awareness only):** sponsor mentions, social posts, signage/logos, marketing-material distribution, logos on website, press-release inclusion, branded clothing, CPM ad reads (podcast equivalent of logos).
  - **High-value (awareness + leads + conversions):** speaking opportunities, media partnerships, tailored website content/guest posts, broadcasting rights.
  - **Premium-value (exclusive, unbuyable elsewhere):** new-customer/client acquisition, naming rights, direct lead capture, pass-through benefits.
  - Keep a MIX — don't drop all low-value; combine to reach revenue goals; trend away from low-value over time. Don't get stuck only in lead-gen or only in awareness.
- **Logos are the LEAST valuable asset** (recurring). Only (weakly) drive brand awareness; lost in clutter (many sponsors, fast race cars). Challenge: **list 50 assets that require NO logo placement.** Exceptions where a logo genuinely works: **runway branding** at fashion shows and **race-vehicle livery** (merch/video-game value) — seen for the whole event.
- **Title / naming / presenting rights:** Title/naming = the property is *named after* the sponsor (Company Name Festival) with the name embedded in the wordmark. "Presented by" alone = presenting rights, NOT title. **Name-in-wordmark** is "necessary but not sufficient" for title sponsorship.
- **Exclusivity:** category exclusivity (no competing brands at all) vs. direct-competitor-only. Must be explicitly defined and lawyer-vetted (cautionary tale: a "bank" deal that also implicated *insurance*). Raises price + credibility.
- **In-kind / contra:** free goods/services/media instead of cash — "really just a fancy word for sampling." Widely viewed as the least-favorite asset (lowest value; $20k of branded pens ≠ $20k cash). Still recognize the partner and run activations + fulfillment.
- **Pass-through benefits / pass-through rights:** your audience connects with one of your (or your media partner's) advertisers/partners.

## 5. Valuation & Pricing

- **Valuation is mandatory** (recurring). You can't talk money until you know your value, and you can't know your value until a proper valuation. Sponsors increasingly send seekers away to get a third-party valuation before negotiating. Threshold rule: if seeking **$50K+ total** (festival), you *must* do a formal valuation.
- **Method:** research the **market rate** of each asset by asking *"Where else / how could my sponsor get this same exposure/benefit/outcome WITHOUT me?"* Compare to ad rate cards, PPC/Google AdWords, banner ads, local-paper ads, LinkedIn InMail, social-ad rates the platforms reveal, what a radio pro charges for an interview, etc. Cost per view × your reach.
- **Value EACH asset individually, one at a time** ("How do you eat an elephant? One bite at a time"). Then optionally assign the whole property a value. Value **each sponsor's property separately** — 5 sponsors = 5 valuations (properties differ per prospect).
- **Never** guess, price off your costs, copy a valuation found online, or "tack on zeroes." Sponsors can tell when numbers don't add up (e.g., "primarily logos but asking $50,000") and will ask your process. "Show your work like math class." Market value changes geographically and over ~6 months. Property value "is as unique as your fingerprint" — no magic list of values exists.

### Brand value (intangible value uplift) — named method
- Brand value = "the *additional* benefit your sponsors receive by reaching your audience through your property as opposed to other forms of media & marketing" (NOT brand awareness; NOT your audience's size).
- Score yourself honestly (a %) on factors sponsors value: knowledge of audience, audience loyalty, reputation/trust, uniqueness/low competition, fully-customized activations, exclusivity, annual sponsor summit/networking. Typically **10–30% per factor.** Sum → total brand value %.
- Convert % to cents and **add to each asset's hard value.** Example: a $1.00/view logo at 92% brand value → **$1.92/view** (nearly doubles revenue).
- **Worked brand-value table (numbers admittedly made up):** Knowledge of audience 12% + High audience loyalty 15% + Fully customized activations 20% + Low competition 15% + High-value/high-trust brand 20% + Annual sponsor summit & networking 10% = **92% total.**
- **Ceiling rule:** if you're charging **more than 3× hard asset value**, you're being too generous with brand value; 7× exists but is "not normal."
- Document the math; never inflate — sponsors will check (they can target your social followers via paid ads without paying you, so guessed valuations get exposed).

### The Sponsorship Marketing Funnel (digital-marketing equivalents; assets get MORE valuable deeper down)
- **CPM** (cost per 1,000 impressions): marketers pay only **$5–$10/1,000.** = logo placement (cheapest, lowest value).
- **CPC** (cost per click): avg **$2–$10/click.** = driving traffic to the sponsor's site (highest when tied to a contest/giveaway).
- **CPS** (cost per send; LinkedIn InMail): avg **$0.80, up to $1/send.** = e-blasting your list (more valuable if segmented + tailored).
- **CPL** (cost per lead): bottom/most valuable, marketers pay **$50–$2,000+ per lead** (e.g., $2,000 PPC / 15 leads = $133.33 CPL). = customizable, data-driven assets that generate leads.

### Value Ladder / Sales-Funnel grading of assets
Map each asset to a stage of the SPONSOR's sales funnel: **Awareness → Lead generation → Conversion → Sales.** The further down, the higher the value. Ask of each asset: Can it drive awareness? Generate leads? Convert? Drive sales? (A logo = awareness only → low. Product placement = all four → high.) The value ladder is the inverse of the (widening) sales funnel — value rises as you climb.

### The 7-Step Valuation Process ("Essential Guide to Sponsorship Evaluation")
1. **Property building** — identify your properties.
2. **Build asset inventory** — brainstorm everything with the whole team; 5 min on logos then ban the word; bundle into categories.
3. **Identify audience segments + run a survey** — niche down (25+ data points each); learn preferred brands + intended purchases.
4. **Find tangible values / market comparables** — divide inventory into like-categories; value one asset at a time; "Where else can a sponsor get the same outcome?"
5. **Find brand value (intangible)** — 10–30% uplift per factor; add to each asset's hard value.
6. **Develop 5 turnkey activation ideas** — run them through the value calculator (NOT GSB; conversation-drivers).
7. **Identify gaps, prospects, and a sales strategy** — set revenue goals; prospect off audience data; improve weak brand-value items.

### Pricing tactics
- **Pricing is verbal, after valuation, during negotiation — never in the proposal** (a written number reads as set-in-stone; you may undersell, or hand them only "take it or leave it").
- A mix of low/mid/high-value assets gives sponsors freedom to choose.
- A sponsor may pay MORE than your valuation if eager for your audience — another reason not to hard-code a price.
- Be willing to take somewhat lower (a few hundred/thousand off); if a sponsor wants a *radical* discount, walk away.
- **Set minimum AND maximum** (esp. cause marketing): minimum recognizes your brand's intrinsic value (you get paid even at zero sales); maximum protects the sponsor (expect them to ask).

## 6. Activation

- **Definition:** an experiential-marketing opportunity that produces a measurable sponsor outcome — "cool, interesting, unique, *effective* ways to engage your audience." "Activations are what you sell to sponsors, not logos." Contrast: "Putting samples on a table is an asset; letting them come, sending e-blasts, running a contest, and giving a speaking slot is activation." "Collecting emails at the gate is an asset; running a raffle for an iPad in exchange for a ballot is activation."
- **The golden rule (recurring):** a great activation fulfills **ONE need of your audience AND ONE need of your sponsor simultaneously** — it lives in the Venn-overlap of sponsor needs ↔ audience needs. "Great sponsorship solutions come from patterns, not individual answers."
- **Substance over flashiness.** Add "pizzazz" only after the activation achieves the required goals. Don't choose activations for "cool" factor or expense — they must be **ultra-effective**. Low-cost (even "free") activations routinely beat sponsors' expectations.
- **Output vs. Outcome:** output = the activation itself (a photo booth); outcome = the measurable result (brand-awareness/email-signup/web-traffic lift). Choose for outcomes. Be specific about how the sponsor *measures* success (they may need 5,000 new followers, not "a few hundred"); don't let your definition of success bleed into theirs.
- **You lead, but co-create.** You (not the sponsor) must bring *most* activation ideas, but "the burden is not on you to come up with all the great ideas — co-create them together." "Who knows a sponsor's pain points better than the sponsor themselves?" Bring loose ideas; let the sponsor modify/nix; speak up if their idea is an obvious mismatch.
- **Best time to brainstorm = right after signing** (book an "activation brainstorming session," NOT an invoice — the first post-signature touch). You have brainpower + rapport.
- **FOMO / scarcity** is a reliable lever (limited tickets/samples, exclusive merch, limited-time pop-ups) — but don't limit so hard the audience can't experience it.
- **Worked example (email-list activation, recurring):** sponsor struggles to build an email list + audience wants interactive moments → run a contest where the prize is the sponsor's product and entry requires an email signup. Sponsor gets list + product trial; audience gets engagement.
- **Reward engagement proportionally** (Amex/Coachella). **Harness the event's atmosphere** (Aperol at hot Coachella → hydration + Italian theming).
- **Budgeting for activations:** budget up front. Rule of thumb: if the sponsor goes **5–10% over** total package value, eat it; beyond that, the sponsor pays (especially if THEY proposed the pricier idea). **Activation credit** = a "spending account" line item a sponsor can draw from (within reason) when your budget has no room.

## 7. Prospecting & Pipeline Building

- **Always prospect FROM audience data, never from "biggest brands in the industry" or by budget/$.** (Recurring.) Prospecting by sponsor budget/fame is the wrong criterion — the audience won't engage with a brand they don't care about, so you miss deliverables (breach risk).
- **Two phases:** (1) research / "suspecting" — identify categories & businesses that fit your audience, then find the right human contacts inside them; (2) outreach to find who has interest.
- **The "who cares about THIS?" test:** take each asset and ask "who cares about this *asset*?" — not who cares about your mission. Who would want to speak to this group? Who'd borrow your logo to talk to their customers?

### Concentric Circle Prospecting (named framework — recurring)
Organize prospects by value, hottest in the center:
- **Circle 1 (hottest):** brands your audience already buys from / consumes / likes (named directly in your survey).
- **Circle 2 (very warm):** brands that already advertise/market to your audience (survey: "name 10 brands you remember advertising to you in the last year").
- **Circle 3 (cooler):** brands that *should* advertise to your audience but don't yet (your research; e.g., audience likes tech → Apple, Samsung, Microsoft).
- **Circle 4 (coolest):** competitors of every brand from circles 1–3 (add 2–3 per brand).
- Yields ~**60 to 100+** brands. A first circle of 10–30 is "excellent shape."

### Expansion / multiplication math
For each brand the audience names → add brands that advertise to that brand's audience → add brands that *should* market to your audience → add direct competitors of every brand listed (extra Excel columns; "if one bank is interested, I bet they all are"). Easily reaches 50–60+ prospects. The "5-Step System" targets ~**200** companies; consultants cap themselves at **~200–300** (bigger lists can't be qualified). **Motorsport: "the sky is the limit"** — almost any non-conflicting brand works, so 100+ is easy.

### Sourcing & rules
- **Don't chase the big fish** (recurring). Fortune-500/household brands (Red Bull, Nike) are inundated with thousands of requests/day and infinitely picky; smaller/mid-sized sponsors have greater NEED and respond. ~99% of big-brand asks don't work out.
- **Don't steal competitors' sponsors / auto-jump Pepsi→Coke.** Use competitor research only to *generate ideas* (for each competitor's sponsor, brainstorm 1–2 similar brands → "two dozen prospects"), then filter through YOUR audience as the litmus test. Those leads are "warm at best."
- **Look locally first** — shared community, audience overlap, less wasted time; recommend local sponsors for the first few deals even if your audience isn't hyperlocal. But prospect local and national identically — your *audience data* decides the fit. Common path: start local, prove value, climb the brand's corporate ladder to regional/national.
- **Look beyond obvious sponsors** to business/marketing media (Ad Age, Mediapost, Strategy, Forbes, Inc.) and niche trade magazines whose readership matches your audience (fashion → Glamour, Happi, GCI).
- **For first-timers/new festivals:** use competitors' sponsor lists, and look at **brands the performers/musicians are affiliated with** (their fans = your audience).
- **Respect "small" sponsors** ($500–$10K) — highest-likelihood upgrades up the ladder; "small to you may be huge to them" (some 2–3-person firms spend $100K+/mo on marketing).
- **No magic database of "decision makers" exists.** No list of direct phone numbers to "the sponsor." You have 100% accountability via old-fashioned prospect research.

### Named pipeline plays
- **The Business Breakfast:** find someone with a conveniently located office + boardroom for up to **20 people**; ask them to host a low-key networking breakfast (**coffee + muffins only**) and invite **3 colleagues**; find **3 others** in your network, each bringing **3 people**. Network for **one hour before the business day**. Host introduces you; in **3 minutes or less** state who you are / what you do / your project / how they can help. The only two ways they can help: (1) buy you a coffee about the project in the next two weeks, or (2) host an identical event for you next month.
- **The Friday Five:** every Friday (except 2 weeks/year), around **1 p.m.**, connect **5 prospects** to your referral network; draft the intro email so your contact can forward it; use LinkedIn to confirm common contacts. Business Breakfast + Friday Five together = the "**394 warm prospects**" play.

## 8. Outreach (cold email/call, follow-up cadences)

- **The only goal of outreach = book the discovery session.** NOT to pitch, NOT to attach a proposal. "Email's only job is to warm up the call." "If you're sending things by email expecting a purchase, you are not prospecting, you are spamming."
- **Short, mobile-friendly, personal. 3 sentences max** (recurring; "more than 3 sentences = too long → deleted"). Direct-outreach formula: (1) who I am / my property; (2) I see you serve niche X, we have audience Y (shared audience) + something specific about their recent work (news item or new public hire); (3) ask for a 15-min meeting "Tuesday or Thursday." ~90 min to draft 4 quality emails.
- **Find an internal/warm contact before going cold** (the single most-repeated outreach tactic). "Find someone who knows someone" — colleagues, vendors, partners, past guests, staff, board, donors. A shared connection is "your foot in the door" (gets consideration, not a guaranteed deal). The contact need not be in the sponsorship department, just inside the company.
- **Target the right human:** titles containing **Brand, Marketing, Sponsorship, Business Development, Communications, Product, Sales, Experiential Marketing**, or business owners — NOT a generic "sponsorship" inbox. CSR-title people handle corporate philanthropy (sometimes a *different* budget/decision-maker). Get as close to the decision-maker as possible (fewer layers); a low contact turns your ask into "a game of telephone." Add: "If I've reached the wrong person, could you point me to the best contact?" — a "wrong contact" reply is a useful answer (removes a dead lead, supplies the right one).
- **Propose a specific date/time yourself** — don't ask "what works for you?" (endless ping-pong). They accept or counter; agree to their counter unless impossible.
- **Skip website sponsorship request forms** — efficient-feeling busywork; everyone uses them, they get buried; "first contact rarely comes from a contact form."
- **Social DMs are fine** — use a *business* account, keep it *private* (never comment "please sponsor me" on a public post).
- **Phone:** schedule cold calls **first thing in the morning** (more energy, less busy). Talking is often faster than emailing and forges human connection. **Voicemail works when email doesn't** (a 30-sec voicemail naming the same audience overlap + same 15-min ask "proves the property is real"). Prefer email over phone if you're not a smooth talker.
- **Avoid in early outreach:** the word **"sponsorship"** (and buzzword swaps like "partnership"); emojis (may render as blank rectangles; less professional); jokes / over-creativity; mission statements, event details, program outcomes, your life story.

### Follow-up / cold cadences (multiple variants across sources)
- **General rule:** wait at least **1 week** before the first follow-up (3 business days if the event is imminent); follow up at most ~2–3 times, ~1 week apart; after **3 attempts/contacts** with no response, move on (silence = no). Never call/email 10× in a day.
- **7-day cadence (blog):** Day 1 Email#1 / Day 2 Email#2 / Day 3 Email#3 / Day 4 Phone#1 / Day 5 Email#4 / Day 6 Email#5 / Day 7 Phone#2. Run on ONE contact at a time; if no response, restart with a 2nd then 3rd contact. **Max 3 contacts ≈ 3 weeks.**
- **The 7×7 method:** a structured 7-touch cadence to warm a lead so the call is "lukewarm" not cold (templates in the Sponsorship Accelerator). Phone-call line after the emails: *"Yep, that's me — I sent you three emails, the 7×7 method says I have to phone now."*
- **Direct-outreach cadence:** wait ≥24 hrs after first contact, then continue ~7 days combining email AND phone before moving on (motorsport: every other day for ~a week).
- **Podcast cold-email follow-up:** wait 2–3 days between emails; up to 5 emails over 14 days (Day 1, ~3, 7, 10, 14), then stop. Follow-up emails shorter (50–75 words).
- **PP / "Persistent but Polite" / "Pleasant but Persistent" / "courting"** (Kevin Grothe; Candace Goar): after initial contact, reach out by email or phone "at least every couple of weeks"; a "no" this year ≠ no next year (Candace courted one org **8–10 years** before landing them as a stage sponsor; each cycle, "What I proposed didn't work — what else are you looking for?").
- **"Always believe the prospect"** (mentor "Travis"): "Not right now" means not right now; "no budget" means no budget — don't go away until they say "please go away."
- **When a sponsor says no, immediately call their competitor** (Kevin) — grew one competitor from local → regional → corporate to **10×** the lost sponsor's spend; when the original wanted back, "No."

## 9. Discovery Meetings

- **Discovery is NOT a sales meeting** (recurring central doctrine). It's exploratory — "sniffing each other out," a "job interview," investigative journalism. Both sides ask questions. "There's no need to close the deal today." A real discovery meeting "shouldn't feel like fun — it should feel like work."
- **The first meeting's real goal = the SECOND meeting** (plus the info to build a tailored package). Expect **several meetings** with a target early on — clear your calendar.
- **Bring NOTHING.** A short question list + notepad/pen (or an approved AI note-taker with consent). NO proposal, pricing sheet, tiers, one-pager, or leave-behind. "How do I know what to give them when I don't know their goals?" Bringing a package signals you're there for you. (Chris brings nothing at all — keeps notes in his head and summarizes back; if you must take notes, ask: "Do you mind if I take notes? I just don't want to forget anything.")
- **Ask 5–10 questions** (ideally 5–7), even in a longer meeting; **tailor** to what the prospect says (go off-script — don't just read the first 10). Memorize them if you can.
- **Ask, then listen.** Summarize answers back to confirm. Listen for emotional cues (frustration, urgency, excitement). Tag insights **H/M/L** priority. Note repeated phrases (reveal core values/pain points). "Sponsors are like onions" — multiple layers; peel back shortcomings with the right questions + persistence.
- **What to uncover:** goals, needs, stakeholders, concerns, target market, latest campaigns, financial shortcomings, future plans, what they've tried and why it fell short — including the sensitive "ugly side" they never reveal online (sales figures, audience decreases, abandoned carts, email bounces).
- **Ask budget directly and early** — "What's your budget?" (not "Do you have a budget?"), then go silent (the "game of chicken / break first"). Drill: "What's your budget to solve this / hit these sales goals?" If they won't answer after ~8 attempts, that's feedback your discovery was weak.
- **Format-agnostic** (in-person, Zoom, phone — substance matters). **Discovery can span multiple calls** (Dave Stewart runs it over 3–4 calls for podcasts). Have a work-appropriate icebreaker; avoid "so how's that [sports team]?"
- **If they say "just send a proposal" on the phone, go straight into discovery right then** (avoids scheduling back-and-forth; "send your pitch deck" = "a fancy way of saying I am definitely going to cancel this call").
- **Qualify the sponsor** (you have the right to walk away). After discovery, ask per asset/activation: **"Would my SPONSOR find this valuable?"** (forget whether YOU think so), then build something tailored and earn the right to a follow-up.

## 10. Proposals & Business Cases

- **Skip the proposal unless asked.** Many deals close with no formal written proposal; Chris has sold "tens of millions" with none. If they want it they'll ask — after 2–3 meetings, 4–5, or never (all fine). A proposal asked for *early* (after ~5 min / 1–2 emails) is usually a brush-off; after several meetings it's genuine.
- **Never attach a proposal to a cold email / never shotgun-blast unsolicited proposals** — straight to trash/spam; you get blocked. ("Like throwing limp spaghetti at a wall." — Mark Sabourin.)
- **A proposal is a sales AID, not a sales tool.** "Proposals don't sell sponsorship, people do." Ideally deliver a *draft* live in a second meeting and shape it together (the "Hybrid Proposal" — standardize the rarely-changing parts like audience data, customize the rest).
- **Business case** = a no-price outline (audience data, activation ideas, media footprint, case studies — NO pricing/values) sent ONLY when a sponsor demands something before agreeing to meet. Enough to decide whether to meet.

### The 6-page / 7-step proposal template
A proposal is **~6 pages.** **NEVER write "sponsorship proposal" or "sponsorship package" anywhere in it** (incl. the title). May exceed 6 pages for *data/case studies* but never because you told your life story. **≥50% must be audience data.**
- **Page 1 — Title / cover:** logo, opportunity/event name, tagline, date, optional website. Keep simple; don't oversize logo/title. (Festival: a great photo + name + dates + location only — "the more you add, the more likely they delete it.")
- **Page 2 — Describe your audience** (BEFORE money): current data (within 3–6 months), demographics/psychographics/geographics, niched, pie charts/graphs + a photo of a packed past event.
- **Page 3 — Describe your opportunity (3 paragraphs, ~6–7 sentences each):** Para 1 = your cause/company/what you do/what you need (ONE paragraph; NO sob story). Para 2 = the event/program (what it is, how long running, objectives, attendance, this year's goals). Para 3 = board/delegates/attendees/users + market research.
- **Page 4 — Menu (NOT "levels"):** title it with the OBJECTIVE — "Engage Leaders in Industry X," "Reach People of a Certain Age/Geography," "Come and Meet X Sector." Open with your philosophy/how you work with sponsors. Frame items as suggestions to start the process; list by what they help with (brand building, product placement, sampling, contests, growing database, thought leadership). **Do NOT** end with "we're willing to customize, contact us."
- **Page 5 — Sample activations:** branding, sampling, attendee experience, contests. They won't buy these off the shelf — they prove you know the game and drive conversation. (Aim to show ~3 distinct activation types: contest, sampling, email collection, social campaign.)
- **Page 6 — Contact:** CTA like "We want to hear from you!"; invite them to say what's missing/what to add/change (make it a conversation tool); list business address, phone, and a **real personal email** (never info@/hello@/sponsorship@). **NO cut-out payment/order form.**

### Things to SKIP / never put in a proposal (recurring)
- **No pricing**, no dollar figures, no cuttable coupons, no check/credit-card cutout, no order form.
- **No Gold/Silver/Bronze tiers** (or any renamed tiers).
- **Don't say "custom"/"customized"** — let the customization speak for itself.
- **No "Don't see something you like? Call us and we'll customize"** — the single worst line (signals you *won't* customize and didn't bother). (Emerson: "Who you are speaks so loudly that I can't hear what you're saying.")
- Don't waste pages on company history (one real proposal: 23 of 36 pages were company history; another, 23 of 36 — "stuff nobody cares about"). A proposal is a marketing document, not a "case for support."
- **Use the prospect's exact language** from discovery in the proposal & cover letter — recognition accelerates closing (see Anissa Cook, §19).

## 11. Negotiation & Closing

- **Money comes up only after valuation, during negotiation** — never upfront. Many meetings happen between discovery and negotiation (assets, activations, data).
- **BAMFAM — Book A Meeting From A Meeting** (recurring; signature). Schedule the next meeting before you part / before ending the call. "Oh you want me to send a proposal? Absolutely — how does Wednesday at 2 look?" If they resist booking the follow-up (or resist shaping the proposal or connecting you to decision-makers), you have **"happy ears"** (a hidden objection). Without a booked next step, momentum dies "like a slippery fish" and power shifts to them.
- **Don't try to close in meeting 1 or 2** — pushing alienates the prospect and kills the deal.
- **Actively try to hear "no."** Reinterpret everything other than "where do I put my credit card" as a no. A firm "no thank you" is worth as much as a firm yes; everything in between wastes time. **No-response is worse than "no"** — "the kiss of death" (you follow up forever and risk spam blacklists).
- **Actively invite objections** in the meeting: "Why might this not work? What concerns do you have? What will your decision-maker say? If in 6 months it didn't work out, what would have gone wrong?" Objections surface either in the meeting (where you handle them) or after (where they look like ghosting).
- **Distinguish a complaint from an objection.** "That's expensive," "that's a lot of money," "I'm too busy" are **complaints**, not objections. "In sales we don't respond to complaints, we respond to objections." Convert: ask *"What do you mean by that?"* "You cannot overcome an objection you believe — stop believing it."
- **Don't believe everything the sponsor says ("happy ears").** "Buyers are liars / prospects are liars." Decode brush-offs (see §16). Sponsors avoid conflict; take polite platitudes at face value as "no."
- **Tiers/options ("door #1 / #2 / #3") are acceptable ONLY after discovery,** presented live, anchored to the problem they told you to solve: #1 solves it at their budget; #2 = "twice as much for only 60% more"; #3 = "sign for 3 years, get 50% off."
- **The "do nothing" close** (million-dollar case): when asked "what would you do with a $1M cheque?" → "Nothing. We'd lock it in the safe, call you, and book a meeting. Until we know what you want to accomplish, what your customers value, what your brand stands for, we don't have the right to spend a penny. If we can't do what you need, we'll give the cheque back." Awkward silence is a powerful tool ("Nothing at all is your most important tool").
- **The "is it fair?" close (the "F word"):** "It's really hard for people to say something is not fair… unless they're being unfair." If your terms are fair, "fair enough" gets a yes; if they're lying, the real objection surfaces.
- **No means no — walk away.** A literal "no" *is* final (stop then); but a brush-off, "not right now," or "no budget" is not.
- **Contract ≠ proposal.** Always have a lawyer-reviewed contract; it triggers the invoice. Only sign deliverables you can fulfill.

## 12. Fulfillment & Reporting

- **The fulfillment report is "the most valuable tool every sponsorship property has"** (recurring) — the proof you delivered, and the on-ramp to renewal. "Saved me more times than I can count." Always provide one regardless of sponsor size ($500 or $1M).
- **Track EVERYTHING you promised, live during the event:** email open rate, click-through, downloads, coupon use, social engagement, attendance, web visitors and which pages, booth visits, activity participation.
- **Report contents (~6 pages incl. title):** event overview; **two-column chart of "everything promised + its value" vs. "everything delivered + its actual value"** (values from your valuation, to prove ROI/over-delivery); a **photo of every single thing you promised** (logos, program ads, samples lined up on a table, the crowd, esp. the sponsor's activation); screenshots of web traffic + logo placements + earned/purchased social media; attendance + other metrics; ROI calc; examples of what your team did; a thank-you page. Be honest about misses — "you can't fudge it."
- **Itemization wins:** show "$20,000 worth of value for $10,000 in an itemized list." If you promised "50 branded flyers" and delivered 125, report the over-delivery.
- **Timing (varies by source):** within **24–72 hrs (ideally 48 hrs)** of the event (blog); within **~3 days, no more than 1 week** (blog); within **~2 weeks** (video). The principle: fast, before the sponsor forgets details and reallocates next period's budget.
- **Process:** finish report → contact sponsor to set a meeting → send report → give a few days to digest → meet → ask candid feedback on delivery → segue to renewal/referrals.
- **Effort reality:** you'll put in **10×–100× more work AFTER** the cash (activation, delivery, ROI proof, fulfillment) than to win it; getting the commitment ≈ **20%** of total work, activation/fulfillment ≈ **80%**.
- **Make-it-right principle** (horror-story lessons): (1) something will eventually go wrong, and that's OK; (2) do everything to make it right; (3) always have an activation strategy; (4) always deliver a fulfillment report; (5) handle delivery personally or delegate to YOUR team (never trust the sponsor's rep or a third party — see Dave & Sally, §19).

## 13. Renewals & Upgrades

- **Renewal is the cheapest revenue** (recurring). "Shiny-object syndrome is lethal." A fulfillment-report meeting lets you open a fiscal year with **60–80%** of revenue already booked. Renewals are usually worth MORE money.
- **Renegotiate immediately after the event** while it's fresh; renew/upgrade "on the spot," multi-year if possible.
- **Re-run discovery and re-value EVERYTHING every year** (new and reused assets) — never resell identical assets. Asset prices should RISE (you're better at the service + inflation), never fall. Even +$200/asset × 10 assets = +$1,000. Don't re-list assets you under-delivered last year.
- **Express interest in extending EARLY,** before the sponsor becomes a "past sponsor" and reallocates budget — once their budget is gone, even a willing sponsor can't help.
- **Implement sponsor feedback** → builds a track record that justifies longer deals and higher fees.
- **Always ask for a referral** before ending the fulfillment meeting: "Who else should I be talking to? Who in your network would enjoy this approach and want to reach our audience?"
- **Stay in touch post-deal** (emails, social messages, forward useful articles) so you're the first call next time.
- **Don't let sponsors go after one deal** — leaves "tens of thousands, sometimes millions" on the table.
- **Podcasts: report quarterly/monthly,** not annually — keeps the sponsor reassured and reports readable (Dave Stewart nearly lost a sponsor who'd declined renewal; after a report showing 2× value/episode, they re-signed). **For title/naming deals,** send fulfillment reports at least yearly (Chris prefers quarterly or every 6 months) — never wait until the end of a multi-year deal.

## 14. Scaling, Team & Operations

- **Don't make sponsorship a solo gig / part-time job.** "Sponsorship is not a part-time job"; you can't reap the whole reward for partial effort. Even one helper + daily review beats going alone. (Joan the CEO raised **$0 over 2 years** insisting on doing it solo; see §19.)
- **Don't cut steps to save time** — produces an incomplete program; you'll fumble the sale. The fix isn't "what can I skip?" but "what can I remove from my schedule?" — and if nothing, delegate.
- **Set small daily goals.** 10 emails/day × 5 days beats one postponed 50-email day. "Momentum > perfection." Sit down every single day and move prospects through the moves-management cycle.
- **Effort scales non-linearly.** "90% effort does NOT equal 90% outcome" — put in 70–90% of the effort for $1M and you get **$0**, not $900K. ~1,000 hrs over 12 months for ~$500K becomes ~1,500 hrs if compressed to 6 months. 12 hrs/week ≠ 36 hrs/week results.
- **Set up a sales tracker with ≤7 stages.** Track activity volume per stage, not revenue. Run rate (booked contracts) matters more than cash flow.
- **Systematize the systematizable, protect the human work** (Warren): automate email templates + calendars ("send the template on the day the calendar says — 10 minutes"); the irreplaceable part is "getting on the phone and connecting with humans" and tailoring your business case.
- **Build a culture of sponsorship internally:** buy coffee + cheap carbs to get colleagues to meetings; invite them to discovery + fulfillment meetings; learn their expertise; host an internal Sponsor Summit (reps hear directly from sponsors); treat colleagues like sponsors (uncover goals, listen more than you speak); ideate + value as a team but **limit valuation participants** ("too many cooks"); find a protégé so the culture survives your departure.
- **Get the board on board:** boards disengage from lack of trust (fear their contacts will be mistreated) — build trust, do what you say, reconnect them to the cause every meeting (first agenda item), thank repeatedly, prioritize fundraising (don't leave it the last 10 min). Give boards a strong **Case for Support** (the language), explain sponsorship ≠ philanthropy, coach them (best role = introductions, not cold-calling), and do the legwork (scripts, drafts, research, scheduling). Board's job is to open doors and have a conversation — NOT to carry a proposal or make offers.

### Brokerage / outsourcing (verdict: avoid full-cycle brokerage)
- **Broker** (sells your inventory for commission) vs. **Consultant** (builds systems/teaches) vs. **Contractor** (executes within your process). "Nobody cares more about your sales, brand and sponsors than you."
- "The ask is **~5%** of the process" yet brokers take **~25%** of value. Commission-only rewards speed/undervaluation, against your interest (the $36/hr vs. $300/hr math, §15).
- **Red flags:** ghosted social presence, no documented process, no case studies/references, promises without discovery, asks for an upfront retainer, or they contact YOU (good brokers have waitlists and are selective). The "magic Rolodex" is a myth — brokers get contacts from their LAST client and will sell YOUR sponsors to YOUR competitors.
- **Charities:** AFP discourages commission-based fundraising; CRA (Canada) flags results-based comp as risking charitable status; US nonprofits avoid % fundraising over IRS private-benefit scrutiny (perception blurs "marketing vs. fundraising").
- **Narrow legit uses:** short-term/seasonal campaign with an immovable deadline; a large brand that already has strategy/valuation/assets and only needs outbound; temporary overflow; purely transactional logo-only deals; a mature property with CRM/pricing/fulfillment already built. Safest model: defined scope, **flat fees, NO ownership of sponsor relationships, full transparency.** ~$50k goal = avoid any broker willing to take you; ~$500k could fund a full-time in-house salesperson with money to spare.

## 15. Funnel Math & Benchmarks (ALL numbers/metrics/$ figures/rates/timeframes/percentages)

### Outreach / cold-contact rates
- Outreach email: **3 sentences max** (recurring).
- Cold email response rate: **~8.5%** (2023, Klenty) — "8 responses per 100 emails."
- Cold call success rate: **~3–10%** (3% the more common figure).
- Cold email reply timing: 19% reply within 1 hr; 77% within 12 hrs (Cience 2021).
- "The ask" = **~5%** of the total sales process (so a broker handles ~5–10%, you ~90–95%).

### Funnel math (two worked examples)
- **$250k/year program** (Direct Outreach): **200 contacts → 45 discovery meetings → 15 proposals → 5 sponsors @ $50,000 = $250,000.** 200 contacts / 50 weeks = **4 direct contacts/week** (real, specific, qualified, named, real meeting ask). Most teams make ZERO/week. A real week ≈ 4 hrs of work = ~18 touches incl. follow-ups, 1 new discovery, 1 bad contact removed, 2 in dialogue. Self-test: 0 qualified contacts = pipeline didn't move; 1–3 = started and stopped; 5+ = a real week regardless of replies. "Reply rate is conversation; meeting rate is pipeline."
- **$350k program** (Moves Management): $350K ÷ $10K avg = **35 sponsors**; 50% conversion per stage → **70 proposals ← 140 discovery meetings ← 280 cold outreaches**, over 12 weeks → per week: **6 proposals (~1+/day), 12 discovery (~2/day), 24 cold calls (~5/day).**
- If you don't know your real conversion rates, guess, start now, learn them over time.

### Close-rate / program-health benchmarks
- **Proposal close rate target: ≥60%** ("If your proposals aren't getting at least a 60% close rate, you're doing something wrong — usually not customizing").
- **GSB litmus test (healthy program, verbatim):** 30–60% close rate, increasing sponsorship dollars every year, retains 60% of last year's sponsors. If you're not hitting these, "it isn't working."
- **GSB reality:** "What about the 97% of your sponsors who threw your GSB package in the garbage?" (~3% engagement with cold tiered packages).
- **Direct-cold-proposal success:** over Chris's career + tens of millions sold, closing purely off a cold/unsolicited proposal "happened a dozen times" — everything else followed the research→discovery→meetings→proposal pattern.

### Audience data benchmarks
- **25+ data points** on the macro audience PLUS **3 avatars/personas** (each with ~25 points) — the single most-repeated benchmark (≥10 videos). Older standard: **30+** points. In a 10-page proposal, **≥5 pages (50%) must be audience data.**
- Data is "current" only if collected within the last **3–6 months**.
- **Survey-size math:** send 5,000 surveys → only **256 replies** needed for meaningful data. Chris's method (Pro SurveyMonkey ~$350/yr, 2 e-blasts, 3×$25 gift-card incentive) → **30+ data points at 95% confidence / 5% margin of error.** "If we survey 50,000 people we might get 2 complaints." Give ~1 month + a few reminders; resurvey every 6 months.

### Pricing / valuation benchmarks
- CPM: **$5–$10/1,000 impressions.** CPC: **$2–$10/click.** CPS (LinkedIn InMail): **$0.80–$1/send.** CPL: **$50–$2,000+ per lead.**
- Email-from-social conversion: **~25%** of click-throughs give up their email (to value email-capture assets).
- Brand-value uplift: **10–30% per factor**; total can ~double revenue. **Don't exceed 3× hard value** (7× exists but "not normal").
- Renewal: +$200/asset × 10 = +$1,000.
- Activation over-budget tolerance: **5–10%** of package value (eat it; beyond that, sponsor pays).
- Commitment ≈ **20%** of total work; activation/fulfillment ≈ **80%.** Post-cash work = **10×–100×** the work to win it.
- Broker commission: avg **~25%** (range 20–35%) + expenses. 25% of $100K = **$25K** removed immediately. **Broker incentive math:** Scenario 1 — 700 hrs to sell at $100K (true value) → $25K = ~$36/hr; Scenario 2 — undervalue, 25 hrs, sell at $30K → $7.5K = ~$300/hr.
- Cautionary: **Linda** paid a "connector" **$25,000**, lost it + **8 months**, business went under.
- Podcast: a 60-sec ad ≈ **$25 CPM**; network ad-revenue share commonly **70/30 (30% commission)** or per-download; advertising networks take **30%**.
- Macro-influencer prices: TikTok post **$1,260–$2,500**; Instagram post **~$10,000**; YouTube video **~$20,000.** Macro avg reach **~61%/post**; engagement **≥3%.** Macro = **100k+** followers (micro = 10k–100k).

### Timing benchmarks
- **Best window to prospect/budget: ~Sept 15 – Dec 15** (companies set next-year budgets Sept–mid-Dec; second half of December is "a wash"; January too late). In that window, every discovery call is worth **~5×** any other time, and you'll often get a "loose agreement" now with cash in January. (Also stated: best time to submit a proposal = **Oct–Dec / Fall**, or whenever the sponsor says.)
- **Surgical timing (Sabourin):** engage **≥3 months before the SPONSOR's fiscal year begins** (nonprofits often Jul 1–Jun 30; retailers Feb 1–Jan 31; check public companies' reports). Missed it? Companies often have **discretionary marketing funds year-round.**
- **Time to land a sponsor:** Good = **60 days** (OK for ~$10K–$75K established property; leaves money on the table). Better = **4–6 months** (~$100K–$200K, presenting-level). Best = **12 months** (full year; full price, ignores budget pressure). Worst = **≤30 days / 2–5 weeks** (from scratch = aggressive discounting, high stress). "60 days before you need it, sponsorship is your full-time job times two." You can't pull off quality event sponsorship in **3–4 months.** Treat sponsorship as a **3–5 year commitment.**
- **Type vs. timeline:** exhibit space / sampling / delegate-bag inserts → sellable up to day-of. **5-figure ($10K+) incl. presenting → 4–6 months.** **Naming/title / multi-year → 12–18 months** (+ legal/compliance + multi-year measurement).
- **Payment terms:** big-company contracts go to legal; expect **net 30** if lucky, up to **net 120.**
- **Title/naming term:** **3–5 years minimum, 10+ better** (one client even renamed the street their building was on → 10+ years).
- **"$50K+ total" → formal valuation is mandatory.**
- **New Year strategy:** every week, move **10/20/30** prospects one stage forward.
- **Audience survey alone** can take ~2 weeks.
- **Renewal leverage:** a fulfillment report → book **60–80%** of next year's revenue before the fiscal year opens.
- **"Record" close times (exceptions that prove the rule):** Chris closed a sponsor **24 hrs before** his conference — but only **~4%** of total revenue; Kevin closed one **at registration** (0 sec) — **<0.5%** of total.

## 16. Scripts & Templates (verbatim)

### Warm-intro outreach email (verbatim)
> Hi there, [name],
> It's [your name]. I'm a colleague of [your connection's name], who mentioned you when I brought up my latest opportunity.
> My event, [name], is hosting its annual [event name] soon, and I'd love to chat with you to see if we have any common ground between our plans and goals for the year.
> Are you available on Thursday at 2:45 for a quick chat? Please let me know.
> Thanks, and have a great day,
> [Your name]

### Cold email for podcast sponsorship (structure + verbatim models)
Order: **Subject → Introduction → Mention mutual connection → Flattery → Meeting date/time → Sign-off.** Length: 50 words can suffice; **max ~125 words.**
- Subject: "Hi Sam, do you have time for a quick meeting this week?" / "Can I pick your brain about something, Sam?"
- Intro: "Hi, Sam. My name is Adam, and I've been hosting the Living Your Best Life podcast for the past three years. The show is about strategies for finding your bliss and maximizing your potential."

### Objection-handling scripts (decode + flip)
- **"Just send me a proposal"** (= usually a brush-off / "no thanks"): "I actually don't have a proposal — we don't have a stock package. Frankly I'm not even sure what I'd propose yet because we haven't had a chance to talk. So why don't we jump on a quick call, you tell me what you're trying to achieve, and I'll create a proposal based on that." If pushed: "Absolutely — what would you like me to include? Tell me what you typically look for when you open a sponsorship package." (People who won't give 20 min to discuss won't read a 30-page proposal from a stranger.)
- **"Budget is already spent"** (the "Cole Gordon response" — "No problem"): "No problem, I totally get that. Let me ask — when do you typically assign your budget? When does your budget cycle renew?" (If they don't know → not a decision-maker.) Then the "F word — fair": "Cash flow is finance's problem. If we want to work together we'll just invoice you whenever you need — even after the event — so you still get all the benefits. Sound fair?" → "How can we make this happen knowing your budget cycle is out of sync?" Get a deposit: "What's fair — 10%? We'll do 10% up front." (Decode: if it's January and the year's budget is "gone," they're lying / too financially irresponsible to work with.)
- **"We don't do sponsorship"** (yet they have past sponsors = they don't do YOUR version): "Yeah, neither do we. The gold-silver-bronze logo-soup thing? What a waste of everyone's time. What we do is help brands connect to their audience in meaningful ways — call it whatever. So let's drop the word 'sponsorship' — tell me what your marketing/sales goals are." (Renaming it "partnership" won't help; customizing assets will.)
- **"We just want a booth / signage / samples"**: "Can I ask an honest question — why do you want a booth? What are you hoping to accomplish? How will you measure success? I frankly don't think a booth is the best way. What if we set up a situation where you're talking to people *with your product in their hands*, getting them to sample it while you're there, with an incentive like winning something cool — do you think that'd get more samples consumed?" ("It's what you do with it and the outcomes.")
- **"We prefer in-kind only / free product"**: "Oh, why?" *(then stop talking).* "It sounds like you want people to try your product — why is that valuable? In my experience the best way is for *both* of us to put effort into getting product into the right hands, and staff time has hard costs. So what's a reasonable budget to make sure we get the outcome you're after?" (Reframe: in-kind = sampling; "sampling is expensive — you pay me per sample.")
- **"Let me think about it"** (hardest to overcome): "Absolutely — but what *specifically* do you want to think about? Let's discuss it right now. Between now and 24 hours from now, what will happen that tells you you've thought enough? Score 1 to 10 — where are you? 8? Great, what would it take to get to a 10? All the info comes from me right now — I don't have a package to send — so let's just have the conversation now."
- **"Marketing isn't part of our local operations / it's all done at HQ"** (often a lie): "Do you mean you have a zero-dollar marketing budget, or you go through an internal approval process? What's the process to get them involved? What can I give you to advocate internally? When can we jump on a call with them? Which departments have a budget — sales, marketing, HR?"
- **"I don't have time for a discovery call, just email me"**: "No problem — I'm happy to jump on a call while you multitask, like on your way to meetings. A 5-minute discovery call is the best way to save time so I don't write a 100-page proposal you have to read. I'm also happy to meet someone else on your team — just tell me who. What's a better time?" (If they name a slot → real; if "not sure" → brush-off.)
- **"We just want a booth/asset" reframe probe:** "It sounds like your goal is branding, that you want people to recognize your logo rather than take an action and engage your company. Am I on the right track?"

### "Stop getting ghosted" two-part tactic
1. **Ask three more questions per statement.** To "just send me a proposal": "Amazing — what would you like to see in the proposal? What budget range should I be thinking about? Who else do we need to talk to before I put something together?"
2. **BAMFAM** at the end of every meeting. "If they resist, you have happy ears. Everything other than 'where do I put my credit card' is a no."

## 17. Named Concepts & Glossary

- **BAMFAM** — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting." Set the next meeting before leaving the current one. (Adopted, not coined, by the Collective. Signature/recurring.)
- **Property** — any sponsorship opportunity / rights-holder (event, festival, podcast, athlete, team, conference, building, esport, nonprofit); also a *collection of related assets serving a specific audience in a specific way.* "Not a physical property." The seller is "the property."
- **Asset** — a single sellable unit (tweet, e-blast, logo, booth, speaking slot, signage, tickets, sampling, naming, VIP). Low value alone unless packaged under a property, valued, and activated. Logos/podium shout-outs = lowest value.
- **Inventory** — the full list of assets you'll sell. **Inventory development** = canvassing your event/venue to enumerate them.
- **Activation** — an experiential-marketing moment that produces a measurable sponsor outcome, living in the overlap of one audience need + one sponsor need. "What you sell to sponsors, not logos."
- **Output vs. Outcome** — output = the activation (a photo booth); outcome = the measurable result (signups/traffic/awareness lift). Sell outcomes.
- **Fulfillment report** (a.k.a. wrap-up / post-event report) — promised-vs-delivered chart + photo/screenshot proof + metrics + ROI; proves reliability, drives renewal. "The most valuable tool every property has."
- **Discovery session / meeting** — the first, exploratory meeting to uncover the sponsor's audience, goals, budget, challenges, and what they've tried; NOT a sale.
- **Valuation** — determining the market rate of assets ("how would my sponsor get this without me?"), then adjusting for quality/expertise.
- **Brand value (intangible value)** — the *extra* benefit a sponsor gets reaching your audience via your property vs. other media (NOT brand awareness; NOT audience size). Added as a % uplift to each asset.
- **Avatar / buyer persona / audience persona** — a niched, highly specific (named) audience segment; minimum 3, each ~25 data points; an amalgamation. Example: "40-year-old, high-net-worth moms, with 2-3 kids and a house in the suburbs"; "Financial Frank."
- **Niching down** — repeatedly segmenting audiences until you can't split further ("Russian nesting doll" / "sieve"). "The riches are in the niches."
- **Macro audience** — your entire audience (vs. the avatar-level breakdowns).
- **Vanity metrics** — shallow numbers ("1,000 attendees," follower counts) that are NOT real audience data.
- **Value ladder** — ordering of assets most→least profitable; the inverse of the (widening) sales funnel.
- **Sponsorship Marketing Funnel** — digital-marketing analogues (CPM/CPC/CPS/CPL) mapping low→high asset value.
- **CPM / CPC / CPS / CPL** — cost per 1,000 impressions / per click / per send / per lead. CPM = the podcast/low-value default (logo equivalent).
- **Concentric circle theory** — prospect-ranking model (4 circles, audience-out).
- **Moves management** — pipeline method tracking activity volume per stage rather than revenue.
- **In-kind / contra sponsorship** — free goods/services/media instead of cash; "a fancy word for sampling." Treat as a real sponsorship and as the *opening of the sales pipeline* (upgrade in-kind → in-kind + cash → mostly cash).
- **Pass-through benefits / rights** — your audience connects with one of your (or a media partner's) advertisers/partners.
- **Title / naming / presenting rights** — Title/naming = property *named after* the sponsor + **name-in-wordmark**; "presented by" alone = presenting rights, not title. Comes with exclusivity, most assets/activations, highest value, multi-year.
- **Name in wordmark** — embedding the sponsor's name into your property's brand name; "necessary but not sufficient" for title sponsorship.
- **Exclusivity** — category (no competing brands) vs. direct-competitor-only; must be defined + lawyer-vetted.
- **Media partner/sponsor** — a media company promotes you (you help them get readers/listeners/viewers); spikes brand value and all asset values; must be secured *before* cash sponsors. Two models: **organic** (unguaranteed, can't be monetized) vs. **cash-leveraged** (you pay them for a contract with **guaranteed minimum impressions** you can resell).
- **Business case** — a no-price outline (audience, activations, media footprint, case studies) sent when a sponsor demands something before agreeing to meet.
- **Happy ears** — hearing what you *want* to hear; taking polite brush-offs ("just send a proposal") as genuine interest. (Taught to Chris by "Mike Mark.")
- **Motion vs. Action** — "motion" = busywork that feels productive (perfecting tiers, harvesting email lists, filling forms, over-testing email headlines, months of prospect research) but doesn't advance the deal; "action" = sending the survey, booking discovery.
- **ROE** — Return on Effort (paired with ROI).
- **Run rate** — booked contracts/deals (what Chris cares about over cash flow).
- **Hybrid proposal** — standardized parts (audience data) + customized parts (assets/activations after discovery).
- **CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility)** — how an org manages social/economic/ethical/environmental concerns; often a *different* budget/decision-maker (Community Engagement Manager) than the Brand Manager. 9 CSR types: green practices, ethical marketing (watch "greenwashing"), HR/diversity, volunteering, corporate philanthropy, socially responsible practices, cause-related marketing, addressing social issues, supply chain.
- **Cause marketing / cause-related marketing** — a for-profit + nonprofit partnership exchanging marketing assets for money/services ("buy product X, $Y to charity"; POS pinups; social campaigns). NOT the same as sponsorship.
- **Cause sponsorship** — charities/nonprofits/associations (incl. hospitals, education) doing sponsorship.
- **Qualified sponsorship payment** (IRS IRC §513(i)) — payment with no expectation of substantial return benefit beyond acknowledgement of name/logo/product lines; NOT advertising; likely deductible.
- **UBIT** — Unrelated Business Income Tax (owed on advertising-like sponsorship activities).
- **7×7 method** — structured 7-touch cadence to warm a lead before the call.
- **PP / "Persistent but Polite" / "Pleasant but Persistent" / "courting"** — long-game relationship persistence (every couple of weeks; across years).
- **The "F word" (fair)** and **"the Cole Gordon response" ("No problem")** — named objection-handling moves.
- **"The prize" / "You are the prize"** — mindset that *you* hold the power because you own the audience.
- **Special sauce** — your unique differentiator (per one chunk, your activation opportunities).
- **Category-timing windfall** — a brief inbound lift tied to a cultural moment/budget shift; mistaken for a working program; vanishes when the moment passes.
- **Macro-influencer** — 100k+ followers, engagement ≥3%, treated like a celebrity.
- **Sponsor types** (motorsport framing): cash/financial, promotional (a company/celebrity that promotes you), media, in-kind/contra.
- **Networking frameworks:** 3 keys = process, people, purpose; 4 Cs = connectivity, clarity, credibility, competence.
- **Gnothi Seauton / "Know Thyself"** — invoked for cause sponsorship: know your audience, what they value, why they support you, how they decide.

## 18. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

- **Gold/Silver/Bronze (GSB) tiered packages** — the #1 recurring anti-pattern. "The mark of an amateur"; a 40–50-year-old tactic sponsors have seen "a million times" and some refuse to read. Forces sponsors to buy junk to reach good stuff (deceptive — holds high-value assets hostage); signals "all or nothing"; shows your hand; no room for customization. **Renaming tiers (Platinum/Ruby/Diamond/Adamantium; dinner/lunch/breakfast) changes nothing — the *existence* of levels is the problem.** (A hybrid model with real post-discovery customization is acceptable; "door #1/#2/#3" options *after* discovery are fine.)
- **Mispricing assets** — "undoubtedly the biggest and most egregious error." Guessing, copying competitors, padding zeros, or pricing off your costs all get exposed. Overpricing repels (AT&T/Cowboys stadium sat unnamed for years partly due to price); underpricing leaves money on the table and makes you look low-value ("More is not better; better is better"; undervaluing → more sponsors → more obligations).
- **Skipping / guessing valuation** — like pawning jewelry without an appraisal; sponsors can verify market value and will ask your process.
- **Not valuing ALL assets / not re-valuing on renewal** — you may miss the highest-priced ones; reselling identical assets destroys goodwill.
- **Creating assets before finding a sponsor** — backward; assets end up generic/low-value.
- **Sending the proposal too early / cold / shotgun-blasting** — buried/trashed/blocked; "We get 10,000 packages a month; nobody on my team has read a proposal in years." Lets prospects self-select (leaves money on the table). The proposal "does not make the sale."
- **Bringing a proposal/one-pager to discovery / treating discovery as a sales meeting** — you'll have guessed wrong; walk out empty-handed; signals you're there for you.
- **Trying to close in meeting 1 or 2** — alienates and kills the deal.
- **Asking for / talking about money too soon** — premature (you don't know your value); festivals: sponsor stops engaging.
- **Filling out website contact forms** — efficient-feeling busywork; almost never yields a response.
- **Over-relying on logos / podium shout-outs / CPM ad spots** — awareness only; can't do lead gen/sales/conversion ("a sign doesn't do lead gen"). "Brand awareness is not enough anymore… and likely never was."
- **No / vague audience data** — "kills sponsorship programs dead"; targeting "the general public" → form-letter "no."
- **Vanity metrics as audience data** — "1,000 attendees," follower counts, "60% female." Endemic-only thinking (a 5K → only Nike/Brooks/Adidas) ignores that attendees buy cars, watch TV, pay taxes. "People are more than just one thing."
- **Pursuing only big/Fortune-500/famous brands** — inundated (thousands of requests/day), selective, ignore unsolicited asks; ~99% don't work out. (A sponsor friend's company receives **10,000 unsolicited proposals/month**, all trashed unread; they have no sponsorship email/form and don't want to be found.)
- **Prospecting by sponsor budget/$/fame instead of audience fit** — audience won't engage → missed deliverables → breach risk. "Sponsors don't sponsor properties, they sponsor audiences."
- **Mass-emailing / e-blasting packages ("dialing for dollars," spamming)** — "very efficient but not effective"; 100% output, 0% outcome = "better off not doing it at all." Write tailored emails one by one.
- **Cause-first proposals / "case for support"** — "Put your cause in the trunk." Ask "why would my sponsor care?" Lead with audience. A proposal is a marketing document, not mission/vision pages.
- **Treating sponsors like ATMs/donors** — they pay for a marketing service. "Speak to the sponsor like a person, not a cash machine."
- **Charities seeking MISSION alignment** — won't exist ("if they shared your mission they'd be a nonprofit — your competitor"); seek **shared audience** instead.
- **Over-branding the event** — too many ads become white noise; the audience cares about branding far less than you do.
- **Assuming a "good cause," "similar company sponsored," or "household-name festival" guarantees a yes** — none do. "If a brand sponsors a property like mine, they'll sponsor me" is false.
- **Assuming sponsorship lasts forever / is multi-year by default** — must ask; nothing is guaranteed.
- **Letting the deal coast once funded** — you owe deliverables; missing them = breach (lawsuits, ruined reputation). Being nice only until the cheque, then ghosting — "Sponsors aren't ATMs."
- **Under-promise / over-deliver (or over-promise / under-deliver) as strategy** — both bad; "overachievers need not apply." Sponsors paid for X and want exactly X; novelty becomes expectation. Promise what you can reasonably do, then deliver it.
- **Promising what you can't deliver** — don't make undeliverable terms legally binding; use a clause for genuinely out-of-control risks (weather).
- **Giving NEW sponsors preferential (custom) treatment while leaving legacy sponsors on stock GSB** — double work, devolving program; loyal sponsors find out and walk (the "cheese & crackers vs. four-course meal" betrayal). Bring them along via feedback meetings; ask every existing sponsor annually what they want.
- **Building too much rapport / becoming besties** — be a friendly champion, not BFF; don't drift into family/pet talk or too many lunches; your contact often isn't the final decision-maker, so chumminess doesn't seal it; you have a deadline.
- **Starting too late / treating it as easy or a solo/part-time job** — multi-stakeholder approval chains drag; a week or two isn't enough. (Joan: $0 over 2 years.)
- **Choosing activations for "cool"/expense only** — must be ultra-effective (sponsor + audience needs).
- **Believing the sponsor blindly ("happy ears") / chasing a brush-off / believing a ghosted meeting "went well"** — "If the meeting went well, they wouldn't be ghosting you."
- **No-response treated as alive** — worse than "no"; "the kiss of death."
- **Value mismatch with audience** (Coca-Cola at an anti-soda/health-conscious audience; meat brand at a vegan audience) — reflects badly on YOU, erodes audience trust. (Alignment with audience values matters, not whether the brand is "inherently" controversial.)
- **Believing "all I need is the right contact"** — getting in front of the right person at the wrong time / unprepared *burns* the relationship (worse than no meeting). Decision-makers are hard to convince.
- **Believing "my opportunity is different"** — internal focus; "Toyota's goals don't change based on your property." Not knowing audience/value/activations/ROI "doesn't mean you're different, it means your skill set needs an upgrade."
- **Believing in a sponsorship marketplace/app or "magic Rolodex" connector** — doesn't exist; sponsors are hard to find because they want to be; shortcuts = scams; "the most expensive form of avoidance."
- **Hiring a full-cycle broker** — high commission, asset devaluation, short-term focus, you don't learn, they may sell your sponsors to competitors, evergreen fees, reputational/ethical risk (esp. charities).
- **Letting the board carry a proposal / make offers** — keep process control; board opens doors only.
- **Skipping steps to save time** — incomplete program; you fumble the sale.
- **Efficiency over effectiveness** ("motion vs. action") — perfecting tiers, harvesting lists, filling forms feels productive but doesn't move the deal.
- **Following the leader (transference, per HBR)** — everyone in the room is doing it wrong; do the opposite.
- **Sending organic-media-only as a monetizable asset** — no guaranteed placement, can't control the story, advertisers can veto your sponsor's mention. Never sign a media-partner contract without a **guaranteed minimum impressions** clause.
- **Leaving activation/fulfillment to chance or to people not on your team** — see Dave & Sally; sponsor's sign hidden behind a door; keynote trashing the title sponsor's own product.
- **Saving sponsorship for last / assuming it's easy** — "I go get sponsors before I even book a venue… if there are no sponsors there's no event."
- **Proposal mistakes (list):** skipping prospect research; targeting wrong sponsors; not communicating value (beyond brand promotion → sales/leads/engagement); unprofessional attitude (typos, lateness); ignoring legal/ethical issues; failing to nurture; no follow-up step; one-size-fits-all tiers; not tracking success; underselling your event.
- **Cause-marketing pitfalls:** misaligned partnerships (women's shelter ≠ beer co.; human-rights org ≠ child-labor co.) → PR backfire; under-budgeting assets; assuming "big shop only"; selling products yourself (walk away); going to your database too many times (natural cap on # of campaigns); over-engineering a campaign with too many steps to trigger the gift.
- **Conference over-expectations:** renting a venue for ideals vs. projected attendance; assuming a sponsor = automatic promotion (the news spike is short-lived — keep promoting); assuming the sponsor plans/funds everything; expecting only cash.

## 19. Case Studies

- **Warren / Black Restaurant Weeks** (food festivals; +Latin Restaurant Weeks, Feed the Soul Foundation): finance background, laid off 2009; founded Houston Black Restaurant Week 2016 so *restaurants* benefit. 15 markets, 50–75 restaurants each, **50–75K diners/market** (started at 25 restaurants; now incl. Expected ~10% (~300); got **3 ballots = ~$8,000/ballot** vs. a $3K starting package. A "Dave" called to invite him to *pitch* (not buy) and revealed: "We haven't read a sponsorship package in 10 years; we get **10,000 unsolicited packages a month (~500/business day)**; a department/online form just says 'no thank you.'" - **$200,000 education-event deal** (Case Study 1): delivered to **3,000 pros via webinar** vs. 200 live; "majority named our sponsor" in a survey. (But the same case warns: the sponsor's sign was hidden behind a door, the VIP table behind a pillar, the host never mentioned them — always run activation personally/with your team.)
- **Sponsorship Levels Gone Wrong** (Case Study 2): a keynote trashed the title sponsor's own product for 10 minutes, not knowing they owned it → the multi-year sponsor pulled out permanently. Lesson: prep speakers/MCs about sponsors.
- **Hospital naming-rights dedication** (Case Study 5): trusting the partner with logistics → wrong machine / competitor's logo at the dedication, with media + CEO + a board member present.
- **Cause-marketing pinup campaigns** (A Tale of Two): two identical national pinup campaigns, same charity, two retailers — **Company X (organic/optional, 3× foot traffic) raised ~$300,000; Company Y (top-down, CEO-to-cashier accountability) raised $1.2M** — a **$1M difference** from approach alone.
- **Small-shop cause-marketing wins:** $14 item, $1 to charity, needed 10,000 units for $10K — by adding low-level sponsorship on product/displays, **raised $50,000 (5×).** A $1-per-Facebook-like campaign (max $2,500) got 5,000+ likes; the charity gained 1,000 likes → converted to attendees; 5 yrs later that company is a major supporter. A **selfie/upload/$5-donation** campaign **failed** (too many steps).
- **Podcast value leap (Dave Stewart):** ~**$150/episode** (CPM-based) → **$15,000** sponsorship after moving to lead-gen + live events/activations; doubled revenue 3 years running. (He runs discovery over 3–4 calls; nearly lost a sponsor who'd declined renewal until a report showed 2× value/episode → re-signed.)
- **Closing-speed (Anissa Cook, SynBioBeta):** used the prospect's exact words from discovery in the proposal + cover letter; prospect said "Oh, this is exactly what we wanted," skipped the sales call → discovery → proposal → contract in **2–3 weeks** vs. normal 30/60/90-day cycle.
- **Motorsport examples:** property valued **$35k → sponsor paid $45k**; property valued **$20k → in-kind sponsor gave $5k of gifts** (rest from others).
- **Ski-Hill Truck Test-Drive (Measuring ROI):** a client set up a test-drive atop a ski hill (truck through a frozen landscape); all attendees in-market for a vehicle within 12–18 months. Measure what's in your control (test drives delivered), not cars sold.
- **Chris & wife buying a car (Sponsorship & Car Sales parable):** every question (roof racks, ground clearance, dog space) got "if you buy this car I'll send my kids to college / go to Disney World." They left. Moral: don't sell by talking about the good *you'll* do with the money — be in asking/listening mode.
- **A simple audience-improving activation** can become a **$7,500 offer.**

### Bad-sponsorship case studies (sports, what NOT to do)
- **Lance Armstrong** (Nike, Budweiser, Trek, Oakley dropped post-2012 doping) — heed early controversy; institute drug testing.
- **McDonald's + Olympics** (fry-monopoly backlash) — choose healthy food brands; don't give one sponsor exclusivity that undercuts others.
- **SpongeBob/TMNT + NASCAR** — promoting a kids' show to a 58-yr-avg audience = audience-data failure.
- **Tiger Woods** (GM, Gatorade, AT&T, Accenture dropped; EA & Nike stayed) — you can't control athletes' personal lives; prep to lose sponsors.

## 20. Industry-Specific Playbooks

### Festivals / Music festivals / Events
- Customize assets/activations **per sponsor** (15 sponsors = 15 discovery sessions + 15 custom batches; if you can't, downsize sponsor count). Don't chase on festival fame; sell **audience + activations.** Don't talk money too early; don't assume multi-year. Ask the real number upfront (e.g., $20K), don't lowball; estimates can rise modestly but not 3× ($20K→$60K is a planning failure). Underpromise attendance conservatively ("8,500–10,000," not 30,000).
- Find sponsors via: renegotiate current → ask current for referrals → vendor/partner referrals → survey brands → concentric circles → competitors → **stay local** (smaller funding, may need to double/triple up). New festival: build projected data from ticket sales.
- **Proposal (Ultimate Festival Proposal):** title page = great photo + name + dates + location only ("the more you add, the more likely they delete it") → describe the opportunity (what's loved, not your founding year) → **≥50% audience data** (25+ macro + 3 avatars @25) → activation ideas (audience first) → vanity/attendance/media footprint → 3 distinct activation types + testimonials → CTA + one-person contact. **No grids, no pricing. Valuation mandatory if seeking $50K+.**
- **9 relationship tactics:** be friendly (not friends); take interest in their problems; stay in touch (≥weekly in first-half planning, ramp to daily near the event); keep them posted; ask opinions/help; implement feedback; say thanks (don't wait); deliver objectives (#1 — niceties never compensate for weak data/low-value offerings/poor delivery); provide value during lulls (partners, tools, reports, data). Communicate before AND after the event; don't go dark 9–10 months then ask for renewal money. Respect "small" sponsors (upgrade pipeline).
- **12 festival activations:** mini lounge (Heineken House/Coachella — kinetic dance floor, phone charging); video/projection (USA Network *Graceland*); bull riding (Boots & Hearts + Durango); henna tattoos; hydration station (Camelbak); portrait drawing (Vans "Off the Wall"); shower / "Human Car Wash" (Glaceau/Vitamin Water, RFID-tracked); fireworks; screenings (Adult Swim Meatwad tent, Bonnaroo); pop-up shops (Harper's Bazaar Coachella); contests (Toyota Prius Family Playground/Lollapalooza — hamster-wheel + phone-charging car); VIP areas (PlayStation lounge/Lollapalooza).
- **20 creative event ideas:** sponsored activities/workshops; event app (+gamification points→prizes); VR/AR; livestream (pre-roll); interactive polling/Q&A (leaderboards); lead-gen tools (real-time reports); targeted social ads/influencers; naming rights (branded lounges); themed décor/signage; branded transportation/kiosks; sustainable sponsorships; experiential giveaways (skydiving/backstage); cause-related; pre-event hype; sensory ("Blindfolded Bite"); themed food/beverage stations; custom scentscapes; skill-sharing workshops; sponsored Wi-Fi/charging (data capture); themed escape rooms.
- Four benefits of event sponsorship: cashflow; name-brand credibility; "think big by thinking small" (local in-kind venue/food/speakers); shared publicity. Partnership types: media, financial, in-kind, promotional (celebrity). Offer exclusivity (one area or whole event) to raise price + credibility.

### Food festivals (Warren)
- Two distinct B2B conversations: brands targeting **restaurateurs/vendors** (food processors, liquor, POS) vs. brands targeting **diners/consumers** — both monetizable. Find "real estate" on-site (posters, table tents) AND on your website (directory by zip/dietary filter) for continuous touchpoints; microactivations (samplings/tastings) > static logos. Beware windfall money with no ROI ask (dries up in feast/famine cycles). Think outside endemic brands (NASCAR for a restaurant week). Charging "$10K because food & beverage is expensive" gets dismissed — show ROI.
- **7 food activations:** food-truck giveaways (branded truck + ≤3 sample menu); mobile soda kitchens (Pepsi Nspire/SXSW — 1,000 drink configs, collect email/phone); customized candy studios (Kit Kat); branded photo booth/wall + sampling (donut/pretzel walls, gate photos behind email/phone); water slides (Lipton "Rise & Slide," outdoor only); portable versions of non-portable foods (Sonic square Instagram milkshakes — free delivery for an IG post); 3D-printed food (Oreo "Trending Vending"/SXSW — crowd-voted flavor, 2-min print).

### Sports / Motorsport
- Most lucrative area (receives the bulk of dollars); avg sports deal **$326k (2022).** Start small/local (community racing) → add naming rights as you grow. Diversify sponsors (one big = single point of failure). Leverage rich existing data (attendance, merch) + survey to fill gaps; extract **25 data points/group**; niche down.
- **Motorsport asset hierarchy (high→low):** naming rights to races/championships (immortalized in history books; Nextel/Sprint/Monster Cup) > branded logo on race vehicle (merch/video-game value) > logo on race uniforms > commercials (targeted airings) > logo on track/other signage (lost in the shuffle). Also: race naming rights, pre-race concerts (Heineken + Tiësto at F1 Dutch/Italian GP).
- Sponsors needn't be racing-related (NASCAR 50+ incl. AWS, Built protein bars, iRacing; F1 incl. Workday, Zoom, Rolex, Crypto.com). **Splitting one title sponsor into multiple "premier partners"** (NASCAR 2020: Coca-Cola, Xfinity, Geico, Busch) reduces each sponsor's burden. Tobacco used sponsorship as an ad loophole (Winston/NASCAR 1971–2003). "The sky is the limit" on prospects (avoid conflicts like alcohol where rules forbid).
- **4 sponsor types:** cash, promotional, media, in-kind (least favorite). **KPI menus by type:** cash (tickets, attendance, TV viewers, F&B, merch); promotional (email open/CTR, web traffic, list growth, NPS, CLV); media (followers/likes/comments/shares/mentions by platform).
- Title/naming deals often require switching *your* products (bank, official credit card, drinks served, corporate-travel airline) and create exclusivity/ambush-marketing risk (Shoe Co. A title sponsor vs. team preferring Shoe Co. B) — always involve a lawyer.
- 2026 motorsport sponsors increasingly fintech, EV/sustainability, AI/analytics, mobile payments, gaming/esports, cybersecurity, health/wellness ("Drive to Survive" effect; younger/female audiences; AR/VR pit-lane, trackside QR, fan-content challenges, predictive race games). Prep for traffic spikes (optimized site, opt-ins, lead magnets, email automation, clean social).

### Podcasts
- A sponsor is **NOT an advertiser** — name-drops/ad reads/CPM only weakly drive awareness ("you'll never make money unless you have ~100M downloads/month"). Your highest-value asset is your AUDIENCE, not a commercial (a radio/podcast ad alone often fails to even raise awareness — listener is driving, forgets).
- Higher-value plays: **host the sponsor as a guest**; run a **contest** with the sponsor's product as prize (promote for weeks; winner back on show). **Bundle:** only run the sponsor's ads on the episode you interview them; interviews/guest spots > standalone commercials.
- Select sponsors by **audience fit/values**, not name (Coca-Cola example). Audience data sponsors want: not "60% female" but "of the 25-yr-old women, how many are from Detroit, make $110k, work in pharma?" (25+ points + 3 avatars).
- **8 ways to find podcast sponsors:** ad networks (Acast, Art19, Midroll, Earwolf, Wondery, PodcastOne, Gimlet, Radiotopia — but you're 1 of thousands; 30%/per-download cuts + forced cross-promo) → network → competitors → local → past sponsors → social media → ask contacts → **audience data (best; save for last).**
- Deals are **short** (5 episodes/weeks); a **1-year contract is the sweet spot** (shortest Dave offers = 6 months). Discovery often takes **3–4 calls.** Podcasters have a discovery edge (trained interviewers). Have a **20–30 sec elevator pitch** (what the show is + who listens). **Report quarterly/monthly** (no event photos — pack with listenership + advertising data). Don't send a rate card/proposal/pricing first; don't be "too salesy" (long negotiation, not a hard sell); get everything in writing + a post-close confirmation meeting.

### Nonprofits / Charities / Associations
- Biggest trap: speaking "funds/donor" language instead of "marketing" ("like a conversation in Japanese when all you know is konnichiwa"). **Sponsorship is NOT philanthropy/major gifts/a case for support.** "The cause is *irrelevant* to sponsors" (a brand said so to Chris's face). The vast majority of global sponsorship goes to sport/motorsport.
- **Your audience = donors, board, volunteers, members, program users, attendees, investors, partners — NOT the people you serve.** Survey and niche them like a company.
- **Put the cause in the trunk;** lead with audience; ask "why would my sponsor care?" Don't seek MISSION alignment — seek **shared audience.**
- **Be explicit you're seeking SPONSORSHIP, not a donation** (contacts will redirect you to the donations department). Set clear value boundaries/policies; pursue corporations aligned with your *values*; don't fear "selling out."
- It is **NOT harder** to get a sponsor as a nonprofit — the only difficulty is over-focusing on the cause or assuming it lets you skip the process. **CEO/decision-maker at meetings = a small-shop superpower** (instant approvals; "shout across the office").
- **Three perceived barriers debunked:** (1) "No brand awareness" — good! Sponsors want YOUR specific audience, not the general public. (2) "Can't give away database access" — sponsors don't want to buy your list (privacy violation + bad practice); they want you to drive a measurable action among your stakeholders. (3) "We're too small" — nobody has advantages in sponsorship; sell size as an advantage.
- Sponsorship can be **totally unrestricted funding** (rare/gold — "a dollar unrestricted is worth $100 of restricted"). But it's harder than donations — lots of work *after* the cash. Obeys **marketing** rules, not fundraising rules; usually owned by fundraisers / the revenue specialist.
- **CSR-vocabulary mapping** (Nazareth): grant→investment, impact→fulfillment. CSR money often comes from a *different* budget/decision-maker (Community Engagement Manager vs. Brand Manager). Help corporations execute CSR — be "the conduit that brings corporations and their customers together." Map your values to a prospect's CSR; research their philanthropic philosophy.

### Cause Marketing (distinct from sponsorship)
- A for-profit + nonprofit partnership exchanging marketing assets for money/services. **3 pillars:** a plan/goal; strategic planning; engaging audience + sponsor to hit objectives.
- **Start with the prospect** — both winning case studies began by asking prospects what they valued (grab a coffee, no proposal), NOT by building a campaign and shopping it.
- Best practices: select a cause carefully (noble/feel-good helps; authenticity is core); team with relevant companies (start from audience); know your value (set min + max); know when to say no; mobilize your database (natural cap on # of campaigns); measure and report back. Pro bono is a legit asset source (designers/SEO/marketers for name+logo). Top-down, metrics-accountable execution dramatically outperforms organic/optional ($1.2M vs $300k). Blending cause marketing + sponsorship beats either alone.
- Strategy basics: revenue goal → research audience → view your cause through the partner's CSR lens → list assets + apply values → build a funnel (Cold prospects → Cold calls → Discovery → Partnership proposal → In/out) → consider ideal partners.

### Trade shows / Conferences / Galas / B2B
- A gala/conference needs only **3–4 sponsors** (don't burn out chasing 10). **Size the venue to PROJECTED attendance, not ideals** (estimate via last event's invited-vs-attended ratio, an interest questionnaire, or competitors' event sizes). A conference is "a giant business meeting" — too many attendees hinders its purpose.
- Sponsors offer more than cash: new customers, web/social growth, in-kind, and **introductions to other sponsors** ("worth its weight in gold"). Keep promoting even with a big-name sponsor (the announcement spike fades). Plan the event yourself with sponsor INPUT (they meet you halfway, they don't plan it).
- B2B sponsors want job titles, industries, **decision-making capacity/authority** of attendees/members. B2B sponsorship is growing (cloud/data/supply-chain/fintech/consulting) but runs the same process; confirm B2B goals in discovery (not all want brand-building).
- **New event, no data:** research the **interest audience**; base the business case on **how you'll guarantee a sellout** (the *theme* determines *who* comes; your job is proving *how many*). De-risk year 1 with a **discount + price-lock** ("half price, never increases if you renew"). "Sponsors are not investors" — they need a return, so spend year-1 energy/budget guaranteeing a full house.
- **Premium conference activation:** each of 10 sponsors gets the stage 5 minutes (no GSB) to "sell their city" → "activation arms race" → sell-out yearly. Free professional headshots booth (sponsor emails the headshot to capture contact info) drives exhibit-space revenue.
- **Conference menu chart (verbatim numbers):** Title $25,000 (stage signage, MC, address crowd, 10 tickets, 1 booth, full-page ad) / Mid-Level $15,000 (address crowd, 5 tickets, 1 booth, half-page) / Dinner $10,000 (address crowd, 2 tickets, half-page) / Wine $5,000 (2 tickets, quarter-page) / Other $3,000 (2 tickets, business-card ad). [Presented as illustrative; the doctrine is to title the "menu" by objective, not GSB.]

### Digital / virtual activations
- Cheaper, lower-risk, unlimited reach (no venue cap), easier to measure. **4 success steps:** understand sponsor goals; understand audience value (sell from a menu of segments/personas); set a budget; leverage social media. Types: branded social content (co-brand / sponsor-produced / "stealth"); contests (email capture / UGC); online communities (research, beta testing, influencer-finding); Buzzfeed-style quizzes. Metrics sponsors understand: landing-page visits, traffic sources, organic search, referrals to sponsor, social views, new vs returning, session time, bounce rate, impressions, engagement rate, email open/CTR. Real examples: Pottery Barn × Sherwin-Williams (matching paint line); Spotify × Starbucks; CoverGirl × Lucasfilm (Star Wars makeup).

### AR / Tech in sponsorship

### Tax (US/IRS; not legal/tax advice)
- **Nontaxable:** sponsor name/location/phone/logo on site & print; ONE static link to the sponsor's homepage; product display/free samples (no endorsement).
- **Taxable (UBIT):** sponsorship framed as advertising; acknowledgements in regularly scheduled publications/newsletters; payment contingent on attendance; preferential treatment (better tickets/reception); a link to a product/service page; listing pricing/phone/endorsements; exclusivity (assumed "substantial return benefit").
- UBIT: Form 990-T if >$1,000 gross unrelated-business income; volunteer-labor exemption can avoid it.

### Trends / global
- 2024 trends: sports↑; tobacco (better-for-you, e.g., Mission Winnow) possibly returning; alcohol↓; digital/VR/AR↑; sustainability↑ (practice what you preach); B2B↑; crypto (volatile but persisting); online gambling↑ (possible future bans); AI (for drafting/ideation only); VIP experiences↑.
- **Three macro disruptions:** (1) digital — brands can target your exact audience and pay only on action, so you must prove outcomes; (2) audience + activation are everything (activation is the one thing digital can't replicate); (3) knowing your value is essential (valuations now expected of all sizes).
- Global expansion via sponsorship: market research → market-entry strategy (sponsorship = the partnership piece) → expect more rejections, re-research/re-niche per culture, navigate language/time-zone/cultural barriers; don't treat sponsors as stepping stones.
