## 1. Frameworks & Processes (named, step-by-step)

### The 11 Stages of the Sponsorship Journey
(From "The 11 Stages of Sponsorship." This is the channel's master process.)
1. **Identify your properties** — A property is your sponsorship *opportunity* (event, naming rights, festival, athlete, sports team, jersey). NOT "give me money so I have money." A property must link a brand's goals to the asset.
2. **Define your audience** — minimum 25+ data points. "People who like what we do," "families," "middle class" are NOT audiences.
3. **Segment audience / build ≥3 avatars (buyer personas)** — get clear on macro audience + micro audiences.
4. **Build out individual discrete assets** — everything you can offer a sponsor (logo placement is one small piece; prioritize audience interaction, product consumption, email/contact capture).
5. **Get internal buy-in BEFORE going to market** — board, boss, marketing, comms. They can't tell you what to do but they *can* say no and kill a deal. "I've had a deal blown up because I didn't do the necessary work internally."
6. **Valuation** — the highly technical component. Know what your opportunity is worth and be able to defend it.
7. **Prospect research** — based on audience/assets/valuation, NOT on "who has money" or downloading the Fortune 500.
8. **Prospect outreach** — invite prospects to a Discovery session.
9. **Discovery meeting** — get to know their marketing goals.
10. **Build custom proposal** per sponsor → **another meeting to review proposal → close → sign agreement.**
11. **Fulfillment** — do what you said, then deliver a fulfillment report proving it.

### The Sponsorship Sales Funnel / Order of Operations
(Recurs across many videos — the canonical sequence.)
- Email/cold call → **Discovery** (1–3 meetings) → **custom proposal** → **negotiation** → **agreement** → **cash**.
- "Email to discovery, discovery to a proposal (a custom proposal), proposal to negotiation, negotiation to agreement, agreement to sale."
- The fatal error is short-circuiting: trying to go straight from email → sale (emailing the proposal/package to strangers).

### Conference Sponsorship — full step-by-step
(From "Conference Sponsorship.")
1. **Build your inventory** — a list of things you can offer based on sponsor interest. NEVER a gold/silver/bronze grid. ("If you have a grid labeled gold silver bronze… stop that immediately.")
2. **Know your audience** — define macro audience by subject/profession, then build ≥3 niche audiences/avatars. "The riches are in the niches." Brands want 25+ data points.
3. **Create activations** — cool experiences for each niche audience (food, networking, beverage tastings, workshops).
4. **Know your value** — proper valuation; understand what a brand would pay on the *free market* to acquire clients the same way without you. Most clients undervalue (charge too little).
5. **Sponsorship proposal = two documents**: (a) **Business case** sent BEFORE a meeting (no values, no grids, no call to purchase; 50% audience data + activation ideas + case studies + call to phone you); (b) **Proposal** sent AFTER the meeting (has dollars/cents).
6. **Get the meeting** via short email inviting a Discovery call (not by sending the proposal).
7. **Discovery meeting** — ask questions, don't pitch. Be an "investigative reporter": understand their audience, how they make money, products, how they measure ROI, current marketing.
8. **BAMFAM** at the end of Discovery — Book A Meeting From A Meeting.
9. **Deliver / over-deliver** on everything promised.
10. **Fulfillment report within days** → feedback → ask to submit draft proposal for next event → BAMFAM to review → close next year.

### Five Steps to Selling Sponsorship That Actually Work (webinar)
1. **Stop thinking of sponsorship as philanthropy.** "Your cause is irrelevant to your sponsor but highly relevant to your audience; your audience is highly relevant to your sponsor." Sponsorship = marketing spend / sales tactic.
2. **Know your value.** Every asset has a verifiable, independent market value. Ask: "What would my sponsor pay for this *without me*?" — i.e., the equivalent in media/marketing, not what other events charge.
3. **Always provide a fulfillment report.** Deliver within 2 weeks of event wrapping.
4. **Stop using the sponsorship package to sell.** The package gets a meeting; YOU make the sale. (50% rule; case studies not grids.)
5. **Know your audience.** 30+ data points: demographics, buying behavior, upcoming purchases, preferred brands, decision-making capacity, ability to influence a purchase.

### Bottleneck Diagnosis Exercise ("How To Find Your Sponsorship Bottleneck")
Score yourself across the pipeline left-to-right, then **fix the lowest, furthest-LEFT point first** (like water finding kinks):
1. **Audience data** — Do you have 30+ data points? Third-party/secondary research? Surveys?
2. **Activations** — gold/silver/bronze/logos (bad) vs. cool activations uniting audience + sponsor.
3. **Sales material** — standard packages for everyone = "a big zero." Is 50%+ focused on audience data? Avatars + case studies?
4. **Outreach** — sending 2000-word emails (bad)? Getting 15–20% replies cold?
5. **Discovery** — following a script/process, or going in to pitch?
6. **Sales/close** — closing 30–50% of prospects on each proposal? 50–75% renewing?
> Key insight: "A problem on the back end is a problem on the front end." Most people wrongly think they need better sales skills / buzzwords / to get past the gatekeeper. Real fix is almost always upstream (audience data first). "You are far better off being weak in the sales department but strong in the data/information department than the reverse."

### Path to Failure — 3 overlapping problem areas (Venn)
(From "The Path to Failure in Sponsorship.") Three circles: **Audience**, **Value**, **Skills (mindset + sales)**. Magic happens only where all three combine → 6/7-figure sponsorship pros. Having audience + value but no skills/mindset = broke. Having amazing sales skills + valuable offer but no audience data = broke. "It doesn't matter how good your opportunity is; if you have a mindset issue and don't have sales skills, you're going to be broke."

### Special Sauce / Activation-Finding Method ("Finding Your Special Sauce")
- Stakeholder 1 = your sponsor. **Stakeholder 2 = your audience (NOT you).** You as the property sit in the *middle* offering activations.
- Steps: (1) Pick 3 things you KNOW your sponsors want (because you asked — don't assume). (2) Pick 3 things your audience wants (fun, free stuff, networking, deeper connection to mission). (3) Brainstorm activation ideas where the two intersect. (4) List 3–5 activation ideas instead of gold/silver/bronze.
- Core trick: "How can we give our audience more free stuff AND satisfy a goal of our sponsor?" Don't just download/hand over your email list — coach the sponsor to do something so incredible the audience *wants* to give up their email.

### Value/Competition Spectrum of Assets ("Finding High Value Sponsorship Assets")
Value ladder (low→high): **Awareness → Conversion experience → Lead gen → Sale.**
- **Awareness (logo placement)** = worth pennies (¼ of one penny per view or less), highest competition (a brand can boost social/billboard cheaper).
- **Lead gen + conversion events** = where the magic/money is. Example: see Jeep logo → go on test drive (conversion event) → lead → sale.
- "The closer you get to the sales outcome, the more you can charge."
- Sweet spot = the **overlap of awareness-raising AND conversion** activities. Logo-only ≈ 7% of total sponsorship opportunity value. Every high 5/6/7-figure deal Chris did had "lots of logo placement AND lots of lead gen AND lots of conversion activities."
- **Low value assets:** branded clothing, logo on press release, logo on website, giving out marketing info, signage, a social media post, a sponsor mention.
- **High value assets:** broadcast rights, content on website, media partners, sampling rights, speaking opportunities.
- (Premium assets list withheld in video — gated behind "high value assets" comment.)

### AI-Powered Prospecting Process (from "Leveraging AI," Elena Saras)
Seven-ish steps, executable with AI tools:
1. **Advanced audience intelligence** — find clusters / deep psychographics (not just age/income).
2. **Competitive sponsor analysis** — find who sponsors similar properties (websites brag about sponsors → look at what *else* those sponsors sponsor).
3. **Strategic brand alignment** — upload your audience + brand, ask AI to find aligned brands based on shared values, prior sponsorships, media statements, spend.
4. **Opportunity scoring** — score brands 1–10 on weighted criteria (e.g. weight sustainability 2×); include a "viability of closing" factor and a "do we have the contact info" factor.
5. **Identify key decision makers** + get contact info.
6. **Executive media intelligence** — pull a prospect's last 7 LinkedIn posts, press releases, podcast appearances, quotes → use in subject line / first paragraph / discovery call.
7. Iterate (vibe-code custom CRM/pipeline tools if desired).

## 2. Tactics & Techniques

### Prospecting
- **Always be prospecting.** Never stop — even during recessions/economic downturns, 10x prospecting (expect 100x more NOs). Competitors who quit "have just taken themselves out of the field."
- Research **people with the right job titles**: sponsorship, marketing, sales, communications, brand, experiential marketing, product. Find the *right person*, not the right online form.
- **Never** email `hello@`, `customerservice@`, `info@`, or `sponsorship@`. Online forms are a sponsor's tool to avoid reading proposals.
- Base prospect research on **audience** (brands your audience buys — which you know from your survey), not on "who's big and has money."
- **Find a champion close to home first**, then expand. (Memphis-in-May / Home Depot: started with the *local store district manager*; "controlling the recruiting base close to home and then expanding out.") It's easy to meet a local Home Depot manager; impossible to get the Home Depot CMO on the phone.
- For motorsport/sports: build audience data on YOUR fans (niche), then approach all brands interested in that niche and have Discovery calls with each. Don't chase one mega-sponsor like a lottery ticket.

### Outreach (email + call cadence)
- **Email AND call** — not either/or. Send ~3 emails/LinkedIn messages to *warm up* a cold call.
- Recommended: **7 outreaches per prospect = 5 emails + 2 phone calls.** If no response after 7 (2 being calls), move to the **next person in the same company** (person 2, then person 3) — you're not leaving the company, just that unresponsive individual.
- One stated cadence: Email1 → next day Email2 → next day Email3 → phone call → Email4 → Email5 → call. (Then move to person 2 with identical treatment, pending spam/privacy law.)
- If person 3 doesn't respond: shorten your email, strip sales language, ensure a compelling reason to reach out.
- **Subject lines:** just use their **name** ("Hi Chris") or company name. Follow-ups: "quick follow-up" then "last message." NEVER "I've got a great business opportunity" or "sponsorship opportunities."
- Goal of the subject line = open. Goal of the email = a meeting/call. Goal of the call = gather info for a custom proposal. Nothing more.
- **Emails must be SHORT** — under 50 words ("even that's pushing it"). No 1000–5000 word emails, no pictures, no attached proposals. "As soon as your emails start to wreak [reek] of sales, they don't work."
- "Any response at all is a win." It's a game of "moves management" / conversions. (If they get angry, soften the tone.)
- **Don't blast** — send to 10 people and see how it goes, not 500. (See Liz case study.)

### Discovery Meetings
- Even if a prospect asks for your gold/silver/bronze package, insist on a meeting first. "Just send me a proposal" = code for "I'm not interested."
- Go in as an **investigative reporter** — ask questions only, don't pitch. Discover: their audience, how they make money, products to sell, how they measure ROI, what marketing they already invest in.
- "Discovery is asking them advice, not trying to sell them stuff."
- **Diagnose then prescribe** — understand their problems, then prescribe the solution (sponsoring you).
- Get to **numbers**: "How many sales? Talk numbers so we can talk ROI." Counter vague "brand awareness" asks with: "How many brand-awareness units would you like?" / "When someone becomes aware of your brand, what's the next step? Let's measure that." / "Do you hit your sales goals for the quarter?"
- Invite the NO: "Don't be afraid of objections… invite the no and respect it." Better to hear no in the meeting than craft a custom proposal and chase a ghost.
- Podcast example (Dave Stewart) runs a **3–4 call discovery process**: call 1 discovery, call 2 business case, call 3 proposal, call 4 BAMFAM tweak/close.

### BAMFAM — Book A Meeting From A Meeting
- The single most-repeated tactic. "The very best properties never chase. They always, always, always book a meeting from a meeting."
- When sponsor says "just send me a proposal," respond: *"Absolutely. And just for my own records so I get this right, what budget range should I stay within? … Is there anything else besides what we talked about today I should include? … Let me summarize what I heard. … Great, let's meet on Thursday at 3 to go over the draft together."*
- Avoid "happy ears" (believing a polite brush-off is a yes). Keep a deadline on the calendar so you don't get ghosted.

### Proposals
- **Business case (before meeting)**: no dollar amounts, no grids. ~50% audience data + activation ideas + case studies + call to action to phone you. Its only job = get a meeting.
- **Proposal (after meeting)**: has dollars/cents, fully custom, reflects exactly what they said they wanted + a relevant case study. ~70% of Chris's closed deals never used a proposal at all; 100% had no proposal at the *start*.
- 5 things every event proposal should include: (1) **Audience data** (25+ data points, ≥3 buyer personas) — most important by far; (2) **Activation ideas** (not levels/grids); (3) **≥1 case study** (prefer 3); (4) **Customization** (after a meeting); (5) **Contact info + strong call to action** (direct email + a phone you'll answer by the 2nd ring).
- 50% rule: half of every business case/proposal should be audience data. (Recurs in motorsport, conference, webinar videos.)

### Negotiation & Closing
- **Assume the multi-year sale.** "This activation is worth ~$30,000, and just so you know, at the $30K level we focus on three-year agreements. Does that work for you?"
- Create a **company policy** you can show prospects (so it's not seen as a negotiating tactic): e.g., "any sponsorship $25K+ requires a 3-year agreement." (Hillary case.)
- Pricing rules for multi-year: **$25K+ → push 3-year; six figures → push 5-year** ("start at five, you might end up with three").
- **Right of first refusal (ROFR) is bad for the property** — never enter it as commonly written; it only benefits the sponsor and makes it hard to replace them. If a sponsor demands ROFR, flip it: tie it to a multi-year deal with a defined 2-week fulfillment-report window where they can exit only if you violate ≤3 pre-defined terms; otherwise you invoice and proceed.
- Leverage the fulfillment report to convert to multi-year: "Option 1: same as last year but our prices have gone up (valuation proves it). Option 2: sign a 3-year now, lock this year's price for all 3 years, plus I'll throw in a couple free activations." (Hillary closed a 3-year this way — same money, 3 years guaranteed revenue → could hire staff + raised business valuation.)

### Fulfillment & Renewal
- See dedicated section 5 (Named Concepts) and the Fulfillment Report case study in section 8.
- **Renewal target: ≥80% of current sponsors** should agree to a feedback meeting + ask to see a draft proposal for next year. If <80% renew using the report → feedback → BAMFAM → draft-proposal method, you have a delivery/fulfillment problem. The ~20% who leave, you want to know within *days* of the event, not 3 months before next year.

## 3. Scripts, Templates & Exact Language

### The "Hey Dave" cold emails (from "Sponsorship Emails That Convert")
The single one-sentence email that worked (led to an $85,000 deal via Ray → Mike):
> "Hey Ray, any chance you can suggest the best contact for those in product X at your company?"
(One sentence. No pitch deck, no proposal, no "sponsorship," no "partnership.")

The "Hey Dave" template (LinkedIn-sourced):
> "Saw on LinkedIn that you're involved in marketing for X product. I would love to connect and ask your thoughts about a cool project that I'm working on. Are you free tomorrow at three? — Chris" *(No title, just name. Giving a specific date/time changes the decision from yes/no to "does that time work?")*

The 3-email sequence (designed/swipe templates):
- **Email 1 — Subject: "Hi [Name]"**: "I saw you're involved in marketing side of [company]. We recently surveyed our database and your company had a strong showing. Do you have a few minutes to talk about what we found, whether or not our audience is your audience? Let me know. — Chris"
- **Email 2 (2 days later) — Subject: "quick follow-up"**: "Hey [Name], no doubt you get a thousand emails a day, sorry to add one more 😉. We recently surveyed our database, your company had a strong showing. Would love to learn about what you're trying to accomplish."
- **Email 3 — Subject: "Hey, happy Monday, me again"**: "I don't want to keep sending you emails if you're the wrong person to follow up with. Before I follow someone else in your org, I thought I would ask if you can suggest someone internally who is looking to connect with [Segment 1: e.g., moms who live in the suburbs] / [Segment 2] / [Segment 3]… would be really interested in your reply. Have a great week." (Then copy the original message.)

Liz proof point: she ignored the "don't send to 100 people" rule, sent "Are you free Tuesday at 2?" — **27 people replied** that they were in fact free Tuesday at 2.

### Event email templates (from "How to Ask for Sponsorship for An Event")
- Email 1: "Hey [Chris], saw on LinkedIn that you work with [company name]. We recently did some analysis of our audience and your company had a strong showing. Wondering if you'd be interested in seeing what information about our audience and what we're up to. Let me know. — [name]"
- Follow-up: "Hey [name], no doubt you get a thousand emails a day, sorry to add one more. Our audience of [insert main hobby/descriptor] may be of interest to you and I thought I'd reach out, wondering if I can send some more information your way."

### Conference discovery-invite email
> "Hey [potential sponsor name], I see that you do X. I have a conference that attracts [these people]. Do you want to jump on a quick call and see whether or not we have an opportunity to work together?" — "If it's three sentences, it's too long."

### "We don't do sponsorship" rebuttal (Joan's script / Chris's reframe)
> "Well, neither do we. The only thing I'm interested in doing is connecting you to your target market. The only person who knows how you get more clients, more customers, and how you measure success is you. Why don't we get together — you fill me in on what you're trying to achieve and how I can help you do that. If putting logos on stuff is part of that, so be it, but I'm not committed to any particular package or process. I want to build something custom just for you."
Joan's pitch that closed: "What if I could put 30 of your ideal clients in a room and give you special access to network with them like peers — a unique experience they've specifically requested through our surveys — no sales pitch, no logo placement?" → Sponsor: "Oh, *that* I have budget for. Tell me how much you need."

### "Just send me a proposal" response (the Five Words You Never Want to Hear)
> "No — not until you tell me what you're trying to achieve, otherwise I wouldn't even know what to put in the proposal. We don't have a stock sponsorship package, we don't have a standardized list of gold/silver/bronze benefits — everything we do is custom. I'd love to get to know your goals, how you're allocating your budget, the things you're trying to accomplish. If we can't help you accomplish that, there's no sense submitting a package."
- Caveat: if you already have verbal agreement and the ONLY next step is a proposal, send it. If you'd have to *guess* at even one thing, don't.

### Call-to-action page language (proposal)
> "Everything we do is custom. Tell us more about your sales and marketing goals and we want to make it happen. Let's connect and talk more about your goals." — one contact name, one email, one direct phone line you WILL answer.

Strong CTA example: "If your goal is to get in front of 40-year-old high-net-worth moms who live in the suburbs with three or more children who play baseball and drive luxury brands — we want to hear from you. Everything we do is custom; tell us what you're trying to achieve and what data you need to make a decision, and we'll provide it."

### Phone-answering script (don't go into pitch mode)
> Greet them, mention who your audience is, and ask if this is a good time to talk about working together.

### AI prompts (from "Leveraging AI")
- "Summarize these survey responses into three audiences. For each, include their values, upcoming purchase plans, and the sponsor categories most relevant to them."
- (Established properties) "Cross-reference the survey results with the ticketing and engagement data. Segment attendees into clusters with shared values and near-term intent (next 18 months). Suggest sponsor categories for each cluster."
- "If a person answers this, this, and this — what brands or category of brand would be more likely to want that combination?"
- Two-tool validation trick: run the same prompt in ChatGPT and Perplexity; pull ChatGPT's output into Perplexity and say "make sure these links are clickable and real" to validate contact info.
- Self-prompting trick: ask ChatGPT "Can you write a prompt for ChatGPT that will help me achieve this goal?" then open a fresh thread and paste it. For agentic tools (Manus): "I want to evaluate 15 brands in this space that correlate with [X]. List these brands… now tell me what I need to do to get you to do this."

## 4. Numbers & Benchmarks

- **Audience data:** 25+ (often stated 25–30, or 30+) data points on the macro audience; same depth on **≥3 avatars**. The single most-repeated number on the channel.
- **Proposal 50% rule:** ≥50% of a business case/proposal must be audience data. (One sample deck = 70% audience data: 10 pages, 7 about audience.)
- **Email length:** under 50 words. Avoid 1000–5000 word emails.
- **Outreach cadence:** 7 touches/prospect (5 emails + 2 calls) before moving to the next person in the org.
- **Email reply/open rates with this technique: ~30%** ("for the vast majority of our clients"). Bottleneck exercise cites a healthy target of **15–20% cold replies**.
- **Close rate target:** 30–50% of prospects close on each proposal submitted. **Renewal:** 50–75% (one video) / **≥80%** come back when using fulfillment-report renewal method.
- **Fulfillment report timing:** deliver **within 2 weeks (often "within days")** of event/campaign wrap, in person or by phone.
- **Multi-year pricing thresholds:** $25K+ → 3-year; six-figure → 5-year. Multi-year recommended only above ~$25K (not on a $2K exhibit space).
- **Valuation logic:** charge a **2:1 or 3:1 ROI**; prove sponsors get 3:1 or 4:1 value. Logo view ≈ ¼ of one penny per impression. Speaking-from-stage ≈ hundreds of dollars per attendee. (Calculator demo used a deliberately fake $1.8M for a 5-min talk to make the point — "do not use these values.")
- **Sample valuation calculator outputs** (representative, not prescriptive): 1 tweet mentioning a sponsor (5,000 followers) ≈ **$66.58**; tools auto-scale logo-in-eblast value by database size. Always add **hard costs** (e.g., $500 sign) to the sponsor's invoice.
- **Survey methodology (Chris's actual method):** Pro SurveyMonkey account = **$350/year**. Two e-blasts to the database (Tue + Thu, 10am), chance to win one of three **$25 gift cards**. Enough for **30+ data points at 95% confidence / 5% margin of error**. "You need way less responses than you think." Survey = 20+ questions; last ~8 unrelated to your property — all about future purchases/brands.
- **Survey question example to add:** "In the next 18 months, are you planning on redesigning your home / buying a car / buying a boat / changing health insurance / going back to school?" (Elena: "that data is gold.")
- **Design cost for a deck:** ~$300–$350 on 99designs (10 designs to choose from).
- **Industry size:** repeatedly cited as a **$60–70 billion/year** industry (numbers used loosely: "60 billion with a B," "70 billion").
- **Calls received by a sponsorship consultancy:** ~300/month; ~20% involve someone who got taken advantage of.
- **Brands' inbound proposal volume:** ~**10,000 unsolicited proposals/month** (cited by multiple brand contacts; one had a 3-person team that does nothing but say no). They deliberately publish NO email/form/address and still get found.
- **Annual price increase advice (anti-pattern):** Do NOT just raise 3–5%/year "because things get more expensive" — that's short-term thinking divorced from delivered value.

## 5. Named Concepts & Terminology

- **BAMFAM** — Book A Meeting From A Meeting. End every meeting by scheduling the next one so you never chase/get ghosted.
- **Property** — your sponsorship opportunity/asset (event, festival, naming right, team, athlete, podcast, newsletter, building). NOT "give me money."
- **Asset** — a discrete, sellable item (logo placement, speaking slot, sampling rights, broadcast rights, eblast). Valued individually.
- **Activation** — "a fancy term for cool experiences for your audience" that connect the audience to the sponsor (tastings, workshops, lead-gen experiences, photo booths, branded clean restrooms). The thing that replaces gold/silver/bronze. "If we're going to replace the word sponsorship with anything, it should be with *experiential activations*."
- **Business case** — marketing document sent BEFORE a meeting; ~50% audience data, no dollars/grids; goal = secure a meeting.
- **Proposal** — sales document sent AFTER a meeting; contains dollar amounts; fully custom.
- **Fulfillment report (wrap-up report)** — "the most important tool in sponsorship." A snapshot proving you delivered everything promised: photos/screenshots/samples of every asset, web/social/media stats, audience data, and a before/after valuation showing actual value delivered (e.g., "promised $20K value, delivered $28K"). Delivered in person/by phone within 2 weeks; used to gather feedback AND close next year.
- **Avatar / buyer persona** — a defined niche segment of your audience (≥3 required), each with its own 25+ data points.
- **Curator of audience** — Mark Sabourand's framing (quoted repeatedly): "Think of yourself in sponsorship as nothing more than a *curator of audience*, not a placer of logos."
- **The 50% rule** — half of every business case/proposal = audience data.
- **Happy ears** — believing a polite brush-off ("send me a proposal," "we love giving back") is a real yes. "The number one cause of budget failure in the sponsorship industry" because it lets you stay optimistic instead of fixing the gap.
- **Logo soup / gold-silver-bronze (GSB)** — the outdated tiered grid of logo placements. The channel's central villain. "There are few absolutes in sponsorship, but this is one of them: if you have a grid labeled gold/silver/bronze, stop."
- **The 50% / "riches are in the niches"** — niche audiences outsell broad reach: "A thousand eyeballs seeing a logo is worth nothing; a hundred people attending a sponsor workshop could be worth six figures."
- **Right of first refusal (ROFR)** — clause giving the sponsor first dibs next year; "terrible for properties," only benefits the sponsor.
- **Brand value** — a calculated % of intangible brand worth added back into the valuation (Collective's tool uses a 20-question brand value calculator).
- **Rate card** — advertising price list used to benchmark sponsorship asset values against real market media costs. Pull rate cards to validate your valuation.
- **In-kind / trade / gift-in-kind** — non-cash sponsorship. Only accept if it's true **budget relief** (offsets a real cost you'd have paid) or convertible to cash (e.g., a media partner you can leverage 10x); otherwise it's "a distraction at best and costly at worst" (still requires activation + assets). [Kevin Grothe]
- **Technician vs. business-owner/marketer** (E-Myth, Michael Gerber) — thinking "I'm a festival owner who needs money" (technician) vs. "I'm a marketer growing a business that produces a festival" (owner). Technician mindset = path to failure.

## 6. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

- **Proposal-first / emailing the package to strangers.** "A shortcut to no thank you." Sponsors haven't read packages in years; you'll get flagged as spam. (The "current model" parody in "Sponsorship Proposal is Dead": write a proposal to nobody, run it through a committee with no sponsor input, email everyone, hear nothing, then *add more* mission/vision/CEO bio.)
- **Gold/silver/bronze grids & logo-only sponsorship.** Sponsors don't want logos; "if you round up, [interest in awareness-only] is zero." GSB is the spaghetti method (throw everything at the wall) based on the false belief every sponsor wants a bit of everything.
- **Making the package all about YOU** — 10 pages of history/mission/vision/CEO bio/how much money you need = "essentially begging for money." (Charity-sector–specific phenomenon.)
- **Waiting too long.** If efforts aren't producing dollars within 30 days, change approach. "Scaling mediocrity only leads to bigger piles of mediocrity" (Keith Cunningham) — if it didn't work a year out it won't work 2 weeks out.
- **Pestering / bump-ins / elevator pitches.** (The banker who found people waiting in his elevator and stacks of packages on his desk.) Don't call personal cells at 8pm/Saturdays; don't ambush at the mall. Be persistent via proper B2B process, not spam.
- **Confirmation bias / closed-mindedness.** "Yeah but the guys up the road use gold/silver/bronze so it must work." You're seeking proof for incorrect beliefs; it doesn't change that sponsors don't want logos.
- **Ignoring small sponsors / not giving them fulfillment reports.** "Way easier to upgrade a small sponsor into a title/multi-year sponsor than to land a $100K stranger." Replacing sponsors who leave is way harder than keeping them. (Memphis: a <$10K teacher's credit union became a $100K multi-year stage sponsor because Chris kept talking to them; Home Depot: $7,500 → $150–200K activation.)
- **Happy ears** (see §5). Believing "send me a proposal" = yes → miss budget.
- **Avoiding the multi-year conversation** / using ROFR instead. Costs thousands–tens of thousands.
- **Outsourcing your sponsorship program** to a commission-only "rep with all the contacts." Doesn't exist as imagined. Real reps charge a large monthly retainer + 15–30% + want exclusivity + 6–12 months + reject 99% of inbound + prefer proven opportunities. "Nobody cares more about your opportunity than you. The person who cares the most wins."
- **Believing in a sponsor marketplace/app/magic Rolodex.** Sponsors' problem is too MANY proposals, not too few; a marketplace is no convenience to them so they won't join. (Linda paid $25K + 8 months chasing this fantasy → went out of business.)
- **Treating sponsors as investors.** (Larry's line: "sponsors are not investors.") They want proven marketing ROI, not ground-floor risk. "If you need an investor, get a bank loan." Going to a sponsor saying "I have no money to run this without you" = you want a loan, not a sponsorship.
- **ROI backwards.** "I'll invest in sales/valuation/coaching once I've made the money." Non-sequitur ("once I lose the weight then I'll give up donuts"). Return comes after investment.
- **Thinking small is easy.** Smaller isn't easier — same process, less profit, and a $1–2K ask makes you look cheap/amateur in a $60–70B industry. "Smaller is not easy, smaller is boring."
- **"We're different / unique."** Usually means "we don't know our audience, haven't valued, are bad at sales" — i.e., exactly like everyone else. Being unique is bad when a car company asks "how do you help me sell cars?" and you say "I don't know, we're different." Ask instead: "How can I be the best marketing investment this company made all year?"
- **Indecisive / slow to act** (running every decision past the board). Sponsors want quick decisions. "You cannot overcome an objection you believe."
- **Not getting internal buy-in** → deals blown up by stakeholders who can veto.
- **Making up your valuation.** Sponsors know what assets are worth; if they're saying yes without asking for a valuation, you're charging too little. Increasingly, brands require a **third-party valuation** before proceeding.
- **Treating sponsorship as a side-of-desk hobby** while wanting pro-team outcomes ("a casual brain surgeon"). Sponsors want professionals/partners, not amateurs.
- **Doing sponsorship only for events.** (Charity case): programming, land, buildings, naming, lunch-and-learns, volunteer days can all be sponsored. Event-only revenue is the hardest to earn and can burn out the team / divert donor dollars.
- **Stopping prospecting in a downturn.** Wrong — 10x it; some businesses flourish in down economies (they just don't advertise it).

## 7. Mindset & Principles (verbatim where memorable)

- **"The sponsorship proposal does not make the sale. You do."** (Channel's signature sign-off, on nearly every video.)
- "Think of yourself as a **curator of audience, not a placer of logos**." (Mark Sabourand)
- **"Nobody buys sponsorship; they buy an outcome."** Sponsorship is a process/mechanism, not a product. Relationships are "necessary but not sufficient."
- **"The person who cares the most wins."** / "The best person to sell sponsorship for your shop is you."
- **"You can either have a fast no or a slow yes. There's no other option."** (a.k.a. "a slow yes or a quick no, but nothing in between.")
- **"Hard things are hard. There is no silver bullet, there is no knight in shining armor."** "Sponsorship seems really hard because it's really hard."
- "**Success leaves clues** — follow those who have walked the path before and who have what you want."
- "Scaling mediocrity only leads to bigger piles of mediocrity." (Keith Cunningham, *The Road Less Stupid*)
- "If you want to increase your success rate, double your failure rate." (Thomas Watson Jr.) / "The person who fails the most wins." (Seth Godin) — failure is required.
- "There's nothing so useless as doing efficiently that which should not be done at all." (Peter Drucker) — re: efficiently making packages nobody reads.
- "Show me your calendar and I'll show you your priorities." — schedule daily prospecting/cold-calls/discovery/follow-up. "If it's not in your calendar, it's not going to happen."
- "Sponsors don't want to work with amateurs. They want to work with professionals." Treat sponsorship as a profession, not a hobby.
- "**Better is better, more is not better.**" / "The less you sell the sponsor, the more you can charge for it" (the customization paradox).
- "The riches are in the niches."
- "The best time to plant a tree is 20 years ago; the second best time is today." (re: getting multi-year deals now).
- "Sales is service" / "I feel like a dirtbag trying to sell stuff people don't want — instead, let me teach you how [podcast] sponsorship works. You're the authority." (Dave Stewart)
- "Sponsorship is broken and it's our fault" — brands have told us for years they don't want logos; we ignored them. "Companies don't do sponsorship" really means "we don't do that 30-years-ago gold/silver/bronze stuff."
- "The problem is you think you have time, and you don't." (Buddha, via Dave Stewart interview) — time is the scarce, non-renewable resource.
- Failure "happens slowly over time like the ripening of a fruit… suddenly is ripe and then is rotten." You have less time than you think.
- (Memphis/Matrix) "I can show you the door, but it's you who has to open it." / "You're going to get out of it what you put into it — just like we tell our own sponsors."
- "Pleasantly Persistent" — Kevin Grothe's **PVP** approach to both prospects and the program.
- "You're not too old, you're not too far behind, you're not too slow — just open the damn thing up and talk to it." (Elena, on AI.)

- **Andrea (conference, anonymized):** Sat across from a prospect with NO pitch deck/proposal, asked only Discovery questions. Sponsor said "nobody has ever tried to understand what I'm trying to achieve… I have ~$175,000, do you have anything we can do together?" → custom proposal → **$175K** (vs. ~$0–$25K with a GSB package).

Rep Sally said it went "great" but told her boss Dave the conference was a disaster (3 ballots vs. expected 300–600). Chris's fulfillment report had photos of Sally on her phone ignoring a packed hall + social/registration screenshots proving a sold-out crowd. Saved the account; next year sent free iPads to auction for ballots → overflowed the box → Dave asked "How do I sign a multi-year?" One Word-doc-to-PDF report → **$125,000 multi-year agreement**.
- **Dave Stewart — Wet Fly Swing (steelhead fly-fishing podcast):** ~3M all-time downloads. Went from **$50–$150 per-episode / sub-$2,000/yr** CPM ads (~$25 CPM) to **$15,000+ individual sponsorships** and **six figures**; doubled revenue 3 years running. Pivoted from podcast ads to experiential: lead gen (gave out 11,000 leads in one event), travel/hosted trips with a dozen partners. Sweet spot = **small-to-medium brands** (can send a travel company 6 new paid customers worth ~$4K each → easy $10–20K ask), NOT the biggest brands. A sponsor about to churn was saved instantly by a fulfillment report showing 2× expected downloads. Moving to **quarterly reports** for all sponsors.
- **Christine Thompson — land trust / charity (Upper Grand River, SW Ontario):** Shifted from GSB to audience-based sponsorship over a multi-year process; valuation calculator number nearly matched her gut number (gave her proof to defend it). Built activations around employee needs: 10 PD-day camp spots per camp (childcare relief for women), movie nights for evening-shift workers, a women's half-day "equal camp," logo on new wayfinding signage (50,000 trail walkers/yr, mostly repeat) linking to their 2050 green plans, bug/tree experts touring their on-site community garden. Also: a **major Telecom** new sponsor used it to go national on green/food-security engagement (45 colleagues came to adopt a garden plot); two merging **real estate associations** Googled "gardens," found her food-bank garden, asked for plants → turned into a sponsorship (plants + seed packets + lunch-and-learns + volunteer days) and an info table in front of 200 realtors = "quadruple win." ## 9. Industry-Specific Nuances

- **Conferences/associations:** Audience easy to define by profession/subject, but still need 25+ data points + 3 avatars. Highest-value asset = getting the sponsor's product/sales funnel in front of attendees (downloads, emails, product testing, meetings, **speaking from the stage** = thought leadership, hundreds of $/attendee). Delegate bags get tossed by the garbage can — nobody wants pamphlets/pens. Use a **hybrid** model (standard booth/exhibit minimums + custom partner levels via discovery) — FOCUS's approach.
- **Charities/nonprofits:** Your CAUSE is irrelevant to the sponsor (relevant to your audience; audience relevant to sponsor). Don't load decks with mission/vision/CEO bios/photos of those you help — a charity-sector-only phenomenon. Decision-making is unique/slow (boards, committees) — being slow is a path to failure. Don't get bumped to the "donations person" (sponsorship ≠ donation, though approval often runs the same internal process). Individuals give in hard times; corporate *donations* get cut in hard times — but *sponsorship* (a marketing spend) is more resilient. Sponsor your PROGRAMMING/land/buildings/naming, not just events/galas (event revenue is hardest to earn, burns out teams, diverts donor dollars). Align corporate goals with mission rather than inventing programs (vs. grant-writing where you make programs to fit grants). Set clear boundaries/policies on which corporations to approach (e.g., be cautious with oil/mining for an environmental org). Done mindfully, corporate involvement isn't "selling out" — brands don't actually want it overly commercial ("as long as we don't end up looking like a race-car driver").
- **Podcasts:** Most can't survive on the **CPM/download model (~$25 CPM)** — only very large shows (Joe Rogan) can. Everyone else must get experiential/creative: lead gen, downloadable show notes, meetups/events, hosted trips. Reframe: "Here's a word from our sponsor" really = "an introduction to a commercial," and listeners hit the 15-sec skip (the exact length of a mid-roll). Sell **6-month or 1-year** deals (not single episodes) using the "7–10 touchpoints" brand-awareness argument as the *entry* point, then layer custom activations. Deliver **quarterly fulfillment reports** since deals run ~a year. Sweet spot = small/medium brands you can move the needle for.
- **Motorsport:** The LAST place where logos still carry real value — but logo-ONLY = a race to be the cheapest billboard. Mistakes: only logos; chasing one mega-sponsor; no audience data (one NASCAR client over-indexed on **upper-middle-class professional women in the Midwest** → won niche-aligned sponsors); choosing sponsors misaligned with your brand/values/audience (you mesh brands — wrong fit alienates fans); not knowing your value (copying others = amateur signal); proposal-first (assuming brands only want logos on the fire suit/car); engaging sponsors too late (a couple months before season = too late — prospect daily, think next season during this season). Build a **business case** (50% audience data + 3–4 activation ideas + case studies + media footprint/social/web), no values, goal = a meeting. Real proposal (with $) comes only after Discovery.
- **Festivals:** Sponsorship should aim to cover ~100% of operating costs (then tickets/F&B = profit, rain doesn't hurt). Survey-driven avatars unlock non-obvious sponsor categories (under-21 → sportswear/travel, not alcohol). Group activations geographically to create a high-traffic hub. Turn activations into points-of-sale. Memphis: spirits category spends heavily; immediate consumer interaction/reaction is the unique value print/TV/billboard can't deliver. Time prospecting to sponsors' Sept–Nov next-FY budget cycles; always be prospecting.
- **Sports / naming rights / buildings:** **Always engage a lawyer** (video is educational, not legal advice). Naming-rights agreement should specify: exact type of naming rights (signage locations, required wording/abbreviations, broadcast rights, **exclusivity**); **duration** (Chris's comfort: 3–5 yrs good, 10 yrs fantastic; uncomfortable with **perpetual** naming rights — bad for the rights holder); opt-out/renegotiation triggers; **name-change approval** process (what if the sponsor rebrands/changes industry/launches a controversial product line); where/how signage is displayed and maintained (community backlash has forced sign moves → triggering re-negotiation/re-valuation); additional **promotional benefits** (hospitality, pouring rights, product placement, VIP suites) — rarely just "name on building"; **payment terms** (upfront vs. dripped; performance criteria; invoicing schedule; non-payment penalties); **termination** clauses (define triggers/penalties up front, before the relationship breaks down).
- **Local businesses:** "Local business ≠ tiny business" — broaden your definition. Advantages: walk in and ask if they do sponsorship; meet decision-makers at local networking events/breakfasts; start with businesses YOU already spend money at (ask the bank/credit-union manager you bank with). Plan for **several** sponsors, not one big one (a food-bank charity raised real money via many **$500** sponsors leveraging its network). Still must define your audience (not "everyone in the community") and know your value relative to other local advertising (e.g., a $1,000 newspaper ad vs. your $5,000 logo+eblast — you must be competitive). Selling is done **in person** — don't drop a package at the front desk and expect it to reach the owner. Collaborations: co-buy postcards with a neighboring business (stretch lab next door) to double reach for the same spend.
- **Recession / economic uncertainty:** Whether a recession is "real" is irrelevant — brands review marketing spend weekly/monthly/quarterly/annually; don't be in the bottom 10–50%. Sharpen audience data, build activations worth paying for, know your value (overcharging for low ROI = first to get cut). Prove **3:1 / 4:1 ROI**. Be proactive: when something under-delivers, go to the sponsor first with the valuation before/after, what you over-delivered, and a make-good (discount/free assets). Ask for feedback at every fulfillment meeting. Lock **multi-year** deals (offer downturn discounts for signing). 10x prospecting (some businesses flourish in downturns).
- **AI (cross-industry):** Use for audience analysis (cluster survey + behavioral data into avatars), brand discovery + opportunity scoring, finding decision-makers + contact info, and pre-call intel (a prospect's posts/quotes/interests for the subject line and discovery). Free entry tools: ChatGPT, Perplexity, Notebook LM, (Canva for media kits). "Their actions prove what they want" — combining stated survey intent with behavioral data (RFID bracelets, booth dwell time, purchases) makes a brand "100× more sure." Even small orgs should pay ~$20/mo for a tool if their sponsorships are $50K–$100K ("I want to drive my car but I don't want to put gas in it").
