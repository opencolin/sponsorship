## 1. Frameworks & Processes (named, step-by-step)

### The 5-Step Sponsorship System that produces deals (from "Direct Outreach")
1. **Audience data** — 25 data points minimum on the macro audience PLUS 3 avatars. "You cannot pitch what you cannot describe."
2. **Prospect selection** — Use audience data to identify ~200 companies whose target customer overlaps with your audience.
3. **Contact identification** — For each company, find the named human inside the marketing function with budget authority (or access to recommend it).
4. **Direct outreach** — Email or call that named contact. Ask for a 15-minute meeting. No deck, no pitch, no proposal attached.
5. **Discovery-led proposal** — After the meeting, build the proposal collaboratively. It closes because it solves the problem they described.
> Steps 1–3 are preparation, step 4 is where revenue happens, step 5 is closing. Skip any one and the system breaks.

### The 7-Step Sponsorship Valuation Process (from "Essential Guide to Sponsorship Evaluation")
1. **Property building** — Identify your sponsorship properties (a property = a collection of assets serving a specific audience in a specific way).
2. **Build an inventory of assets** — Brainstorm EVERYTHING you can sell with whole team (sales, marketing, programs, ticket sales, comms, volunteers, board). Spend only 5 min on "what can we put logos on?" then ban the word "logo." Bundle into categories.
3. **Identify audience segments + run a sponsorship survey** — Niche segments down (25+ data points each); learn preferred brands and intended purchases.
4. **Find tangible values / market comparables** — Divide inventory into like-categories, value one asset at a time. Core question: "Where else can a sponsor get the same outcome?" Compare to ad rate cards, PPC, etc.
5. **Find your brand value (intangible value)** — Assign a % uplift (typically 10–30% per factor) for things sponsors value (audience knowledge, loyalty, customization, exclusivity, low competition, reputation). Add to each asset's hard value.
6. **Develop 5 turnkey activation ideas** — and run them through the value calculator. (NOT gold/silver/bronze; conversation-drivers.)
7. **Identify gaps, prospects, and a sales strategy** — Build revenue goals; prospect off audience data; improve weak brand-value items.

### The 7-Step / 6-Page Sponsorship Proposal Template (from "Definitive Guide" + "Conference Proposal Template" + "Event Sponsorship")
A proposal is ~6 pages. NEVER call the document a "sponsorship proposal/package" anywhere in it.
- **Page 1 — Title page / cover letter:** Logo, opportunity/event name, tagline, date, optional website. Keep it simple. Don't oversize logo/title.
- **Page 2 — Describe your audience:** Present audience data BEFORE you mention money. Current (survey within last 3–6 months). Demographics, psychographics, geographics, niched. Use pie charts/graphs + a photo or two of a packed past event.
- **Page 3 — Describe your opportunity (3 paragraphs):**
  - Para 1: Your cause/company/what you do/what you need. One paragraph only — NO sob story.
  - Para 2: The event/program/opportunity (what it is, how long running, objectives, attendance, this year's goals).
  - Para 3: Your board, delegates, attendees, users; add market research to beef it up. (Avg paragraph = 6–7 sentences.)
- **Page 4 — Menu (not "levels"):** Title it with the objective, e.g. "Engage Leaders in Industry X," "Reach People of a Certain Age/Geography," "Come and Meet X Sector." Open with your philosophy/how you like to work with sponsors. Frame items as suggestions to start the process and invite them to contact you with their own ideas. List assets/opportunities by what they help with (brand building, product placement, sampling, contests, growing database, thought leadership). **Do NOT** put the "we're willing to customize, contact us" line at the end.
- **Page 5 — Sample activations:** List activation ideas (branding, sampling, attendee experience, contests). Sponsor won't buy these off the shelf — they prove you know how to play the game and drive conversation.
- **Page 6 — Contact page:** CTA title like "We want to hear from you!" Invite them to say what's missing/what to add/change. Make proposal feel like a conversation tool, not set in stone. List all contact info (business address, phone, real personal email — NOT info@). NO cut-out payment/order form.

### Brand value calculation example (verbatim, numbers admittedly made up)
| Offering | Percentage |
|---|---|
| Knowledge of audience | 12% |
| High audience loyalty | 15% |
| Fully customised activation opportunities | 20% |
| Low competition in your sector for your audience | 15% |
| High-value brand with high trust | 20% |
| Annual sponsor summit and networking | 10% |
| **Total Brand Value** | **92%** |
Then add brand value to each asset's hard value: e.g. a $1/view logo becomes $1.92/view — nearly doubling revenue. **Ceiling rule:** if you find yourself charging more than 3x hard asset value, you're being too generous with brand value (very rare cases hit 7x — "not normal").

### Sponsorship Marketing Funnel (digital-marketing equivalents — from "Digital Marketing and Sponsorship")
Assets get MORE valuable the deeper down the funnel:
- **CPM (cost per thousand views/impressions)** — top of funnel. Marketers pay only $5–$10 per 1,000 impressions. Sponsorship equivalent = **logo placement** (cheapest, lowest value).
- **CPC (cost per click)** — avg $2–$10 per click. Equivalent = **driving traffic to sponsor's site/page** (highest when tied to a contest/giveaway).
- **CPS (cost per send)** — LinkedIn InMail; avg $0.80, up to $1 per send. Equivalent = **e-blasting your list** (more valuable if segmented + tailored).
- **CPL (cost per lead)** — bottom, most valuable. Marketers pay $50–$2,000 (sometimes more) per lead. Example: $2,000 PPC spend / 15 leads = $133.33 CPL. Equivalent = **customizable, data-driven, highly-researched assets** that generate leads.

### Sales-Funnel / Value-Ladder method for grading assets (from "Finding High-Value Assets" & "Opportunities")
Map each asset to a stage of the SPONSOR'S sales funnel: **Awareness → Lead generation → Conversion → Sales.** The further down, the higher the value.
- Ask of each asset: Can it drive awareness? Generate leads? Convert? Drive sales? (A logo = awareness only → low value. Product placement = all four → high value.)
- For a target (e.g., a car dealership), reverse-engineer their funnel: email/marketing → invited to lot → arrives at lot → test drive → purchase. Then ask "what's the value of ___?" at each step. You can't sell the car, but a test-drive-driving activation is the most valuable asset you can offer.

### Inventory-building brainstorm exercise (from "Corporate Sponsorship Strategies")
Gather committee, board, leadership, comms staff, AND program staff. 30-min session. Ask:
- What can we sell?
- What speaking opportunities are we missing?
- What product placement opportunities are we missing?
- What were the 3 coolest sponsorship/cause-marketing things you saw in the last 3 months?

### Asset categories to check during inventory (recurring list)
Naming rights of physical spaces; naming rights of programs; signage; product giveaways/samples; exhibiting; speaking opportunities; webpage; social media; newsletters/mailings; pass-through benefits; use of venue; paid media; traditional media; employee benefits.

## 2. Tactics & Techniques

### Prospecting
- **Prospect off your audience, not off brand fame.** Survey your audience → note the brands they mention using/loving → those become warmest prospects (after research).
- **The "who cares about this?" question:** Take each asset and ask "who cares about THIS [asset]?" — not who cares about your mission. Who would want to speak to this group? Who would borrow your logo to talk to their customers?
- **Competitor multiplication:** For every prospect, look at who your competitors work with; then add 2 competitors of each prospect (extra Excel columns). "If one bank is interested, I bet they all are."
- **Target ~200–300 prospects** (consultants cap themselves at 300; lists bigger than that can't be qualified). Funnel math (below) needs ~200.
- **If your prospect list is too small, your asset inventory is too small** — go build more assets to drive prospecting.
- **Motorsport nuance:** "the sky is the limit" on prospects — you don't have to stick to automotive brands; almost any non-conflicting brand works, so you can build 100+ easily.

### Finding the right contact
- Look for titles containing: **Brand, Marketing, Sponsorship, Business Development, Communications, Product** (Event post adds Sales). CSR-title people handle corporate philanthropy (can overlap with sponsorship); if only a CSR contact is found, ask them to point you to the right person.
- **Best contact = "the one who'll take your call."** Warm contact always beats the "right" title. Warm up every call via a mutual friend, board member, or donor intro.
- Add a line: "If I've reached the wrong person, could you point me to the best contact within the company?" — most people oblige.
- **Recognize when you're NOT talking to the decision-maker** (totem-pole metaphor). A low contact turns your request into a game of telephone. Get as close to the decision-maker as possible.

### Outreach / cold contact
- **Less is more.** If your intro email is more than 3 sentences, it's too long and gets deleted.
- **Only goal of first contact:** see if there's interest in moving forward / get a 15-min meeting. NOT to sell.
- **The "advice visit" / "advice ask":** ask the prospect for advice on your project — "I'd love your thoughts on my event/program, would love to work with your company in some way, and would appreciate your thoughts on the best approach." Then USE their advice to build a package just for them.
- Don't pad cold outreach with mission statement, event details, program outcomes.
- **Direct-outreach email formula** (from "Direct Outreach"): 2–4 sentences. Name the audience overlap + name something specific about the brand/contact's recent work (recent news item or new public hire) + ask for 15 min "Tuesday or Thursday." ~90 min to draft 4.
- **Follow-up cadence:** Wait at least 24 hrs after first contact, then reach out again. Continue for ~7 days using a combo of email AND phone before moving to someone else in the company. (Motorsport post: reach out every other day for ~a week, both channels; if >1 week of attempts with silence, move on.)
- **Voicemail works when email doesn't.** A 30-second voicemail naming the same audience overlap + same 15-min ask "proves the property is real and the request is specific."
- **A "wrong contact" reply is a useful answer** — it removes a dead lead and supplies the right one in one exchange.
- **Pick up the phone.** Talking is often faster than ping-ponging emails; forges human connection.

### Discovery meeting (the first meeting)
- **Bring NOTHING.** No proposal, no one-pager, no leave-behind. "How do I know what to give them when I don't know anything about their goals?" Bringing a package signals you're there for you, not them.
- **First meeting's real goal = the SECOND meeting** (and the information to build a tailored package). NOT to close.
- It's an exploratory "job interview." Ask about goals, needs, stakeholders, concerns, target market, latest campaigns, financial shortcomings, future plans, what they've already tried and why it fell short.
- "There is no better research tool than the question mark."
- Have a work-appropriate icebreaker ready; avoid "so how's that [sports team]?"
- **Discovery uncovers sensitive data** sponsors never reveal online: sales figures, audience decreases, abandoned carts, email bounces — the "ugly side." You need it to solve their problem.
- After discovery: go back to office, build something tailored, then earn the right to a follow-up call or office drop-by.

### Proposals
- **Never send unsolicited proposals.** Always meet/talk first. Don't shotgun-blast.
- **Wait until the prospect ASKS for the proposal** — could be after discovery, after several meetings, or never (that's fine). Follow the sponsor's lead.
- A proposal asked for early = usually a brush-off. After several meetings, an ask is genuine.
- **Don't attach a proposal to a cold email** — gets flagged as spam/trash, you get blocked.
- **Customize, but never SAY it's customized** — that's trying too hard; if it's truly customized it shouldn't need announcing.
- **Don't write the proposal before the discovery session** ("cart before the horse").
- **Use the prospect's exact language** from discovery in the proposal & cover letter (see Anissa Cook case below) — recognition accelerates closing.

### Negotiation / pricing tactics
- **Don't put a dollar figure in the proposal** — a written number reads as set-in-stone; you may undersell. Discuss price verbally; be ready and willing to negotiate.
- Be willing to take a somewhat lower price (a few hundred/thousand off). If a sponsor tries to *radically* discount, walk away.
- A sponsor may pay MORE than your valuation if eager for your audience — another reason not to hard-code a price.
- Keep a mix of low/mid/high-value assets so sponsors have freedom to choose (but trend away from low-value over time).

### Closing
- **Don't try to close in the first or even second meeting.** Pushing the sale alienates the prospect and kills the deal.
- **The "do nothing" close** (million-dollar case study): when asked "what would you do if we mailed you a $1M cheque?" answer "Nothing… we'd lock it in the safe, call you, and book a meeting — until we know what you want to accomplish, what your customers value, what your brand stands for, we don't have the right to spend a penny. If we can't do what you need, we'll give the cheque back." Awkward silence is a powerful tool. "Proposals don't sell sponsorship, people do!"
- **BAMFAM** — Book A Meeting From A Meeting. Always schedule the next meeting before you part, or you both fall off each other's priority lists.

### Activation / fulfillment / renewal
- **Right after signing: book an "activation brainstorming session"** (NOT an invoice). It's the first post-signature touch.
- Confirm sponsor OUTCOMES (not outputs). A photo booth is an output; the outcome is the brand-awareness/web-traffic/email-signup lift it produces.
- Ask how the sponsor MEASURES success (be specific — e.g., they may need 5,000 new followers, not "a few hundred"). Don't let your definition of success bleed into theirs.
- Brainstorm activations matching sponsor needs ↔ audience needs; bring loose ideas but let the sponsor modify/nix; speak up if their idea is an obvious mismatch.
- **Budget for activations up front.** Rule of thumb: if sponsor goes 5–10% over the total package value, eat it; beyond that, put the onus on the sponsor (especially if THEY proposed the pricier idea).
- **Activation credit** = a "spending account" line item in the menu a sponsor can draw from (within reason) to build out their activations — use if your budget has no room.
- **Fulfillment report** is mandatory after every event. Chart of promised vs. delivered + audience data + attendance + activity participation + social/web/engagement metrics + ROI calc + examples of what your team did. Be honest about misses; you "can't fudge it."
- **Fulfillment meeting** = reiterate value, gather constructive criticism, and segue into renewal/referrals.
- **Renewal:** re-value EVERYTHING (new and reused assets) every year; never resell identical assets — run a fresh discovery. Asset prices should rise (better at the service + inflation), never fall. Don't re-list assets you under-delivered last year.
- **Always ask for a referral** before ending the fulfillment meeting: "Who else should I be talking to? Who in your network would enjoy this approach and want to reach our audience?"
- **Stay in touch post-deal** — emails/social messages, forward useful articles — so you're the first call next time.

### Time management for sponsorship sales (from "Finding the Time")
- **Don't cut steps** to save time — that's an incomplete puzzle; you'll fumble the sale. Sponsors don't care that you're busy/CEO.
- The fix is NOT "what can I skip?" but "what can I remove from my schedule?" — and if the answer is nothing, delegate to a person/small team.
- **Don't make sponsorship a solo gig.** Even one helper + daily review beats going alone.
- **Set small daily goals:** 10 emails/day × 5 days beats one 50-email day you keep postponing. Momentum > perfection.
- **Expect longer timelines** if you put in fewer hours (12 hrs/week ≠ 36 hrs/week results). Plan earlier for time-sensitive events.

## 3. Scripts, Templates & Exact Language (verbatim)

### Phrases to USE
- Menu page openers: **"Engage Leaders in Industry X"**, **"Reach out to People of a Certain Age or Geography"**, **"Come and Meet X Sector."**
- Menu philosophy/customization invite: **"we're open to your suggestions and request that you contact us with your feedback on how you can engage our network."**
- Contact-page CTA: **"we want to hear from you!"**
- Advice-visit ask: tell them you want their **"thoughts on a project you are working on"** and would love their **"advice on how to tailor it to their needs and determine whether or not there is a fit."**
- Re-engaging a "no" / asking for the decision-maker (verbatim from case study): **"I would love to work with you guys in the future. What is a good time to sit down with you and build something that gives you exactly, and only, what you need?"**
- The "do nothing" close (verbatim): **"We would lock it in the safe, call you immediately and book a meeting. You see, until we know what you want to accomplish through our partnership together, or what your customers value, or what your brand stands for, we don't have the right to spend a penny. If we can't do what you need us to do, we'll give the cheque back."**
- Discovery questions to ask before submitting a proposal (verbatim set):
  - "Who is your target audience?"
  - "How do you normally engage in sponsorship?"
  - "What does your target market value?"
  - "What can you tell me about your sales goals for the coming year?"
  - "What would you consider to be the most important elements of a sponsorship proposal?"
- Case-study opening discovery question (verbatim): **"Tell me about your marketing goals and what you want to achieve through sponsorship?"**
- Probe to reframe a branding request (verbatim): **"It sounds like your goal is branding, that you want people to recognize your logo rather than take an action and engage your company. Am I on the right track?"**
- ROI/valuation brainstorm questions (recurring):
  - "What do our sponsors want to achieve?"
  - "What actions do they want our audience to take?"
  - "What do they want us to measure to prove that we delivered?"
  - "What do our sponsors want more of and what do they hate?"
- Audience-side activation questions: "What do our customers/clients/donors/volunteers want? Why do they engage in this property? Why do they come to this networking event? What does our audience want more of? What problems are they trying to overcome? What complaints do we hear?"

### Phrases to AVOID (and why)
- **"Don't see something you like here? Give me a call and we can customize something just for you."** — The single worst line to put in a package. Like a burger joint that serves only a plain burger with a sign offering customization: just offer the custom version from the start. Your actions (a rigid tiered package) speak louder than the line. (Quotes Emerson: "Who you are speaks so loudly that I can't hear what you're saying.")
- Never write the words **"sponsorship proposal"** or **"sponsorship package"** in the document (incl. the title page).
- Never label the menu **"sponsorship opportunities"** or use **Gold/Silver/Bronze** (or Platinum, or themed renames like dinner/lunch/breakfast).
- Don't say the proposal is **"customized."**
- Don't use **info@company.com** as your reply address.

### Sponsor brush-off lines = "no" (decode these — "happy ears")
- **"Let me share this internally and get back to you"** — means your contact isn't the decision-maker; "kiss of death."
- **"Just send me the proposal!"** — usually a platitude; they don't mean it.
- **"We'll get back to you."** — they typically never do.
- **"We'll call you!"** — they don't mean it.
- **"Oh, just send me a proposal"** — code for "no thanks."
Sponsors avoid conflict; take what they say at face value.

### Sponsor survey questions (verbatim, from valuation guide)
- "Who do you bank with? Would you consider a change in the next two years?"
- "Do you have a mortgage? How much?"
- "When do you plan to buy a house or move?"
- "Do you have life insurance, retirement savings? With who? Do you plan to change?"
- "What is your household income?"
- "What is your preferred brand of car? Do you lease or own? When do you plan to buy a new car?"
- "What is your level of education?"
- "What industry do you work in? How many years? Level of responsibility? Do you control a budget? What size?"
- "How many staff report to you?"
- "How would you rate brands XYZ?" (ask before AND after campaign to measure lift)

### Networking-breakfast script (from "Corporate Sponsorship Strategies")
Have a board/committee member host coffee + croissants at their office and invite 3 of their contacts; get 2 more members each to bring 3 contacts. Host introduces you. In **3 minutes or less**: who you are, what you do, your cool project, how they can help. The ONLY two ways they can help: (1) meet you for coffee about the project, or (2) host another event like this one.

## 4. Numbers & Benchmarks

### Funnel math for a $250k/year program (from "Direct Outreach") — KEY
- **200 contacts → 45 discovery meetings → 15 proposals → 5 sponsors at $50,000 each = $250,000.**
- 200 contacts / 50 working weeks = **4 direct contacts per week** (real, specific, qualified, named person, real reason, real meeting ask).
- Most teams make ZERO direct contacts/week. A real week ≈ 4 hours of work = ~18 touches incl. follow-ups, 1 new discovery meeting, 1 bad contact removed, 2 prospects in dialogue.
- Self-test: count qualified direct contacts this week. 0 = pipeline didn't move; 1–3 = started and stopped; 5+ = a real sponsorship week regardless of replies.
- "Reply rate is conversation. Meeting rate is pipeline. The funnel runs on meeting rate, not proposals sent."

### Survey / audience benchmarks
- Audience data must be **current = within last 3–6 months** (proposal) / "more than six months ago is not current enough."
- **25 data points minimum per audience segment** (and 3 avatars on the macro audience).
- **Survey response math:** send 5,000 surveys → you only need **256 replies** to get meaningful audience data.

### Pricing / valuation benchmarks
- US sponsorship spend: **$37.9B (2007) → $65.8B projected (2018)** (+$27.9B). Trend up.
- CPM: marketers pay **$5–$10 per 1,000 impressions.** CPC: **$2–$10/click.** CPS (LinkedIn InMail): **$0.80–$1/send.** CPL: **$50–$2,000+ per lead.**
- Email-from-social conversion rate example: **~25%** of click-throughs give up their email (use to value email-capture assets).
- Brand value uplift: **10–30% per factor**; total can ~double revenue. **Don't exceed 3x hard value** (7x exists but "not normal").
- Renewal: even +$200/asset × 10 assets = +$1,000.
- Activation over-budget tolerance: **5–10% of package value** — eat it; beyond that, sponsor pays.
- Getting the commitment ≈ **20% of the total work**; activation/fulfillment ≈ **80%** still ahead after the money arrives.

### Case-study dollar figures
- $200,000 education-event deal (Case Study 1) delivered to 3,000 pros via webinar vs. 200 live; "majority named our sponsor" in a survey.
- $1M+ "do nothing" close (multi-year, seven-figure; landed 1st, 2nd, 3rd million).
- Pharma "proposal-first" failure → 10 months later closed at **double** the prior ask (illness missed 90% of the time → awareness campaign).
- Cause-marketing selfie campaign: $5 donation/photo, failed (too many steps).
- Motorsport examples: property valued $35k → sponsor paid $45k; valued $20k → in-kind sponsor gave $5k of gifts (rest from others).
- A simple audience-improving activation can become a **$7,500 offer**.

### Best timing
- Best time to submit a corporate sponsorship proposal: **October–December** (Fall) — or whenever sponsors say is best.
- New Year strategy: every week, move **10/20/30 prospects** one stage forward.
- You can't pull off a quality event sponsorship in **3–4 months**; start planning the next deal as soon as the current event wraps (annual events).
- Macro-influencer prices: TikTok post **$1,260–$2,500**; Instagram post **~$10,000**; YouTube video **~$20,000**. Macro avg reach ≈ **61% per post**; engagement ≥ **3%**.

### Industry sizing (cited)
- Motorsport market: **$5.11B (2022) → $7.47B (2028)**.
- Fashion influencer marketing market: **$1.5B (2019)**, CAGR **35.7%** (2020–27). Fashion industry **$1.7T globally (2023)**.
- Virtual events: **>90%** of companies that switched planned to keep them. **98%** of surveyed users more inclined to buy after an activation. VR desire: travel 56%, music 52%, fantasy 45%. Avg phone use **3h15m–4h/day**.

## 5. Named Concepts & Terminology

- **BAMFAM** — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting." Always schedule the next meeting before parting.
- **Property** — a collection of assets serving a specific audience in a specific way, against which you sell and recognize sponsors (e.g., a gala, golf tournament, race series, naming rights, networking-event series). A rights holder can have many.
- **Asset** — a single sellable thing (one tweet, one e-blast, one logo, a booth, a speaking slot, signage). Low value alone — "little more than advertising unless packaged under a property, valued, and activated."
- **Inventory** — the full list of all assets you're willing to sell.
- **Activation** — the process of helping a sponsor get the most out of their investment + measuring ROI. "Putting samples on a table is an asset; letting them come, sending e-blasts, running a contest, and giving a speaking slot is activation." "Collecting emails at the gate is an asset; running a raffle for an iPad in exchange for a ballot is activation." Lives in the Venn-overlap of sponsor needs ↔ audience needs. = experiential marketing.
- **Output vs. Outcome** — output = the activation itself (a photo booth); outcome = the measurable result (brand-awareness/email-signup/web-traffic lift). Choose activations for outcomes, not coolness.
- **Brand value (intangible value)** — the extra benefit a sponsor gets reaching your audience via your property vs. other media (alignment with their customers' favourite team/charity/profession). Added as a % uplift to each asset.
- **Fulfillment report** (a.k.a. post-event / wrap-up report) — promised-vs-delivered chart + metrics + ROI; proves reliability, drives renewal. "Saved me more times than I can count."
- **Avatar / audience segment** — a niched, highly specific persona (good: "40-year-old high-net-worth moms in suburban Chicago with 2+ kids who play baseball"). "The riches are in the niches."
- **Value ladder** — ordering of assets most→least profitable; the inverse of the (widening) sales funnel — value rises as the ladder climbs.
- **CPM / CPC / CPS / CPL** — cost per thousand impressions / per click / per send / per lead (digital-marketing analogues mapping low→high asset value).
- **Discovery session** — the first, exploratory meeting to learn the sponsor's goals/problems before proposing anything.
- **In-kind / contra sponsorship** — goods/services instead of cash or promotions. Widely viewed as the least favorite asset (lowest value; e.g., $20k of branded pens ≠ $20k cash).
- **Pass-through benefits** — an asset category (rights the sponsor can pass to others).
- **CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility)** — how an org manages social, economic, ethical, environmental concerns; nonprofits can help corporations execute it. Distinct from sponsorship (often a different budget/decision-maker than the Brand Manager).
- **Cause-related / cause marketing** — joining a corporation to promote a cause while boosting their brand/sales; "win/win/win" when audience also gains. NOT the same as corporate sponsorship.
- **Sponsorship-marketplace dependency** — paying a third party for promised meetings; "the most expensive form of avoidance" — you pay someone else to not do direct outreach.
- **Category-timing windfall** — a brief inbound lift tied to a cultural moment/budget shift; mistaken for a working program; vanishes when the moment passes.
- **"Happy ears"** — hearing a sponsor's polite brush-offs as genuine interest.
- **Special sauce** — your unique differentiator; per this chunk, it's your activation opportunities (sponsor-needs ↔ audience-needs overlap).
- **Macro-influencer** — 100k+ followers (vs. micro = 10k–100k), engagement ≥3%, treated as a celebrity.
- **Sponsor types** (motorsport framing): Cash/financial, Promotional (a company that promotes you), Media (a media outlet), In-kind/contra. Plus Promotional partners (celebrity/public figure) in events.

## 6. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

- **Gold/Silver/Bronze (tiered) packages** — the most-repeated anti-pattern across the chunk. Signals you're an amateur; sponsors have seen them "a million times" and some refuse to read them. Holds high-value assets hostage to force a bigger buy; saddles sponsors with assets they don't want; no room for customization. Renaming tiers (platinum, dinner/lunch/breakfast) changes nothing.
- **Proposal-first / shotgun-blasting** — emailing/attaching a templated proposal to many prospects unsolicited. "We get 10,000 sponsorship packages a month. Nobody on my team has read a proposal in years." Leaves money on the table by letting prospects self-select.
- **Filling out website contact forms** — efficient-feeling busywork that almost never yields a response; first contact "rarely comes from a contact form."
- **Bringing a proposal/one-pager to the discovery meeting** — you'll have guessed wrong; signals you're there for you.
- **Trying to close in meeting 1 or 2** — alienates and kills the deal.
- **Not valuing assets at all** (Valuation Mistake #1) — like pawning jewelry without an appraisal; you'll never know if you're undercharging by thousands.
- **Guessing your value** (#2) — sponsors can tell when numbers don't add up (e.g., "primarily logos but asking $50,000"); they'll ask your process.
- **Copying someone else's valuation** (#3) — their assets aren't yours; sponsors ask where your numbers came from.
- **Not valuing ALL assets** (#4) — valuing a few and stopping means you may have missed the highest-priced ones.
- **Over-inflating value / "tacking on zeroes"** (#5) — fastest way to kill a deal; you'll hear radio silence.
- **Not re-valuing on renewal** (#6) — reselling identical assets or not valuing new ones destroys goodwill.
- **Failing to value before you can pitch** — sponsors increasingly send seekers away to get a third-party valuation before negotiating.
- **Leaving activation/fulfillment to chance or to people not on your team** (Case Study 1: sponsor's sign hidden behind a door, VIP table behind a pillar, host never mentioned them). Always run activation personally or delegate to your team.
- **Not prepping speakers/MCs about sponsors** (Case Study 2: keynote trashed the title sponsor's own product for 10 min, not knowing they owned it → multi-year sponsor pulled out permanently).
- **Over-engineering a cause campaign with too many steps to trigger the gift** (Case Study 4 — selfie/upload/$5 donation failed; "we knew it" but stayed silent to avoid waves).
- **Trusting a partner (e.g., hospital) to handle naming-rights logistics** (Case Study 5: wrong machine/competitor's logo at the dedication, media + CEO + board member present).
- **Chasing Fortune 500 / famous brands just for clout** (Red Bull, Nike) — they're inundated with thousands of requests/day and won't respond; pick prospects by audience fit.
- **Selling only logos / "brand awareness"** — "Brand awareness is not enough anymore… and likely never was." Digital marketing now beats "logos on stuff."
- **Becoming besties with the sponsor** — don't mix business and pleasure; your contact often isn't the final decision-maker, so chumminess doesn't seal it.
- **Being nice only until you get the cheque, then ghosting** — "Sponsors aren't ATMs."
- **One-sided sponsorship** — asking for money while offering nothing (or offering low-value commercials and expecting a fat cheque). That's a donation, not sponsorship.
- **Following the leader** (transference, per HBR) — everyone in the room is doing it wrong; do the opposite.
- **Efficiency over effectiveness** — perfecting tiers, harvesting email lists, filling forms feels productive but doesn't move the deal ("motion vs. action"; spinning in circles).
- **Offering customized packages only to NEW sponsors** (Evolution post) — double the work, devolving program, insults loyal old sponsors when they find out (the "cheese & crackers vs. four-course meal" betrayal). Instead, ask every existing sponsor annually what they want.
- **Skipping steps to save time** — produces an incomplete program; you fumble the sale (Joan the CEO made ~$0 over years doing it solo/part-time).
- **Over-delivering recklessly / promising the moon** — "overachievers need not apply"; promise only what you can deliver, or it hurts renewals.

## 7. Mindset & Principles (verbatim where memorable)

- **"The sponsorship package is NOT the sales tool, you are."** / "Proposals don't sell sponsorship, people do!"
- **"Fall in love with the problem you solve — not your solution."** Paired with Keith Cunningham: **"Shoes that don't fit are not a bargain at any price."**
- **"Efficiency is doing things right; effectiveness is doing the right things."** (Drucker) — chase effectiveness.
- **"Nothing, nothing at all, is your most important tool."** Awkward silence is your best sales tool.
- **"Sponsors aren't ATMs."** Treat them as people/partners, not cash machines. "Speak to the sponsor like a person, not a cash machine."
- **"A sponsor isn't a donor. They're a business."** Sponsorship = producing marketing outcomes in exchange for cash/promotions; it is marketing, not philanthropy.
- **"The riches are in the niches."** Smaller, specific segments are worth more.
- **"There is no better research tool than the question mark."** / "The only person on earth who knows exactly what your sponsor wants is your sponsor. So ask them." (the "top secret consultant trick" = ask your audience AND your sponsors).
- **"DO listen more, talk less."** Master active listening; don't plan your reply while they speak.
- **Sponsors are like onions / shark attacks** — multiple layers; ask the right questions + persistence to peel back shortcomings.
- **"Even the largest companies are run by people."** Build real relationships so asking forgiveness is easier when something goes wrong.
- **Do's/Don'ts of relationship-building:** DO be genuine; DON'T become besties; DO listen more; DON'T fear asking questions (sometimes twice); DO follow BAMFAM; DON'T be nice only until you get what you want; DO keep the connection going.
- **"You can close deals without a formal proposal."** A proposal is an augmentative tool, not a crutch.
- **Property value "is as unique as your fingerprint."** No magic list of values exists.
- **"How do you eat an elephant? One bite at a time."** Value a 50/100/1,000-asset inventory one asset at a time.
- **Sponsorship is an evolution** — neither strictly tiered nor strictly custom; do whatever works for each sponsor as their needs change (revisit annually).
- **"Sponsorship is not a part-time job."** Put in full effort or delegate; you can't reap the whole reward for partial effort.
- **Take the pressure off** — see yourself as a partner helping prospects hit their goals; be comfortable that your offering may not fit, and that's OK (keeps dialogue alive after a "no").
- **The make-it-right principle** (horror-story lessons): (1) something will eventually go wrong, and that's OK; (2) do everything to make it right; (3) always have an activation strategy; (4) always deliver a fulfillment report; (5) handle delivery personally or delegate to your team.

## 8. Industry-Specific Nuances

### Conferences / expos / conventions
- 6-page proposal template works for conferences, expos, conventions; tweakable for sports.
- Hyper-target audience segmentation to the conference; sort by salary, interests, geography→attendance, sometimes age/gender.
- Premium activation: each of 10 sponsors gets the stage 5 minutes (no Gold/Silver/Bronze) to "sell their city" → "activation arms race" → sell-out event yearly.
- Free professional headshots booth (sponsor emails the headshot to capture contact info) drives exhibit-space revenue.

### Events / festivals
- Four benefits of event sponsorship: cashflow, name-brand credibility, "think big by thinking small" (local in-kind: venue/food/speakers), shared publicity.
- Partnership types: media, financial, in-kind, promotional (celebrity). Offer exclusivity (one area or whole event) to raise price + credibility.
- Pricing from ad comparables: trade-mag ads, Google AdWords, local-paper ads, banner ads → cost per view × your reach.
- Signs you're ready: deeply segmented audience data; a general financial value; ideas for assets/activations; goals bigger than you can self-fund. "No such thing as too soon."
- 20 creative ideas list: sponsored activities/workshops, event-app (+gamification points→prizes), VR/AR, livestream (pre-roll), interactive polling/Q&A (leaderboards), lead-gen tools (real-time reports), targeted social ads/influencers, naming rights (branded lounges), themed décor/signage, branded transportation/kiosks, sustainable sponsorships, experiential giveaways (skydiving/backstage), cause-related, pre-event hype, sensory ("Blindfolded Bite"), themed food/beverage stations, custom scentscapes, skill-sharing workshops, sponsored Wi-Fi/charging (data capture), themed escape rooms.

### Nonprofits / charities / CSR
- Help corporations execute CSR; nonprofits become "the conduit that brings corporations and their customers together."
- CSR money often comes from a DIFFERENT budget/decision-maker than sponsorship (Community Engagement Manager vs. Brand Manager).
- 9 CSR types: green practices, ethical marketing (watch for "greenwashing"), HR/diversity, volunteering, corporate philanthropy, socially responsible practices, cause-related marketing, addressing social issues, supply chain.
- Map your values to a prospect's CSR; research their philanthropic philosophy; build a customizable campaign proposal; track engagement.

### Boards / fundraising (guest post — Mena Gainpaulsingh)
- Boards disengage from lack of trust (fear their contacts will be mistreated). Build trust; do what you say; reconnect them to the cause every meeting (first agenda item); thank repeatedly; prioritize fundraising (don't leave it as the last 10 min).
- Give boards tools: a strong **Case for Support** (gives them the language), explain sponsorship ≠ philanthropy, coaching (their best role = making introductions, not cold-calling), and do the legwork for them (scripts, drafts, research, scheduling).
- Best board contribution: introductions to the people you want to reach.
- Involve the board in ideation, valuation (pare down to key members), audience recommitment, and fulfillment meetings.

### Podcasts
- Biggest mistake: assuming commercials are the best asset. A radio/podcast ad alone often fails to even raise awareness (listener is driving, forgets). Your highest-value asset is your AUDIENCE, not a commercial.
- Audience data sponsors want: not "60% female" but "of the 25-yr-old women, how many are from Detroit, make $110k, work in pharma?"
- Bundle: only run the sponsor's ads on the episode you interview them; interviews/guest spots > standalone commercials.
- Value assets vs. market (e.g., what a radio pro charges to interview someone).

### Influencers (macro)
- Find via Klear, Influencity, Traackr, Find Your Influence. Build a list of 5–10 prospects (specialization limits list size).
- Same process: value proposition → audience research → discovery → custom assets/activations → deliver → fulfillment report → propose longer-term.
- Pricing baselines above; reach ~61%/post; engagement ≥3%.

### Motorsport
- Prospect off attendees/TV viewers; "the sky is the limit" on brand types (avoid conflicts like alcohol where rules forbid).
- 4 sponsor types: cash, promotional, media, in-kind. In-kind is least favorite (lowest value, brand-recognition focused).
- Naming rights (venue/race/team) = 5–7-figure opportunities; logos on uniform/vehicle should be a part, not the star.
- KPI menus by type: cash (tickets, attendance, TV viewers, F&B, merch); promotional (email open/CTR, web traffic, list growth, Net Promoter Score, Customer Lifetime Value); media (followers/likes/comments/shares/mentions by platform).

### Fashion
- Best opportunities: Fashion Weeks (Paris #1, then NY/London/Milan) and sports crossovers.
- Examples: Hugo Boss × PSG (branded collections sold on team site), Evian × NYFW (Virgil Abloh bottles), Colgate × Richard Quinn #PoutFree after-party (faux bathroom set).
- Activation/asset ideas: runway branding (the ONE logo exception — seen for the whole event), exclusive clothing collections, intermission entertainment, swag bags, sponsored pre/after-parties, contests/giveaways, live interviews/podcasts, sponsored brunches/dinners.
- Small-brand tip: skip the huge houses; several small sponsorships can equal one big one and are more attainable.

### Digital / virtual activations
- Cheaper, lower-risk, unlimited reach (no venue cap), and easier to measure than physical events.
- 4 success steps: understand sponsor goals; understand audience value (sell from a menu of segments/personas); set a budget; leverage social media.
- Types: branded social content (co-brand / sponsor-produced / "stealth" branding), contests (email capture / UGC), online communities (market research, beta testing, influencer-finding), Buzzfeed-style quizzes.
- Metrics sponsors understand: landing-page visits, traffic sources, organic search, referrals to sponsor, social views, new vs returning, session time, bounce rate, impressions, engagement rate, email open/CTR.
- Real examples: Pottery Barn × Sherwin-Williams (matching paint line), Spotify × Starbucks, CoverGirl × Lucasfilm (Star Wars makeup).

### Activation idea bank (notable, from "8 Cool Ideas," "February 2024," "Experiential 2023," "Corporate Activation," "Creative Ideas")
- Hollywood-quality superhero photo station (insurance co.; entry = request-a-quote card — ran out of cards).
- Fire breathers/dancers spelling sponsor names in flames (recurring example — people still talk about it years later).
- Finish-line outdoor branded grocery store (fresh produce/juices) for runners.
- "Social media army" / selfie-hashtag contest the sponsor pays to run.
- Cell-phone charging station (email = "payment"); free pro headshots (email capture).
- Reverse raffle for "the world's ugliest statue" (only way out = buy a ticket; CEO becomes the talk of the event).
- Larger-than-life art installations, pop-ups (= FOMO + RFID/email gate), VR/AR (virtual try-ons/showrooms), advocacy (MISEREOR "Social Swipe" donation billboard).
- 2023 trends: virtual events, food activations (3D-printed Oreos at SXSW; Google donut truck), VR, live spectator events, art installations, advocacy, apps/games, pop-ups.
- Feb 2024: House of Campari (SAG), Nat Geo holographic-animal runway (NYFW), Puma × Cheetos "Bag Experience" maze (NBA All-Star), FanDuel "Kick of Destiny" ($10M bonus bets, Super Bowl), Modelo × Snoeman Nets pop-up, Slack Huddles music activation, Doritos × Luxor hotel wrap (first Super Bowl building wrap), Toyota Super Bowl Experience theme park, YSL Candy Shoppe, Marriott Super Bowl Sleepover.
- Low-cost/high-impact audience adds: free valet, breastfeeding stations, charging stations, coat check, free Wi-Fi, local craft beer/coffee/wine tasting.

### Asset value tiers (from "Finding High-Value Assets")
- **Low-value** (awareness only): sponsor mentions, social posts, signage/logos, marketing-material distribution, logos on website, press-release inclusion, branded clothing.
- **High-value** (awareness + leads + conversions): speaking opportunities, media partnerships, tailored website content/guest posts, broadcasting rights.
- **Premium-value** (exclusive, unbuyable elsewhere): new customer/client acquisition, naming rights, direct lead capture.
- You still need a MIX — don't drop all low-value assets; combine to reach revenue goals; don't get stuck only in lead-gen or only in awareness.

### Three macro trends to watch (from "Disruption")
1. Digital disruption — brands can now target your exact audience and pay only on action; you must prove outcomes.
2. Audience (and activation) are everything — sponsors demand buying habits/interests/employment/education, not just age/geo/gender/income; activation is the one thing digital can't replicate.
3. Knowing your value is essential — valuations now expected of all sizes; sponsors send clients for third-party valuations before negotiating.

### Closing-speed case (Anissa Cook, SynBioBeta)
Used the prospect's exact words from discovery in the proposal + cover letter; prospect said "Oh, this is exactly what we wanted," skipped the sales call, went discovery → proposal → contract in **2–3 weeks** vs. normal **30/60/90-day** cycle. Lesson: mirror the prospect's real language; substitutes (mass proposals, networking, content) have no specific human/words to mirror.
