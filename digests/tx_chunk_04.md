## 1. Frameworks & Processes

### The Sponsorship Funnel / Funnel Math (recurs across many videos)
The core conversion funnel that produces **5 sponsors at $50K each ($250K)**:
- **200 qualified contacts → 45 discovery meetings → 15 proposals → 5 sponsors.**
- "Qualified contacts" = specific, qualified people contacted with a real reason, asking for a 15-minute discovery meeting (NOT eblasting unsolicited proposals).
- 200 contacts/year = **4 real contacts per week**; 45 meetings/year ≈ **1 meeting/week**.
- Numbers shift with fundamentals: strong audience data + a good prospect list improve them; weak fundamentals make them worse, but the *process* doesn't change.
- This assumes you've done a valuation and actually have an opportunity worth ~$250K (wanting sponsorship ≠ valuation that supports it).

### Reply Rate vs. Meeting Rate (the diagnostic distinction)
- **Reply rate** = every response (including "I'm not the right person," "send more info," "not now," "no thanks"). It's just a conversation.
- **Meeting rate** = only qualified contacts who agree to a 15-min call to check alignment. **The funnel runs on meeting rate.** "If you're not having meetings with sponsors, you are not participating in sponsorship."
- Benchmark: a property running **40% reply rate and 25% meeting rate is excellent.**
- Self-diagnosis: add up all direct contacts (real phone/video conversations with sponsors) over the last 12 weeks, divide by 12. **If below 4 contacts/week and 1 meeting/week, your funnel is not working.**

### "Find Your Number" (Sponsorship Accelerator process)
Reverse-engineer daily activity from your revenue goal:
- Goal $100K ÷ avg sponsorship value $5K = **20 sponsors needed.**
- If 1 in 10 cold prospects converts → need 200 cold outreaches.
- 200 outreaches by a deadline → tells you exactly how much outreach you must do **every day** (daily cadence).

### Moves Management System (simple pipeline in Excel)
Stages a prospect moves through; you track date + notes at each:
1. **Contact made / request referral** (with date; e.g., "sent LinkedIn message + connection request")
2. **Second email** (if no response by your follow-up date)
3. **Third email**
4. **Phone call** (4th outreach)
5. **Response received** (ANY response moves them here)
6. **Meeting booked**
7. **Proposal sent**
8. **Yes / No (Declined)** — when declined, log *which step* they declined at (e.g., "email") to diagnose.
- Purpose: it's a **diagnostic tool**. Calculate % who respond, % who book, % who get a proposal, % who say yes. If everyone leapfrogs from "sent email" to "stop bothering me," ask what's in your emails driving people away.
- Columns: company name, contact name, email, LinkedIn URL, last contact date, notes.
- "Simplicity scales… complexity does not scale well." Keep it simple so it works at 6/7/8 figures.

### The 3-Step Renewal Process (renew within 30 days of property wrapping)
1. **Build a fulfillment report** and deliver to sponsor **within a week** of wrapping.
2. **Book a FEEDBACK meeting** (not a pitch meeting) — go through the report live, hear what they loved and hated, solve problems on the spot.
3. **At the end of the feedback meeting**, if you've earned the right, place your offer (see Renewal tactics below).
- Done this way, within 30 days you should know exactly who's coming back (signed agreements/agreed proposals, even if checks come later).

### The six-part lifecycle framework
Six parts: **(1) Clarify** (property, audience, activations) → **(2) Differentiate** (valuation, pricing, defending price) → **(3) Launch** (prospecting, outreach, discovery calls) → **(4) Amplify** (media/promo partners, social + video assets) → **(5) Renewal & Upgrade** (fulfillment reports, multi-year deal structures, sponsor summits) → **(6) Scale** (build a team — "the four key roles of a scalable sponsorship team," hiring).
- Same framework lands a first sponsor or renews a multi-million-dollar multi-year sponsor.

### Sponsorship Cookbook (vs. Recipe)
- "Amateurs have a recipe. Masters have a cookbook." A recipe = gold/silver/bronze grid (makes one thing). A cookbook = a pool of components you draw from to build infinite custom combinations.
- Build the cookbook from: (1) **audience data** (≥25 data points on macro audience + ≥3 micro audiences/avatars), (2) a **catalog of activation ideas**, (3) **valuation** of each individual asset.
- After a discovery meeting, mix-and-match ~5 specific "recipes" (activations/assets/segments) that get the sponsor their outcome.

### Hybrid Proposal
- Some parts standardized (history, audience data), some fully custom (activations drawn from your bank). You're not building from scratch each time, but not sending stock packages either — "find that sweet spot right in the middle."
- Most important rule: **build proposals WITH your sponsors** (co-build after a discovery meeting), don't send cold.

### The Three Questions Every Sponsorship Must Answer (recurs in multiple talks)
1. **Who is our shared target audience?**
2. **What action do we want that audience to take?** (sponsorship is a verb — it requires an action)
3. **How will we measure success?** (proving the campaign/activation worked)
"In the absence of these three questions, you are not practicing sponsorship — you're doing something else."

## 2. Tactics & Techniques

### Prospecting / List Building
- Build your prospect list **based on audience data** — the brands your audience told you they buy or are considering. Do NOT build lists from directories, "people who have money," conference attendee lists, or companies that sponsor other properties ("that they have money is not a compelling reason to speak to you").
- Contacts are **actual people with real emails/phone numbers** who are actively selling products to your audience — not `info@`, `contact@`, `sponsorship@`, `help@` addresses. "Nobody inside the company forwards your message to info@ to the right person." Finding the right people (e.g., the marketing director who can action your file) is the hardest part — that IS sales.

### Cold Outreach Cadence
- Standard cadence: **email, email, (maybe a 3rd email), phone call.** ("two emails and a phone call" / "two emails maybe a third and a phone call"). Set the cadence based on your number, execute daily.
- Sample sequence language:
  - Email 1: "Hey, we should talk."
  - Email 2: "Hey, following up to make sure you're the right person. I'll give you a call tomorrow at two."
  - Then actually call tomorrow at two; if no answer → email follow-up → follow-up call.

### Email Best Practices (why sponsors ignore emails)
- **Keep emails SHORT — 2 to 4 sentences.** Structure: your property → who your audience is → why you see overlap with their target market/goals → ask for a **15-minute call.**
- **No attachments, no one-pagers, no proposals** in cold emails (higher spam-filter risk + signals "read this and pick what to buy").
- **Be specific:** the only goal of the email is to get a phone call or live meeting (discovery) — not to make the sale.
- The phone is the most important sales tool. "If emails not working, pick up the phone."

### Discovery Meetings
- Sell via the **discovery model** (consultative B2B sales), not hard sales. Decision-makers are senior and can't be tricked.
- **Bring nothing to the meeting** (Travis lesson): no pitch deck, no annual report, no PowerPoint. "How could I possibly make a sale if I don't know what they want?"
- Use a **discovery script** (ask the right questions in the right order).
- Key discovery questions:
  - "Who are you trying to reach? / Who's your target market/audience?"
  - "What have you done to connect with those folks?"
  - "How can we help you connect with our folks?"
  - "What action do we want that audience to take?"
  - "How will we measure success?"
- The **"What do you mean by that?"** technique (Tech Support video): after a seemingly obvious answer, ask "What do you mean by that?" to get people talking and digging deeper. Ask **at least 5 more questions** even once you think you know the answer.
- The "high value asset" discovery walk (car-dealer example): work *down* the sponsor's sales funnel by asking — "How do you measure success?" → "What do customers do right before they buy?" (test drive) → "What before that?" (arrive on the lot) → "How do they get to the lot?" (invite/marketing/email). The deeper down their funnel you can bring your audience, the more valuable the asset.

### Wins to Measure (Moneyball "base hits" — take the pressure off)
Stop expecting one-call closes; measure base hits in order:
1. ANY reply at all to a first-contact email.
2. The prospect shows up to the meeting.
3. During the meeting, they invite a proposal AND agree to a follow-up meeting on the spot.
4. Verbal yes + they explain next steps.
5. Next meeting booked to review/close.
6. Only then → "cross home plate" (close).
- "Just send me a proposal is usually a smokescreen" — if they ask, get really clear on what they want to see, then **book the follow-up review meeting on the spot.**

### Proposals
- Never send sales material/proposal **before the first meeting.** Custom proposals (via the hybrid/cookbook method) don't take long.
- Co-build proposals with the sponsor based on discovery.
- The proposal **describes a sale you've already agreed on** — it does not make the sale. "The sponsorship package does not make an appearance until after multiple meetings with your sponsors."

### Objection Handling (see also Scripts section)
- Treat soft objections as timing/logistics, not "no": "We don't have budget right now" = wrong timing → "When's a better time to follow up?" / "When do you go into budgeting mode?"
- Only "No thank you, I'm not interested in this opportunity" means remove from funnel. Everything else requires more questions.
- The "hallway of bombs" model (Greg Hickman, Alt Agency): each door hides a "bomb" (hidden objection) you open by asking the right questions; defuse it before you reveal price. The minute you share price, every undefused objection goes off ("let me think about it," "give me two days to move money," "I need to talk to…"). The **three bombs** in sponsorship: (1) no audience data, (2) no real activations / using tiers, (3) made-up pricing.

### Activation Generation
- Process: list everything **sponsors want** (profit → leads, customers, emails, move product; some branding) on one side; list everything **your audience wants** (fun, food, alcohol/drinks, networking, meet someone, learn, get a job, time with family) on the other. The **overlap in the middle is the activation.**
- Frame: "What if we help you get your product in front of our audience by creating a really amazing family experience for them?" (= activation) vs. "Give me 5K so I'll have 5K" (= not sponsorship).
- Test of a great activation: your audience should walk away saying "if the sponsor wasn't there, my experience would have been worse."

### Title / Naming Rights (increase value)
- Don't assume the wordmark/name is enough. Ask what they're trying to achieve with the title sponsorship, then "write them a prescription" for additional activations (live, contests, virtual, social/digital). Run those through your valuation calculator (don't give them away free) — they become part of the overall package and significantly raise title value.

### Renewal & Upgrade Tactics
- **Keeping a sponsor is worth two new sponsors**; it takes **10× more effort** to bring in a new sponsor than to keep one. Don't treat existing sponsors like an ATM/cash machine — "treat them like royalty."
- The renewal offer (placed at end of the feedback meeting), two options:
  1. A one-off package worth **~50% more** (more of what they liked + a few new things), OR
  2. The **same package at old pricing if they sign a minimum 3-year (multi-year) agreement.**
- Always **ask for a referral** in wrap-up/feedback meetings: "Is there anyone else in your network you think should be at our property / could benefit from being involved with us?" If yes → "Could I ask you for an introduction?" If they agree → **follow up within 30 minutes** of getting back to the office / ending the call. (Don't leave the intro to them.)
- Treat even longtime partners' renewal calls "almost as if it's a brand new discovery call" — strategies, objectives, and people change. (Juneteenth case: a longtime partner said they'd have walked away if the seller had said "rinse and repeat exactly what we did last year.")

### Using AI (5 do's / 3 don'ts)
**AI CAN help with:** (1) writing/testing emails, subject lines, hooks (goal = book a discovery call, NOT 2,000-word spam); (2) content (social/blog) — but verify Google & platform AI policies, which change often; (3) generating activation ideas (input audience + event type, ask for 10–1,000 ideas); (4) segmenting audience into personas/avatars (overcomes analysis paralysis — but vet it); (5) creating images/graphics (inject your own brand + real photos) and structuring proposals/fulfillment reports (use SC's free templates as the structure, not AI's default which produces gold/silver/bronze).
**AI CANNOT do:** (1) conduct a discovery session / brainstorm live with the head of marketing; (2) collect audience data / run focus groups (you're the audience expert); (3) negotiate or renegotiate deals, upgrade to multi-year, or hand over a fulfillment report.
- "Think of AI more as a lever which makes heavy things lighter than as the individual that's going to take this off your hands. It's a tool, not a replacement."
- Caveat: confirm your sponsor's AI policy (some have strict no-AI rules for content); violating it can damage their brand.

## 3. Scripts, Templates & Exact Language

### The "Double the Rights Fee" Question (the single best question to ask a sponsor)
Asked when presenting a fulfillment report after over-delivering. Frame it playfully first:
> "Hey sponsor, I've really enjoyed working with you and I love this relationship we've built. So I want to ask you a question — it's a bit of a bold question. Are you all right if I ask you something that may seem a bit cheeky or out of line?" *(wait for permission)*
> "What would we have to do as the rights holder / as the property to get you to agree to give us twice as much money next year — and for it to be the best marketing investment you've made in the last five years?"
- After they answer, list all the assets/outcomes, run through the valuation calculator to confirm they're not asking for something worth 10× the fee. Then: "Do you want to give it a try? Do you want to see if we can put together a proposal that achieves all these goals?"
- The close: charge **1.5× instead of the 2× they said they'd pay**, in exchange for a **2-year agreement**: "You told me this would be worth double — instead of $25,000 you said this'd be worth $50,000. I'm suggesting you only pay $X for the exact same assets and outcomes, but I want you to sign a two-year agreement."

### Renewal pitch (after feedback meeting)
> "Dear sponsor, this feedback is outstanding, I really appreciate this. If I fix all the things you said you didn't like and we double down on all the things you said you did like, would you be interested in seeing a draft proposal about how that would look?" *(then wait)*

### Objection responses (verbatim)
- **"We don't have budget right now"** → "When's a better time for me to follow up? I'd love to work with you. You tell me when to call you, I'll put it in my calendar." / "When do you go into budgeting mode so I can plan for this?" / (zero budget) "How do we plan to get into your budget process in future years?"
- **"I'm too busy / send me an email"** → "When would a better time to call be?" / "Is there someone on your team you think I should be working with instead, so I don't take up more of your time?" / "What should I be providing you in that email to help give you more information?"
- **"Just send me a proposal"** → "I really can't do that because I don't know what you're trying to achieve. We don't have stock proposals that we just send out hoping for cash — everything we do is custom. But I'd be happy to send you info about our audience, some activations we've done, some case studies. Just tell me what you'd like to see." / "What can I send you to help you determine whether or not we should have a discovery meeting?"
- **"Let me think about it"** → "Of course, take your time. These are important decisions, this is a big investment, this is a significant part of your marketing strategy — you should think about it. Is there anything I can provide to help you make that decision? Any questions for me? Would it help if I put you in touch with an existing partner of ours so you could hear how they're seeing value?" (Do NOT use it as a cue to "hammer them.")

### Declining to send info before a meeting (How to Lose a Meeting)
> "I really don't have anything to share with you until I get a better sense of what you're trying to achieve. Everything we do is custom and I don't want to make any assumptions that could be wrong. I'd much rather hear from you about what you're trying to achieve and what you look for in a good partnership — and if at that point it makes sense for me to share something, I will."
- (Alternative: ignore the request entirely and just have the meeting.)

### Respecting a "no" + asking for a referral
> "No problem at all, I appreciate you letting me know. And hey, while I have you — is there anyone in your network who you think would be interested in this audience?"

### Discovery audience question (segment selection)
> "My property is a conference/podcast with 375 event professionals — 30% are VPs and C-suiters, 50% are mid-level professionals, 20% are consultants. Which of these audience segments appeal to you most?"

### Charity case-study opener (works extremely well — guest Wayne)
> "I have an audience of high-net-worth families and individuals from lower Fairfield County, Connecticut, and your bank really came up nice in our survey. Is this an audience you're interested in?"
> "70% of our donors earn $150K or more. 24% of them would consider changing their financial institution over the next few months." → (sponsor) "Okay, let me connect you with our VP of marketing."
- Note: leads with audience + survey data, says **nothing about money yet.** Different question than "support our community" → prospects respond "Tell me more" / "Yes."

### "Lock the check in my desk" close (Travis/Trevor story, recurs)
When a prospect tests you ("We'll write you a check for $1M / slide $1M across the table — what's the first thing you'd do with it?"):
> "I'm going to take your check and lock it in my desk, and I'm going to call a meeting with you — because until I know exactly what you're trying to achieve and whether I can deliver, I haven't earned the right to cash the check. My job is not to make magic happen — we'll do that. My job is to make sure that at the end of a year together, you beg me to work with you again."
- Outcome (cause talk): the $1M test became a **$2.5M check**, doubling Travis's small org. (Seven Deadly Sins version: a $1M test → "I'd do nothing… I'd give you your check back unless I can deliver your marketing goals" → won the deal 3 days later because everyone else made assumptions.)

### Sales-material/proposal-first email opener to AVOID
> "Which level caught your eye — gold, silver, or bronze?" (Chris's own embarrassing line; the prospect "Dave" replied they hadn't read a sponsorship package in 10 years.)

### Phrase to remove from packages
> "Don't see something you like here? Give me a call and we can customize something just for you." / "If these levels don't line up with your goals, let us know and we'll customize." — **Remove this line.** A truly custom package doesn't need it. "Who you are speaks so loudly that I can't hear what you're saying" (Emerson) — a package full of tiers/pricing/assumptions tells the sponsor (by your actions) you don't actually want to customize.

### Recurring sign-off / mantra
> "The sponsorship proposal does not make the sale. You do." (Also extended: "and in fact neither does the fulfillment report — you do.")

## 4. Numbers & Benchmarks

- **Funnel:** 200 qualified contacts → 45 meetings → 15 proposals → 5 sponsors ($50K each = $250K). 4 contacts/week, ~1 meeting/week.
- **$4M/year goal** referenced as requiring a 200-contact year framing (the "Four Contact" funnel video frames 4 real outreaches/week as the unit; combine with valuation to scale).
- **Benchmarks:** 40% reply rate + 25% meeting rate = excellent. Below 4 contacts/week & 1 meeting/week = broken funnel.
- **Find Your Number example:** $100K ÷ $5K avg = 20 sponsors; 1-in-10 cold conversion → 200 outreaches.
- **Renewal economics:** keeping a sponsor = worth 2 new sponsors; new sponsor costs **10× more effort** than retaining one.
- **Renewal upgrade:** offer +50% package OR same price for 3+ year deal; close the "double" question at **1.5× for a 2-year deal.**
- **Best prospecting window:** **mid-September to end of November** (variously "September 15 to December 15"). Most companies budget for next year in this window regardless of fiscal year and have leftover/unassigned cash now. Chris assigns an arbitrary **5× value** to a discovery call in this window. You get the yes now, cash comes next year (Jan/Feb/Mar). Window closes ~4 weeks out, after which everyone says "I put it in the budget for next year."
- **Audience data depth:** **20–30 data points minimum** (25+ ideal; SC gets clients to **25–35**) on the macro audience, plus **≥3 micro audiences/avatars** (also 25 data points each per cookbook video).
- **Asset inventory:** build **100–150 individual assets** before going to market.
- **Logo value:** **a quarter of one penny (¼¢) per person who sees it.** SC's valuation firm assigns **zero** added value to a logo under "gold" vs "silver" vs "bronze."
- **Unsolicited package volume:** the recurring "Dave" sponsor receives **10,000 unsolicited sponsorship packages per month** (= ~500/business day) and has a **team of 3 who do nothing but say "no thank you."** Hasn't read a package in 10+ years. (Confirmed across multiple videos and the cause talk — same figure.)
- **Industry size figures (cause talk):** sponsorship ~**$2B+/year in Canada** (growing 13.5%, ~3× the N. American/international rate), **$22B/year North America**, **$60B/year globally**; cause sponsorship ≈ **$330M/year**. Logo placement/advertising ≈ **13% of total activation spend.** Average org spends **$828,000** to activate/leverage a sponsorship.
- **Test-drive activation ROI (cause talk):** ski-hill truck test-drive activation, **87 people test-drove** over **4 hours**, tracked **23 sales** (took 6 months to convert) = **$700K USD / $8.5M CAD in sales**; "1 in 10 test drives leads to a purchase." Sponsor offered to **double the rights fee** the next year; client countered with a multi-year agreement instead ("a license to print money").
- **$10M case study (2 years):** ~$10M in naming rights, major activations, campaigns, conferences. Every deal had: several well-defined audience segments; complex interactive experiences; clearly defined goals per audience; agreed-upon measures; third-party valuation proving ROI; **quarterly reporting** on benchmarks. **None** used a stock package — many used no package at all.
- **Fulfillment report ROI (Sally/iPad story):** report cost **$0** and **~20 minutes** to make (Word → PDF, phone photos) → salvaged a relationship → **5-year agreement, ~$150K–$200K** in sponsorship.
- **Two-marathons story:** Property A (charity) got free shoes + $0 cash; Property B got free shoes + **$50K/year** — same sponsor, same contact "Dave." Over **6 years**, Property A left **$300K** on the table because it didn't do a valuation.
- **Chris's own track record:** changed to the audience-research approach and raised **$3M+/year** in sponsorship (held up through the Great Recession).
- **Brave Enough to Fail gala (case study):** previously cleared ~$10K–$12K/event; after switching to the audience/discovery model, hit **$30K before the day of the event** (~tripled), best-ever year.
- **Opal's Walk (case study):** ~2,000–2,500 in-person walkers (DFW) + virtual; goal of branded walks in 10–15 cities this year, all 50 states by 2030; valuation cut by **2:1 or 3:1** to over-deliver; aiming to convert **$10K local "donations" into $25K–$50K sponsorships.**
- **Property timing rule:** "Think of your property/event as ending two weeks after it actually does" — the 2 weeks after wrap is the best time to lock next year's revenue. Deliver the fulfillment report within a week.

## 5. Named Concepts & Terminology

- **Property / Opportunity** — what you're selling sponsorship for (event, festival, podcast, gala, walk, conference, athlete, org). "The prize" (Juneteenth: "your property is a prize").
- **Asset** — an individual sellable thing (logo placement, e-blast, sample, speaking slot, naming a station, etc.). Build an inventory of 100–150.
- **Activation** — "the way you connect your audience and a sponsor in meaningful ways." Where the sponsor's needs and audience's needs overlap. "Sponsorship is nothing more than activation" / experiential marketing. The sole purpose of sponsorship. Sponsorship : logo placement :: making risotto : putting rice on it.
- **Fulfillment report** — a document listing everything you promised vs. delivered (quantities, quality, valuation before/after, predicted vs. actual media, social screenshots, web traffic, event photos, audience/participant feedback). Delivered within a week of wrap; basis for the feedback meeting. "Fulfillment reports save lives — or at least bring in lots of money."
- **Avatar / Persona / Niche (micro audience)** — a granular, well-defined segment within your broader audience (e.g., "40-year-old high-net-worth moms in the suburbs who drive a Lexus/BMW, 2+ kids, kids play hockey"). Have ≥3.
- **Macro audience** — total people connected to you (followers, attendees, donors, listeners, fans). Lowest value when undifferentiated.
- **Valuation** — determining what a sponsor would pay **on the free/open market** to reach the exact same audience without you (NOT what some similar event charged). SC sells a cloud-based **valuation calculator** (Accelerator) + a simpler **calculator template**.
- **Discovery (call/meeting/script)** — consultative B2B sales conversation to learn the sponsor's goals before proposing anything.
- **Sponsorship marketing funnel** — Awareness → Interest → Desire → Action, mapped to asset value:
  - **Awareness** = logo placement, mentions, logo-in-social → worth ¼¢/person.
  - **Interest** = sampling, trials, driving to a website (suspect).
  - **Desire** = test drive, get them on the lot/in the store, on-site brand interaction (prospect).
  - **Action** = actual purchase/sign-up (customer) → sponsors pay the most.
  - The deeper down the sponsor's funnel you bring your shared audience, the more valuable the asset. Best packages offer assets from *every* stage; avoid black-and-white "logo placement is dead" thinking.
- **Audience segments / the prism analogy** — raw audience numbers (sunlight) become valuable only when passed through "the lens of a sponsor" (prism) into segments. Hierarchy of value: **B2B audience (highest)** > VIP/wealthy > **general population/gen pop (lowest)**. "The riches are in the niches."
- **Title sponsor vs. Presenting sponsor** (Juneteenth): there can be **co-presenting sponsors** (more than one entity presents something), but only **one title sponsor.**
- **Sponsor as "Customer"** — reframe (Sponsors are Customers): stop saying "sponsor"/"partner"; think "customer." It exposes lazy thinking ("I want customers because I want money" is obviously absurd) and forces the question "What are our customers actually purchasing/expecting?"
- **Exploitable commercial potential** — IEG/Forbes definition of sponsorship: "cash paid for access to exploitable commercial potential." Sponsorship is a **verb, not a thing** ("no one goes to the sponsorship store and buys sponsorship units"); it lets you pull from advertising, sampling, experiential, content marketing, employee engagement to hit a goal.
- **BAMFAM** — *not present in this chunk* (no "book a meeting from a meeting" acronym used, though the behavior — "book a meeting from a meeting always to go over the draft proposal" — is taught).
- **Hybrid approach / Hybrid proposal** — SC's method: standardized + custom components combined.
- **Shoulder shrug method** — the wrong way to price: shrug, decide how much you need, then back-fill arbitrary tier values. ("Deadly sin #2.")
- **Spaghetti method** — tiered packages throw everything at the wall hoping something sticks; based on the false belief every sponsor wants a little of everything.

## 6. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

- **Tiered packages (gold/silver/bronze, by any name — platinum/ruby/diamond/major-medium-minor).** Sponsors don't see them as neutral — "they despise them." They force you to guess, focus on logos, signal you won't customize, waste months of committee debate, convert the most powerful marketing tool into the weakest (direct mail), and leave money on the table. "If you're getting sponsorship with a tiered package, it's *in spite of* your tiers, not because of them." **~95–99% of stock packages are thrown straight in the garbage.**
- **Emailing/eblasting unsolicited proposals to strangers.** Doesn't matter if you or AI wrote it; "the problem isn't who's writing the emails, it's that anyone's writing them at all." Scraping the 1,000 biggest companies + AI-blasting = "what everyone's been doing since 1970. It doesn't work."
- **Long emails (2,000–3,000 words).** Guaranteed deletion.
- **Sending attachments/one-pagers/proposals in cold emails.** Spam-filter risk + "read this and pick what to buy" message.
- **Trying to make the sale by email or on the first call.** Email's only job = book a discovery call.
- **Reaching out to `info@`/`contact@`/generic inboxes** instead of the named decision-maker.
- **Sending info/sales material before the first meeting** (How to Lose a Meeting — Dave sent "1.5M impressions" to a Fortune 500 that gets hundreds of millions/quarter and may not even want awareness → meeting cancelled). "Just because something is impressive to you doesn't mean it's impressive to the sponsor." Every premature share increases the risk something goes wrong.
- **Making up your value / pricing on your expenses** ("food & beverage is expensive this year, so it's $10K" → sponsor: "We don't care, that's your problem, deliver ROI"). Sponsors know you're guessing.
- **Treating sponsorship as philanthropy / fundraising** (the #1 nonprofit mistake; "deadly sin #1"). Your mission/cause does NOT bring in the prospect — your *audience* does, and your audience caring about your cause is what makes the sponsor want to connect through you.
- **Leading with mission/vision/impact, big-check ceremonies, program photos.** Confuses the message; sponsorship is a marketing decision.
- **Logos as the primary goal.** Most visible → mistaken as most important (especially by boards). Nobody studies your sponsor wall, thank-you page, or annual-report back page (except people trying to steal your sponsors). Find-the-Mitsubishi-logo exercise proves how little attention logos get.
- **Assuming a soft objection ("no budget," "too busy," "send me an email," "let me think about it") = "no."** The "cardinal sin of all sales": making assumptions on behalf of your prospect. (Chris sold an angry sponsor's renewal spot to a competitor because he misread "no budget right now" as "no.")
- **Being "pushy"/sleazy framing** — but also the opposite: don't let fear of being pushy make you abandon legitimate follow-up. If they haven't said "no thank you," keep them in the funnel.
- **"Any sponsor is better than no sponsor."** Rooted in lack of audience data. (Winter outdoor event with a sugary-soda title sponsor → health-conscious family audience complained → client got the brand to swap to an on-values product.) Working with brands your audience dislikes damages your brand.
- **Seeing a sponsor only as a cash machine.** They can also give referrals/intros, underwrite costs, open their network. (Client told "wrong geography" asked for a referral → intros → a deal worth tens of thousands.)
- **Solving the problem too fast (Tech Support analogy).** If you arrive at "I can help you" before the prospect feels heard, they reject the solution — like the "hard reset" trick that was really just plugging the computer in. Earn the conclusion with discovery.
- **Looking only at what worked, not what failed (survivorship bias — WWII plane/bullet-holes story).** "A small number of packages succeeded" only proves they work on *those* sponsors. To learn what to fix, examine the **rejection pile / recycling bin / bottom of the ocean** (the packages that got thrown out), not the few that converted. Sending more of the same to fix a 0% conversion rate is "hitting your thumb harder with a hammer."
- **Waiting until 1–2 months (or 2 weeks) before an event to seek sponsorship** — proof you secretly believe "sponsorship is easy." It's hard work.
- **Customizing-everything excuse / good reasons for not having data.** "A good reason for not having something is not a good reason" — the prospect doesn't care that you haven't surveyed yet; get the data (even after the fact, even if you must pay/MacGyver it).

## 7. Mindset & Principles

- **"The sponsorship proposal doesn't make the sale. You do."** (Recurring closer.)
- **"Sponsorship is a verb, not a thing companies buy."** It's the most powerful tool in a marketer's toolbox; lets you target a niche with laser precision, in exactly the way that audience wants, toward exactly the action the brand wants.
- **"The riches are in the niches."** / Minimum Viable Audience (Seth Godin): "When you seek to engage with everyone, you delight no one. Stake out the smallest market that can sustain you and delight them." You don't need 100,000 people — you need the *right* people. The biggest deals Chris has worked were in front of the **smallest, best-defined audiences.**
- **Sponsorship is marketing/sales, never philanthropy.** Audience cares about cause; brand cares about audience. If cause goes heavy on mission and brand goes heavy on audience, "you'll miss each other."
- **"It's hard to say no to passionate people"** (mentor Mena) — but only if you "speak the right language, and that's the language of marketing." Reframe from "beggar, cap in hand" to "I can help this company connect to their target market; what I have is of value."
- **Flip the script: you're the prize.** "You're not begging — you're giving them an opportunity to meet their needs by connecting them with your audience." Sponsors "need you."
- **"The cave you fear to enter holds the treasure you seek."** Cold calls are scary; do them anyway. (Wayne hung up on his first ~50 cold calls; it gets easier after ~20.) "The scariest thing was when I heard *yes*" — then "now what do I do?"
- **Be willing to be wrong / act before you're ready (Amelia Earhart "always think with your stick forward").** Stay in forward momentum; have conversations before you're "ready." Expect prospects to put your back against the wall asking for things you don't have — say "no problem" and create them after.
- **"What gets measured gets done."** Clearly defined goals ("as much money as humanly possible" is not a goal) — know your number, sponsors needed, proposals, calls, pipeline size.
- **Learn from failure / change inputs.** "A 0% conversion rate at scale is still a 0% conversion rate." If results are wrong, the activities are wrong — try something different.
- **Respect the "no thank you"** — it builds reputation and earns referrals/feedback.
- **Pursue sponsors of all sizes** — not one giant whale (rare + risky). Build a ladder/pipeline to move small sponsors up so you can back-fill a lost big one. ("If they've given you ten bucks, they're more likely to give you $10,000 than a cold prospect.")
- **Take sponsors at their word** — "think of your sponsors as grown adults who know what they want. If they don't want to work with you, they'll tell you."
- **The art of doing nothing** — earn the right to "cash the check" by understanding their goals first; refuse to spend a penny (or make assumptions) until you know what they want.
- **Sponsorship is hard work, but worth it.** "You're trying to get sponsors, not trying to do a thing that takes as little time as possible." Custom is more work — irrelevant.
- **"If a point is worth making, it's worth over-making."** (Chris's speaking style.)
- **Consultative, not trickery:** "Decision-makers in the sponsorship space are senior enough to not be tricked. There are no secret sales words." It's about presenting an opportunity and working with the prospect until you've built something they can't wait to buy.
- **Belief sustains you through the no's** (Juneteenth): "A no is just yes, not right now… the people want to say yes, they just don't know it yet — find the sweet spot." Mission belief keeps you cheerful through rejection even as the *sale* runs on marketing logic.

## 8. Case Studies (named)

### Brave Enough to Fail — guest Dwayne/"Wayne" (nonprofit, southwestern Connecticut)
- Small nonprofit (2 staff, 9-person board, a few volunteers; ~15 total). Founder is also a full-time middle-school history teacher. Properties: the **Person of Courage Gala** and the **BOSS Academy** school program.
- **Shift:** from "gold/platinum package, give us money for a great cause" → audience-first discovery ("We have an engaged audience of upwardly mobile families in southwestern CT — wouldn't you like to connect with them?"). The internal resistance was *himself* ("This is sales and I hate sales"); reframed via an SC course line — "think of it as: I have this wonderful thing and I want to know if you're the right fit for our people."
- **Bank win:** opened with audience + survey ("your bank came up nice in our survey… 70% of donors earn $150K+, 24% would consider switching banks") → VP said "Oh" → connected him to VP of Marketing.
- **Spa win:** call rescheduled into his class; recruited a student ("Bella") whose mom is on the board to describe the gala audience ("a lot of rich ladies who'd love a day at the spa") → owner bought one of the largest (signature) sponsorships.
- **Activations used (all from SC):** **sample opportunities** (spa gift bags with exfoliant on every table — "flew off the tables"); **valet sponsorship** (a bank sponsored the valet — idea from SC team member "Marie," who also pitched the unused "dessert/food course brought to you by" activation); **scholarship sponsorship** tied to mission — families could fund a **legacy/memorial scholarship** (honoring a WWII-veteran patriarch), which drew extended-family donations and required buying a table too → **largest profit center of the event**; now being productized on the website.
- **Result:** prior best ~$10K–$12K/event → **~$30K before event day** (tripled). Restructured into three revenue streams: individual donorships, grants, and sponsorships (now its own thing).
- **One thing to stop:** "Stop looking at sponsorship as fundraising. Start looking at it as a wonderful marketing opportunity." Now he "sees marketing opportunities everywhere" (parking lots, yacht-club net worth) — "now it's a game."

### Opal's Walk for Freedom / Unity Unlimited — guest Dion Sims (Juneteenth, Fort Worth TX → national)
- Property: **Opal's Walk for Freedom** (2.5-mile walk — representing the 2.5 years it took enforcement of emancipation to reach Texas). Founder's grandmother "Opal" (the "grandmother of Juneteenth," age 99) walked it to push for the federal holiday (signed 2021). Plan: branded simultaneous walks across time zones, 10–15 host cities this year, all 50 states by 2030; in-person ~2,000–2,500 (DFW) plus a virtual walk with a hashtag/social strategy.
- Team of 8, each owning a functional area (volunteers, logistics/route, programming, comms/marketing, faith-based activations, recording/history); steering committees per host city; biweekly alternating meetings.
- **Why SC:** strong locally ("big fish, small pool") but going national = "small fish, big ocean / Finding Nemo." Needed valuation + the language of marketing.
- **Light-bulb moments:** sponsors ≠ donors (different goals; their past "corporate sponsors" were really *donors* giving for the mission with no ROI expectation); "get our eyes off us and onto them"; **"you're the prize"**; they had activations all along (warm-up, pre-walk light snacks, post-walk celebration w/ alcohol, **kid zone**, **vendor row**, **VIP lounge** for dignitaries) but never **named/sold them as activations** = money left on the table.
- **Valuation:** as a software QA manager ("I break software before release"), Dion valued understanding the inputs/outputs "science behind the number." Felt "deflated" when told to cut valuation **2:1 or 3:1** to over-deliver — until learning the value **multiplies** across cities/activations.
- **Strategy ahead:** convert existing $10K local *donors* into larger *sponsors*; use the local contact as a **champion** for a **warm transfer** to the company's marketing arm in other markets (turning $10K into $25K–$50K). Concierge (done-for-you) over DIY because of a short runway.
- **Hardest part:** closing — "a no is just yes, not right now."

## 9. Industry-Specific Nuances

### Nonprofits / Cause / Charity (heaviest focus in this chunk)
- Biggest trap: conflating sponsorship with fundraising/philanthropy/donations. **Sponsors ≠ donors** — different goals. Donors give for the mission (no ROI expectation); sponsors are making a **marketing investment** and care about *audience*, not your cause per se.
- "Convincing the board I'm not just asking a company for a handout" is the hardest internal battle; "if I can connect them to their target audience, then budgets are infinite."
- Cause sponsorship is its own discipline, ~$330M/year; measured as marketing ROI, not social impact. **Not "philanthropy + logo."** Mixed messaging (ask via your *foundation* for impact, then demand marketing benefits) confuses sponsors. "The best sponsorship I've ever seen has no logos anywhere."
- Audience caring about the cause is the *bridge* — the brand wants the audience; the audience's affinity for your cause is what makes connecting through you valuable.
- Keep revenue streams separate: individual donorships, grants, sponsorships (Brave Enough to Fail did exactly this).

### Events / Festivals / Galas / Walks
- Name and sell every component as an **activation** with ownership: warm-up, pre/post programs, food/beverage stations (incl. "dessert course brought to you by"), **valet**, **kid zone**, **vendor row**, **VIP lounge**, scholarship/legacy naming. Sponsor "ownership" of a specific activation (water stop, beverage station, post-race party) lets you have many sponsors without each feeling diluted.
- "Think of your event as ending two weeks after it actually does" — lock renewals immediately with a fulfillment report.
- Branded multi-city/franchise model (Opal's Walk): consistent branding/flyers/look-and-feel ("a McDonald's is a McDonald's"); local sponsors on the back of the city-specific t-shirt; valuation **multiplies** across cities.

### Conferences / B2B Audiences
- **B2B is the highest-value audience segment** — people making business decisions with business budgets. "If you can find people making business purchases, you've got an opportunity / you're fishing with dynamite."
- Segment the room by seniority/role for discovery (VPs/C-suite vs. mid-level vs. consultants) and let the sponsor pick the segment.

### Sports / Athletes / Influencers
- Same rules: inventory of assets, valuation against open-market rates, audience niches, activations that improve the fan experience, fulfillment/ROI measurement. (Athlete/influencer named alongside events as a property type in the Bible.)
- Title/naming-rights: don't stop at the wordmark — prescribe additional activations (see Title section).

### Podcasts / Creators
- "And now a word from our sponsors" = the fast-forward-15-seconds moment → that's advertising, NOT sponsorship. Sponsorship must serve the audience too.

### Universities / Colleges / Museums / Associations
- Listed as valid property types (Bible). College example (BOSS Academy): a college can pay for a **branded activation** inside the program's video/content tied to the mission ("we are part of the path to your dream"). Match the contact to the offer — talk sponsorship with the sponsorship/marketing lead, employee-engagement opportunities with the head of HR.

### Seasonality (all industries)
- **Mid-Sept to end of Nov** is the prospecting magic window (next year's budgets being set; leftover/unassigned cash now). Even fiscal-April/June/August companies use January as the start of their marketing cycle. Discovery calls worth ~5× now; the yes comes now, cash next year.
