# Sponsorship Playbook — Merged Digest D

Consolidated, deduplicated knowledge on how to GET corporate sponsorships. Sources: blog chunks 09–11 + transcript chunks 03–04 (~169 articles/talks). Everything is about treating sponsorship as a **marketing discipline**, not philanthropy.

---

## 1. Core Philosophy & Mindset

**The central thesis (recurring across nearly every source):**
- **"Your audience is the greatest asset you can sell to a sponsor."** Companies don't want to sponsor *you* — they want to sponsor your *audience*. "If your assets are a crown, your audience data is the crown jewel" / "the heart of the program — once the heart is gone, your sponsorship program is lifeless."
- **"The sponsorship proposal does not make the sale. You do."** (The signature sign-off; appears in 20+ videos. Extended: "and neither does the fulfillment report — you do.")
- **Sponsorship is marketing, not philanthropy.** "A sponsor will never hand over cash out of the goodness of their hearts — that's a donation." If you want straight cash, take a loan, seek investors, or ask for donations. Sponsors buy marketing assets and expect marketing ROI.
- **"Sponsorship is a verb, not a noun / not a thing companies buy."** "Sponsors simply don't exist." Asking what "sponsors" want is like asking "what does running want?" — it's an activity to achieve a goal. A director of marketing doesn't *become* "a sponsor"; they're still measured on leads/meetings/sales. IEG/Forbes definition: "cash paid for access to exploitable commercial potential." "No one goes to the sponsorship store and buys sponsorship units."
- **"Nobody buys a product called 'sponsorship.' They buy an outcome."** Sell the *outcome* (customers, leads, sales, brand shift), not the *output* (logo, booth, gold package). "No sponsor ever woke up needing a booth or a gold package."
- **The win-win-win:** every party must win — sponsor (audience access), audience (great experience), property (better opportunity). Litmus test: "If your attendees don't walk away saying 'isn't it great the XYZ sponsor was involved,' then you are not practising sponsorship."
- **"The riches are in the niches"** (recurring). Smallest, best-defined audience wins. Seth Godin's Minimum Viable Audience: "When you seek to engage with everyone, you delight no one." Biggest deals Chris has worked were in front of the **smallest, best-defined audiences.** "More is not better. Better is better."
- **You are the prize / curator of audience.** Reframe from "beggar, cap in hand" to "I can help this company connect to their target market; what I have is of value." "Think of yourself as a curator of audience, not a bowl of logo soup." Businesses can't exist without acquiring customers, so an audience-holder has leverage.
- **Sponsors are customers, not ATMs / investors / donors.** "A partner you're invested in; a sponsor, to many, is a walking ATM." Treating a sponsor as an investor (they get no profit cut) = "essentially stealing their money." Reframe "sponsor" → "customer": it exposes lazy thinking ("I want customers because I want money" is absurd) and forces "what are they actually purchasing?"
- **Curiosity beats selling.** "Stop trying to sell anything — including sponsorship." Approaching with curiosity (not selling) makes you a better salesperson "because you're not trying to sell." The "perfume salesperson" analogy — push too early and the prospect gives you "a wide berth." Einstein: "The important thing is not to stop questioning."
- **Think like a marketer, sell like a (discovery-based) salesperson, fulfill like an account manager.** Sponsorship is genuinely hard — you're a salesperson + marketer + account exec + SDR + BDR at once. "If it feels like it's not working most of the time, that usually means you're doing it right."
- **Action vs. Motion** (James Clear, *Atomic Habits*): Motion = busywork that *feels* productive (building decks, debating page 37, perfecting the proposal, e-blasting cold, LinkedIn posting). Action = discovery calls, surveying audience, fulfillment reports, calling reps, booking follow-ups. "Motion feels like progress; action produces outcomes."
- **He who fails the most wins** (Seth Godin): "the person with the biggest discard pile wins." Lean INTO failure like leaning forward on skis. "Failure can only be a teacher if you let it be." Expect to be rejected dozens of times. Ira Glass "taste gap" — early work disappoints because your taste exceeds your skill; fight through it.
- **All prospects are liars** (riff on Seth Godin's *All Marketers Are Liars*): 50–70% who say "yes/send a proposal" don't mean it (conflict avoidance, cites *International Review of Law and Economics* 2019). "The worst answer is 'maybe'; next-worst is an insincere 'yes.'" Be skeptical until the contract is signed — "don't even be cautiously optimistic. Be skeptical."
- **Redefine the unit of progress as "contacts made," not "yeses."** "A no clears the runway." "A specific person telling you they're not a fit is the most useful info your program can receive." Distinction: "trying to grow sponsorship" (anxious) vs. "running a sponsorship program" (operational). "The fastest programs aren't the ones with the most yeses — they're the ones with the most nos in the shortest time."
- **It's all about relationships.** "Corporate giving is a relationship — get to know what people care about, find common ground." Build personal relationships like you would with major donors. Be friendly but not necessarily friends — a warm attitude can NEVER compensate for a weak program, and don't befriend at the expense of the program.
- **Simpler is better** (the "three-ingredient cake"): a great program needs only **Data, Discovery, Delivery** — don't over-engineer.
- **Get out and do it / dive in.** "There's no replacement for experience — get out of the office, make some asks, take a few rejections, score a few wins." "The water's fine — you have to dive in." Amelia Earhart: "always think with your stick forward." "The cave you fear to enter holds the treasure you seek."
- **Growth is parabolic, not linear.** $50K→$100K feels great; $100K→$120K feels like "only 20%" but is still +$20K. Talk in dollars; annual contracts/residuals compound.
- **Other adages:** "Assuming anything in sponsorship is a mistake." "You cannot overcome an objection you believe." "What gets measured gets done" ("as much money as humanly possible" is not a goal). "If a point is worth making, it's worth over-making." "Over-delivery equals renewals year after year." Decision-makers are senior and "can't be tricked — there are no secret sales words."

## 2. The End-to-End Process (overall sequence/funnel)

The canonical end-to-end sequence ("How to Get Sponsorship for Anything," recurring), with the 11-stage and 5-stage variants reconciled:

1. **Identify Properties / the Property** — define what you're offering (event/gala/expo/program/podcast/team). Don't skip it, but spend little time.
2. **Collect & Identify Audience Data** — get crystal clear on who your audience is (attendees/listeners/viewers/fans/social followers/donors/volunteers). This is "the heart and soul." Without it you can't pick prospects, build activations, or prove ROI. (Send the audience survey.)
3. **Segment the Audience** — niche down hard into 3–5 segments / ≥3 avatars (25+ data points each). "The riches are in the niches."
4. **Build Inventory of Assets** — brainstorm every relevant asset (50–250); keep only those you can realistically produce.
5. **Get Internal Buy-In** — sit with senior staff/board; the list shrinks here.
6. **Complete a Valuation** — price every asset at adjusted market value; sort most→least valuable, then trim. **Prospects fall out of the valuation as its last output** ("by the time you finish valuation, it's incredibly obvious who you should be talking to").
7. **Build Activations** — experiences at the overlap of audience-wants and sponsor-wants (often refined after discovery / after signing).
8. **Research & Build the Prospect Pipeline** — concentric-circle method; aim for ≥300 (some say 60–70/100/200; transcripts push 300–1,000+).
9. **Direct Outreach** — short 1–2 sentence email to a *specific named person*, asking for a 15-min discovery call (NOT a proposal).
10. **Discovery Meeting(s)** — book the discovery session; ask ~10 of the 37 discovery questions; gather info, don't sell; BAMFAM.
11. **Build a Custom Proposal/Package** — co-created with the prospect after discovery; presented live.
12. **Negotiate & Close** — sign paperwork; hire a lawyer; reread the contract.
13. **Fulfill the Deal** — deliver everything promised (plus a bit more).
14. **Fulfillment Report → Renewal** — prove delivery, then ask for the renewal in that same meeting.

**The TSC 5-step Direct-Outreach "system":** (1) Audience data → (2) Prospect selection → (3) Contact identification → (4) Direct outreach → (5) Discovery-led proposal. Direct outreach is the ONLY step requiring a specific human to respond (mostly "no"); discomfort there causes avoidance that stalls everything. "The properties that close deals run this sequence. The properties that don't almost always blame timing or the market. The sequence is the deciding difference."

**The Five Stages of Sponsorship Sales:** (1) Inventory Building & Valuation ("No assets, no sales") → (2) Prospecting (start from inventory) → (3) Getting the Meeting (bring nothing; only gather info + get the 2nd meeting) → (4) The Proposal (max 6 pages, always custom, never gold/silver/bronze) → (5) Activation, Fulfillment & Renewal.

**The 3 Ds (Three Components of a Winning Program):** **Data** (audience data + media footprint + your own marketing strategy) → **Discovery** (NOT a sales meeting) → **Delivery** (assets/activations + contract + fulfillment report).

**The Sponsors-on-Demand 3-system model:** *Leads every day* (audience → prospect research → CRM) → *Meetings every week* (7×7 outreach) → *Sponsors every month* (close via discovery + BAMFAM). Target: "3–5 cash sponsorships within 90 days."

## 3. Audience Research & Data

**Why it's first:** "Your audience determines the sponsors you work with." It feeds prospect lists, valuation of segments, activation design, and fulfillment reports. Audience is the exception in valuation — *the sponsor* sets its value, so be flexible / omit pricing on it.

**The Audience Survey Process:**
1. **Talk to current sponsors FIRST** to learn what audience data they need; add it to your next sponsor-meeting agenda ("tell them you're working to improve how you report").
2. Build the survey in sections (demographics, geographics, psychographics) — see §16 for the full question bank.
3. Distribute by email and/or physical mail; announce in advance. ("Old-school" physical survey cards stand out.)
4. **Incentivize:** giveaway entry, discount code, early-bird/free tickets, free parking, coupon, contest prize, "money can't buy" experience.
5. Wait ~2–3 weeks (some say a month for stragglers); send periodic reminders.
6. Tabulate → segment into 3–5 niches → niche down further (25+ data points/segment).
7. Use segments to build prospect lists, value segments, and feed fulfillment reports.

**Depth & cadence:**
- **≥20–35 data points per segment** (25+ ideal; SC pushes clients to 25–35; big-ticket ≥30). **≥3 avatars/personas** per property.
- **3–5 audience niches** to start; broad segments are the *starting point*, never the finish — extrapolate ≥25 data points down to boroughs/neighborhoods and precise job titles.
- **Survey response rate:** never 100%; **25% is enough** ("a quarter, still okay; half, excellent").
- **Re-survey every 6 months** (then maybe annually once known); influencers every 3 months. **Re-survey even when renewing** an existing sponsor — treat renewal as a brand-new opportunity.
- Use only *current* data — 5-year-old data ≠ today's audience.

**Psychographics = "the real meat":** lifestyle, interests, attitudes, opinions, values, personality, usage habits, motivations, brand loyalties, purchasing drivers — what makes them buy.

**Segments must be specific.** "Middle class," "families," "everyone," "general public," "millennials," "working people in their 40s" are "nebulous descriptors," not audiences ("everyone" = code for "I have no idea who my audience is"). Aim for endpoints like:
- "35-year-old female accountants in Thornton Park, Orlando making $124,000/year."
- "40-year-old high-net-worth moms in suburban Chicago, 2+ kids who play baseball, bank with TD but unsatisfied."
- "55-year-old partner-track corporate lawyers who make purchasing decisions, own two cars, looking to switch from BMW to Mercedes."

**The audience value hierarchy (prism analogy):** raw numbers (sunlight) become valuable only through "the lens of a sponsor" (prism). **B2B (highest) > VIP/wealthy > general population (lowest).** B2B is "fishing with dynamite" — 50 attendees controlling corporate budgets beat a room of high-net-worth individuals ("a rich person can only buy so many BMWs; someone controlling a $5M legal budget = thousands of Mercedes"). B2B/VIP audiences worth **200%–1000%+** more than "general public."

**Using data in conversation (make it concrete):** "20% of your audience earns $85,000–$110,000 and has attended five industry events in 12 months"; "54% of my audience uses your products"; "2000 Honda drivers, 24% looking to buy within 12 months"; "250 senior-level executives"; "70% of our donors earn $150K+, 24% would consider switching banks."

## 4. Properties & Inventory (Assets)

**Property** = your sponsorable entity (event, gala, marathon, conference, concert, festival, team, podcast, building, VIP boxes, each individual game, athlete, walk, school program). "A fancy word for sponsorship opportunity" / "the prize."

**Asset** = a tangible or intangible thing you sell that helps the sponsor connect with your audience — essentially borrowed marketing tactics (logos, signage, naming rights, speaking ops, social posts, sampling rights, e-blasts, booths, demos, VIP experiences, venue use, your audience itself). "Inventory = your ingredients."

**Inventory-building process:**
- Brainstorm with stakeholders → list EVERY possible asset. "You want as many assets in the early stages" — **build 100–150 individual assets** (some say 50, 100, 250) before going to market.
- Keep only assets you can realistically produce.
- A **logo is "about the weakest asset you can sell"** — "don't spend more than five minutes on logos."
- No emotional attachment — your favorite may be low-value; a sleeper may be worth most.

**Tangible vs. Intangible benefits:**
- **Tangible** ("quantity assets") — measurable: impressions, interactions, leads, products handed out, logo placement, booths, demos, speaking, live social feeds. Metrics: lead gen, ROI.
- **Intangible** — no marketplace proxy value; where naming rights, brand alignment, storytelling, cause marketing derive value. Value factors: audience knowledge, activation strength, fulfillment/reporting practices, marketing plan/budget, media partnerships, org capacity, org reputation, audience buying power.

**Asset value rises down the marketing funnel** (see §6 funnel mapping): awareness assets (logos, posts) are lowest; action/conversion assets are highest.

**The Prospecting Formula: A1 + I (A2 × V) = P** — your **A**udience + your **I**nventory (Assets × Value) → your **P**rospect "presents itself." When clients can't find prospects, send them back to asset-building. Examples: heart surgeons + (branding, speaking, education events, giveaways) = pharma, professional insurers, BMW/Lexus dealers; apprenticeship students + inventory = trade unions, post-secondary schools, tool manufacturers, safety/training, small-business lenders.

## 5. Valuation & Pricing

**Why it matters:** "The #1 biggest problem is incorrectly pricing your assets." Even a perfect package/proposal fails with wrong prices. "Not knowing your value = selling a $20,000 ring for $200." Knowing your value is YOUR job alone — "your sponsor doesn't know it, your competitors don't know it."

**The valuation process (recurring core method):**
1. Brainstorm → list EVERY possible asset (50–250).
2. **Research the market value** of each asset/activation — what a professional/agency would charge for the *same* outcome.
3. **Gut check:** is your service equal to / better than / worse than market? Price accordingly (e.g., "we're not as experienced as a marketing agency, so maybe don't charge $3,000 for social posts").
4. Discard/set aside low-value assets; keep ~a dozen strong ones.
5. **NEVER** value on your expenses, never make up numbers, never copy someone else's valuation found online.

**The valuation north-star question:** "What would a brand pay for this *exact same outcome* without me?" (open/free-market rate — NOT what a similar event charged).

**Advanced valuation analyses (to justify higher value):**
- **Brand Equity Analysis** — branded vs. unbranded sales difference (why people attend *your* festival).
- **Bang-for-Buck Analysis** — for multiple properties under one umbrella (racing teams/racers): spreadsheet annual fee, brand, reach, media, social → weighted average + pie chart.
- **Financial Analysis** — vertical, horizontal/base-year, technical, fundamental/intrinsic value.
- **ROI Analysis** — sponsor spend since starting vs. cash-flow advantage you provide (e.g., spent $10K on failed campaigns, pays you $20K, generates $40K → $20K profit → renews). Essential for renegotiation.
- **Stakeholder Behavioral Analysis (mapping)** — primary/secondary/key stakeholders; who influences the deal +/−. Interests reduce to: mental health, security, safety, physical health, environment, time, work, social change, economics.
- **Business Valuation Analysis** — customer-behavior changes, sponsorship costs, top-line revenue.

**Pricing rules & anchors:**
- Price every asset **one at a time** ("How do you value 1,000 assets? One asset at a time").
- Market value = baseline/litmus test; then decide if yours is worth more or less.
- Exceed market value when: very unique services, deep expertise, long record of happy clients, proven results.
- **Geography matters:** "A snowblower is worth a premium in Minnesota but all but worthless in California."
- **The audience is set by the sponsor** — be flexible / omit pricing on it.
- **Logo value ≈ a quarter of one penny (¼¢) per person who sees it** (recurring). SC's valuation firm assigns **zero** added value to a logo across gold/silver/bronze tiers.
- A coffee-station activation ≈ "several thousand dollars."
- **Custom-agreed value sets the price:** "If you only price your assets at $5,000, the sponsor will only pay $5,000" even if worth 2–3×. Custom-agreed $15,000 → they pay $15,000.
- **Impression-value math (sponsor's view):** value 1 impression at $0.10 → a sign seen by 500,000 = "$50,000" face value. But the sponsor's brand strategy demands **3:1 ROI**, so they'd pay ~**$16,600** ("$16K on a $50K sign is average job performance" — good sponsors push lower; find the brand that values it higher).
- **Cut valuation 2:1 to 3:1 to OVER-DELIVER** (recurring) — the value multiplies across activations/cities and drives renewals.
- **Don't nickel-and-dime:** if an activation extra is within **5–10%** of total package value, eat the cost.
- **Marketing budget benchmark:** most companies dedicate **≥10% of income to marketing** (up to **15%** in some industries). For every $1 a company might donate, ~$100 exists in marketing.
- **Show your math** — sponsors WILL ask "how did you come up with these numbers?" Have an overview ready (you can't say "I made them up").

## 6. Activation

**Definition (reconciled):** an **experiential marketing opportunity that fulfills one need of your audience AND one need of your sponsor simultaneously.** "Switching on" your sponsorship. Lives at the **overlap of (what your audience wants) and (what your sponsor wants).** "Sponsorship is nothing more than activation" — its sole purpose. *Sponsorship : logo placement :: making risotto : putting rice on it.* The antidote to gold/silver/bronze tiers. Also called **leverage**.

**Activation-generation process:**
- **Step 1:** identify an audience niche / target market.
- **Step 2:** ask your audience — what's positive about their experience? what didn't they enjoy? what do they want more/less of? why are they involved?
- **Step 3:** brainstorm ideas that improve audience experience based on that feedback, THEN develop the package.
- **The overlap method:** list everything sponsors want (leads, customers, emails, move product, some branding) on one side; everything your audience wants (fun, food, drinks, networking, learn, get a job, family time, warmth/A.C.) on the other. The **overlap = the activation.** Example: in February in Ottawa ("coldest capital city"), audience wants warming stations/fires/hot drinks while skating → give email to enter the warming activation.

**Activation secrets/rules:**
1. You MUST do a discovery session first (can't build a good activation blind).
2. You MUST know your audience deeply (25+ data points/segment).
3. **Budget isn't as important as you think** — "spend $50; if audience and sponsor are happy, it did its job." Don't blow your whole budget on one activation. The best activation isn't the biggest one.
4. Borrow/be inspired by activations you like — but ADD something new (never lift wholesale).
5. **Involve the sponsor in planning** — bring loose ideas, ask their opinions ("a sponsor understands their challenges best, so they'll have the best activation ideas"). Don't have activations "set in stone" — rigidity turns sponsors away.
6. Simple is OK — complexity correlates with cost; ask "does it really need to be this complex?" Don't tack on lasers/holograms.
7. **Photos and videos are a must** — capture footage *during* the event with the audience interacting (hire/station photographers). Proves enjoyment to a rep who didn't attend.
8. Track KPIs (attendance, email-list growth, social followers, leads, conversions, sales — before/after) → feed the fulfillment report.

**Test of a great activation:** your audience should walk away saying "if the sponsor wasn't there, my experience would have been worse."

**Cost of activation:** activation often COSTS money (ad design, product placement) — educate the sponsor and **budget activation costs into the price.** "Average org spends $828,000 to activate/leverage a sponsorship."

**The brainstorm-after-signing:** "Do This Immediately After Signing a New Sponsor" → run an activation brainstorm with them.

**Free stuff ≠ sponsorship:** getting free product is *sampling* — you should CHARGE a brand to give it away, not get it free (Chris's photobooth story: boss wanted a free $300 booth → Chris landed a $25,000 cash sponsor instead). Don't let your event be "ambushed" by sub-level free raffle giveaways (lowers your assets' tangible value).

## 7. Prospecting & Pipeline Building

**Golden rule: start with your AUDIENCE, not a list of big companies.** Prospecting is NOT pulling names from hats, filling out Fortune-500 forms, or targeting "people who have money." The two WRONG criteria are **name value** and **money** — "your audience doesn't know how much a sponsor paid you, and they don't care." "Coca-Cola at your health conference tells your audience not to bother attending." **Prospecting is an OUTPUT of valuation**, not a starting activity.

**The Concentric-Circle / funnel-of-warmth method (4 circles, warmest→coldest):**
1. **Hot (warmest):** brands your audience named directly in the survey (brand-loyalty/preference questions). Order most→least mentioned (typically 30–40 brands). "Served up on a silver platter."
2. **Warm:** companies that advertise/market TO your audience — read the papers / watch the shows your audience consumes for a week; note the advertisers (proof they pay to reach this audience).
3. **Cooler:** brands that *should* advertise to that audience but don't (audience likes Gibson guitars → add Fender, ESP, Rickenbacker, PRS).
4. **Coolest:** 2–5 direct competitors of every brand already on the list (this is how you reach ~100+). Research-heavy; cap the time spent.
- (Transcript variant adds: **endemic** brands aligned to your property type — but these are NOT the warmest since they already reach the niche; plus verticals/categories of every other audience interest; plus competitors *of your property* — see who sponsors them, for inspiration not poaching.)

**Survey brand categories to ask about:** travel, retail, insurance, automotive, telecom, credit card, food, financial services, entertainment — "covers pretty much every basis of consumerism."

**Pipeline targets (sources vary, reconciled):** blog posts say **60–70, up to 100+** (min "more than 10"); the Direct-Outreach post says **200 companies**; transcripts push **≥300** before outreach, with clients ending up at **1,000+** ("the hard part is *limiting* to 300, not finding them"). A long list is "protection" against ghosting/rejection/dropouts. "Bigger is better."

**A data-built list "tends to be shorter and stranger"** — includes off-category brands (luxury at a running event, financial services at a music festival, gaming at a nonprofit gala) because the audience data shows overlap. "The off-category brands are often the most interested."

**Vetting / the "sniff test"** (apply consistently): research website → most-active social platforms → press releases/news (last several years, older if needed). Check: what they sell, whether sponsorship is available, brand values, partner types, controversies/scandals, location (overseas = time-zone + travel costs). Cut mismatches and any sponsor with a *recent* controversy (past-and-resolved is okay). Eliminating on research cuts the list only slightly (5–10 prospects). Separate, don't delete — the opportunity may return.

**Niche/over-index angle:** a motorsport client over-indexed on female fans (15% of DB but far above norm) → pivoted to brands selling typical products to women → competitive advantage no competitor had.

**Always-Be-Opening / ambient prospecting:** watch the news for rebrands, new products, market entrants, new companies (all signal marketing dollars). Kevin's stories: got a Comcast/Xfinity sponsor via a rep met at a Best Buy table; got a law-firm sponsor by hearing their radio ad on a station targeting his audience ("if they're spending to reach my audience…") and offering to beat their ~$30k/month spend. "Hunt where the buffalo are."

**Document your prospecting process** (Google Doc / Word / voice-to-text) so you can repeat it next year.

**Omnichannel prospecting:** SEO (brands ranking for your audience's keywords), social search/groups, content marketing, email. Tools: CRM, survey builders, email automation, marketing software (segmentation), event-management software. Don't use digital tools to mask weak fundamentals.

## 8. Outreach (cold email/call, follow-up cadences)

**The only goal of first contact = book the discovery session.** Never lead with the proposal. Direct outreach is THE conversion event: a specific email to a *specific named person* (NOT info@/contact@/sponsorship@/help@) at a specific company, with a real reason, asking for a **15-minute discovery meeting.** "A perfect email to the wrong person is identical to a bad email to the right person — both produce silence."

**Contact identification:** find a specific human in marketing with budget authority. Right titles: Director of Marketing, Brand Partnerships Manager, Head of Sponsorships, VP Marketing (also sales, product, communications, business development, branding). Find via LinkedIn (company → People → filter for titles containing marketing/sponsorship/sales/communications/product/brand), the leadership page, or prospecting tools (Apollo.io, Hunter.io, plain Google). Free LinkedIn is enough — skip Sales Navigator. **Don't message via LinkedIn DM** (hidden folder, not reliably checked) — use it for research + first connection, then move to professional work email; the phone still works.

**Email best practices:**
- **Short: 2–4 sentences.** Structure: specific reason (shared audience) → useful offer → concrete ask (15-min call). "Come in hot" — make the first sentence count (mobile preview); skip "Hope this finds you well."
- **No attachments / one-pagers / proposals** in cold emails (cardinal sin — spam-filter risk + signals "read this and pick what to buy").
- **Be specific** about why you're emailing; **don't ask for money** ("reeks of Nigerian-prince scams"). Make it scannable in 30 seconds.
- **Don't mass-email / e-blast** (unprofessional; logistically broken — offer one slot and 25 people say yes, you can meet one).
- Match the sponsor's preferred channel (omnichannel).
- Use warm intros wherever possible ("the other person vouching for you"); mention them in the first contact. Don't play "seven degrees of separation" with flimsy connections.

**Subject-line formulas:** "Met you at ___, let's connect!" / "___ recommended I reach out." / "Had an idea for ___." Personalized subject lines ~22% higher open rate; 64% of recipients open based on subject line alone.

**Phone:** the touch most properties skip and the one that disproportionately moves replies. Warm the cold call with 2–3 emails first. Voicemail proves the property is real + request specific. "The phone is the most important sales tool — if emails aren't working, pick up the phone."

**Follow-up cadences (sources vary — all compress into ~7 days / 7 touches):**
- **Five Stages:** once/day, alternating phone & email, over 7 days, one contact at a time.
- **Festival explicit:** Day1 email, Day2 email, Day3 email, Day4 call, Day5 email, Day6 email, Day7 call = **5 emails + 2 calls.**
- **Direct-Outreach:** **7 touches/prospect across 2–3 weeks = 5 emails + 2 phone calls**, each email a different angle (audience-data / activation-idea / case-study / why-now / final check-in).
- **Ghost-You cadence:** Day1 email → Day3 email + call → Day5 email + call + voicemail → after 7 days (≈ a dozen touches), move on.
- **7×7 method (Sponsors on Demand):** per company, per department: Mon email → Tue email → Wed email → Thu call → Fri email → Mon email → Tue call (3 emails + 1 call + 2 emails + 1 call = 7). Then move to the NEXT department. 7 departments: **Sales, Sponsorship, Marketing, Brand, Communications, Product, HR/Recruiting** — ~2–3 named people each ≈ 21 contacts/company.
- **Standard transcript cadence:** email, email, (maybe 3rd email), phone call.
- **After 7 days / no response: move on** ("dormant — try again next year"), barring illness/long vacation.

**Don't pester:** no daily emails forever ("get blocked / restraining order"); no calls at odd hours (no 9pm Wed / 2pm Sat for a Mon–Fri business; event weekends excepted). Wait ≥1 week before a follow-up; one follow-up fine, a second after another week, then assume lost.

**Most sales close on the 5th–10th follow-up.** "If you won't follow up, you probably shouldn't do the original outreach."

**Wrong-contact line:** "apologies if I'm reaching out to the wrong person, but if you could advise me who I should speak to it would be most appreciated."

## 9. Discovery Meetings

**The single most important tactic — and "the one thing every sponsor wants that nobody is doing."** Sponsors openly say they trash unread packages and wish people would "just pick up the phone — I can tell you in 2 minutes whether there's a fit."

**It is NOT a sales meeting.** It's an informational/qualifying conversation. Walk in "with the mindset of an investigative journalist / detective." Goal: gather info you can't find on LinkedIn or in press releases (real challenges, what they've tried that failed, how they define brand awareness) + book the next meeting. Determine whether you can fix their problems with your solutions.

**Mechanics:**
- Can be in-office, Zoom, or phone. Informal.
- **Bring NOTHING** — "nothing but a smile and a warm handshake" (pen and paper OK). No deck, no annual report, no PowerPoint, no proposal, no pricing. "How could I make a sale if I don't know what they want?"
- **Ask ~10 questions max** (5–7 ideal; "never 40–50"). Ask questions NOT answerable by a Google search (don't ask what products they sell).
- **Peel the onion** — ask follow-ups on vague answers ("tell me more," "what do you mean by that?"). Ask **≥5 more questions** even once you think you know.
- Take notes. Don't show a deck, quote a price, or try to close. Leave sponsorship materials at the office.
- Let conversation flow naturally — don't interrogate (not a job interview); you own/set the agenda and respect their time.
- Use **parroting** — repeat their last few words to show listening.
- **Surface objections on purpose** ("we have no budget / not interested / only do branding"). If the meeting ends with "just send a proposal" and you haven't heard the objections, you'll be ghosted.
- **Strong icebreaker** — open-ended, anyone can answer ("seen a good movie recently?"); avoid yes/no questions. Keep it PC/PG (no innuendo, racism, sexism, religion, politics). But don't be *too* friendly (no trading pet photos at the expense of the program).
- **Memorize your discovery questions verbatim** → more natural conversation.
- If you discover you can't solve their problems → be upfront, thank them, move on.
- **BAMFAM** — always book the follow-up before leaving.

**Discovery question areas (the 37 discovery questions, categorized):** Target Market / Audience · Existing Audience · Marketing & Advertising (what they've done + results) · Business Goals (short & long term) · Success Measures · ROI. Plus **budget** (extract casually — see §16). The "discovery staircase": rapport → their audience → their marketing goals → their roadblocks → diagnose → prescribe → BAMFAM.

**Discovery is a sequence, not one meeting (the 4-call process, Dave Stewart):** Call 1 — Discovery (goals, audience, budget cycle, "what would have to be true") → Call 2 — Business case (how your audience + inventory line up) → Call 3 — Discovery-led proposal review (draft on screen, adjusted live, approved/rejected in the room) → Call 4 — Negotiation (adjustments for their CMO/CFO). Most skip calls 2 and 3, treat call 1 as the pitch, run out of conversation, default to "I'll send the proposal" → silence. "The slow process is the fast process." Booking the follow-up before leaving closes **~2× as often** as sending the proposal cold.

## 10. Proposals & Business Cases

**Never go "proposal first."** Write the proposal ONLY after discovery AND only when the prospect asks — and even then, present it **live** (call/Zoom/in person), never sent cold. Many deals close with **no proposal at all** (the $10M case used none; biotech six-figure deal skipped it). "The package describes a sale you've already agreed on — it does not make the sale."

**The proposal is an information tool** (esp. for sharing rich audience data), NOT a sales tool. It "proves you were listening at the discovery session" — "proving you listened in class when you sit down to do your homework."

**Hard rules:**
- **Max ~6 pages** ("just enough to pique interest"; yours may run slightly longer/shorter).
- **NO grids, NO pricing, NO gold/silver/bronze.**
- **The 50% Rule:** ≥50% of the proposal must be **audience data.** The other 50% = intro, assets, activations, contact info. (Some posts: dedicate 2–3 pages to niched-down audience groups; "3+ pages / at least a full page" in charts/graphs — a single paragraph is NOT enough.)
- **Intro = 3 sentences / ONE paragraph** on company/cause/mission ("write, rewrite, edit").
- Avoid the words **"sponsorship package"** AND "sponsorship opportunities" — may call it a **menu**, noting it's "always customizable per the sponsor's recommendations."
- Include **case studies** of past sponsor wins (growing database, leads, conversions, sales) with copy, data, photos.
- Restate the prospect's **objectives verbatim from discovery** at the top, then strategy/plan.
- Load with **CTAs**; put contact info front and center.

**Six-page proposal structure:** P1 Title (org/logo/event name; avoid "sponsorship package") → P2 Audience → P3 ¶1 org/history/causes (ONE paragraph) + ¶2 the event → P4 introduce the "menu" (avoid "packages"/"opportunities") → P5 list the packages (without that language) → P6 CTA + contact info.

**Festival proposal structure (variant):** Title page → Your Opportunity (≤2 paragraphs: org, then festival + lead-in to audience) → **Audience Data** (charts/tables, appealing colors, crowd photos — spend the MOST time here) → Activation Ideas (yours + the prospect's from discovery) → Media Exposure (relevant press only; no negative; don't overload) → Case Studies & Testimonials (attendance, harmony with past sponsors, raw numbers, booth visits) → Contact + CTA. **Explicitly NO grids, NO pricing.**

**Business case** (use when a prospect insists "just send me a proposal" before meeting): audience-only, **no pricing** — designed to get them excited to talk but not enough to decide without you. For podcasts/influencers, a media-kit-style business case (total audience, web traffic, social, impressions, interviews, DB size, open rate → 3 avatars → case studies/activation ideas → strong CTA, no pricing).

**Three scenarios that DO demand a proposal:** when a prospect, your boss, or a volunteer/board demands one — you can build it, but strip sales language + prices and load it with CTAs.

**The hybrid proposal / cookbook method:** some parts standardized (history, audience data), some fully custom (activations from your pre-priced bank). After discovery, mix-and-match ~5 specific "recipes" (activations/assets/segments) that get the sponsor their outcome. "Amateurs have a recipe; masters have a cookbook." Build proposals WITH your sponsors.

**Ana's tactic:** record discovery calls (with permission), mirror the sponsor's exact language in the cover letter + proposal → "this is exactly what we need" → six-figure deal in 2 weeks.

## 11. Negotiation & Closing

- **You cannot close on the first meeting — and that shouldn't be the goal.** Don't go in trying to hit a sales quota. Expect several meetings/calls (the sponsor must review budget + consult internal stakeholders). **Wait for the verbal yes** before celebrating.
- **Move conversations off email/phone into meetings** sooner than later.
- **BAMFAM every time** — never leave a meeting without the next one booked.
- **Negotiate asset pricing LIVE, when the time comes** — never via a price list.
- **Use a RANGE, not a single dollar figure**, walking into negotiation. Once negotiated (especially once in a binding contract), dollar amounts shouldn't change. Changing pricing after presenting without telling the sponsor = "a huge bait and switch."
- **Be realistic; don't highball.** "Playing hardball with the sponsor often results in playing the game by yourself." Be flexible. Back out if continually lowballed ("don't get taken for a ride"). DON'T promise what you can't deliver.
- **Multi-year ask:** simply ask "Do you want to do this for three years or five?" — that tells you what to put in the proposal. Insist on **≥5 years for name/word-mark (title) deals.**
- **First-time/new property:** offer a **20–40% discount** framed as "I want to share the burden/risk with you," and lock multi-year at that price in year one.
- **"I can get this cheaper elsewhere"** → "Great — now what would they have to do for it to be worth twice as much? What if I just did those things for you?" (they negotiate down; you add value).
- **The sales-rep "advocate" framework** (when you can't get internal buy-in): ask the prospect (Gary) for must-haves, nice-to-haves, and an honest best price (explicitly NOT a tactic). Then: "Don't think of me as the salesperson — think of me as your advocate / an extension of your team. I'll negotiate on your behalf." Present internally as the sponsor's "final take-it-or-leave-it offer." (Sold ~$1.5M this way.)
- **The "lock the check in my desk" close:** when tested ("we'll write you a $1M check — what's the first thing you'd do?") → "I'll lock it in my desk and call a meeting. Until I know exactly what you're trying to achieve and whether I can deliver, I haven't earned the right to cash the check. My job is to make sure that at the end of a year together you beg me to work with you again." (Turned a $1M test into **$2.5M**.)
- Engage a **lawyer**; reread the contract.

**Objection handling** — treat soft objections as timing/logistics, NOT "no." Only "No thank you, I'm not interested" removes a prospect from the funnel; **everything else requires more questions.** The "hallway of bombs" model (Greg Hickman): each door hides a hidden objection you open by asking the right questions; defuse it BEFORE you reveal price (the minute you share price, every undefused objection detonates — "let me think about it," "I need to talk to so-and-so"). The **three bombs in sponsorship:** (1) no audience data, (2) no real activations / using tiers, (3) made-up pricing. (Verbatim objection responses in §16.)

## 12. Fulfillment & Reporting

**Fulfillment / wrap-up report** = a document listing everything you promised vs. delivered (quantities, quality, valuation before/after showing ROI, predicted vs. actual media, social screenshots, web traffic, event photos, audience/participant feedback). One page can = a chart of every asset + delivered (yes / over-delivered / no + why). "Fulfillment reports save lives — or at least bring in lots of money." It's the renewal tool + legal cover against breach-of-contract.

**Mechanics:**
- **Deliver within 24 hours to 1 week** of the event (pre-build it). For title/multiyear deals: **quarterly preferred** (at minimum annually/semi-annually) — don't wait until after for big agreements.
- Include photos, attendance data, ticket-sales data, ROI metrics, charts/graphs.
- **Write the report even if you don't want to renew** ("like showing your work").
- It can cost **$0 and ~20 minutes** (Word → PDF + phone photos).

**Activation/fulfillment phases (sports):** Sponsor Onboarding → Clarify Agreements (checklist) → Confirm Execution (sign-offs, on-site reps, photos, screen captures, social proof).

**Delivery discipline:** deliver everything legally promised (plus a bit more). Over-delivery = renewals. Overpromising/underdelivering jeopardizes the long-term relationship (you get one check, nothing more) and risks breach of contract.

**"Sally / iPad" lesson:** a fulfillment report (photos of the rep ignoring 1,000+ attendees + Analytics showing 5,000 hit the thank-you page + a media write-up) flipped a "the event was dead" complaint into a **5-year, ~$150K–$200K** renewal. Reframes the problem and proves value the rep didn't capture.

## 13. Renewals & Upgrades

**Economics:** keeping a sponsor is worth **two new sponsors**; a new sponsor costs **10× more effort** than retaining one. "Treat them like royalty," not an ATM.

**The best window = right after you deliver** ("think of your property as ending two weeks after it actually does" — the 2 weeks after wrap is the best time to lock next year's revenue). Ask while they're "still excited." Don't wait 9–11 months and send a package — that's an *anti*-renewal strategy.

**The 3-step renewal process:**
1. Build & deliver the **fulfillment report** within a week of wrapping.
2. Book a **FEEDBACK meeting** (not a pitch) — walk the report live, hear "everything you loved, everything you hated, everything you wish we'd done differently," solve problems on the spot.
3. **At the end of the feedback meeting**, if you've earned the right, place your offer.

**The renewal offer (two options):**
1. A one-off package worth **~50% more** (more of what they liked + a few new things), OR
2. The **same package at old pricing in exchange for a minimum 3-year (multi-year) agreement.**

**The "double the rights fee" question** (the single best question, asked after over-delivering): frame playfully + ask permission, then — *"What would we have to do to get you to agree to give us twice as much money next year, and for it to be the best marketing investment you've made in the last five years?"* After they answer, run the list through your valuation calculator, then **close at 1.5× (not the 2× they offered) in exchange for a 2-year agreement.** Small financial hit, but more than last year + a locked second year.

**Treat every renewal as a brand-new discovery call** — re-survey, re-discover, re-value, create new assets. "Strategies, objectives, and people change." (Juneteenth case: a longtime partner said they'd have walked away if told "rinse and repeat exactly what we did last year.")

**Always ask for a referral** in wrap-up/feedback meetings: "Is there anyone else in your network who should be at our property?" → "Could I ask you for an introduction?" → if yes, **follow up within 30 minutes** of getting back / ending the call (don't leave the intro to them).

**Renew 80% of sponsors within 30 days** of the event (recurring; Stonyfield conference: 80% within 24 hours, 46% bought next-year tickets before the agenda was released, 87% said it exceeded expectations).

**Multi-year structure:** must be IN the contract (never assume). **Start at 3 years** (some say 3–5; "10 is best"; arena deals 20+). Negotiate money year-by-year (rate rises if value rises; plateau = flat/slight increase) — or lock the same amount for the term.

## 14. Scaling, Team & Operations

**The six-part lifecycle framework** (same framework lands a first sponsor or renews a multi-million-dollar one): **(1) Clarify** (property, audience, activations) → **(2) Differentiate** (valuation, pricing, defending price) → **(3) Launch** (prospecting, outreach, discovery calls) → **(4) Amplify** (media/promo partners, social + video assets) → **(5) Renewal & Upgrade** (fulfillment reports, multi-year structures, sponsor summits) → **(6) Scale** ("the four key roles of a scalable sponsorship team," hiring).

**Sales pipeline stages + weekly goals:** Cold prospects → Cold calls → Discovery meetings → Proposals → Deals closed. Set per-stage weekly goals (cold calls/week, discovery sessions/week, proposals/week); feed more prospects as the pipeline narrows.

**Moves-Management System (simple pipeline in Excel — a diagnostic tool):** track each prospect through Contact made/request referral → 2nd email → 3rd email → phone call → response received → meeting booked → proposal sent → Yes/No, logging the date + the *step they declined at*. Columns: company, contact, email, LinkedIn URL, last-contact date, notes. Calculate % who respond / book / get a proposal / say yes. If everyone leapfrogs from "sent email" to "stop bothering me," your emails are driving people away. **"Simplicity scales; complexity does not"** — keep it simple so it works at 6/7/8 figures.

**A working week (Direct-Outreach):** 4–8 new outreaches/week; 3–5 active prospect conversations; 2–4 discovery meetings/week; 1–2 discovery-led proposals/month → that property **closes ~5 sponsors/year.** (Recommended weekly rhythm: 4 named decision-makers/week, 4 two-sentence emails Tuesday, 4 follow-up calls Thursday; hold the line a full month; measure in **contacts made, not yeses.**)

**Sponsor summit:** an annual event to thank sponsors, network, and collect improvement ideas (private rep time for top sponsors).

**Brokers — math overwhelmingly favors building internal capacity:**
- Brokers charge **$3k–$5k/month retainer, 6-month minimum (~$18k) PLUS ~20% commission** (lower retainer ↔ higher commission; **no-retainer doesn't exist** — anyone offering "commission-only 1–2%" lacks the experience to know it's nonsensical → run). Commissions are **EVERGREEN** (apply year over year, even after you part ways, even on sponsors you grow yourself).
- On a **$500k** opportunity: ~**$118k** year-one fees (≈24% effective yr1, then 20% — ~80% max margin). DIY (~$10k one-time to build internal capacity) = **~98% margin yr1, ~100% yr2** (~2% effective).
- Brokers won't take you unless the opportunity is **≥$500k** AND they're ~100% sure of success — "the very fact a broker will work with you proves you'd have succeeded anyway." They do ~10% of the workload; you do ~90%. They don't bring magic contacts (CMOs don't await broker calls; if they "have contacts," they got them from past clients — and you're the future past client).

**Using AI (5 do's / 3 don'ts):**
- **CAN:** write/test emails, subject lines, hooks (goal = book a call, not 2,000-word spam); draft content (social/blog — verify platform AI policies); generate activation ideas (10–1,000); segment audience into personas/avatars; create images/graphics (inject your own brand + real photos) and structure proposals/fulfillment reports (use SC's free templates as the structure — AI's default produces gold/silver/bronze).
- **CANNOT:** conduct a live discovery session / brainstorm with the head of marketing; collect audience data / run focus groups; negotiate/renegotiate, upgrade to multi-year, or hand over a fulfillment report.
- "AI is a lever that makes heavy things lighter, not the individual who takes this off your hands." "AI lies — triple-check." Confirm the sponsor's AI policy (some forbid AI content).

## 15. Funnel Math & Benchmarks (ALL numbers/metrics/$ figures/rates/timeframes)

**The canonical conversion funnel (recurs across many sources):**
- **200 qualified contacts → 45 discovery meetings → 15 proposals → 5 sponsors @ $50,000 = $250,000** (also framed as $4M/year at scale).
- 200 contacts/year = **4 real named-person contacts/week**; 45 meetings/year ≈ **1 meeting/week.** (The test: <4 qualified named-person contacts this week = you didn't do sponsorship.)
- Assumes you've done a valuation supporting a ~$250K opportunity. Numbers shift with fundamentals; the *process* doesn't change.

**Reply rate vs. Meeting rate:**
- **Reply rate** = every response (incl. "not the right person," "send more info," "not now," "no thanks") — just a conversation.
- **Meeting rate** = qualified contacts who agree to a 15-min call — **the funnel runs on this.**
- **Benchmark: 40% reply rate + 25% meeting rate = excellent.** Below 4 contacts/week & 1 meeting/week = broken funnel.
- Diagnostic: pick 4 prospects, find named decision-maker, send a 2-sentence email asking for 15 min, schedule a follow-up call for non-responders 5 business days later. **Meeting rate ≥ 1 in 4 = working; below 1 in 20 = list/email/contact is wrong.**

**Close / conversion rates:**
- **Proposal-submission rate should be 30–50% of prospects you speak to** (Chris's ~40%) → implies **~60% (range 50–70%) who say "send a proposal" are lying.** Chris has "never" heard a flat "no thank you."
- **Close rate on proposals submitted: 30–50%** ("standard"; Chris ~40–50%).
- **Discovery → sale: 1 in 4** (Sponsors on Demand) → to get 10 sponsors, ~40 meetings. Brendan (endurance, after qualifying budget): **~50%.**
- **"Find Your Number":** Goal $100K ÷ avg $5K = 20 sponsors; 1-in-10 cold conversion → 200 outreaches → defines daily cadence.

## 16. Scripts & Templates (verbatim)

**Cold email — the "Hey Dave" go-to ("works ~10% of the time"):**
> "Hi Dave, I noticed on LinkedIn that you are involved in the sponsorship program at Company X, focused on high net worth moms who live in the suburbs. We did some research and found that around 30% of our attendees match this demographic and thought you would be interested in a conversation. Are you around on Tuesday at 3:00 for a 15-minute discovery call? Best, Chris"

**Cold email — Direct-Outreach version (2–4 sentences):**
> "I am reaching out because we just finished an audience survey for our event, and a meaningful percentage of our attendees identified your category as a top consideration this year. We have audience data your team would probably find useful regardless of whether sponsorship turns out to be a fit. Open to a fifteen-minute call this Tuesday or Wednesday?"

**Cold email — sequence language:** Email 1: "Hey, we should talk." Email 2: "Hey, following up to make sure you're the right person. I'll give you a call tomorrow at two." (then actually call).

**Subject lines:** "Met you at ___, let's connect!" / "___ recommended I reach out." / "Had an idea for ___."

**Wrong-contact line:** "apologies if I am reaching out to the wrong person, but if you could advise me who I should speak to it would be most appreciated."

**Cold-call / "Do the Opposite" line:**
> "I have 40-year-old high-net-worth moms who live in the suburbs, have two or more kids, drive a Lexus, and their kids play soccer or hockey. Does that line up with your target market?"

**Strong CTA (replaces "send a cheque" / "check a box"):**
> "If you want to get in front of 40 year old, high net worth moms, with 3+ kids, who plan to buy a new house in the next 18 months, let's talk. Everything we do is custom, so you only pay for the benefits you want. Give us a call, let's find something that fits your budget and your goals together. It will only take five minutes, guaranteed."

**LinkedIn-research / motorsport flow (verbatim shape):**
> "I just use the free version of LinkedIn… search for people who work for that company with the word marketing or sponsorship or sales or communications or product directly in their job title… connect… introduce myself and ask if they want to have a meeting."

**BAMFAM scripts:**
> "Absolutely, I'll put together something just for you. Are you free Tuesday at 3:00 so we can go over the proposal together?"
> "Sure, let me put something together — and just so I know, what budget range should I stay within?" … "Great, what day next week would you like to meet to go over the proposal?"

**Discovery openers (Brendan):** "Tell me about it." / "Tell me about your goals." / "Tell me about your brand." → then parrot the last few words.

**Discovery starter questions:** "Who is your target audience?" · "How do you normally engage in sponsorship?" · "What does your target market value?" · "What can you tell me about your sales goals for the coming year?" · "What would you consider the most important elements of a sponsorship package?" · "What have you done to connect with those folks?" · "How can we help you connect with our folks?" · "What action do we want that audience to take?" · "How will we measure success?"

**The 9 discovery questions to ask before any proposal:** (1) Who is your target audience? (2) How do you typically connect with them? (3) What tactics best help you engage your target market? (4) What sales activities produce the best results? (5) What are the least valuable assets in your opinion? (6) How do you normally engage in sponsorship? (7) What does your target market value? (8) What about your sales goals for the coming year? (9) What are the most important elements of a sponsorship package?

**The one discovery question to always ask:**
> "Our audience is X, Y, Z. Is this an audience segment you typically try to engage?" (If not, you aren't a match — and that's okay.)

**Audience-segment selection question:**
> "My property is a conference with 375 event professionals. Of that group, 30 percent are VPs and C-suite, 50 percent are mid-level professionals, and 20 percent are consultants. Which of these audience segments appeals to you the most?"

**Curiosity/elaboration techniques:** "What do you mean by that?" (ask repeatedly to get 100% clarity — e.g., on "brand awareness") · "Why do you think that is?" · "What have you tried that has worked?" · "What assumptions are you making about your sales goals?"

**"High value asset" funnel walk (car dealer):** "How do you measure success?" → "What do customers do right before they buy?" (test drive) → "What before that?" (arrive on the lot) → "How do they get to the lot?" (invite/email). The deeper down their funnel you bring your audience, the more valuable the asset.

**Budget-extraction script (never ask "what's your budget?"):**
> "If I could come up with the ideal package for you and it was $250,000, would you consider it?" → "No." → "OK, obviously that amount isn't right — but how wrong is it? Is it wrong by $10,000 or by $200,000?"
Brendan variant: "We have people spending a couple thousand to people in six figures — what would more accurately reflect your budget?"

**"Your consumer is everyone" rebuttal:** say it back passively — "Your consumer is everyone?" (they'll narrow it). Sarcastic version: "When was the last time you sold a product to a four-year-old?"

**"Lock the check in my desk" close:**
> "I'm going to take your check and lock it in my desk, and I'm going to call a meeting with you. Until I know exactly what you're trying to achieve and whether I can deliver, I haven't earned the right to cash the check. My job is to make sure that at the end of a year together you beg me to work with you again."

**"Sponsorship is a bad word" challenge** (when a prospect says "sponsorship"/"partnership," react as if hearing it for the first time):
> "What do you mean by sponsorship?"
Then for 10 meetings in 10 business days, don't sell — just ask: "How does your business work? What are you trying to achieve? How do you achieve those goals? How can I help? What can I do to make your job easier?"

**Reframing "we don't do sponsorship" (Joan's script):**
> "What if we could put you in front of a room of 30 of your ideal buyers, all of whom are in a position to make a purchase within the next 18 months? And what if you were the only one in the room from your industry? And rather than sponsoring the event, you simply paid for the whole thing and gave people a chance to network in a casual, low pressure environment where they could relax and you could get to know them more directly?"
> Result: "Oh, well THAT we have budget for! I have a business development budget of $75K I could tap into."
> When a sponsor says "we don't do sponsorship," reply: **"neither do we."**

**Audience-data objection-handler (segment appeal):**
> "My property is a conference with 375 event professionals — 30% VPs/C-suite, 50% mid-level, 20% consultants. Which of these segments appeals to you most?"

**Charity case-study opener (works extremely well):**
> "I have an audience of high-net-worth families and individuals from lower Fairfield County, Connecticut, and your bank really came up nice in our survey. Is this an audience you're interested in?"
> "70% of our donors earn $150K or more. 24% of them would consider changing their financial institution over the next few months." → (sponsor) "Okay, let me connect you with our VP of marketing."

**Endurance intake-form data hook:**
> "83% of our attendees said they would change a purchase based upon sponsorship of one of our events. So get ready to make an impact."

**The "double the rights fee" question (full):**
> "Hey sponsor, I've really enjoyed working with you and I love this relationship we've built. So I want to ask you a question — it's a bit of a bold question. Are you all right if I ask you something that may seem a bit cheeky or out of line?" *(wait for permission)*
> "What would we have to do as the rights holder to get you to agree to give us twice as much money next year — and for it to be the best marketing investment you've made in the last five years?"
> Close: "You told me this would be worth double — instead of $25,000 you said $50,000. I'm suggesting you only pay $X for the exact same assets and outcomes, but I want you to sign a two-year agreement."

Softer alternative: "I don't want to suggest you should give us more money or not. I just want to know, what would it take for us to be your preferred partner, to double our engagement, and for it to be the best thing you've done in five years?"

**Renewal pitch (after feedback meeting):**
> "Dear sponsor, this feedback is outstanding, I really appreciate this. If I fix all the things you said you didn't like and we double down on all the things you said you did like, would you be interested in seeing a draft proposal about how that would look?"
> Also: "If we make all the changes you just said, do you want to work together again? Yes or no." (Maybe is not acceptable.)

**Objection responses (verbatim):**
- **"We don't have budget right now"** → "When's a better time for me to follow up? You tell me when to call you, I'll put it in my calendar." / "When do you go into budgeting mode so I can plan for this?" / (zero budget) "How do we plan to get into your budget process in future years?"
- **"I'm too busy / send me an email"** → "When would a better time to call be?" / "Is there someone on your team I should be working with instead?" / "What should I be providing you in that email to help give you more information?"
- **"Just send me a proposal"** → "I really can't do that because I don't know what you're trying to achieve. We don't have stock proposals we just send out hoping for cash — everything we do is custom. But I'd be happy to send you info about our audience, some activations we've done, some case studies. Just tell me what you'd like to see." / "What can I send you to help you determine whether we should have a discovery meeting?"
- **"Let me think about it"** → "Of course, take your time — this is a big investment and a significant part of your marketing strategy. Is there anything I can provide to help you decide? Would it help if I put you in touch with an existing partner so you could hear how they're seeing value?" (Do NOT use it as a cue to hammer them.)

**Declining to send info before a meeting:**
> "I really don't have anything to share with you until I get a better sense of what you're trying to achieve. Everything we do is custom and I don't want to make any assumptions that could be wrong. I'd much rather hear from you about what you're trying to achieve and what you look for in a good partnership — and if at that point it makes sense to share something, I will."

**Respect a "no" + ask for a referral:**
> "No problem at all, I appreciate you letting me know. And hey, while I have you — is there anyone in your network who you think would be interested in this audience?"
> "Who should I be talking to in your network that you think would be interested?"

**Bank rebuttal on logo placement (when a prospect doesn't need awareness):**
> "Do you think there's anyone left in this country who hasn't heard of our bank? We don't need you to build our brand. What we need is for your audience to take one action and one action only: to bank with us." / "We spent $50 million last year on media — we don't need your help to get our logo in front of a couple thousand people."

**Gold/silver/bronze self-deprecating joke (used to make a point):**
> "Before I show up — can you tell me, was it gold, silver, or bronze that caught your eye?" (Prospect: hadn't read a sponsorship package in 10 years.)

**Phrase to REMOVE from packages:**
> "Don't see something you like here? Give me a call and we can customize something just for you." — Remove it. A truly custom package doesn't need it. (Emerson: "Who you are speaks so loudly I can't hear what you're saying.")

**Influencer pitch template:**

> "Hey, I found X asset that I think could really help with Y problem. What do you think of that?"

- **Geographic & Demographic:** Age; Gender; Annual pre-tax income; Number of children; Highest education completed; Employment sector.
- **Motivations & Opinions:** What motivates you to participate? Why did you choose to attend? Favorite parts? What could use improvement? What holds you back from participating further?

- **Brand Preference & Loyalty categories:** Automotive; Financial Services; Insurance; Credit Card Provider; Retail; Telecom; Travel.
- **B2B bonus:** How long in their sector? Which industry association? Annual budget controlled? Types of business purchases? Number of staff managed? Products purchased in last two years? How much do they travel for work? Team national or local? Industry trends/concerns? What they spend most on / wish they could spend more on? Org publicly traded / private / non-profit?

## 17. Named Concepts & Glossary

- **Property / Opportunity** — your sponsorable entity (event, gala, marathon, conference, festival, team, podcast, building, VIP boxes, each game, athlete, walk, school program). "A fancy word for sponsorship opportunity" / "the prize."
- **Sponsor / Brand** — the party with the money buying marketing assets. Reframe to **"customer."**
- **Sponsorship (definition)** — "reaching across the entire marketing spectrum and borrowing the tactics that get the desired outcome" (sales, branding, lead gen, sampling, experiential, content marketing, employee engagement). Marketing, NOT philanthropy. A **verb, not a noun.** IEG/Forbes: "cash paid for access to exploitable commercial potential." Doesn't change based on the seeker (team/charity/college/athlete — same process).
- **Asset / Inventory** — sellable components (logo placement, mentions, speaking, booths, sampling rights, e-blasts, experiences, naming rights, your audience). Each has a market value. "Inventory = your ingredients." Build 100–150.
- **Activation (a.k.a. leverage)** — a customized experiential marketing opportunity that fulfills one audience need AND one sponsor need simultaneously; the overlap of audience-wants and sponsor-wants; "switching on" sponsorship. The antidote to tiers.
- **Tangible vs. Intangible benefits** — measurable "quantity assets" vs. no-marketplace-proxy value (naming rights, brand alignment, storytelling).
- **Audience survey** — multi-section sheet (demographics, geographics, psychographics); starting point for prospecting + segmentation.
- **Psychographics** — behaviors, motivations, attitudes, values, opinions, brand loyalties, purchasing drivers — "the real meat."
- **Avatar / Persona / Niche (micro audience)** — a granular, named segment given 25+ data points (e.g., "Busy Bobby" = too much to do, too little time; "40-yr-old high-net-worth suburban moms, 2+ kids, drive a Lexus"). "Easier to sell to one Busy Bobby than 500 people." Have ≥3.
- **Macro audience** — total undifferentiated people connected to you (followers/attendees/donors/listeners/fans); lowest value until segmented. Plus "concentric circles" (attendees + database + social + speakers'/media-partners' reach).
- **Discovery session/meeting** — informal info-gathering, pre-proposal, consultative-B2B; "not a sales call"; bring nothing.
- **BAMFAM** — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting." Always schedule the next meeting before leaving the current one (or you never reconnect / get ghosted). Only exception: prospect outright says they're not interested. (Note: the behavior is taught even where the acronym isn't used.)
- **Fulfillment report (a.k.a. wrap-up / post-event report)** — proves you delivered each promised asset (photos, quantities, before/after valuation, ROI); basis for the feedback meeting + renewal; legal cover. Delivered within a week.
- **Happy ears** — taking a prospect's polite enthusiasm at face value; believing you have the sale with no money in hand. "Top cause of budget failure." Antidote: skepticism + BAMFAM.
- **Output vs. Outcome** — Output = logo/sample/booth/speaking (a means). Outcome = customers, leads, brand shift, sales (what the sponsor actually wants). "No sponsor ever woke up needing a booth."
- **Tangible/market value** — what a brand would pay on the open market for the *exact same outcome without you.*
- **Brand value / brand equity** — value a brand adds beyond a generic equivalent (iPhone vs. any phone).
- **Concentric circle method** — the 4-circle warm→cold prospecting model.
- **Sponsorship marketing funnel** — Awareness → Interest → Desire → Action, with asset value rising at each stage ("Action" not "Purchase" since the outcome can be a donation/signup).
- **Naming rights** — name affixed to a physical thing (building/stadium/library/hospital wing); broad reach, exclusivity, long/perpetual; traffic can outweigh audience fit.
- **Title sponsorship** — brand name *in the wordmark* ("The Company XYZ Wellness Expo" / "Blue Enterprises Art Festival 2023," NOT "Art Festival by Blue Enterprises"). If you won't do name-in-wordmark, you aren't offering title sponsorship. Requires multiyear + exclusivity + quarterly fulfillment reports + activations + sometimes product/service adoption + VIP/hospitality + sampling rights.
- **Presenting rights** — name *within* the title but not the wordmark ("The Napa Auto Parts 200 presented by Dodge"); most cost-effective; may be shared. Can have **co-presenting sponsors**, but only **one title sponsor.**
- **Category exclusivity vs. Competitor exclusivity** — no other automotive sponsor at all vs. only no direct competitor. Must be defined in the contract — "you can't assume the sponsor shares your definition."
- **In-kind / contra / value-in-kind (VIK)** — goods/services instead of (or alongside) cash (t-shirts, hotel rooms, food/drink, vehicle giveaways, discounted services). The provider still pays to obtain the items. Don't reflexively refuse it.
- **Media sponsor / promotional partner** — provides publicity/coverage (TV/radio/print/online) or a celebrity draw; often heavily brands the event. Doesn't always cost money. Securing a media partner can **multiply** valuation.
- **Financial sponsor** — gives cash for promotion/branding/advertising.
- **Four primary types of sponsorship:** Financial; In-Kind; Media; Promotional Partnerships (influencer/blogger/creator).
- **Donation / Corporate Philanthropy** — cash/in-kind with recognition handled PRIVATELY; org controls use; harder to get than sponsorship (most companies want recognition).
- **Pass-through benefits** — a listed asset category passed through to the sponsor/audience.
- **Self-liquidators** — discounted merch offers (highlight reel, official jersey) at the game/sponsor location.
- **Endemic sponsors** — brands naturally aligned to your property type (ski brands for a ski event); aligned but NOT warmest (they already reach the niche).
- **Sponsorship form** — an online request form on a sponsor's site; mostly exists to DETER unsolicited requests; "a cold email dressed up." Only fill one out when deep in talks and the sponsor requires it for recordkeeping.
- **Omnichannel sponsorship** — various media/tactics with consistent brand message/voice/tone across channels ("omni" = all). 5 ingredients: **Agility, Empowerment, Relevance, Consistency, Convenience.**
- **Conversion** — turning a lead into a paying customer.
- **NPS** — Net Promoter Score (detractors/passives/promoters from a single 0–10 question).
- **First to market** — being first in an unexplored niche (Wife Carrying World Championship, Baby Jumping Festival, Burning Man) — flips the dynamic so sponsors court you.
- **Procurement (in sponsorship)** — brands allocating procurement resources to manage sponsorship (compliance, deal management, negotiations); a 2024 growth trend.
- **Motion vs. Action** — busywork that feels productive vs. activity that produces outcomes (James Clear).
- **Stakeholder mapping** — primary/secondary/key stakeholders + who influences the deal.
- **Sponsorship menu** — preferred euphemism for the package; signals customization.
- **Moneyball / baseball framing** — heavy hitters (one-shot home runs) strike out; **base hitters** (consistent small wins) win. Bases: response (1st), proposal interest (2nd), verbal yes (3rd), signed deal (home). Your audience = your base hitters.
- **Always Be Opening (ABO)** — replaces "Always Be Closing" (Greg Hickman); open relationships daily — "the second you make a sale you lose your warmest prospect."
- **Hybrid model / proposal** — a common pool of baseline assets + a library of pre-priced activations slotted in after discovery; avoids both rigid tiers AND from-scratch building.
- **Cookbook vs. Recipe** — "Amateurs have a recipe (gold/silver/bronze = makes one thing); masters have a cookbook (a pool of components for infinite custom combinations)."
- **Shoulder-shrug method** — the wrong way to price: shrug, decide how much you need, back-fill arbitrary tier values.
- **Spaghetti method** — tiers throwing everything at the wall hoping something sticks.
- **Media kit / press kit** — the influencer's proposal equivalent: bio, testimonials, past collabs, case studies, social stats (growth over time / viral posts, not just current follower count).
- **The 50% rule** — ≥50% of the proposal must be audience data.
- **1,000 true fans** — you don't need millions; a niche of loyal fans is high-value.
- **Curator of audience** — the correct self-identity ("not a bowl of logo soup").
- **Social dismissal** — being instantly deemed an amateur for showing up proposal-first / talking logos, donations, "investment."
- **RFID** — radio-frequency ID wristbands enabling cashless, hands-free purchases + attendee data (attendance, ROI, dwell, access control, safety, gamification). UHF (~40 ft), HF (payments/ticketing), LF (~0.33 ft).
- **Exploitable commercial potential** — the IEG/Forbes core of sponsorship.

## 18. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

**The cardinal sins (recur across many sources):**
- **Going "proposal first" / cold-attaching/-mailing the proposal.** "The iron is ice cold." Like film studios refusing unsolicited scripts. ~95–99% of unsolicited packages are trashed unread (Scenario 1, 95%); the rest get opened, hit a tier grid, and get trashed (Scenario 2). Hold the proposal until they ask (2nd–3rd meeting+).
- **Gold/Silver/Bronze tiers (by ANY name — platinum/ruby/diamond, first/second/third, major/medium/minor).** Sponsors "despise them." The *rigidity* is the problem. They signal you're guessing (no discovery), are misguided/generic (a bakery and a hair-care brand get identical logo + 5 posts + 3 e-blasts), and won't customize; force "this-or-nothing" (buy A–E to get B) → resentment → no renewal; convert the most powerful marketing tool into the weakest (direct mail); force "vanity benefits" (logos/tweets/e-blasts) — and "the money is NOT in the easy benefits." They waste **weeks/months** of committee debate (e.g., "logo with hyperlink vs. two tweets"). Came from the **finance department** (easier to forecast), not from what sponsors want. "If you get sponsorship with tiers, it's *in spite of* them." Big companies succeed in spite of it (they have other leverage); small orgs can't.
- **Including a price list / pricing grid in the deck/proposal.** "If you haven't talked to the sponsor, how can you guess how much to charge?" When they ask, say "I'd never dream of charging a sponsor for a bunch of assets they don't want" → open a budget/goals conversation. Budgets "magically increase" when sponsors see custom options.
- **Overpricing ("'cause I said so" / on your expenses).** Every sponsor (even deep-pocketed) sees through it. The killer follow-up: "How did you come up with these numbers?" Sponsors know market rates / return on ad spend.
- **Underpricing / not valuing at all** — leaves thousands–tens of thousands on the table; sponsors happily pay low and never correct you. Both over- and under-pricing kill deals.
- **Copying someone else's valuation / competitors' packages** — you don't know if theirs worked, their audience/goals, or whether the rep succeeded *in spite of* the package. "Like following someone's map without checking the destination."
- **Vague audience data** — "middle-class families," "20-somethings," "general public," "people who care about [topic]," "everyone," "all of Canada," "people who like ribs and beer." Like a bakery menu that says "cake for people who like desserts." General public = lowest value (needs millions of eyeballs). A single paragraph isn't enough — needs 2–3 pages in charts/graphs.
- **Relying on logos & social blasts as primary assets** — they deliver only brand awareness (no leads/conversions/sales). Logos are "tired"/forgettable amid 6,000–10,000 ads/day; worth ~¼¢/impression; getting stuck on logos is "a novice move." Boards over-value them because they're most visible. "Nobody studies your sponsor wall / thank-you page / annual-report back page (except people trying to steal your sponsors)."
- **Treating the discovery session like a sales meeting** — pitching, dumping your mission/cause/event story, trying to close → you glean nothing and get ghosted. "Until they tell you their goal, you're not in a position to ask for the sale."
- **Saying "we customize" without actually customizing** — "merely lip service." A "we'll customize if you ask" asterisk makes it worse (looks like CYA).
- **Assuming you know what the sponsor wants** (Drucker: "The customer rarely buys what the company thinks it sells him"). Pushing Asset A because *you* make it while they need Asset B → under-deliver → no renewal / breach.
- **Treating sponsors as ATMs / investors / donors / banks** — they're marketers demanding ROI, accountable to stakeholders.
- **Treating sponsorship as philanthropy/fundraising** (the #1 nonprofit mistake) — your mission/cause does NOT bring in the prospect; your *audience* does. Mission belongs in ONE paragraph; most charity decks waste 6–10 pages on mission/vision/CEO bio/board.
- **Blending sponsorship + CSR/philanthropy** ("the fox chasing two birds catches none") — the proposal bounces between marketing and foundation until trashed.
- **Selling outputs not outcomes / "I do partnerships not sponsorship" as a get-out-of-delivery card** — renaming changes nothing; they still want marketing ROI.

**Process / behavior anti-patterns:**
- **Chasing only the biggest fish** (Fortune-500s) — flooded with ~10,000 packages/month; slight misalignment = discarded; like the lottery. A startup/nonprofit usually can't solve a F500's problems. Start local/small, build a track record. Pursue sponsors of **all sizes** — build a ladder so you can back-fill a lost whale ("if they've given you $10, they're likelier to give $10,000 than a cold prospect").
- **"Just send me a proposal" syndrome** = symptom of trying to close on the first call + going in cold + targeting only the biggest. Move to "declined"; use the decline email to build next year's relationship.
- **Skipping research under time pressure** — "tone-deaf"; like a doctor doing an untrained procedure. "A good reason for not having data is not a good reason — the prospect doesn't care that you haven't surveyed yet."
- **Spending too long on any one step** (perfecting the proposal/assets forever) — comfort masquerading as progress. **Start before you feel ready.**
- **Indecisiveness / being too slow** (6–12 weeks / a month+ to decide) — "sponsors want quick decisions." Be ready to turn around documentation in <24 hrs.
- **Requesting sponsorship too late** (1–2 weeks / 1 month / 3 months out) — far too little; proof you secretly believe "sponsorship is easy."
- **Pestering** — daily emails/calls → blocked; no in-person visits; no odd-hours contact.
- **Waiting forever for a "no"** — a flat "no" is clear; don't decode it. But DO decode *soft* replies ("let me think," "no time," "budget's thin") with more questions. **Assuming a soft objection = "no" is "the cardinal sin of all sales"** (Chris sold an angry sponsor's renewal spot to a competitor by misreading "no budget right now").
- **Mass-emailing / e-blasting; reaching out to info@/contact@/generic inboxes.**
- **Trying to make the sale by email or on the first call** — email's only job is to book a call.
- **Long emails (2,000–3,000 words)** — guaranteed deletion.
- **Sending info/sales material before the first meeting** (Dave sent "1.5M impressions" to a F500 that gets hundreds of millions/quarter and may not want awareness → meeting cancelled). "Just because it's impressive to you doesn't mean it's impressive to the sponsor."
- **Funneling too much budget into one activation** — leaves other sponsors/areas underfunded; can't count on 100% of the audience gravitating to one sponsor.
- **Solving the problem too fast (Tech Support analogy)** — if you reach "I can help you" before the prospect feels heard, they reject the solution. Earn the conclusion with discovery.
- **Survivorship bias (WWII plane / bullet-holes)** — examine the rejection pile / recycling bin (packages that got trashed), not the few that converted. "A 0% conversion rate at scale is still a 0% conversion rate" — sending more of the same is "hitting your thumb harder with a hammer."
- **Scaling up mediocrity ("He Who Fails the Most")** — 10 emails → 0 replies means 1,000 → 0; a 1% open rate stays 1% at scale (Keith Cunningham). Test/change variables.
- **Making major event changes (theme/venue/date) without telling the sponsor** — their themed signage/booth becomes useless ("they look stupid because you didn't keep them in the loop").
- **Pairing with a controversial/scandal-ridden partner** — other sponsors + audience may pull out; damages the smaller party more.
- **Trash-talking the sponsor (esp. on social media)** — "torching a bridge you may again have to cross."
- **"Any sponsor is better than no sponsor"** (rooted in no audience data) — a vegan org taking a Tyson-affiliated sponsor / a health-conscious family audience getting a sugary-soda title sponsor → audience trust destroyed + bad press.
- **A sponsor should NEVER fund 100% of an event** — desperation breeds skipped vetting.
- **Getting free stuff and calling it sponsorship** (it's sampling — charge them to give it away). Letting your event be "ambushed" by sub-level free raffle giveaways lowers your assets' value.
- **Putting "sponsorship" on your website as a passive afterthought** ("download this package & send a check") — instead: "Want to talk sponsorship? Let's have a conversation."
- **Trying to get *around* the gatekeeper** — turn them into your **advocate** (their job is to bring GOOD opportunities up); bypassing = meeting an angry decision-maker who already passed.
- **Risk-based decision-making** (e.g., hiring a broker because you secretly don't believe you'll succeed) — you pay a huge premium for that fear.
- **Only capturing audience on one platform** (influencers) — brands can just run their own ads at your followers and pay you nothing. Capture email; diversify platforms.
- **Blanket "Who do you know?" asks to board members** — yields huge cold names (Microsoft, Coca-Cola) with no connection. Instead, research connections yourself, then ask about specific relationships by name. (Board "warm intros" usually fizzle.)
- **Podcast-specific:** only offering commercials/ad reads (= advertising, not sponsorship); pitching before valuing; including pricing; deferring to everything the sponsor says (act like the expert); being too salesy.
- **Festival-specific:** waiting too long; making beautiful decks instead of selling; focusing only on money (sponsors also save costs + give referrals); skipping audience data / discovery / valuation / fulfillment report; not going for the decision-maker; bypassing smaller sponsors (need a mix); erasing "not right now" refusals (re-approach at fiscal year-end / early Q1).
- **Multiyear-specific:** assuming a deal is multiyear (must be in the contract); not negotiating for more money; not producing a fulfillment report.

**"Worst Advice" to ignore:** "Sponsorship is easy"; "cold-send your proposal"; "skip research under time constraints"; "overprice assets to get more money"; "money is the only thing a sponsor offers"; "any sponsor is better than no sponsor."

## 20. Industry-Specific Playbooks

### Events / Conferences / Galas
- Sponsorship adds more than money: more **location options** (a sponsor once offered Chris the top two floors of their office), better immersive experiences, attendee conveniences, larger audience → more leads.
- **Name and sell every component as an activation with ownership:** warm-up, pre/post programs, F&B stations (incl. "dessert course brought to you by"), valet, kid zone, vendor row, VIP lounge, scholarship/legacy naming. Ownership of one activation lets you have many sponsors without dilution.
- Immersive examples (engagement > free samples): Optimum Nutrition VR workout booth (The Arnold 2019); Lush Instagrammable installations (2017); Sleep Number nap stations (CES 2018); phone charging stations; couches/chairs for rest.
- Capture leads via attendee contact info at signup → post-event survey + nurture sequence.
- **Coffee bar** is the perfect activation for a tired, flown-in B2B conference crowd (Keurig example).
- Gala caution: "Companies don't really like going to your gala" — they view seats as work ($175/plate); engage new contacts via volunteering first.
- **B2B is the highest-value audience** ("fishing with dynamite"); segment the room by seniority and let the sponsor pick the segment.

### Festivals (film/music/food — process identical regardless of type)
- Be a **curator of audience:** ask "will this make my festival better for my target audience?" If no, walk away from the sponsorship.
- Audience is the primary asset; use only current data; ≥20–25 datapoints/segment in graphs/tables/charts; festival assets are inherently more exciting than a business conference — leverage that.
- Need a **mix** of sponsors: 4–5 mid/large + smaller fillers (avoid large-only or empty-looking small-only).
- **Planning timeline:** 9–12 months out (theme, goals, attendance projection, budget, venue, ticket pricing, staff/vendors/performers, prospect list + outreach) → 6 months (permits, safety, secure sponsors, cement activations, tickets on sale) → 3 months (finalize) → 1 month (test app, maps, RFID, ramp marketing, rain plan) → 1 week (weather, cleanup, rundown, setup) → day-of → after (fulfillment report, thank staff, renegotiate). Permits often needed: premises/temporary-use, event, seller's, building, business license, health, fire, alcohol, noise.
- **7 must-have activities:** Contests/Giveaways, Competitions, Merch/Shopping, F&B, Games/Play Zones, Lounge/Recharge, Photo/Social-Share — measured by entries, QR scans, leads, dwell, revenue/attendee, RFID tap-ins, hashtag/UGC volume.
- **Festival social media assets (low→high value):** promotional posts < teaser videos < live streams < Instagram Lives (~10 min) < UGC < social contests < giveaways. Co-streaming value hinges on YOUR following eclipsing the sponsor's. Create an event hashtag; skip LinkedIn as a festival; a small YouTube presence beats none.
- **What festival sponsors want (6):** lots of research/audience data, a proactive approach, consideration as people, productive discovery sessions, detailed fulfillment reports, customized opportunities.
- **12 KPIs sponsors want:** audience data, attendance data (scan codes; sold ≠ attended), sales, conversions, website traffic (use opt-in forms), online searches, social mentions (HootSuite/Talkwalker/Social Searcher), email signups (lead magnet), brand reputation, NPS, email open rate, email CTR. Weave into the fulfillment report → become a "sponsorship magnet."
- **Best festival sponsor types:** Coca-Cola (king), Red Bull, Tito's, Monster, Pepsi — but "almost any company" fits given variety.
- **RFID for festivals:** cashless/hands-free payment, accurate attendance, ROI tracking, dwell analytics, access control, gamification, personalized check-in, cash-free VIP lounge. Build a sponsor activation around RFID when their goal = email growth/leads/conversion.
- **27 festival-planning apps** by job: management (EventMobi, Bizzabo, Attendify, Eventzilla, Boomset, Odoo Events), ticketing (Eventbrite, Brown Paper Tickets, Yapsody, Setster), POS (Square), team/comms (Slack, Asana, Blossom, HeyTell, Planning Pod), notes/files (Evernote, Dropbox, 24me), audio (AudioTools, Decibel Ultra), weather (The Weather Channel), layout (MagicPlan), photos (Capsule), social walls (Everwall, LiveTweetApp), waivers (Smartwaiver).

### Nonprofits / Charities / Cause / Corporate Philanthropy
- **Sponsorship ≠ philanthropy / fundraising** (heaviest recurring theme). **Sponsors ≠ donors** — donors give for the mission (no ROI expectation); sponsors make a marketing investment and care about *audience.*
- Your sponsors are **NOT** the Foundation/CSR/community-engagement/corporate-philanthropy people — talk to **marketing/brand/BD/sales/product/communications.** Use the philanthropy contacts only to ask *who handles marketing/comms/sales* — that's the road to sponsorship.
- For every $1 a company might donate, ~$100 in marketing could be spent on you. Corporate philanthropy is tiny (~$330M/yr cause sponsorship) + hyper-competitive — "a nice idea that doesn't exist."
- **Mission/vision → ONE paragraph.** Spend the rest on audience + activations. "The best sponsorship I've ever seen has no logos anywhere."
- Audience caring about the cause is the **bridge** — the brand wants the audience; the audience's affinity for the cause makes connecting through you valuable.
- If a company explicitly says "this is a donation, no recognition," honor it — then cash the check, ask for a marketing meeting, and do something free for them that year to start a sponsorship relationship.
- Keep revenue streams **separate:** individual donorships, grants, sponsorships.
- **5 steps to corporate philanthropy:** set goals + mission (why you exist, who you help, how) → understand your value prop → list assets (in-kind, pro bono, volunteer hours) → be smart about partners (align with mission; approach asking for *advice on a project*, not a donation) → present assets after a discovery meeting. Corporate giving types: employee giving, foundations, matching gifts, skilled volunteering, grants, pro bono/in-kind, internal events, days of giving. Fit fails: child-health org × candy company; good fits: Habitat × Lowe's/Valspar.
- Legal exposure: calling it sponsorship but delivering like philanthropy (or vice versa) creates real risk.

### Sports / Athletes
- Shift from **billboard thinking** ("logo on my car") to **fan/audience thinking** — your competitive advantage is your fan base + how you interact with them.
- Audience segments get *different* benefits: home viewers, attendees, dedicated vs. casual fans, pass holders, corporate ticket holders.
- Sponsor decision criteria: buying habits, audience intimacy with the property, reach, lifestyle/brand alignment, whether the audience is too broad (reduces ROI), activation image fit, whether team/sport "is bigger than the brand," co-sponsors, logistics/staffing, past activation performance.
- Deloitte: fans want (1) safety/comfort/cleanliness, (2) a good view, (3) high-quality game, (4) exciting atmosphere — "get #4 right and you can fund #1–3." Fan bases differ (NHL/NFL care little about pre/post-game entertainment; NASCAR wants unique entertainment; NFL highest on "exciting atmosphere").
- **7 best sports activations:** RFID wristband contests; free fantasy betting; playing games (Goodyear Plinko/"Throwdown" at NASCAR; Puma cornhole); branded photo walls (Dove greenscreen; Veuve Clicquot golden frame); exclusive experiences (AmEx US Open); memorabilia giveaways (McDonald's inflatable thundersticks); pop-up bars (Heineken).
- **Fan engagement (6):** interactive social polls with stakes, contests (UGC), email marketing (personalized + segmented), great sponsors, experiential activations, VIP experiences. Measure via post-event surveys (1–2 days after), social engagement *rate* (interactions ÷ followers × 100; avg 1–5%), app downloads + *usage*.
- **Top sports companies:** Pepsi, Red Bull, Coca-Cola, Optimum Nutrition, Reebok, Dick's, Puma, State Farm, Budweiser, Castrol (smaller teams target Optimum Nutrition/Reebok). NASCAR comp: write into contracts that "your logo shows up in others' stuff and vice versa — it lifts all boats"; big-name sponsors amplify everyone (evaluators ask "do you have big-name sponsors?").
- Annual **sponsor summit** for goodwill + input.

### Insurance in Sports (sub-playbook)
- Insurers chase affluent/loyal older-skewing audiences (**25–64 sweet spot**), borrowed brand trust, risk/performance alignment, and first-party data + lead gen via digital activations. Top: **State Farm** (NBA, college, esports), **Allianz** (Olympics/soccer/motorsport), **Allstate** (college football, soccer; likes endurance racing), **Geico** (NASCAR/MLB/NHL/NCAA). **Start LOCAL** (Google "[state] insurance companies"), lead with audience data, design digital-first measurable activations, report ROI not impressions. Match: a 32-year-old married homeowner earning $50k is a perfect insurance fit.

### Alcohol in Sport (sub-playbook)
- **Pros:** large adult segment (fans skew 35–49, then 18–34); rising spend (~$106M new deals); very profitable; many targets. **Cons:** off-limits for under-21/youth-heavy audiences; clashes with kid-friendly image; normalization concerns; big brands unreceptive to small orgs.
- Modern model = brand alignment + premium experiences + responsible-drinking programs (Anheuser-Busch + TEAM Coalition; age-gating, moderation, WHO SAFER). Sober-curious trend + non-alc options (Athletic Brewing, Heineken 0.0, Budweiser Zero, Guinness 0.0). **Start local** (breweries/distilleries).

### Motorsport (NASCAR / F1 / Formula E)
- Standard = logos on car/uniform; **elevate it.** Examples: Ty Dillon/Death Wish authentic tweet; Coca-Cola Racing Family video series (5 of 6 videos >15M views, >10% engagement); Busch's ever-changing "driving billboard" car (Busch +10.1%, Busch Light +17.7% early 2020); Heineken Neon House (themed hospitality, airbrushed helmets — "anything but a photo wall"); Heineken Greener Bar (sustainability matched to Formula E's eco audience); AmEx Fan Experience (tiered card access → FOMO). **Lesson:** themed zones, green initiatives, and "make a standard activation better" beat reinventing the wheel; NASCAR athletes self-source sponsors. **F1: >$2B sponsorship 2024.**
- **Finding motorsport sponsors:** prospect on attendees AND your own fans; niche/over-index angles (female fans) open non-traditional prospects; free LinkedIn + "Hey Dave" outreach.

### Podcasts / Influencers / Creators
- A 20-sec ad-read / logo / "and now a word from our sponsors" (the fast-forward moment) = **advertising, NOT sponsorship.** Real sponsorship spans the whole marketing spectrum → higher fees + better ROI.
- **High-value assets are NOT commercials** — use show interviews, product placement, giveaways/contests with the product as prize, brand ambassadorship, account takeovers, sponsored Q&As, affiliate.
- Need a **media kit** (not a proposal): short bio, testimonials, past collabs, case studies, social stats focused on *growth over time / viral posts.* Build a media-kit-style business case → 3 avatars → case studies/activation ideas → strong CTA, NO pricing.
- **Capture audience on multiple platforms + get emails** (or brands just ad-target your followers for free). ≥20 data points; psychographics matter.
- **Influencer process (9 steps):** understand your brand → know your audience → choose niche companies → build a media kit → write a great pitch → price fairly → connect → discovery session → follow up (always BAMFAM).
- **Pricing:** $10/1,000 likes baseline; state exactly what's delivered ("$200 = three IG posts + two IG Stories"); under-promise then hit it exactly ("don't promise 50,000 likes and deliver 8,500"). Micro (10k–100k) beat macro/celebs (more attainable, affordable, higher engagement; audiences "desensitized" to celebs). Reach out **directly via DM/email** (DMs from non-followers land in a side folder). Tips: post often/consistently; delete weak old posts (prospects dig through); 3 hashtags (up to ~9 sweet spot — vet meaning, cf. #susanalbumparty); geotags; easy contact info; real website. Brands using micros: Nivea, La Croix, Coca-Cola, HelloFresh, Glossier, Target, Adidas, Starbucks, Airbnb, Nike, Sephora.
- Audience by platform: IG/FB 25–34; Twitter/Pinterest 30–49; LinkedIn 46–55; TikTok 18–24; Snapchat 13–34; YouTube 15–25.

### Title / Naming / Presenting Rights (big-ticket)
- Single title sponsor (larger, exclusive fee) + several presenting sponsors = perfect balance; leverage high-profile brands as a selling feature.
- Requires **name in wordmark + multiyear (3–10+; arena 20+) + exclusivity + frequent (quarterly) fulfillment reports + possible product/service adoption + VIP/hospitality + sampling rights**; always lawyer-reviewed. For naming rights, traffic can outweigh audience (any major corp for a stadium); for a hospital wing/college library, be more audience-specific. Deeper name changes (renaming a street) only at 10 years.
- **Increase title value:** don't assume the wordmark is enough — ask what they're trying to achieve, "write a prescription" for additional activations (live, contests, virtual, social/digital), run them through your valuation calculator (don't give them away free).
- Ask a signed sponsor for their competitor list to avoid conflicts. **25+ "sweeten the pot" ideas:** named awards, product endorsements, venue-location input, backlit/larger signage, upgraded/VIP hospitality, dedicated event website, hosted live social feeds, branded contests, content re-shares, professional videography for their YouTube, blog guest features, DM insert samples, sponsoring a special room (arcade/escape room), celeb appearances at their booth, contra, charity drives.
- **Unforeseen risks:** title sponsors may require you to switch products/services (bank, official credit card, drinks served, even shoes) — conflicts can be accidental or deliberate **ambush marketing.** Make it explicit; involve a lawyer; ≥5-year deals for name/word-mark changes.

### Endurance Events (Elite Feats sub-playbook)

- Trackable digital activations (post-race digital goodie bags, codes) beat "slashy but unmeasurable" billboard-style assets (bus wraps, mile markers) — "someone running by sweaty doesn't note a mile-marker sponsor."

### Crisis / Refunds (any industry)
- Three real options (moving forward is NOT "no decision"): **Cancel / Postpone / Move forward.** Process: lawyer → accountant → venue (loop) → sponsors one-on-one BEFORE any e-blast. Decide on principle: best for the **sector**? best for **attendees**? in **5 years**, which best serves the event?
- Gold/silver/bronze sellers likely owe **full refunds** (no valuation = can't prove partial delivery). Custom/valued packages let you argue partial (e.g., "social + webinar + e-blast = 60% delivered → refund the rest"). Alternatives: credit for next year; another property same fiscal year/audience/outcomes. **"You cannot over-communicate."** Custom-package sponsors are far easier in a crisis (Chris & Brad postponed their conference, offered out-of-scope digital activations free, lost zero sponsors).
