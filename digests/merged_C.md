# Sponsorship Playbook — Merged Digest C

Consolidated, deduplicated knowledge (blog chunks 06–08 + transcript chunk 02) on how to GET corporate sponsorship. Organized for sponsorship-seekers (the "property" side).

## 1. Core Philosophy & Mindset

**The one master idea (recurring, stated dozens of times):** Sponsorship is MARKETING, not philanthropy/fundraising/donation/investment/charity. "Marketing is the language of sponsorship. When you speak any other language — sales, or cash, cash, cash — you can't communicate with the sponsor."
- "A sponsor doesn't want to work with YOU. They want to work with your AUDIENCE." / "Sponsors want to sponsor your audience," not your organization. (Recurring, the single most-repeated principle.)
- "Too many sponsorship seekers believe that sponsors want to sponsor your organization. This is not correct."
- A sponsor is **not a donor** (gives from generosity), **not an investor** (gives to see you succeed), and **not an ATM/cash cow**. "Even if you're curing children's cancer, the sponsor is not obligated to give you money." "If you want a donor, look outside of sponsorship." "If you need an investor, get a bank loan."
- "Your cause is irrelevant to your sponsor but highly relevant to your audience; your audience is highly relevant to your sponsor." (Recurring.)

**Audience is the #1 asset.** "The riches are in the niches" (recurring motto; applies to audience AND your own services). "The most valuable part of any sponsorship deal isn't your social media mentions or premium booth space — it's audience data." Mark Sabourand framing (quoted repeatedly): "Think of yourself in sponsorship as nothing more than a *curator of audience*, not a placer of logos."

**You make the sale, not the document.** "The sponsorship proposal does not make the sale. You do." (Channel's signature sign-off.) "The sponsorship package is not the starting point, it's an outcome." "The sponsorship package doesn't sell your sponsors… YOU DO!"

**Sponsors are customers / people / partners — not adversaries or marks.** Reframe sponsors as CUSTOMERS internally (clearer than "partners") → prioritizes their needs, back-burners the dollars. "Talk to your sponsor like a person." You are an equal, NOT a beggar — groveling "comes across as very substandard." Believe your opportunity is worthy.

**Outcomes, not assets.** "Nobody buys sponsorship; they buy an outcome." "Sponsors want outcomes." They don't care whether you use assets A–Y or just asset A, as long as you hit their objectives without blowing the budget.

**ROI is everything to a sponsor.** They're investing, not donating; a failed event = "a stock crash" for them.

**Action over motion; start before you're ready.** "Action is any tactic that brings you closer to your goal." "Build the plane as you fly it" — a partially built plane is easy to fix; a finished wrong one means starting over. "Fortune favors the bold." "Don't be afraid to fail. Expect it and embrace it." "He who fails the most wins the most" (Seth Godin) / "If you want to increase your success rate, double your failure rate" (Thomas Watson Jr.).

**No silver bullet / hard things are hard.** "If sponsorship was easy, everyone would be doing it." "Hard things are hard. There is no silver bullet, there is no knight in shining armor." No shortcuts; treat sponsorship like a profession (late nights/weekends), not a side-of-desk hobby ("a casual brain surgeon"). "Sponsors don't want to work with amateurs."

**Retention > acquisition.** "The quickest way to double your money is to fold it in half and put it in your back pocket" (Will Rogers). The fastest way to grow revenue is to never lose a sponsor. "The second you make a sale, you just lost your warmest prospect."

**Listen, don't pitch.** Switch from Asking Mode to Listening Mode — "Just listen. And maybe drop your agenda too." "If you want to catch anything, you have to think like the fish." Would YOU say yes to your own proposal? "Coming up with the right answer is only half the battle" — proving you HEAR them is the other half.

**You're not "different."** Claiming uniqueness is "usually a codeword for saying the sponsorship seeker has no idea what they're doing." Treat your program "like a suit of armor" — patch the gaping holes (audience data, activations, value, proposal) instead of calling them "different."

**Sponsors lie / "happy ears."** Sponsors say pleasant things ("send me a proposal," "we'll call you") to avoid conflict, and ghost rather than say no. "In the world of sales, only one word means no — and that's 'NO.'" Hearing "no" is GOOD; hearing "maybe" is bad.

**Technician vs. owner (E-Myth, Michael Gerber):** "I'm a festival owner who needs money" (technician = path to failure) vs. "I'm a marketer growing a business that produces a festival" (owner).

**The 5%/95% rule.** Handing over the proposal is ~5% of the process; the other 95% is prep. "There are no shortcuts." (House-painting analogy: 20+ hrs prep, minutes of painting.)

**Other recurring lines:**
- "You can either have a fast no or a slow yes. There's no other option."
- "The person who cares the most wins." / "Nobody cares more about your opportunity than you."
- "Show me your calendar and I'll show you your priorities." (Schedule prospecting/discovery/follow-up daily.)
- "Better is better, more is not better." / "The less you sell the sponsor, the more you can charge for it" (the customization paradox).
- "Success leaves clues."
- "There's nothing so useless as doing efficiently that which should not be done at all" (Peter Drucker).
- "Sponsorship is broken and it's our fault" — brands have said for years they don't want logos; we ignored them. "We don't do sponsorship" really means "we don't do that 30-years-ago gold/silver/bronze stuff."
- "If your bucket is leaky, it doesn't matter how much water you pour into it." / "Pumpkins will not become carriages no matter how much we wish they could."
- "Scaling mediocrity only leads to bigger piles of mediocrity" (Keith Cunningham, *The Road Less Stupid*).

**Consultancy's own North Star values:** Extreme Ownership ("We do not blame external factors"), Seek Feedback, Team First, World Class. Goal: help clients generate $25M in sponsorship revenue by end of 2026; be the #1 English-language sponsorship resource.

## 2. The End-to-End Process (overall sequence/funnel)

The canonical sequence is consistent across every source; package/proposal is an OUTCOME, not a start. Several named framings:

### The 11 Stages of the Sponsorship Journey (channel's master process)
1. Identify your properties (a property links a brand's goals to an asset — NOT "give me money").
2. Define your audience (min. 25+ data points; "families/middle class" is NOT an audience).
3. Segment audience / build ≥3 avatars (buyer personas).
4. Build out individual discrete assets (logo is one small piece; prioritize audience interaction, product consumption, email/contact capture).
5. Get internal buy-in BEFORE going to market (board, boss, marketing, comms can't tell you what to do but CAN kill a deal).
6. Valuation (the highly technical component; defend your number).
7. Prospect research (based on audience/assets/valuation, not "who has money").
8. Prospect outreach (invite to a Discovery session).
9. Discovery meeting (learn their marketing goals).
10. Build custom proposal → review meeting → close → sign agreement.
11. Fulfillment + fulfillment report.

### The 9-Part Sponsorship Blueprint (do NOT skip steps)
Define your offering → Identify your audience → Create activations → Find your value → Bundling strategies → Develop a proposal → Identify prospects → Build connections → Launch partnership + measure ROI. (Multi-tier-cake-without-supports analogy: skipping steps yields low-quality prospects, then collapse.)

### The Sales Funnel / Order of Operations (most-stated short form)
Email/cold call → Discovery (1–3 meetings) → custom proposal → negotiation → agreement → cash. The fatal error is short-circuiting straight from email → sale (emailing the proposal to strangers). "Imagine doing an oil change and skipping to the end — your car wouldn't even start."

### Detailed correct order (chunk 08, full)
Goal setting (pick cash/media/in-kind; get internal buy-in early) → identify opportunities/specialties → audience data/survey → build assets/inventory (logos first to "get it out of the way," then banish) → create properties (group assets by shared audience + comms goals) → create activation ideas → valuation → prospect research → first contact (only to book discovery) → discovery → custom proposal (only if asked) → negotiation → contract → activation → fulfillment → fulfillment report → renewal.

**Funnel to TRACK (not "proposals submitted," a vanity metric):** Prospects → Contact → Meetings → Proposal Submitted → Follow Up → Outcome (Approved/Declined). Tells you the prospect count needed to trigger one agreement and where the funnel leaks.

**Anatomy of a Great Program (6 parts):** audience research, defined goals, asset valuation, activation opportunities, sponsorship package, sponsorship proposal.

## 3. Audience Research & Data

**Audience is the crown jewel.** "Quality of audience > quantity." A sponsor "would rather have 50 event attendees who fit their target market than 6,000 who don't." Niche down until you "can't segment your audience any further." Segment by demographics, geographics, psychographics; build ≥3 avatars/buyer personas, each with its own data depth.

**Data depth (the most-repeated number on the channel):** 20–25 data points per segment minimum; frequently stated as 25+, 30+, "no less than 20," "at least 25," "≥35 data points." Niching example: "millennials in NYC" is useless (millennials = born 1981–1996); "millennials born in 1981 living in Queens" or "45-year-old executives in Chicago earning $125,000/year" = the "400-karat gold" data sponsors crave. Specific ("26 male audience members from Chicago, mid-40s, $100K, in finance") beats "15,000 male audience members."

**What to capture:** location (down to borough), exact age (not "early 30s"), gender, occupation, industry, income, marital status, # children, brand preferences, favorite smartphone/podcast, last tech bought + when, **decision-making capacity / ability to influence a purchase**, and **upcoming purchase intent**.

**Audience segment tiers (ranked):** B2B audience (highest — make business decisions, access business budgets) > VIP audience (wealthier, donate/buy generously) > General Population (lowest — unqualified leads; goal is to make this your smallest, even nonexistent, segment).

**Surveys (recurring: "Sponsorship Surveys Don't Work" is a lie):** Survey your audience 1–2× per year; data older than 6 months is stale. "Every single time we deliver a survey we are surprised by the results" — don't decide for your audience.
- Design: 10–20 questions (keep open-ended to a minimum); the last ~8 unrelated to your property (all about future purchases/brands). Give ≥1 month to respond; remind ~1 week before deadline. Incentivize with freebies/discounts/gift cards.
- Sample size: you need far fewer responses than you think. Database of 5,000 → only 256 responses needed for a decent sample (SurveyMonkey calculator). 30+ data points achievable at 95% confidence / 5% margin of error. Wait ~2 weeks for responses.
- Charities most fear "donors will be offended by personal questions" — false; this audience is *most* eager to give feedback. People who unsubscribe over a survey "were never really your Tribe."

**Behavioral data + AI (chunk 02):** Combine stated survey intent with behavioral data (RFID bracelets, booth dwell time, purchases) → makes a brand "100× more sure." "Their actions prove what they want." Use AI to cluster survey + behavioral data into avatars (see §16 for prompts).

**Free audience-data hack — LinkedIn Insight Tag:** Create a free LinkedIn Ads account → Campaign Manager → install the Insight Tag (JS pixel) after the `<body>` tag in your global footer → wait several weeks. No personal data (IPs hashed/truncated, pseudonyms after a week, deleted after 180 days; doesn't slow the site). Yields job function, title, industry, seniority, location, charted most→least prevalent ("30% of my audience works in finance at a manager level"). Still run a real survey too (captures brand usage).

## 4. Properties & Inventory (Assets)

**Property** = a sponsorable opportunity (event, festival, naming rights, podcast, team, athlete, building, communications platform, stakeholder group). Defined by a **shared audience + common communications goals**. Anything can become a property. "NOT 'give me money so I have money.'"

**Asset** = any discrete, sellable benefit (tangible/intangible, physical/virtual): logo placement, signage, speaking slot, e-blast, booth, naming rights, sampling rights, broadcast rights, social posts, audience access. The most valuable assets are **intangible**: speaking, co-branding, endorsement, thought leadership, category exclusivity, influence over audience, sampling rights, broadcast/content rights, media partnerships.

**Inventory** = the full list of assets across all properties. **Build the BIGGEST list possible first; do NOT judge value yet** (garment analogy: grab every garment that might fit, then try them on in the "dressing room" = valuation, keeping only what fits). Visit the venue, photograph it, note logo spots, traffic flow, interactive opportunities. Build assets in a team workshop AND interview ≥5 prospects.

**Logos are the worst/least valuable asset.** "Logos are the worst sponsorship asset." Get them out of the way in ~15 minutes, then banish logo talk. Logo-only ≈ 7% of total sponsorship opportunity value; logo achieves only brand awareness (which sponsors often already have or don't want).

**Value/competition ladder of assets (low→high):** Awareness (logo placement) → Conversion experience → Lead gen → Sale. "The closer you get to the sales outcome, the more you can charge." Awareness = pennies and highest competition (a brand can boost a billboard cheaper); lead gen + conversion = "where the magic/money is." Sweet spot = the OVERLAP of awareness AND conversion. Every high 5/6/7-figure deal Chris did had "lots of logo placement AND lots of lead gen AND lots of conversion."
- **Low value assets:** branded clothing, logo on press release, logo on website, giving out marketing info, signage, a single social post, a sponsor mention.
- **High value assets:** broadcast rights, content on your website, media partners, sampling rights, speaking opportunities.
- "Low value is not the same as no value." Aim for a mix of low-/mid-/high-value (as many high as possible). Discard truly worthless assets.

**Asset categories to remember:** pass-through benefits (passed to the audience), pass-through rights (limited use of a brand's assets), naming rights, category exclusivity/entitlement.

**Value every asset on your list** (50 assets = value all 50; 150 = all 150). No shortcut.

## 5. Valuation & Pricing

**Core method (recurring):**
1. List every property; build a per-property inventory of every asset (no consolidating).
2. Research the independent market rate for each asset, one by one. The defining question: **"What would my sponsor pay for this WITHOUT me?"** — i.e., the equivalent in media/marketing, NOT what other events charge. (Sources: student newspapers/magazines for that audience; run an ad campaign to price social; compare leadership-conference speaking fees for speaking slots; pull **rate cards** for real media costs.)
3. Adjust each asset up or down based on what you uniquely bring (production quality, ultra-targeted audience, exclusivity, reputation).
4. Add **brand value** (intangible) back in — your earning power vs. your regular rate of return; factors: events you participate in, exclusivity, reputation, offer uniqueness, direct-sales opportunities, activation opportunities, audience loyalty & size. (Collective's tool uses a 20-question brand-value calculator.)
5. Group assets into CUSTOM packages built WITH the sponsor.
6. Re-valuate at least yearly (also on significant audience-size change, when channels change, before a new cycle). "Unless it's Snapple, prices fluctuate."

**Eventbrite 3-step pricing:** (1) determine market rate (research competitors' prospectuses; you also compete with all other ad channels); (2) find perceived value (sponsor's shoes — if perceived value > price they buy; if activation cost < price you profit); (3) DON'T send your proposal — get a meeting and ask them how much the opportunity is worth to them. Treat pricing as "test-and-iterate."

**Always factor HARD COSTS** (signage production/install, shipping, booth build, staff time) into the valuation/invoice or you subsidize the sponsorship. (FOCUS improved margins by putting screens/production hard costs into sponsor packages instead of eating them.)

**Never print prices in the package.** Value everything, keep numbers available, but don't print them. "The only person responsible for knowing the price is you." If you price naming rights at $20K and it's worth $40K, the sponsor won't correct you.

**Don't copy competitors' prices / don't guess** ("the shoulder-shrug method" / "copying off a kid who also doesn't know the answers"). Overpricing gets you ghosted; underpricing means quiet money loss. Sponsors increasingly require a **third-party valuation** before proceeding. If a sponsor says yes without asking for your valuation, you're charging too little.

**See §15 for all valuation numbers, ROI ratios, multiyear thresholds, and CPM/logo figures.**

## 6. Activation

**Definition:** Activation = bringing a sponsorship to life via experiences/touchpoints that achieve the sponsor's OUTCOMES while delivering real value to the audience; "switching on" / "leveraging" the sponsorship; a form of experiential marketing; "where the rubber hits the pavement." "Sponsorship = activation. Period." "If we're going to replace the word sponsorship with anything, it should be with *experiential activations*." Activation is what replaces gold/silver/bronze.

**The two-need rule (recurring):** A good activation must do exactly two things — **(a) fulfill one need/interest of your audience AND (b) fulfill one need of your sponsor.** If it hits both, cost is irrelevant.

**Special Sauce / activation-finding method:** You (the property) sit in the MIDDLE between Stakeholder 1 (sponsor) and Stakeholder 2 (your audience — NOT you). (1) Pick 3 things you KNOW your sponsors want (because you asked). (2) Pick 3 things your audience wants (fun, free stuff, networking, deeper mission connection). (3) Brainstorm activations at the intersection. (4) List 3–5 activation ideas instead of gold/silver/bronze. Core trick: "How can we give our audience MORE free stuff AND satisfy a sponsor goal?" Don't just hand over your email list — coach the sponsor to do something so incredible the audience *wants* to give up their email.

**Activation made easy — 3 steps:** (1) survey your audience (what they value/dislike/want more of); (2) talk to the sponsor about the results → app sponsorships, contests, co-created content; (3) discuss measurable outcomes (leads, awareness lift, engagement) → design around BOTH audience value and sponsor goals.

**Cost doesn't matter / can be free.** Activation can cost $500 to $50,000 — or be free. Doesn't need to be novel; improve/tweak an existing idea. Get the sponsor to co-create (they understand their own problems best). Activation spend often **doubles or triples** the sponsorship fee (esp. sports/festivals/experiential). On the brand side, budget ≥10% of total sponsorship value on activation (significantly more for coupons/products/parties/national sales-team travel); spending $0 on activation = wasting the investment.

**Build an activation plan/strategy** using the package as a guide: key dates, deadlines, and the team member accountable for each piece. Sets up the fulfillment report. Capture social content from every activation; design activations to be shareable (event + sponsor hashtags).

**Booth lesson:** order NO chairs so staff must engage the crowd — people rarely interrupt a seated rep.

**Emerging activation types:** Phygital (physical + digital, onsite tied to digital rewards/data), AR/VR engagement, NFT activation, metaverse, creator-economy collabs.

## 7. Prospecting & Pipeline Building

**Prospect on AUDIENCE, not money/fame.** Quiz answer: prospect on (B) your audience's preferences — NOT (A) how much money you think they have or (C) brand popularity. "The more money they have, the harder it will be to get them to give you some." Big brands are oversaturated (~10,000 unsolicited proposals/month) → low response odds.

**The Prospecting Formula:** `A1 + I(A2 × V) = P` → **Audience + Inventory (Sponsorship Assets × Market Value) = Prospects.**

**Source #1 = your audience survey.** Brands your audience names = "wish list of dream sponsors"; more mentions = higher on the list ("straight from the horse's mouth").

**The Four Prospect Circles / Groups (warmest → coolest) — concentric ripple:**
1. **Circle 1 (warmest):** brands the audience already buys/spends on (and *plans* to buy from — best survey question: "Which brands do you plan to purchase from in the next 18 months?" → 20–30 brands).
2. **Circle 2:** brands that advertise/market TO your audience ("Which 5–10 brands that advertised to you in the last 12 months stand out?" + study the media they consume and note who advertises there).
3. **Circle 3 (cooler — the WEAKEST category, where most first-timers wrongly start):** brands that *should* be interested based on audience data ("possible interest"). Branch by category (loves iPhones → Samsung, Google, Amazon). Interest NOT guaranteed → lots of rejection/silence.
4. **Circle 4 (coolest but still > Circle 3):** direct competitors of every brand in Circles 1–3 (add 3–4 each). Research-heavy; can yield hundreds.

**Endemic sponsors** (products naturally align, e.g., food brand at a food festival) have value but are NOT your warmest prospects — "should not be your bread and butter."

**Find a champion close to home first, then expand** (Memphis/Home Depot: started with the *local store district manager*; easy to meet, vs. impossible to reach the CMO). "Controlling the recruiting base close to home and then expanding out."

**Research people with the right job titles**, not the right form: sponsorship, marketing, sales, communications, brand, experiential marketing, product development, HR. The Seven Access Points to sponsorship dollars: Sales, Sponsorship, Marketing, Branding, Communications, Product Development, HR → get 1–3 contacts per department (~21 per company).

**Warm-network tactics ("Cowboy Way" — warm beats cold):**
- **Host a networking breakfast:** a board member hosts at their office + pays for coffee/snacks; that board member + 2 others each invite 3 professional contacts; after 20 min networking, host introduces you; you speak ≤3 minutes (who you are + what you want); ask for only TWO things — (1) each person meets you for coffee, (2) someone hosts the next event. Costs you nothing; "I have raised tons of sponsorship dollars with this approach."
- **Use your board 1-on-1:** coffee + ask their *advice* on connecting to a sector → ask who they know → intros. Pre-research their LinkedIn; ask for a specific intro.
- **Referrals from current partners:** once you've delivered, ask for a warm intro to someone at a NON-competitive company.
- **Tap network beyond CEOs/CSR/HR:** colleagues, vendors, past partners; maintain quarterly check-ins so the relationship isn't cold when you ask.
- **Social media ask:** post publicly that you're seeking a sponsor, describe the type, invite DMs (mostly duds + a few diamonds).

**List size:** Generate 20–50 (even 100+) prospects even if you only have 1–2 slots (backups for sponsors who can't afford you, are overbooked, out of budget, or ghost). "The more the merrier." Caveat: the further from brands your audience names directly, the "cooler" the prospect.

**Always be prospecting** — never stop, even in recessions (10x prospecting, expect 100x more NOs; competitors who quit "have just taken themselves out of the field"). Re-verify aged lists (company still exists, still in the niche that drew you). Start prospecting the day after your current event ends.

**Tools (Elena Saras):** Apollo, Wiza, Lusha (paid prospecting); ChatGPT, Perplexity (best for factual contact discovery), Notebook LM, Manus/Genspark/agentic tools. See §16/§20 for AI prospecting workflow.

## 8. Outreach (cold email/call, follow-up cadences)

**Single goal of every outreach: book a DISCOVERY MEETING.** Never to pitch or send a proposal. "You should have the meeting scheduled by the time you hang up the phone or ping-pong a few emails."

**Go direct to a named person**, not contact forms. **Never** email `hello@`, `customerservice@`, `info@`, or `sponsorship@`, and never use website contact forms (a sponsor's tool to avoid reading proposals; message goes to an inbox checked "once a month or never"). A mutual/warm contact raises reply odds but is "nice to have," not required.

**Never use the word "sponsorship" (or "partnership") in outreach.** Frame as "pick your brain about a cool project," "see if there's common ground." (See the "Don't Say Sponsorship" 10-call challenge in §16.)

**Emails must be SHORT — 50 words or under** ("even that's pushing it"; chunk 07 cites 50–125 words / Campaign Monitor). No 1,000–5,000-word emails. "As soon as your emails start to reek of sales, they don't work."

**YOU propose a specific date/time** ("Does Thursday at 4:15 work?"), don't ask "when are you free?" (causes ping-pong). Giving a specific time changes the decision from yes/no to "does that time work?" (Liz proof: "Are you free Tuesday at 2?" → 27 people replied yes.)

**Email AND call — not either/or.** Send ~3 emails/LinkedIn messages to warm up a cold call.

**Outreach cadence (two compatible framings):**
- **7×7 method:** 7 contact attempts per access point (department). Per contact: 3 emails → 1 call → 2 emails → 1 call. Only reach out once per business day.
- **7-day sequence:** Email d1 → Email d2 → Email d3 → Call d4 → Email d5 → Email d6 → Call d7. (Chunk 02: 5 emails + 2 calls = 7 touches.)
- If no response after the full cadence, move to the **next person in the SAME company** (person 2, then person 3) — you're leaving the individual, not the company. After 2–3 contacts each get a cadence with no reply → "read the writing on the wall" and cross off the company.
- If a sponsor replied once then went quiet, **restart** the cadence (a prior reply = higher odds of another).
- If person 3 doesn't respond, audit your emails for faux pas (below); shorten, strip sales language, ensure a compelling reason.

**Subject lines:** just their **name** ("Hi Chris") or company name; follow-ups "quick follow-up" then "last message." NEVER "I've got a great business opportunity" or "sponsorship opportunities." Goal of subject = open; goal of email = a meeting; goal of call = info for a custom proposal.

**Cold-email faux pas to AVOID:** expecting instant money; 5,000-word emails; attaching photos (spam filters); no subject line (reeks of spam); mass-emailing your whole list (recipients see each other; and if 30 say yes to "2:30 Tuesday," you can take only one); emojis in the subject line ("straight to the trash"); **attaching your proposal (the biggest faux pas).**

**Don't blast — send to ~10, not 500** ("scaling mediocrity only leads to bigger piles of mediocrity"; mass-emailing that failed 5× won't work at 100×). "Any response at all is a win." It's a game of moves management/conversions.

**Goal of outreach is never to chat about weather** — book the discovery session.

## 9. Discovery Meetings

**Purpose:** Learn the prospect's challenges, goals, target audience, how they acquire customers, what they've tried, where they've succeeded/failed — to decide fit. NOT a sales meeting. "Discovery is asking them advice, not trying to sell them stuff." Be an **investigative reporter**. **Diagnose, then prescribe** (a doctor doesn't prescribe before hearing a symptom).

**Channel: any** — in person, phone, FaceTime, Zoom, even DM to set it up.

**Bring almost nothing:** pencil + paper, or ask to record. NEVER bring the proposal (not even to the 2nd meeting). No binder of printouts (rookie tell). It's "the key to the door of customized services."

**Insist on a meeting first**, even if a prospect asks for your gold/silver/bronze package. "Just send me a proposal" = code for "I'm not interested."

**Flow:** Build rapport (5–7 min small talk — weather/sports/pets; avoid sex/religion/politics/anything too personal) → set the stage / tie-down (confirm how long they have) → audience questions first ("makes me look like I know what I'm doing because I'm starting with the important stuff") → marketing goals → challenges/roadblocks → diagnose (recap their problem verbatim) → prescribe solution → close → BAMFAM. Whole discovery ≈ 30–40 min.

**Ask 5–10 questions MAX** (prepare/memorize 5–7, some posts say 10–11), interspersed casually between talking points — NOT back-to-back ("not a good-cop/bad-cop interrogation"; "grilling the prospect comes across as too accusatory and destroys the vibe"). Choose questions that bridge what you know vs. don't — **never ask anything answerable on their website/LinkedIn** (a wasted question). Ask permission to take notes/record at the start.

**Listen, don't plan your reply while they talk.** "Dig deeper" relentlessly. Repeat their language back verbatim ("Do I have that right?"). Get to NUMBERS: "How many sales? Talk numbers so we can talk ROI." Counter vague "brand awareness": "How many brand-awareness units would you like?" / "When someone becomes aware of your brand, what's the next step? Let's measure that."

**Invite the NO:** "Don't be afraid of objections — invite the no and respect it." Better to hear no in the meeting than craft a custom proposal and chase a ghost.

**Don't try to close in discovery** — but if the sponsor offers (rare), take it (Andrea closed $175K–$250K mid-discovery). End EVERY discovery by booking the next meeting (BAMFAM).

**Sponsor summit = "a discovery session on steroids"** (informational, two-way; NEVER a place to close).

## 10. Proposals & Business Cases

**Two documents (recurring):**
- **Business case (sent BEFORE a meeting):** no dollar amounts, no grids, no call to purchase. ~50% audience data + activation ideas + case studies + media footprint/social/web + a call to PHONE you. Its ONLY job = get a meeting. (For far-away/international prospects, the business case focuses on: **Where** the audience comes from, **Who** they are, **What** they earn, **Why** they're important, **What** they buy.)
- **Proposal (sent AFTER a meeting, only if asked):** has dollars/cents, fully custom, reflects exactly what they said they wanted + a relevant case study.

**Never send the proposal first / unsolicited / before any meeting.** "Most unsolicited content goes straight into the trash." Wait until the prospect asks — may be the 3rd/4th meeting or never. ~70% of Chris's closed deals used NO proposal at all; 100% had no proposal at the start. Don't ask *when* they want it — "they'll tell you." Sending early signals genericness ("I don't customize my services").

**The 50% rule (recurring):** ≥50% of every business case/proposal = audience data. (One sample deck = 70% audience data: 10 pages, 7 about audience; the first 3 pages = audience.)

**Five things every event proposal should include:** (1) **Audience data** (25+ data points, ≥3 buyer personas) — most important by far; (2) **activation ideas** (not levels/grids); (3) **≥1 case study** (prefer 3); (4) **customization** (after a meeting); (5) **contact info + strong CTA** (direct email + a phone you answer by the 2nd ring).

**Length:** Proposal ≤ 6 pages; proposal + package together ≠ 30 pages. Only ONE paragraph on ONE page for your cause/mission (charities: a few sentences, not a 10-page ode — the bulk = audience data + case studies). Use charts/graphs/tables for audience data so it's parseable.

**Design < content.** A bombastic design usually hides weak assets/tiers. "You can put lipstick on a pig, but it's still a pig." (Deck design ≈ $300–350 on 99designs/10 options if needed.)

**Customize the bulk of every proposal.** The package is an OUTCOME, not a sales tool. "The sponsorship package doesn't sell your sponsors… YOU DO!" Customization is the DEFAULT, not a bragging point — "customized" is one of the worst words to print.

## 11. Negotiation & Closing

**You close, not the document; price is negotiated here, not in the package.** A strong audience can earn MORE than your valued price (perceived ROI) — but "sponsors won't ever fight you to pay more." Closing in the package leaves no wiggle room; you need a negotiation stage.

**Believe every objection literally.** "No matter what a prospect tells you — believe them." "Only one word means no, and that's 'NO.'" "You cannot overcome an objection you believe." (See the 4 objections + responses in §16.) Pushing AFTER a firm no = actually being pushy. "Find solutions until there are no more solutions, then wish them luck, thank them, and ask for a referral."

**Decision-maker discovery (sly):** ask "Who else should be on that call on your side?" — most prospects falsely claim to be the decision-maker (a rep can't approve $10K without clearing it up the chain; that's why review takes time).

**Pricing-range ask:** "Everything we do is customized, so we can hit any budget range. Based on our conversation, what range should I stay within?" On "no budget": "No budget means we can try some really cool stuff!" → "they'll instantly remember they can only allocate $30,000."

**Use SILENCE.** After "What else can I share?" or "What do you think our next steps should be?" — say nothing until they respond.

**Multi-year (recurring):**
- **Assume the multi-year sale:** "This activation is worth ~$30,000, and just so you know, at the $30K level we focus on three-year agreements. Does that work for you?"
- Make it a **company POLICY** you can show (so it's not seen as a tactic): e.g., "any sponsorship $25K+ requires a 3-year agreement."
- Thresholds: **$25K+ → push 3-year; six figures → push 5-year** ("start at five, you might end up with three"). Not worth it on a $2K exhibit space.
- Multi-year guarantees consistent cashflow (lets you hire staff + raises your business's valuation); the alternative (same deal, higher price each year) eventually prices the sponsor out.

**Right of First Refusal (ROFR) is BAD for the property** — only benefits the sponsor, makes them hard to replace; "never enter it as commonly written." If demanded, flip it: tie it to a multi-year deal with a defined 2-week fulfillment-report window where the sponsor can exit ONLY if you violate ≤3 pre-defined terms; otherwise you invoice and proceed.

**Don't call a deal a "win win"** without provable ROI ("isn't going to cut it"). Replace "always be closing" with "always be opening."

**Contract: weak vs. strong language — see §16. Contract red flags — see §18. Naming-rights contracts — see §20 (sports).**

## 12. Fulfillment & Reporting

**Fulfillment** = making sure the sponsor uses EVERY asset they paid for AND achieving their OUTCOMES (not just delivering outputs). Good fulfillment starts with good activation. Deliver/over-deliver on everything promised; have a photographer with a shot-list of every sponsor asset.

**The fulfillment report (a.k.a. wrap-up / post-event report) — "the most important tool in sponsorship."** Produce one for EVERY sponsor of EVERY property (including in-kind deals; including any sponsor who could become a $100K sponsor — "which could be all of them"). It is your proof of delivery, ROI proof, legal protection, and the renewal springboard while the sponsor is "still excited."

**Process:** Go asset-by-asset: list the original valuation value → recast a NEW value based on actual performance → note over/under/met. Recast on actuals (promised 500 attendees worth $X but got 600 → recompute; promised 1 e-blast but did 4 → show real value delivered, e.g., charged $20K, delivered $28K). **Be HONEST about underperformance — don't hide it; sponsors see the same metrics (web traffic, leads) and will find out.** Use it as the meeting/renewal launching pad.

**Timing:** within 24–48 hrs of the event (ideally within 24 hrs / "within days"); certainly within 1–2 weeks (the renewal post allows ~2 weeks to collect + format data). Deliver it **in person or by phone**, not just emailed. Dave Stewart and Memphis are moving to **quarterly** reports for year-long deals.

**5-page template (verbatim structure):**
- **Title Page:** company name, opportunity name, location, dates, logo, website (reuse the proposal title page).
- **Page 2 — Overview:** ¶1 mission statement; ¶2 the event + testimonials from noteworthy attendees + whether it exceeded expectations; ¶3 updated audience data (pie charts/graphs), segmented (e.g., "25% of attendees are accountants earning $78k/year").
- **Page 3 — Asset/Activation/Benefit Chart (5 columns):** (1) asset/activation; (2) status [Delivered / Did Not Deliver / Over-Delivered]; (3) notes; (4) what you charged; (5) what the asset is actually worth. (Col 4 vs Col 5 = ROI proof.) Expect honesty, not all "delivered."
- **Page 4 — Photos/Samples:** pro photos of every sponsor asset; website screenshots for digital assets.
- **Page 5 — Thank-You:** brief, genuine; segue into a feedback request.

**Use it to:** invite the sponsor to a follow-up meeting (reviewed beforehand) → gather feedback → ask for renewal → ask for referrals. Gives sponsors something to "circulate internally to justify the investment again next year." Skipping it → "terribly low sponsor renegotiation rates."

## 13. Renewals & Upgrades

**Begin renewal ASAP after the event** (waiting risks the sponsor filling their schedule; local sponsors' budgets may be spent by Q2/Q3). The ~20% who leave, you want to know within DAYS, not 3 months before next year.

**Treat renewal like a NEW opportunity:** new discovery-style meeting → new audience data → new custom assets/activations → **re-value** (the renewed opp is usually MORE valuable; don't reuse the old value). **You CANNOT reuse old assets** — the sponsor's needs changed.

**The Productive Wheel / renewal cycle:** discovery → custom proposals → activations both sponsor AND audience love → deliver + fulfillment report (~within 2 weeks) → get feedback → ask for renewal → ask for referrals → nurture year-round.

**Renewal feedback questions:** What did they love/hate? What would they change/add? Which assets to increase/decrease? Did customized assets meet expectations? What did they hear from their customers? Then mention next year's plans and ask how you fit. If positive, invite back **on the spot**.

**Upgrade tactics:**
- Draft a proposal with **2 options:** Option 1 = same deal, higher price (justified only by higher-value assets or proven growth; valuation proves it); Option 2 = 3-year deal at the SAME price + a few free extra activations as thank-you (locks this year's price for all 3 years). (Hillary closed a 3-year this way → guaranteed revenue → hired staff + raised business valuation. A real client capped auto-multiyear mention at $30K deals.)
- "If you signed up for a few years and I offered a discount, would that work better?"
- **Upgrade small sponsors** — "way easier to upgrade a small sponsor into a title/multi-year sponsor than to land a $100K stranger." (Memphis: <$10K credit union → $100K stage sponsor; Home Depot: $7,500 → $150–200K activation.)

**Renewal benchmark: ≥80%** of current sponsors should agree to a feedback meeting + ask to see a draft proposal for next year. If <80% renew using the report→feedback→BAMFAM→draft-proposal method, you have a delivery/fulfillment problem. (One video cites 50–75% renewal as healthy.)

**Retention > acquisition** (recurring). Happy sponsors refer without being asked and take you with them when they change companies. "I don't care how good the bowl of spaghetti is — it's never good enough to keep your sponsors coming back for years."

## 14. Scaling, Team & Operations

**Sponsorship Moves Management System (pipeline tracking):** a diagnostic spreadsheet — one tab per prospect (50 prospects = 50 tabs). Add contact info (title, email, social, phone), a **Last Contact Date** column (spot cold contacts at a glance), and a **Notes** column. Six stages: Referral Requested → Contact Mode (log method + date; log 2nd/3rd attempts) → Response Received (any reply counts; "don't contact me again" → straight to Declined) → Meeting Booked → Proposal Submitted (only after they ask) → Approved/Declined. After several hundred prospects, compute conversion rates at each stage.

**Get internal buy-in EARLY and fast.** Sponsorship moves fast; 6 weeks of internal approval + 6 weeks of sponsor approval can blow your event deadline. "I've had a deal blown up because I didn't do the necessary work internally." Show stakeholders that expense is tied to revenue (no revenue = no expense).

**Boards of directors:** their job is ONE new introduction per month — NOT cold-calling, NOT making the sale, NOT bringing a package. Trust the professional fundraiser. Think audience, not cause.

**Bring your boss along:** tell them about the new approach; start small with a few prospects and let results (more meetings/replies) speak; involve them ("I'd really like a second, more senior person at the table") so they trust your judgment.

**DIY vs. broker/consultant (verdict: DIY for most).** "Nobody cares more about your opportunity than you. The person who cares the most wins." Outsourcing to a commission-only "rep with all the contacts" doesn't exist as imagined — real reps charge a big monthly retainer + 15–30% commission + want exclusivity + 6–12 months + reject 99% of inbound + prefer proven opportunities, and they require a $500K+ property. DIY profit margins (98–100%) crush brokerage (rarely >80%). A non-commission *consultant* can occasionally help but has long waitlists + premium fees. (Full math in §15; full anti-pattern list in §18.)

**Build a "talent pool"** — a maintained database/list of potential sponsors tied to your org's short/long-term goals; nurture relationships year-round so they're not cold when you ask.

**Treat sponsorship as a profession** — schedule daily prospecting/discovery/follow-up. "If it's not in your calendar, it's not going to happen."

**Multi-year deals reduce prospecting load** and enable hiring (guaranteed cashflow). Hillary used a 3-year deal to justify a new staffer.

## 15. Funnel Math & Benchmarks

**Pipeline / funnel math:**
- **~40 meetings on average** to hit most sponsorship sales goals.
- **1–2 meetings/week** benchmark (easier than cramming 40 right before the event).
- **~1 in 10 prospects becomes a sponsor.**
- Required prospect volume: usually **100–200+** ("shouldn't be under 100" unless deep-pocketed sponsors); realistic 60–100+; "hundreds and hundreds," sometimes thousands. Daily outreach = needed prospects ÷ days to event.
- 7×7 = 7 attempts × 7 access points; ~21 contacts/company (1–3 per dept).
- Spaghetti/gold-silver-bronze package goes to the garbage **95% of the time.**
- Diagnostic: proposal-submitted success rate of **only ~5%** → problem with pricing, proposal format, product, or follow-up. Healthy **close rate: 30–50%** of prospects on each proposal submitted.
- "10 in a row say no → change course" (don't wait for 40).

**Email / open / reply benchmarks:**
- Email reply/open rate with the cadence/technique: **~30%** ("for the vast majority of our clients"); healthy cold-reply target **15–20%.**
- Avg email open rate: **21.33%** (2023, Mailmunch, all industries); **18–22%** early-2020s (considered high). Nonprofit open: **≥26%**, some estimates 40%. **Sports** avg open: **24.57%.**
- Email length: **under 50 words** (chunk 02) / **50–125 words** (Campaign Monitor). Not 500/1,000/5,000.
- General email CTR: **2.5%** (2023, Emma); good range **2–10%.** Nonprofit CTR **~3.27%**; **sports** avg CTR **3.09%.**
- Nonprofit conversion rate: **17–20%** (good). Nonprofit bounce: **60–70%** typical, target ~40% or below. Nonprofit cart abandonment: **82–83%.**

**Audience-data depth:** **20–25 data points/segment** (also 25, 30+, ≥35); ≥3 avatars at the same depth.
**Survey methodology (Chris's actual):** Pro SurveyMonkey ≈ **$350/year**; two e-blasts (Tue + Thu, 10am); win one of three **$25 gift cards**; → **30+ data points at 95% confidence / 5% margin of error**; 5,000 database → **256 responses** suffice; survey = 20+ questions (last ~8 unrelated). Mallory: **100+ responses in 30 minutes.** Christine Thompson: 100–250 responses enough (#1 brand at 15 of 250 when next was 2–8).

**Timing / cadence:**
- **Best window to approach sponsors: mid-September to mid-December** (companies plan in fall, Sept–Nov, set next year's budget). Miss it → fight for leftover dollars.
- **"Too late" = event < 6 months out.** Start prospecting the day after your current event ends.
- Survey every 6 months (data ≤6 months old); give ≥1 month to respond.
- Fulfillment report: within **24 hrs** (some say 24–48 hrs / "within days" / within 1–2 weeks).
- Valuation review: ≥1×/year.
- Internal buy-in: beware 6 wk internal + 6 wk sponsor approval missing the event.
- A program running 6–9 months (or a year+) with zero direct sponsor contact = stuck in "motion."

**Valuation / pricing numbers:**
- **Logo value: ~1/3 of a penny per view ("if you're lucky")** / "three cents per view at most" / "a few pennies to a few dollars for 1,000 views" / "¼ of one penny per impression." 150–200 views × 3¢ = trivial. Logo-only ≈ **7%** of total opportunity value.
- **ROI ratios sponsors demand: 2:1, 3:1, or 4:1.** 2:1 means a $30K-valued opportunity → they won't pay >$15K, OR you build a $60K package to justify $30K. "A $50K sign sold at $50K = $0 ROI." A buyer spending $16K on a $50K sign = "average job performance." Aim for ≥2:1 per activation; include an ROI ratio on the front end as your negotiation range.
- **Activation budget (brand side): ≥10%** of total sponsorship value (significantly more for coupons/products/parties/travel). Activation spend often doubles/triples the fee.
- **Podcast CPM baseline:** 30–60-sec mid-roll ≈ **$30** (2022) / ~$25 CPM; up to $50 for quality/targeting; down to $20–25 for a new show. Most podcasts can't survive on the CPM/download model (only Joe Rogan-scale can).
- **Multi-year thresholds:** ≥$25,000 → 3-year; six figures → 5-year. Client policy example: auto-mention multiyear at $30,000+.
- **Valuation formula example:** cost-per-name-in-wordmark **$0.0063 × 5,000,000 views ≈ $31,000** (naming rights). New website asset ≈ **$5,000.** Twitter campaign: firm charges $10K → undercut at **half ($5K)** to win a social-inept sponsor. 1 tweet (5,000 followers) ≈ **$66.58.** (Calculator demo used a deliberately fake **$1.8M** for a 5-min talk to make a point — "do not use these values.")
- **2025–26 valuation reference ranges:** program/initiative naming rights ≈ low five figures/yr; event/venue naming rights = five-to-six figures (by reach/duration/prominence); digital impressions CPM-style; email value driven by list quality/segmentation; booth value driven by foot traffic + activation.
- Speaking-from-stage ≈ hundreds of dollars per attendee.

**Sponsor expectation stats (Festivals — what sponsors anticipate receiving):** reinforce image **76%**, increase brand loyalty **72%**, boost awareness/visibility **70%**, show social responsibility **43%**, push product usage/sales **42%**, generate more traffic **39%**, introduce a service/product **36%**, get more databases **31%.**

Worked example: $3K–$5K/mo × 6-mo minimum = **$18,000** even at zero results, PLUS 20% of $500K = **$100,000** commission (evergreen). DIY: ~$10K internal staff on a $500K program → **98% profit margin yr 1, 100% yr 2**; brokerage rarely >80%.

## 16. Scripts & Templates

### Cold email — "find the right contact" (one sentence; led to an $85,000 deal)
> "Hey Ray, Any chance you can suggest the best contact for those in the product X side at your company? Thanks, [your name]"

Typical reply: "Hey Chris, Yes, you would contact Mike. He is the product X marketing manager. I've copied him on this email."

Then to Mike:
> "Hi Mike, Just following up on Ray's note. I would love to set up a quick call to discuss some of your areas of focus and see if there is some common ground between your priorities and our plans for the year. How does your schedule look on Wednesday? Does 3:00 work for a phone call?"

### Cold email #2 (ego/advice angle)
> "Hey [name], I saw [on social media/website] that you are involved in related products/projects. I would love to connect and ask your thoughts about a cool project I'm working on. Are you free tomorrow at 3:00? [your name]"

### "Hey Dave" template (LinkedIn)
> "Saw on LinkedIn that you're involved in marketing for X product. I would love to connect and ask your thoughts about a cool project that I'm working on. Are you free tomorrow at three? — Chris" (No title, just name.)

### Audience-research email #3 (company had a strong survey showing)
> "Hi [name], I saw on [social/website] that you are involved in the marketing side of [company]. I recently surveyed our database and [company] had a strong showing. I'm wondering if you have a couple of minutes to talk about what we found and whether our audience is one of your priorities? Any chance you're free this week for a quick call? [your name]"

### The 3-email sequence (swipe templates)
- **Email 1 — Subject "Hi [Name]":** "I saw you're involved in the marketing side of [company]. We recently surveyed our database and your company had a strong showing. Do you have a few minutes to talk about what we found, whether or not our audience is your audience? Let me know. — Chris"
- **Email 2 (2 days later) — Subject "quick follow-up":** "Hey [Name], no doubt you get a thousand emails a day, sorry to add one more 😉. We recently surveyed our database, your company had a strong showing. Would love to learn about what you're trying to accomplish."
- **Email 3 — Subject "Hey, happy Monday, me again":** "I don't want to keep sending you emails if you're the wrong person to follow up with. Before I follow someone else in your org, I thought I would ask if you can suggest someone internally who is looking to connect with [Segment 1] / [Segment 2] / [Segment 3]… would be really interested in your reply. Have a great week." (Then copy the original message.)

### Event outreach
- "Hey [Chris], saw on LinkedIn that you work with [company]. We recently did some analysis of our audience and your company had a strong showing. Wondering if you'd be interested in seeing what info about our audience and what we're up to. Let me know. — [name]"
- Follow-up: "Hey [name], no doubt you get a thousand emails a day, sorry to add one more. Our audience of [main hobby/descriptor] may be of interest to you and I thought I'd reach out — wondering if I can send some more information your way."

### Conference discovery-invite (≤3 sentences)
> "Hey [sponsor], I see that you do X. I have a conference that attracts [these people]. Do you want to jump on a quick call and see whether we have an opportunity to work together?" — "If it's three sentences, it's too long."

### Small-shop first-email content rule
Contain "as little as possible: No details about the event, no gold-silver-bronze levels, no titles in your email signature, no asks of any kind — simply a request to pick their brain about a project you're working on and a proposed date and time for a meeting."

### Athlete cold-email rules
Use their name; intro with a credibility hook ("five-time winner of X marathon," "podcast host," "athlete with 100,000 followers"); ≤3 sentences (who you are, what you want, why they'd care); do NOT ask for money — offer a solution ("I love your products and would like to introduce them to my fans"); suggest a call time; NEVER attach a proposal (email signature with social links is fine).

### The "Don't Say Sponsorship" 10-call challenge
For your next 10 calls, never use "sponsor/sponsors/sponsored/sponsorship" — and NOT "partner/partnership" either. Forces you to *describe* your offering. If the prospect says "that sounds like sponsorship," ask what *they* mean (they reveal the gold/silver/bronze stereotype), then explain yours is custom. Awkward at first; smooth by call 3–6.

### "We don't do sponsorship" rebuttal (Joan's script)
> "Well, neither do we. The only thing I'm interested in doing is connecting you to your target market. The only person who knows how you get more clients and how you measure success is you. Why don't we get together — you fill me in on what you're trying to achieve and how I can help. If putting logos on stuff is part of that, so be it, but I'm not committed to any particular package or process. I want to build something custom just for you."
Joan's closer: "What if I could put 30 of your ideal clients in a room and give you special access to network with them like peers — a unique experience they've specifically requested through our surveys — no sales pitch, no logo placement?" → "Oh, *that* I have budget for. Tell me how much you need."

### The $25,000 reframe (Mena Gainpaulsingh — most-quoted script)
Ask: "Why have you never sponsored this property?" → "We have no money for sponsorship, purse strings are tight, limited ROI." Reframe (avoid "sponsorship"):
> "What if we could fill a room with your target market and your most desired customers? And what if you could invest some of your business development money to host them in a private VIP event — just you and them, chatting about their goals, getting to know them in a laid-back environment — and then run a contest to get them to give you their contact information for a future cup of coffee?"
→ "Well, that's different! I have a budget for exactly that type of thing… I could approve $25,000 as a starting point by the end of the week."

### "Show up empty-handed" close
> "Go to your next meeting with nothing in your hands — no sponsorship package or one-pagers — and ask them about their goals, their target market, and how they get new customers."
> "If you don't have your prospect's target audience, don't try to sell them a sponsorship package. Instead, ask them who in their network would have interest in your audience and turn a 'no thanks' into a handful of referrals."

### "Just send me a proposal" response (the Five Words You Never Want to Hear)
> "No — not until you tell me what you're trying to achieve, otherwise I wouldn't even know what to put in the proposal. We don't have a stock sponsorship package, we don't have a standardized list of gold/silver/bronze benefits — everything we do is custom. I'd love to get to know your goals, how you're allocating your budget, the things you're trying to accomplish. If we can't help you accomplish that, there's no sense submitting a package."
Caveat: if you already have verbal agreement and the ONLY next step is a proposal, send it; if you'd have to *guess* even one thing, don't.

### The 4 objections + exact responses
Rule: "No matter what a prospect tells you — believe them." Only "NO" means no.
1. **"We don't have the budget right now"** → not a no. Find out when they budget; offer to split payments across their fiscal if they come on now; or ask what budget they're working with. "Either they tell you or give you a firm no. Either way, it's a win."
2. **"I'm really busy and don't have time"** → not a no. "I'm sorry for interrupting a busy time — when should I plan to get back in touch?" OR offer a 5-minute call (they can hang up at the 5-min mark and you promise never to call again).
3. **"Just send me something by email"** → sounds like yes, isn't. Don't send a deck; say everything you do is custom and ask about their goals.
4. **"Let me think about it"** → ask what they need to decide; ask them to bring their decision-makers and you'll do the same.

### Frontline "Advocate" script (turned 45 No's into ~$1.5M)
Call a "no" back and ask them to: (1) name which outcomes matter most; (2) split into **must-haves vs. nice-to-haves**; (3) give an honest price they'd pay for those outcomes. Take the goal-list + budget back to your boss as an **ultimatum** → you become the sponsor's advocate, not a salesperson.

### Sponsorship Sales Meeting Script (discovery, step-by-step, verbatim lines)
**Set the Stage / Tie-Down:**
- "Quick check-in. Are you still okay to talk until 4 o'clock?"
- "I want to be respectful of your time, and we only have X minutes, so let's dive in!"
- "The reason for the call is to see whether we share common goals or a common audience and whether we have the potential to work together. If it's okay with you, I'd like to learn more about…"

**Digging Deeper (use constantly):**
- "Interesting! Why do you think that is?" / "Tell me more about that." / "And what else?"
- **"What do you mean by that?"** (Chris's favorite — busts BS/jargon, forces specifics; "if anything, you sound smart." Use SPARINGLY — every reply is "a lot weird.")

**Identify Challenges:**
- "I'd love to hear what's working for your company and what isn't, if that's okay."
- "What is working well for you right now? Why?"
- "What could be a potential solution to X problem? What have you tried?"
- "What kinds of marketing are you doing now? What's working and what isn't?"
- "What feedback do you hear from your customers that we might be able to help with?"
- "What challenges are you experiencing right now in finding new customers?"
- "What metrics are you responsible for achieving? What are your biggest problems achieving them?"
- "What are you afraid will happen if it doesn't get solved?"

**Diagnose / Recap:**
- "Do you mind if I recap, just so we're both clear? Your company is having X problem preventing you from achieving Y. You want to do Z by A time."
- "[ideal customer restated verbatim]… do I have that right?"

**Prescribe / Frame Solution:**
- "Does it make sense for me to share a bit about how we work with the audiences you've mentioned?"
- "We focus on a sponsor's goal outcomes and then match it up with their target audience, creating an experience that engages people in meaningful ways."
- Share a past example with a concrete outcome ("$10,000 more sales, 1,000 new customers, 5,000 new Facebook followers").
- "What else can I share with you?" — then **say nothing** until they respond.
- "It sounds like we may have a fit. Do you agree?"

**Close / Get Requirements:**
- "What do you wish you could do to attract your audience?" / "Tell me some of your ideas for engaging your audience?"
- "What kind of metrics should we be focusing on measuring?"
- "What assets and opportunities do you see as essential for a successful sponsorship?"

**Next Steps + Pricing:**
- "I hate to cut our conversation short, but you said you had only 45 minutes, and we're coming up on that. What do you think our next steps should be?" — then **wait, say nothing**.
- If they ask for the proposal (often a brush-off): offer "a draft you can review to determine if we're on the right track," then book the day/time to discuss it.
- Pricing: "Everything we do is customized, which means we can hit any budget range. Based on our conversation, what range should I stay within?"
- On budget pushback: "No budget means we can try some really cool stuff!"
- BAMFAM version of the proposal brush-off: "Absolutely. And just for my records, what budget range should I stay within? … Is there anything else besides what we talked about I should include? … Let me summarize what I heard. … Great, let's meet Thursday at 3 to go over the draft together."

## 17. Named Concepts & Glossary

- **BAMFAM** — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting." Schedule the next meeting while still in the current one (any meeting type) so you never chase or get ghosted. The single most-repeated tactic. "The very best properties never chase."
- **Property** — your sponsorable opportunity (event, festival, naming rights, podcast, team, athlete, building, communications platform); unites assets via a shared audience + common communications goals. NOT "give me money."
- **Asset** — any discrete sellable benefit (tangible/intangible, physical/virtual); valued individually. Most valuable = intangible. Logos = the least valuable.
- **Inventory** — the full list of assets across all properties; build the biggest list possible.
- **Activation** — experiences/touchpoints that achieve the sponsor's outcomes while delivering audience value; "switching on" / experiential marketing; "cool experiences for your audience" connecting them to the sponsor. Replaces gold/silver/bronze.
- **Leverage** — using/activating all elements of a sponsorship to achieve outcomes (vs. passive exposure).
- **Valuation** — research-based market value assigned to each asset; required, annual. Guessing = the "shoulder-shrug method."
- **Brand value (intangible value)** — your earning power vs. your regular return; a calculated % added back into valuation.
- **Fulfillment** — making sure sponsors use every asset they paid for AND achieving their outcomes.
- **Fulfillment report / wrap-up report / post-event report** — document proving delivery; ROI proof; legal protection; the first step to renewal.
- **Discovery / exploratory session** — first, informal, no-selling meeting to learn the sponsor's goals/challenges/audience and test fit.
- **Business case** — informal marketing doc sent BEFORE a meeting (~50% audience data, no dollars/grids); goal = get a meeting. For international prospects covers Where/Who/What-earn/Why/What-buy.
- **Proposal** — sales doc sent AFTER a meeting (has dollars; fully custom).
- **Avatar / buyer persona** — a defined niche audience segment (≥3 required), each with its own 20–25+ data points; an amalgamation of traits used to infer which brands advertise to them.
- **Curator of audience** — Mark Sabourand's framing: "a curator of audience, not a placer of logos."
- **The 50% rule** — half of every business case/proposal = audience data.
- **Happy ears** — believing a polite brush-off ("send me a proposal," "we'll call you") is a real yes; "the number one cause of budget failure in the sponsorship industry."
- **Logo soup / gold-silver-bronze (GSB)** — the outdated tiered grid; the channel's central villain. "If you have a grid labeled gold/silver/bronze, stop."
- **Spaghetti method vs. Sniper** — Spaghetti = throw everything at the wall (GSB, apologetic, wasteful). Sniper = perfect clarity on assets/value/audience/the prospect's ideal customer/how they measure success/what they'll pay/the activation plan; brings NOTHING to meetings; talks only to perfect-fit prospects.
- **Motion vs. Action** — Motion = internal busywork without the sponsor (building packages, writing sales letters, brainstorming, guessing values, GSB). Action = external steps toward the goal (discovery calls, surveying, building the fulfillment report). "Motion is revving your engine while the car is in park; action is putting it in drive."
- **Lead vs. Lag measures** (from *The 4 Disciplines of Execution*) — Lag = KPIs/outcomes you can't directly control (revenue, attendance, open rates). Lead = daily activities you control that drive lags. 5 sponsorship lead measures: (1) prospect-research count/day; (2) outreach quality + response rate; (3) follow-up cadence + results; (4) discovery sessions booked; (5) custom proposals prospects actually asked to see.
- **Tie-down** — confirming how much time the prospect has; exposes lies/discomfort about later topics (e.g., paying).
- **In-kind / contra / trade / gift-in-kind** — non-cash sponsorship. "An in-kind donation is NOT a sponsorship." Only accept if it's true **budget relief** (offsets a real cost) or convertible/leverageable (e.g., a media partner you can leverage 10×); otherwise "a distraction at best and costly at worst." The "free" value is unrelated to hard cost — run it through your valuation. (Kevin Grothe.)
- **Cash vs. promotional vs. contra sponsors** — the 3 sponsor types. Cash = most flexible; promotional = reach/audience/guests (great for new podcasts); contra = in-kind.
- **Endemic sponsor** — products naturally align with your property (food brand at a food festival); has value but not warmest.
- **Pass-through benefits / pass-through rights** — benefits passed to the audience / limited rights to use a brand's assets.
- **Right of First Refusal (ROFR)** — clause giving a sponsor first dibs; "terrible for properties," only benefits the sponsor. Does NOT equal a multiyear deal.
- **CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility)** — corporate-giving program operating "much like foundation grants"; one stream of corporate fundraising (keep separate from sponsorship, which is unrestricted revenue).
- **Cause marketing** — a distinct third stream beyond CSR and sponsorship; for-profit + nonprofit campaigns (point-of-sale, BOGO, matching gifts, pledge drives) tied to a social cause.
- **BOGO** — "buy one, give one" (customer buys → you donate one).
- **Big Brand Redemption** (trendwatching.com) — big brands *owning* an issue (becoming synonymous with resolving it) rather than merely *sponsoring* a cause; "the difference between soloist and choir." "The risk is not in overreaching and failing, but in not reaching at all."
- **Presenting sponsor** — prominent recognition tier.
- **Naming rights** — temporary (occasionally permanent) moniker bestowed on a sponsor (e.g., NASCAR Coca-Cola 600; name a festival stage).
- **Category exclusivity / entitlement** — industry exclusivity and granted rights.
- **Sponsorship fit / congruence** — perceived alignment between sponsor and property.
- **Sponsee** — the party receiving sponsorship.
- **Make-goods** — compensation for unmet deliverables.
- **ROO (Return on Objectives)** — non-financial outcome measure (vs. ROI = financial).
- **BATNA** — Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement. **BAFO** — Best And Final Offer.
- **Ambush marketing** — gaining event exposure without paying official fees.
- **Above/Below-the-Line** — mass-media (broad) vs. targeted/experiential/digital.
- **CPM / CPP** — cost per thousand impressions / cost per reach point.
- **Media equivalencies** — estimated value of earned exposure.
- **Rate card** — advertising price list used to benchmark sponsorship asset values against real media costs.
- **Local champion** — an accessible insider (e.g., a store manager) who becomes your foot-in-the-door vs. the unreachable CEO.
- **RFID** — tech to gate VIP areas / limit participation / capture identity at events.
- **ABO (Always Be Opening)** — Greg Hickman's mindset replacing ABC.
- **Technician vs. business-owner/marketer** (E-Myth) — the failure mindset vs. the owner mindset.
- **Emerging terms:** Metaverse sponsorship, NFT activation, Creator economy, **Phygital activation** (physical + digital), AI-powered measurement, AR/VR engagement, Web3 sponsorship, **Zero-party data** (data audiences intentionally share via quizzes/gated content).

## 18. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

**The Seven Deadly Sins:** (1) lack of valuation; (2) proposal-first method (emailing a proposal without permission = spam list + reputation damage); (3) misusing in-kind as primary; (4) treating sponsorship as philanthropy ("pulling on heartstrings ≠ pulling on purse strings"); (5) no activation strategy/budget; (6) missing fulfillment report; (7) not understanding the sales cycle (launching sales in spring for a fall event).

**Seven MORE Deadly Sins:** (1) not knowing what you want (define needs/figures + sponsor type first); (2) being afraid to follow up; (3) following up too much (don't re-contact within an hour/day; give ≥1 week / ≥3 business days); (4) having happy ears; (5) misusing discovery as a sales meeting; (6) being too cause-focused (99% of causes don't drive marketing outcomes); (7) befriending the sponsor instead of partnering (your contact rarely has final say).

**Festival dealbreakers (7):** waiting too long; sending the proposal early; assuming "it's all about the Benjamins"; skipping audience data; no discovery; not knowing your value; no fulfillment report.

**Package/proposal mistakes:**
- **Starting with the package/proposal** (it's the outcome, not the start; guessing page-17 for a hypothetical sponsor wastes time).
- **Sending the proposal too early/unprompted** (trash; the Shark-Attack/ice-cream confounding-variable fallacy — the proposal doesn't cause the sale, YOU do).
- **Gold/silver/bronze tiers** — sponsors hate them; "to the garbage 95% of the time"; all-or-nothing (great assets trapped with junk to force a purchase); renaming the tiers doesn't help (content is the problem); limits your earnings ceiling. Hybrid model is "second-best" if you can't fully customize.
- **Printing prices in the package** (sets them in stone; risks underselling; sponsors won't correct a low price).
- **Making the package all about YOU** — 10 pages of history/mission/vision/CEO bio/how much money you need = "essentially begging" (a charity-sector phenomenon).
- Writing/rewriting 15–20 drafts by committee — "very few prospects will even read your proposal."
- Bombastic design hiding weak assets — "lipstick on a pig."

**Money/ATM mistakes:** treating sponsors as walking ATMs/cash cows; selecting prospects for deep pockets/name recognition (big brands oversaturated → low odds); **asking for too little** ("a few hundred dollars" = same work, not worth it, makes you look cheap in a $60–70B industry; "Go big or go home," "smaller is not easy, smaller is boring"); treating sponsors as **investors** ("sponsors are not investors"; "if you need an investor, get a bank loan").

**Discovery/sales-mindset mistakes:** treating discovery as a sales meeting; elevator pitch; sales jargon (gatekeeper, MQL, puppy-dog close, sandbagging, tire-kickers); walking in with no questions prepared; pelting with too many questions; jumping to a quick solution too fast (kills trust even when right — "you earn the right to offer a quick solution" only after listening); pulling on heartstrings/guilt (Car Sales — empathy plays backfire).

**Audience mistakes:** no research / stale data (>6 months); bragging about raw size over niched quality ("a sponsor would rather have 50 attendees who fit than 6,000 who don't"); "we sell to everyone" (massive red flag → ghosting; even Coke/Apple don't sell to everyone); believing a small audience disqualifies you (self-fulfilling; broadcasts lack of confidence).

**Logo/vanity mistakes:** over-relying on logos (~1/3¢ per view; achieves only brand awareness sponsors often have/don't want); focusing on vanity follower counts at every size.

**Follow-up/objection mistakes:** not following up (out of fear); interpreting objections as a "no" (a career mistake that lost a longtime sponsor to a competitor); pushing AFTER a firm no.

**Fulfillment/renewal mistakes:** skipping the fulfillment report → "terribly low renegotiation rates"; letting sponsors go instead of renegotiating; reusing old assets/activations on renewal; **lying/fudging metrics** ("your sponsor will find out"); skipping the report on in-kind deals.

**Outreach mistakes:** website contact forms (worst channel; message disappears); mass-blasting; 5,000-word emails; pestering / bump-ins / elevator pitches (the banker who found people waiting in his elevator + stacks of packages on his desk; don't call personal cells at 8pm/Saturdays; don't ambush at the mall); "scaling mediocrity."

**Booth with chairs** — reps sit and text; people won't interrupt them.

**"We're different / unique"** — code for "I don't know my audience, haven't valued, am bad at sales." The Sponsorship/Partnership distinction is the same trap. ("My opportunity is different" = the #2 red-flag phrase after gold/silver/bronze.)

**"Jack of all trades" (Donuts)** — offering to do everything when mediocre at most → can't deliver → no renewal. Pick your "donut" (strongest asset) and be the best at it. Sponsors want outcomes, not a specific asset set.

**Skipping valuation / making it up** — copying competitors' numbers ("copying off a kid who also doesn't know the answer"); sponsors ask where numbers came from; lying creates an unrepeatable hole. Increasingly, brands require third-party valuation.

**Board-of-directors anti-patterns:** "You know who you should contact?" (assuming sponsorship is intuitive); confusing sponsorship with fundraising/philanthropy/donations; thinking board members need a package or must make the sale; choosing deepest pockets; asking 2 weeks before the event; failing to value/renegotiate.

**Contract red flags:** vague deliverables (biggest); unclear payment terms; **broad exclusivity** (e.g., "technology" blocks software/telecom/hardware/IT/AI — keep narrow + define duration/geography/online/on-site); one-sided cancellation; unclear reporting expectations; overlooked usage/content ownership (esp. creators/podcasts); automatic renewals (budget surprises).

**Activation mistakes:** focusing on size/visual appeal; not customizing; failing to value the activation; relying too heavily on automation (QR-only, no human → fails to convert); ignoring social-content capture; reusing activations. **Backfire tale:** a charity race scheduled long sponsor speeches before the start → delayed the race, runners left early and complained publicly → sponsors associated with negative sentiment. (Timing matters as much as content; activation should enhance, not interrupt.)

**Cause-marketing/charity mistakes:** seeing companies as cash cows; focusing only on CSR/philanthropy; "we have no audience" (you do — it's WHO, not how many); logo focus; proposal/one-pager focus; "leaving it to the big shops"; not exploring the full spectrum (CSR + sponsorship + cause marketing); doing sponsorship only for events (sponsor programming/land/buildings/naming/lunch-and-learns too — event revenue is the hardest to earn and burns out teams).

**Strategy/operations anti-patterns:** waiting too long (no dollars in 30 days → change approach); confirmation bias ("the guys up the road use GSB so it must work"); indecisive / slow to act (running every decision past the board — "you cannot overcome an objection you believe"); not getting internal buy-in (deals blown up by stakeholders); ROI-backwards ("I'll invest once I've made the money" — "once I lose the weight, then I'll give up donuts"); raising prices 3–5%/year "because things cost more" (short-term thinking divorced from delivered value); treating sponsorship as a side-of-desk hobby; stopping prospecting in a downturn (10x it instead).

**Outsourcing/marketplace fantasies:** outsourcing to a commission-only "rep with all the contacts" (doesn't exist as imagined; see §14); believing in a sponsor marketplace/app/magic Rolodex (sponsors' problem is too MANY proposals — Linda paid $25K + 8 months → went out of business).

**Lessons-learned hard way (named):** taking the first media sponsor that replies (Alan Diamond — wait for best fit; impatience cost a year); not training new staff / no artwork sign-off (Goldman — sponsors must approve artwork before printing); lack of research (ordering a Diet Coke at a Pepsi renewal lunch; FedEx-ing a UPS proposal; pitching social co-promo to a brand that doesn't promote nonprofit partners on social); proceeding with a half-baked (~65%) event (media partners drop, attendance/traffic crater, empty hotel blocks you must pay for); greenwashing (announcing "carbon neutral in 6 months" then taking 3× longer looks worse than honesty).

**Keeping money on a cancelled event = "stealing"** — even if legally allowed, it torches the relationship (half-the-clothes cashier analogy).

Both a hundreds-of-thousands property and a hundreds-of-people property each landed million-dollar deals. (Memorable charity: 100 attendees buying $500K homes → real estate/lawyers/accountants.)

## 20. Industry-Specific Playbooks

### Events / Festivals
- Sponsorship should aim to cover **~100% of operating costs** (then tickets/F&B = profit; rain doesn't hurt). Survey-driven avatars unlock non-obvious categories (under-21 → sportswear/travel, not alcohol). Group activations geographically into a high-traffic hub; turn activations into points-of-sale. Need LOTS of sponsors → big list (100+); expect non-responses. Send the fulfillment report ~1 week after.
- 5 steps: inventory building → valuation → audience segmenting → proposal → fulfillment.
- "Sponsorship magnet" methods: niche down; data-rich case studies (don't name the sponsor; show pain points solved + photos); focus on the sponsor not your festival; learn from mistakes; promise only what you can deliver.
- Activation ideas: ugly-statue/creative contest (auto-enroll), bathroom branding, phone-charging stations (or branded portable chargers), branded food/cups.
- **2025 major-event trends:** pickleball activations (DinkDunk Pickleball Spa × Destination Canada; The Kitchen at Coachella); temporary pop-ups (Laneige Watermelon Pop truck + free sorbets); "strange bedfellows" (Nat Geo holographic animals at NYFW; Tide oversized bottle at SXSW); non-sports brands at sports (LG live podcast at NCAA Final Four; Puma × Cheetos at NBA All-Star); hybrid/phygital + AR/VR + data-driven measurement now expected. **Adapt trends to YOUR audience; don't copy.**

### Local businesses
- "Local business ≠ tiny business." Advantages: walk in and ask if they do sponsorship; meet decision-makers at local networking breakfasts; start with businesses YOU already spend money at (your bank/credit-union manager). Plan for SEVERAL sponsors, not one big one (a food bank raised real money via many **$500** sponsors). Still define your audience (not "everyone in the community") and know your value vs. local advertising (a $1,000 newspaper ad vs. your $5,000 logo+eblast — be competitive). Selling is **in person** — don't drop a package at the front desk. Co-buy postcards with a neighboring business to double reach. Local benefits (10): easier to acquire; strengthens community; cuts costs; familiar audience; expands brand; new audiences; differentiation; local SEO; goodwill; trust-by-association. Raise repeat business at report delivery (their budget may be spent by Q2/Q3).

### Nonprofits / Charities
- "Small shop" = under $500K/yr (Hydra) OR under $2M / <3 revenue-staff (Charity Edition). Most dangerous trap: philanthropy mindset (cause-first, sponsor-as-donor, accept whatever's given). 5 failure modes: too cause-focused; assume sponsors just give; don't know your value; don't get what you actually need (asked $20K, needed $40K → cut corners → underwhelmed sponsor); won't retain.
- Fixes (6): know your audience (25 data points/group incl. donors + attendees); don't skip discovery; minimize the cause (a few sentences, not a 10-page ode — bulk = audience data + case studies); value your opportunity; skip GSB; produce a fulfillment report.
- "Best way to secure a deal is through personal contacts." Evaluate every solicitation case-by-case (Linda Schwab, ThinkLA) — more labor-intensive but "much more favorable." Volunteer-committee model: volunteers solicit using THEIR relationships / seek introductions to decision-makers ("they could send an email that gets a response, where my email goes into a black hole"). Furniture-retailer example: connecting them to college students (dorm push) beats a logo at a wealthy-donor gala.
- Sponsor your **programming/land/buildings/naming/lunch-and-learns/volunteer days**, not just events/galas (event revenue is hardest to earn, burns out teams, diverts donor dollars). Align corporate goals with mission rather than inventing programs. Decision-making is slow (boards) — being slow is a path to failure. Individuals give in hard times; corporate *donations* get cut, but *sponsorship* (a marketing spend) is resilient. Charities most fear personal survey questions offending donors — false. In-kind/contra deals still require fulfillment reports.
- **Cause marketing — employee-engagement playbook** (drove $1.5M across 2 multiyear campaigns): mobilize frontline staff via speaking engagements at participating stores, stewardship trips, year-round internal newsletters, free admission to fundraising events, volunteer days, storytelling (arm staff with stories). Plus **BOGO**, **matching gifts** (verify funding first), **customer pledge drives** with an incentives chart ($50 = website thank-you, $100 = tote, $500 = podcast feature).
- **Cause-marketing ethics (Mena Gainpaulsingh):** every org needs an **Acceptance of Funds / ethical policy** ("Reputation is everything for a charity"). Most at risk: orgs advocating for a beneficiary group (human-rights charity taking arms-trade money). Policy covers acceptable company/support types, over-burdening terms, review process. Watch reputational risk, perception of influence, database-access requests (privacy law). Ethical sponsorship = transparency + honesty (rise of ethical consumerism); source companies with CSR policies.

### Sports / Athletes
- Why brands sponsor athletes: brand image, sales, community/PR, product testing, personal touch/face. Types: Financial, Product/In-kind, Service, Affiliate, Ambassador.
- Treat yourself as your OWN brand, separate from your team/org. Build brand (pick a persona) → build audience (social, blog, YouTube, podcast, newsletter) → become a thought leader → then prospect. Start with lower-value sponsorships and climb (snowball effect). Secure a media partner if you can't get recognition alone.
- 8 personal-branding tactics: media partnerships (give exclusives — early field access, interviews); advertise YOU specifically (org-funded); social (own accounts, same handle, prune personal content, pro headshot + booking link); host a contest (meet & greet/Q&A; easy entry); engage fans (reply to PG comments); network relentlessly (galas/conferences, business cards, nurture 1–2×/year); speak at events (start in grade-school auditoriums); appear on podcasts (39,000 sports podcasts).
- Athlete proposal (6 pages): Title/letter; Audience; Opportunity (brand + opportunity + audience); Menu & pricing; Sample activations tied to their goals; Contact + CTA.
- **Sports ROI — 9 metrics:** tickets sold; TV viewers (Nielsen vs. prior years); NPS; social mentions (TweetDeck); website traffic (needs opt-ins/lead magnets — a spike alone won't convert); leads generated (Google Analytics, sponsor CRM, hidden form fields); email open rate (24.57% sports avg); email CTR (3.09% sports avg); sponsor company sales.
- **Sports trends (2023–24):** fan content rises (Snapchat filters, live votes, unboxings); sustainability; female athletes key to Gen Z; digital integrations (Kumho virtual tire-hoops in NYC subway; LG 4,500 sq ft RFID/NFC facility at Final Four); interactive activations (Bud Light Social Media Lounge / virtual field goals at FedEx Field); data matters more than a big-name star; sponsorships going international via Zoom/email.
- **Naming rights / buildings (always engage a lawyer):** specify exact naming-rights type (signage locations, required wording/abbreviations, broadcast rights, exclusivity); **duration** (3–5 yrs good, 10 yrs fantastic; uncomfortable with **perpetual**); opt-out/renegotiation triggers; **name-change approval** (if the sponsor rebrands / changes industry / launches a controversial line); signage display + maintenance (community backlash has forced sign moves → re-valuation); additional promotional benefits (hospitality, pouring rights, product placement, VIP suites — rarely just "name on building"); payment terms (upfront vs. dripped; performance criteria; non-payment penalties); termination clauses (define triggers up front).

### Motorsport
- The LAST place where logos still carry real value — but logo-ONLY = a race to be the cheapest billboard. 5-step racing-sponsor process: choose a target affiliated with/experienced in motorsport → list & valuate assets → build agreeable package + proposal (no GSB) → discovery meeting → put it in writing (use visuals: car mockups, track maps, activation sketches).
- Mistakes: only logos; chasing one mega-sponsor (lottery-ticket thinking); no audience data (one NASCAR client over-indexed on **upper-middle-class professional women in the Midwest** → won niche-aligned sponsors); misaligned sponsors (you mesh brands; wrong fit alienates fans); not knowing your value (amateur signal); proposal-first; engaging too late (a couple months before season = too late — prospect daily, think next season during this one).
- Build a **business case** (50% audience data + 3–4 activation ideas + case studies + media footprint/social/web), no values, goal = a meeting; real proposal (with $) only after discovery.
- Motorsport assets: branded logos on cars/gear/track, signage/props, race naming rights; modern: on-board camera sponsorship, driver-led social/BTS content, livestream/OTT integrations, fan-engagement/data-capture zones.
- 2026 sponsor categories expanding: EV/e-mobility, battery/charging, sustainable fuels/materials, safety/AI vehicle analytics, health-tech wearables, crypto/Web3/fan-engagement, DTC.
- 10 race-team marketing methods: media partnerships (start micro — podcasts, YouTube racing, TikTok, sim-racing; offer exclusives like lap telemetry); paid advertising (geo-target around races; programmatic; internet ads are trackable, billboards aren't); social (short-form video #1 — GoPro POV laps, pit-crew transitions, "day in the life"); contests/giveaways (platform-tailored; cheap prizes — meet & greet, virtual hot-lap, Zoom coaching); product reviews (storytelling; tag manufacturers); crew marquees (add QR codes); experiential/activations (VR hot laps, AR helmet try-ons, fan zones); email marketing (race-week reminders, lead magnets; keep team list separate); Discord community; Twitch/YouTube Live. NASCAR's roster spans unrelated brands (Xfinity, Geico, Coca-Cola, Busch) — but 2026 sponsors expect digital amplification, measurable engagement, value alignment.

### Marathons / Triathlons / Endurance
- Audience: ~79% college-educated, ~73% income >$75K, near-even gender split, ages 25–54, year-round training, high charity overlap. Pain points now: recovery, sleep, hydration, burnout, longevity.
- Go beyond athletic-wear → luxury, social-consciousness, recovery tech, health-tech wearables, plant-based/functional nutrition, hydration science, mental-health/meditation apps, telehealth.
- Modern assets: GPS app integrations, livestream branding, sponsored performance data; experience upgrades (digital pacer boards, AR course previews, personalized post-race data, app challenges tied to sponsor rewards).

### Golf Tournaments
- Affluent, educated audience (PGA: C-suite, lawyers, top management) → target LUXURY brands + sophisticated activations. Standard assets: title; hole/tee/green sponsors; cart; food/drink; ticket/table/prize branding; speaking; custom emails; signage.
- 6 tips: (1) multiple sponsors per hole (tee-off AND green) — be transparent, avoid conflicts; (2) in-kind ≠ sponsorship (pay $500 for wine, then sell a **$3,000** wine sponsorship); (3) get rights to add high-end gifts to the prize table (iPads, trips); (4) make sponsorship a requirement to play (sponsors theme a hole + enter teams — no scramble to sell foursomes); (5) sell a VIP pass at registration (pre-pay on-course games + first raffle round via credit card); (6) be strategic about logo placement (a "name-tag sponsor" once got mistaken for the title sponsor). Even charity golf sponsors want to reinforce image with the right audience.

### Podcasts / Influencers / Creators
- Most can't survive on the **CPM/download model (~$25 CPM)** — only Joe Rogan-scale can. Reframe: "Here's a word from our sponsor" = "an introduction to a commercial," and listeners hit the 15-sec skip (the exact length of a mid-roll). Get experiential: lead gen, downloadable show notes, meetups/events, hosted trips. Sell **6-month or 1-year** deals (not single episodes), using the "7–10 touchpoints" brand-awareness argument as the *entry*, then layer custom activations. Deliver **quarterly** fulfillment reports. Sweet spot = small/medium brands.
- Three sponsor types: contra (skip for podcasts), promotional (great for new shows), cash (most flexible). "How many sponsors is too many? However many you need" — 1 sponsor with A+ activations beats 5 recycled ones.
- Readiness = steady (not explosive) listenership growth; some sponsors set genre/listener/download minimums. Charge: value each asset off the CPM baseline (~$30 mid-roll), adjust for quality, sum.
- Social media to attract podcast sponsors (8 tips): professionalize socials (business accounts; prune personal posts — "even a post from 10 years ago is fair game"); ask your audience for sponsor recommendations (hottest leads); showcase audience interaction/clout; learn about sponsors (but social never reveals declining sales — still do discovery); show follower counts; share case studies; advertise ($15–$200/day); work with influencers (free, but must align fully + ask rates upfront).
- Modern formats beating one-off ad reads: recurring branded segments (SmartLess, Huberman Lab, The Daily); livestream/multi-platform (YouTube/Twitch/Spotify video + pre/mid-roll + social clips); creator-economy collabs (Emma Chamberlain, Colin & Samir); integrated storytelling ads (This American Life, Heavyweight); data/proof deep-dives (Lex Fridman, Diary of a CEO).
- **Creators / personal brands:** brands now invest in niche, highly-engaged, trusted creators over celebrities. Sponsors evaluate audience relevance, engagement consistency, demographics, credibility, content quality, brand safety, community trust, posting consistency, conversion potential — NOT just follower count. A cybersecurity consultant with 8,000 engaged subscribers can beat a general creator with 250,000 passive followers; a B2B SaaS sponsor prefers 5,000 engaged ops leaders over 100,000 weak followers. Package by outcomes/integration (newsletter placements, sponsored podcast segments, LinkedIn partnerships, webinars, community sponsorships, branded series, dedicated emails, product integrations). Report honestly (open rates, CTR, downloads, referral traffic, lead quality); don't overpromise.

### Conferences
- Same rules regardless of org type. Audience easy to define by profession/subject, but still need 25+ data points + ≥3 avatars. Highest-value asset = getting the sponsor's product/sales funnel in front of attendees (downloads, emails, product testing, meetings, **speaking from stage** = thought leadership, hundreds of $/attendee). Delegate bags get tossed — nobody wants pamphlets/pens. Use a **hybrid** model (standard booth/exhibit minimums + custom partner levels via discovery — FOCUS's approach).
- 7 activation ideas: interactive experiences (multi-sense displays, scavenger hunts); games (numbers game for accountants, measurement-guessing for contractors); storytelling (origin story, testimonials); virtual reality; unique giveaways (all-expense trip to HQ, lifetime free service); unique experiences (mechanical bull, tailgate recreation — "know your audience"); charging stations (require an Instagram/Twitter/Facebook tag or email for the charge). Combine elements.

### Associations / Membership Orgs
- Audience is "already segmented" → a "sitting duck"; prospecting easier; charge a PREMIUM for member access. Sell far beyond events: conferences, webinars, contests, social/digital, networking, lunch-and-learns, newsletters, educational opportunities, product launches, tournaments, discount programs, members-only library. Price by benchmarking industry publications (full-page/digital ad rates) + industry conferences (booth/level pricing). Make sponsorship LESS intrusive to members. Run an annual sponsor summit; can even send a sponsor's survey to members.
- **Sponsor summits (3 formats):** **The Beast** (invite-only, keynotes, off-site, multi-day, organizer pays ALL costs incl. travel — for six/seven-figure packages); **The Mid-Sized** (2–4 hrs, one known speaker, food + networking + group work + Q&A; tack onto an event you run; you pay event costs not travel — for five/six-figure sellers); **The Summit Over Easy** (small breakfast, focus groups of 5–30, ~$1,200, several across geographies — "everyone should do these"). Universal rules: at least once/year; NEVER a sales/closing event (recognition, access, info-gathering only); use the invite list as a bargaining tool (top 5–20%); offer top sponsors a private closed-door session with senior leadership (no competitors); fund by dividing cost across all opportunities + billing sponsors.

### Schools / Small Audiences / Niche Markets
- Even 100 attendees can be a goldmine if niched (young professionals buying $500K homes → real estate/lawyers/accountants). "A small audience isn't a weakness but a superpower"; "it's not the size of the dog in the fight but how strong its bite is." A niche 2,000–3,000 highly-engaged audience can beat 50,000–100,000 passive.
- **Strategy by audience size:** **<1,000** — sponsors value niche alignment/engagement/credibility/trust; position as a targeted channel, don't apologize. **1,000–5,000** — opportunities accelerate (esp. B2B); want reliable engagement, frequency, clear demographics. **5,000–50,000** — structured/scalable; expect reporting, defined deliverables, multi-platform, brand safety; move beyond simple packages → custom partnerships. **>50,000** — sophisticated reporting, segmentation, strategic integration; sponsors scrutinize authenticity, conversion, audience overlap, reputation; reach alone rarely enough.
- **Niche markets** — pros: differentiation, easy audience ID, fewer competitors (depends — health/wellness, pet care now bloated), stronger loyalty, easier thought leadership (but thought leadership alone ≠ a deal). Cons: limited earnings (glass ceiling); limited growth (sponsor satisfied yr 1–2 but expects steady growth; once they convert ~80% of your customers, ongoing appeal drops); "too niche" risk (hot-and-cold interest); competitors can usurp on website/social/SEO; expanding products dilutes the niche.
- **Three-Headed Hydra (small shops):** Head 2 (Valuation) — list properties → per-property inventory → custom packages → ask the sponsor what they want → price by comparing similar properties for YOUR audience. Head 3 (Boss/Board) — tell your boss; start small; involve them ("a second, more senior person at the table"). "YOU CAN DO IT!"

### Municipalities (cities/townships)
- Funding for capital projects beyond tax revenue. Sponsorship = recognition/marketing partnership; advertising = sale of a specific public space. Assets: facility naming rights, arenas, pools, fields, museums, libraries, community centers, marinas, parks/trails, tourist routes, vehicles, equipment, programs, services, publications, websites, social. Segment by asset (a playground attracts parents/kids; a rink hosting other towns attracts a broader audience — more specific = more value). Price via trade-mag ads, Google AdWords/PPC, local paper/radio/TV. Use exclusivity carefully (one presenting sponsor + official category sponsors; also a negotiating tool). Multi-year suits naming rights; set a threshold above which multi-year is required. **Municipal sponsorship policy is mandatory** (no tobacco naming a kids' playground; define execution, roles, revenue types, approvals/council involvement, legal implications, funding allotment not toward salaries, third-party rules; consult a lawyer). City reps get less-threatening responses than event reps.

### eSports / Gaming
- Works like traditional sports (solo players, teams, leagues, tournaments). Major sponsors: Mastercard/Riot, Mountain Dew Ice/100 Thieves, Coca-Cola (since 2013), Microsoft, Mobil 1, Ally. Asset/activation ideas: collaborative/custom logos (best with audiences in the tens of thousands–millions); naming rights (can be millions); **Twitch partnerships** (140M active users; giveaways/banner ads/affiliate links); sponsored live performances (halftime); contests/giveaways (easy entry — RFID watch/wristband, email, phone); sponsored live broadcasts; digital scavenger hunts. Player path: know your audience cold (a sponsor will ask "how many of your 18–20 viewers earn $42k+?"), build a following, quality content, prospect via audience's favorite brands, informal discovery, brainstorm assets, negotiate, deliver, follow up. Trends: spending is **down** (exposure isn't enough; ROI-driven); top stars price out SMBs; **too many sponsors** dilutes ROI; **samples** = #1 preferred engagement; market still growing.

### Virtual Events / Conferences / Metaverse
- Virtual tactics ≈ physical, with caveats: confirm the sponsor is still on board; be upfront about changed funding needs (could be more OR less); platforms — Eventbrite, Vimeo, Brandlive, Cramer, Jack Morton. If a sponsor already paid and you cancel/go virtual → refund or credit (keeping money + not delivering invites lawsuits). Virtual perks: wider audience (no travel), per-session metrics reveal popular topics. In crises, silence from a prospect = a "no."
- **Metaverse:** platforms Roblox, The Sandbox, Decentraland (Adidas, Domino's, Coca-Cola, Nike, Samsung); NFL Zone (Fortnite), Warner's Rhythm City (Roblox), Decentraland Fashion Week. Treat like EVENT sponsorship (prospecting, discovery, valuation, budget, metrics). Activations: custom skins (vet — permanent), virtual storefronts (Pacsun's Pacworld), digital concerts, virtual apparel (Fortnite × Balenciaga). Benefits: cheaper, more visibility (~400M users), younger (~80% under 16). KPIs: consumption behavior, attention/path tracking, participation, conversions, sales, signups, followers. May be paid in crypto.

### International / Geography
- Sharpen your value proposition (more risk for an international sponsor); vet whether a national/international sponsor is worth the strife vs. a local one. Send a **business case**, not a proposal. "Go to where your sponsors are located"; YOU bend on time zones. Use tech to avoid travel (sold a deal to an Australian company for a US event from Canada — zero trips, just middle-of-the-night calls). Use trips to create DEADLINES ("I'm in town 3 days"). Stack travel meetings: A-listers first, breakfast for A-listers only, 1 hr/meeting, ≤30 min between, make sponsors come to you (set up at a café). Sample 3-day haul: 2 breakfast + 3 lunch + 3 dinner + 1 networking + 15 coffee + 1 business breakfast = **35 prospects.** Build international social following (global contests); hire a HUMAN translator; get clear on currency (1,000,000 yen ≈ ~$6,600 USD). National events that change location yearly = a feature (don't exhaust local sponsors; prospects aren't geography-specific if attendees travel).

### Crisis / Recession / Sustainability
- **Crisis (Cancel / Postpone / Move Forward):** every option (incl. "change nothing") is a decision that costs money. MUST consult lawyer + accountant + venue BEFORE deciding, and talk to sponsors first ("Talk now and talk often"; hearing your plans via social = "a very serious error"). Four decision questions (chose Postpone): What's best for the sector? For attendees? If it were us, how would we want to be treated? In 5 years, which choice looks best? Over-communicate; ask each sponsor how much updating they want. Real partnerships are built in a crisis through generosity — one organizer offered postponed-event sponsors NEW digital activations FREE: "If we expect our sponsors to be flexible, we must do the same." Refunds: a stock GSB (logos-only) package → partial-refund chance "near zero" (no valuation done); a real valuation → compute % delivered; alternatives = credit toward next year / other properties.
- **Recession:** whether a recession is "real" is irrelevant — brands review spend constantly; don't be in the bottom 10–50%. Sharpen audience data, build activations worth paying for, prove **3:1/4:1 ROI**, be proactive with make-goods, ask for feedback, lock multi-year (offer downturn discounts), 10x prospecting.
- **Sustainability/green:** be accountable (admit footprint), make a realistic phased plan (energy audit; don't over-promise timelines — greenwashing backfires), drop "earth offenders" as sponsors, vet the supply chain (local sourcing, renewable materials), embrace hybrid/digital, seek sustainable venues, cleanup/recycling plan, LED lighting (last 50,000–100,000 hrs vs 1,000), local catering + biodegradable dinnerware, donations-to-green-causes as contest prizes, food-donation plan (to shelters while warm), map/publicize milestones.

### AI (cross-industry — see §16 for prompts)
- Use AI for audience analysis (cluster survey + behavioral data into avatars), brand discovery + opportunity scoring (1–10 weighted criteria incl. "viability of closing" + "do we have contact info"), finding decision-makers + contact info, and pre-call intel (a prospect's last 7 LinkedIn posts/quotes/interests for the subject line + discovery). Free entry tools: ChatGPT, Perplexity, Notebook LM (+ Canva for media kits). Re-verify contact info (LLMs persist stale "still works at Subaru" data — validate in Perplexity). Even small orgs should pay ~$20/mo for a tool if sponsorships are $50K–$100K ("I want to drive my car but don't want to put gas in it").
