## 1. Frameworks & Processes

### The 5-Stage Sponsorship Sales Process (from "What is Sponsorship?")
1. **Inventory Building and Valuation** — identify everything you can offer a sponsor; based on audience feedback + pre-defined goals; determine the market value of each asset.
2. **Prospecting** — only AFTER you know what you have and its worth, identify companies interested in marketing to your audience. (Doing this before step 1 risks landing sponsors who don't fit your audience.)
3. **Getting the Meeting and Discovery** — first rule of the first meeting: bring NOTHING (no proposal, one-pager, or org info). Goal = gather info + get the second meeting, NOT make a sale. Only ask questions; don't tell prospects anything about what you do unless they ask.
4. **Building Activations** (a.k.a. leverage) — an experience/bundle of assets that brings the sponsor closer to their audience.
5. **Developing the Sponsorship Package** — custom packages per sponsor; discard one-size-fits-all gold/silver/bronze.

### Activation-Building Process (3 steps)
- **Step 1:** Identify an audience niche / specific target market.
- **Step 2:** Ask your audience:
  - What about their experience is positive?
  - What did they not enjoy?
  - What do they want more of? Less of?
  - Why are they involved with your property?
- **Step 3:** Brainstorm ideas that improve audience experience based on that feedback. THEN develop the package.

### The 5-Step Sponsorship "System" (from "Why Sponsorship Teams Avoid Direct Outreach")
1. Audience data
2. Prospect selection
3. Contact identification
4. **Direct outreach** ← the only step requiring a specific human at a specific company to respond (mostly with a "no"). Discomfort here causes avoidance that stalls the whole system.
5. Discovery-led proposal

### Discovery Session Question Areas (from "What Every Sponsor Wants")
Ask ~10 questions only (NOT 40-50), focused on four areas:
- **Target Market** — what market the sponsor wants to reach; how far it is from their current audience; which segments matter most; what they've tried.
- **Existing Audience** — most valuable segments; which parts aren't driving sales and why. (Optimizing existing audience beats replacing it.)
- **Marketing and Advertising** — what they've done, what results it drove → informs which assets/activations matter.
- **Business Goals** — short- and long-term goals.

### 9 Discovery Questions to Ask Before Submitting Any Proposal (verbatim, from "Why to Avoid Tiered Sponsorship Levels" v1)
1. Who is your target audience?
2. How do you typically connect with them?
3. What are some of the most valuable tactics you've found help you engage your target market?
4. What kind of sales activities do you find produce the best results?
5. What are the least valuable assets in your opinion?
6. How do you normally engage in sponsorship?
7. What does your target market value?
8. What can you tell me about your sales goals for the coming year?
9. What would you consider to be the most important elements of a sponsorship package?

### Follow-Up Cadence (from "Why Sponsors Ghost You")
A sales-style multi-touch cadence compressed into **7 days** (sponsorship is time-sensitive):
- **Day 1:** Email only.
- **Day 3:** Email + phone call.
- **Day 5:** Email + phone call + voicemail.
- After 7 days (≈ a dozen touches across 3–4 methods): MOVE ON.

### Recommended Direct-Outreach Weekly Rhythm (from "Why Sponsorship Teams Avoid Direct Outreach")
- **4 named decision-makers / week.**
- **4 two-sentence emails on Tuesday.**
- **4 follow-up calls on Thursday.**
- Hold the line for one full month through all resistance (half-finished proposals, webinar invites, LinkedIn drafting urge). Measure the week in **contacts made, not yeses received.**

## 2. Tactics & Techniques

### Discovery sessions are THE silver bullet
- The single thing sponsors want most and "nobody is doing." Sponsors openly say they throw out unread sponsorship packages and wish people would just call.
- You can "call every sponsor in your pipeline in two days and qualify them on the spot."
- Find a warm contact through your network; ask for an introduction. Pitch the call as wanting their *advice* on whether there's a fit and whether a more detailed conversation is worth it.
- If they ask for a proposal: **tell them you don't have one.** Say "everything you do is custom and you don't want to waste their time sending something when there wasn't a fit to begin with."

### Discovery Session Do's & Don'ts
- **DO** lead with a good icebreaker — an open-ended question anyone can answer (e.g., "seen a good movie recently?"). Avoid yes/no closed questions like "have you heard the latest Taylor Swift song?"
- **DON'T** touch anything inappropriate — keep it PC/PG: no sexual innuendo, racist/sexist remarks, off-color jokes, religion, or politics.
- **DO** try to memorize your discovery questions verbatim → more natural conversation.
- **DON'T** bring your proposal — bring "nothing but a smile and a warm handshake." Having a proposal ready this early signals these are the same assets you offer everyone.
- **DO** keep the meeting moving — you set/own the agenda; respect their time.
- **DON'T** interrogate — keep it conversational (not a job interview); ~10 questions max; ask leftover questions at the end.
- **DO** follow BAMFAM (book a meeting from a meeting). (Note: 7-questions cap appears in one post; 10 in another — Chris's general rule is "around 10, never 40-50.")

### The "No Budget" Counter-Tactic (Charlie & the law firm story)
When a prospect says "we have no sponsorship budget":
- **Don't quit.** They're defining sponsorship too narrowly (logos, gold/silver/bronze) and have an aversion to it.
- **Change tack: ask about adjacent budgets** that DO exist — VIP experiences, food & beverage, entertainment, hospitality. (The law firm freely described its VIP, hospitality, and entertainment budgets because those drive new clients + retention.)
- Essentially sell sponsorship **without ever using the word "sponsorship."** Talk about how they obtain and retain clients, then offer suggestions to meet those objectives. (Charlie got asked to submit a proposal.)
- **Watch your vocabulary:** don't use sales jargon to seem smart — it creates disconnect. "Show them your way without explaining it."
- **Be solution-minded, not salesy:** treat the prospect like a friend with a problem; recommend what genuinely works; admit it if you can't help.

### Email Outreach Rules (from "Why Sponsors Ignore Your Emails")
The ONLY goal of the first email = book the discovery session. Six reasons sponsors ignore emails:
1. **Too long** — don't cram the whole sponsorship process / company history into one email. No one reads a 3,000-word email from a stranger. Use sentences, not paragraphs.
2. **Too salesy** — no sales tactics before the discovery session. None.
3. **You attached the proposal (or ANY attachment)** — cardinal sin. Also: many email filters block first-time senders with attachments → spam folder.
4. **You don't specify why you're emailing** — say explicitly you want to set up a discovery session. Don't ask for money ("reeks of Nigerian prince scams"). Make it scannable in 30 seconds.
5. **Only emailing, never calling** — email is fine to open contact, but you must be willing to use the phone (discovery can't realistically happen over email — too many Q&A round-trips).
6. **Mass-emailing / e-blasting prospects** — unprofessional, lacks personalization; recipients ask to be removed. Also logistically broken: if you offer one meeting slot ("Wed the 13th at 9:45") and 25 people say yes, you can only meet one.

### Handling "Happy Ears" / Lying Prospects (from "Your Sponsors Are Lying to You" + "Why Sponsors Ghost You")
- **Happy ears** = taking a prospect's words at face value. Default stance in early stages: be skeptical (not even cautiously optimistic). Only start believing the sponsor once a contract is signed.
- When a prospect says "Sure! Send me the proposal," ask follow-up questions to test sincerity:
  - "What do you want to see in the proposal?"
  - "What budget range did you have in mind?"
  - "Who else should be involved in the process?"
- Interested prospects answer happily. Fakers excuse themselves / abruptly end the meeting → "just show me the proposal" was a brush-off.
- **Follow up** after a "good" meeting going in with the mindset that the prospect is lying. Still excited = deal may be alive; anything less = bad sign.
- **Don't stop prospecting** until a deal is signed (dating analogy: don't stop seeing others until you're "going steady").
- **No rash decisions** — don't pay vendors/secure space/sell tickets until the contract is signed, or you foot the bill.

### Sponsorship Form Tactics (from "What Is a Sponsorship Form?")
- Online sponsorship forms (e.g., Goodyear's) are mostly NOT worth filling out — they exist mainly to STOP unsolicited requests.
- No counter on submissions, no response timeframe. One client waited **180 days (~6 months)** to be turned down — and missed their quarterly financial milestones.
- A form is "a cold email dressed up in a nice package" → low response rate.
- **When to fill one out:** only when, deep into talks, the sponsor says you must complete it for their recordkeeping/formality. The form didn't win the deal — it was a step on the road.

### Renewal / Multi-Year Tactics
- Always have a discovery session AGAIN for repeat sponsors — their needs have likely changed.
- After an event, don't just share metrics and part ways — broach a longer-term/multi-year deal even if no next event is scheduled yet.
- Strong activation planning and good ROI framing both improve renewals.

## 3. Scripts, Templates & Exact Language

### Verbatim sponsor complaints (quote these to justify the discovery-first approach)
From sponsor summits (recur across two posts):
- "No more sponsorship packages…I don't even read them anymore"
- "Every day I throw out a huge pile of sponsorship packages- unread"
- "Properties come to me asking for money without any sense of our goals or their audience. I just can't invest in something so risky"
- "I wish people would just pick up the phone and call me. I could save them hours and tell them in 2 minutes whether or not we have a fit"
- (Favorite) "Why is nobody telling sponsorship seekers that we don't want sponsorship packages! We're people - just give us a call and give us your elevator pitch. If there is a fit, we will ask for more information"

### The lying-prospect script (what prospects actually say)
- "Wow, that sounds really interesting. I would love to learn more. Can you send me a proposal?" → ~60% of the time this is a polite lie (conflict avoidance).

### Positioning rewrites — Weak vs. Stronger (from "Why Sponsors Say No")
- **Audience:** Weak — "Our festival attracts over 40,000 attendees annually." / Stronger — "Our audience consists primarily of young families and homeowners between 30–45, which closely aligns with regional insurance and financial service customer targets."
- **Package:** Weak — "Gold sponsors receive logos on signage, websites, and social media." / Stronger — "We can build a customized audience engagement strategy around your customer acquisition goals and community visibility priorities."
- **Activation:** Weak — "Your logo will appear throughout the event." / Stronger — "We can create interactive audience touchpoints through product sampling, branded experiences, digital engagement, and content integration."

### Common-benefits-pool language (instead of a tier grid)
- Write at the bottom of the package: "these are the benefits we offer all sponsors," followed by a bulleted list (logos, mic recognition, e-blasts, social posts, logos on shirts, etc.). Keeps low-value assets out of the way of what sponsors care about (activations + audience data).

### Brand-discovery questions for omnichannel work
- "What colors exemplify the brand? What are the exact color codes?"
- "What is the brand voice?" — ask them to use adjectives (sexy, friendly, mysterious, smart).
- Ask for brand materials (blog posts, website copy, emails, social copy) to learn the voice.

### Audience-segment example phrasing (to make data concrete for sponsors)
- "500 20-to-22-year-olds in the city who earn $50,000 and 700 30-to-32-year-olds who make $75,000" → easy for a sponsor to say "yes, that's my target market."
- Avoid broad labels: "single mothers," "millennials," "middle-class families."
- Example specific segment: "mid-20s males from Chicago who earn $75k a year."

### Case-study phrasing
- State the partner's struggle clearly, then the outcome: "saved $40,000," "gained 700 new customers," "saw a spike in website traffic by 40 percent."

## 4. Numbers & Benchmarks

### Proposal/close-rate benchmarks (KEY, from "Your Sponsors Are Lying to You")
- **Proposal submission rate should be 30–50% of prospects you speak to.** Chris's is ~40%.
- Implication: ~**60% of prospects who say "yes, send a proposal" are lying** (conflict avoidance). Chris has "never" heard a flat "no thank you / don't call again" — so verbal enthusiasm is not signal.

### Funnel math — the "quarter of direct outreach" (from "Why Sponsorship Teams Avoid Direct Outreach")
Two directors, identical **200-company** lists in January.
- **Director 1 (does outreach):** ~4 cold emails/week → ~50 companies contacted by end of March. Of 50: **30 replied** (20 stated "no" with a reason; 10 asked follow-ups or booked discovery). 20 went through her **7-touch cadence** with no engagement and rolled off. Active list now 30; **12 discovery meetings** on calendar; **3 discovery-led proposals** in flight.
- **Director 2 (avoids outreach):** still 200 companies; 0 advancing; 0 meetings; empty pipeline.
- Lesson: "The fastest sponsorship programs are not the ones with the most yeses. They are the ones with the most nos in the shortest time, because the no clears the runway."

### Outreach ramp expectations (6-week hold)
- Pipeline starts producing discovery meetings: **1–2 in the first month, 4–6 by month two.**
- Cited target program scale: "How many direct outreaches per week to hit a quarter-million-dollar sponsorship year."

### Pricing / valuation numbers
- A "gold tier" sale of **$20,000** is "nothing" relative to custom deals; Chris has closed multiple **million-dollar / multi-million-dollar** deals.
- Custom packaging math: instead of selling 10 Bronze + 5 Silver + 3 Gold to make $100,000, create **4 custom, highly engaging opportunities for the same $100,000.**
- Goal-setting example: an event needs **$8,475** (allowed to drift to ~$10,000; NOT to $100,000). Never tell a sponsor you need LESS than you do (e.g., $4,500 vs. real $8,475) unless you have a concrete plan to cover the rest.
- Other goal examples: grow attendance from **1,500 → 3,000**; **5,000 website visitors/month.**

### Audience-data depth rule (RECURS — stated in multiple posts)
- Take each broad audience segment and **extrapolate at least 25 data points** from it. Niche down to boroughs/neighborhoods, precise job titles. Broad segments are the starting point, never the finish.
- Dedicate **2–3 pages** of your proposal to niched-down audience groups.

### Timing benchmarks
- A 1–2 week runway before an event is **far too little** for a sponsorship program. Audience research + survey replies + analysis alone can take a week+; building/valuing the package takes weeks → up to a month before you even reach out. Start the day you realize you need sponsorship.
- Follow-up timing: wait **at least a week** before following up; one follow-up is fine; a second after another week, but then assume it's a lost cause. Don't email/call daily ("get blocked... restraining order").

### Advertising statistics & desensitization (from "Why Nobody Cares About Logo Placement")
- Average person sees **6,000–10,000 ads/day** (2020, Lunio).
- "Unless you have an audience of 10 million people you won't make a cent with just [logos] in your inventory."
- Society has been desensitized to advertising since ~2009 (Social Media Today).

### NPS scoring bands (from festival data post)

- **Detractors 0–6** (worst 0–3 = deeply unhappy; 4–6 = neutral-but-still-detractors).

- **Promoters 9–10** (most loyal/enthusiastic).

## 5. Named Concepts & Terminology

- **Property** — the party seeking/selling sponsorship (sports team, nonprofit, festival, etc.). "The people who want the money."
- **Sponsor / Brand** — the party with the money buying marketing assets.
- **Sponsorship (definition)** — "the act of reaching across the entire marketing spectrum and borrowing the tactics that will get the desired outcome" (sales, branding, lead gen, etc.). It's MARKETING, not philanthropy. Does NOT change based on the seeker (team, charity, college, athlete — same sale/fulfillment process). "Sponsorship is a verb."
- **Asset** — tangible & intangible valuables you sell to a sponsor (logos, signage, naming rights, social posts, venue use, etc.).
- **Activation (a.k.a. leverage)** — an experience/bundle of assets designed to bring the sponsor closer to their audience in meaningful ways; smashes an audience need together with a sponsor need. "Sponsorship activation is your means of fulfilling your audience's needs/goals AND your sponsor's needs/goals concurrently."
- **BAMFAM** — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting." Always schedule the next meeting before leaving the current one (or you never reconnect). Only exception: prospect outright says they're not interested.
- **Happy ears** — a sales term: taking what someone says at face value; believing a prospect's polite enthusiasm.
- **Follow-up cadence** — a sales concept: multiple outreach methods sequenced over days (email → email+call → email+call+voicemail).
- **Feedback loop** — reviewing some/all of a system for efficiency; use after being ghosted to self-assess (without rumination).
- **Discovery session / discovery meeting** — first real conversation; gather info + book the next meeting; NOT a sales meeting; bring nothing.
- **Sponsorship form** — an online request form on a sponsor's site; mostly exists to deter unsolicited requests.
- **Omnichannel sponsorship** — using various media forms/tactics to fulfill a sponsor's goals, keeping brand message/voice/tone consistent across channels. ("Omni" = "all.") Quality and branding must be identical across channels even if the experience differs.
  - 5 ingredients of a stellar omnichannel experience: **Agility, Empowerment, Relevance, Consistency, Convenience.**
- **Four primary types of sponsorship:** Financial; In-Kind (prize, venue, food, digital); Media; Promotional Partnerships (influencer/blogger/creator).
- **Value-in-kind (VIK) / in-kind / contra** — goods/services instead of (or alongside) cash. Examples: free/discounted catering, hotel rooms, airline seats, vehicle giveaways, discounted services.
- **Donation / Corporate Philanthropy** — cash or in-kind given with recognition handled PRIVATELY; org controls how funds/goods are used; harder to obtain than sponsorship because most companies want recognition.
- **Conversion** — turning a lead (not-yet-customer) into a paying customer.
- **NPS (Net Promoter Score)** — detractors/passives/promoters from a single 0–10 recommendation question.
- **First to market** — being first in an unexplored niche (e.g., Wife Carrying World Championship, Baby Jumping Festival, Burning Man); can flip the dynamic so sponsors court you.
- **Procurement (in sponsorship)** — brands allocating procurement resources to manage sponsorship (compliance, deal management, negotiations); a 2024 growth trend.

## 6. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

### 8 Things NOT to Do (from "What NOT to Do with Your Sponsorship Program")
1. **Make it all about you** — me-centric proposals (pages of org history) get trashed. Also: overpricing assets ("laughed out of the room"); skipping research ("tone-deaf").
2. **Request sponsorship too late** — 1–2 weeks out is far too little; start the process the day you realize you need sponsorship.
3. **Pester the sponsor** — daily emails/calls get you blocked. One follow-up, wait a week; a second, then assume lost. Don't visit in person.
4. **Contact the sponsor at all hours** — no 9pm Wednesday or 2pm Saturday calls for a Mon–Fri business (event weekends excepted).
5. **Fail to provide what the sponsor needs when they need it** — have the proposal/package ready; be ready to turn around documentation in <24 hrs.
6. **Be unyielding / turn down all input but your own** — sponsorship is a partnership; rigid pricing gives only "yes or no" with no room to collaborate → they leave.
7. **Only go after big fish** — fierce competition (50–100+ orgs in line); slight misalignment = discarded. Smaller sponsors are less picky, align more, but you may need several.
8. **Don't pursue a multi-year agreement** — broach a longer-term deal instead of parting after one event.

### Why sponsors say NO (from "Why Sponsors Say No")
- Poor **audience fit** (sponsors prioritize relevance over reach).
- **Generic packages** (logos, tiers, inventory lists, broad visibility).
- **Weak ROI framing** ("great exposure," "brand awareness," "lots of impressions" are incomplete — sponsors need approval-defensible, measurable links to business goals).
- **Unclear activation ideas** → low sponsor confidence.
- **Bad timing** (budgets allocated, calendars full, leadership shifts, economy, restructuring, delayed launches) — not necessarily a value problem.
- **Lack of discovery** → assumption-based, disconnected proposals.
- **Weak follow-up** — sending one proposal and disappearing; OR following up too aggressively; treating silence as instant rejection.

### Tiered (Gold/Silver/Bronze) packages — the cardinal anti-pattern (RECURS across 4 posts)
Why sponsors HATE them:
- Signals you're **guessing** at what they want (no discovery).
- Assets are **misguided/generic** (bakery and hair-care brand get identical logo + 5 social posts + 3 e-blasts).
- Signals **unwillingness to customize** — and a "we'll customize if you ask" asterisk makes it worse (looks like CYA; "if you were serious about customizing you'd have done it").
- **Forces "this-or-nothing" / "take it or leave it"** → most brands leave it.
- Converts the most powerful marketing discipline (sponsorship) into the **least powerful (direct mail).**
- Forces "**vanity benefits**" (logos, tweets, e-blasts) that are easy to sell/activate/deliver — but **the money is NOT in the easy benefits.**
Why YOU should hate them:
- **Huge time waste** (feels productive but isn't — naming "levels" can take weeks/months in big orgs debating "logo with hyperlink vs. two tweets").
- **Leaves significant money on the table** (thousands–tens of thousands+).
What actually happens with an unsolicited package:
- **Scenario 1 (95%):** sponsor trashes it immediately, unread.
- **Scenario 2:** sponsor sees a possible fit, opens it, finds info they don't care about + a Gold/Silver/Bronze chart → trashes it.
- The forced-bundle problem: even if a sponsor wants ONE asset in the gold tier, forcing them to buy the whole tier (thousands more) = feeling swindled (fast-food "you must buy large fries + drink to get the sandwich") → they decline to renew.

### Logo over-reliance (from "Why Nobody Cares About Logo Placement")
- Logos are only "somewhat valuable" assets; getting stuck on logos is "a novice move."
- A logo alone drives almost none of a sponsor's goals (lead gen, sales, loyalty, audience growth, new role, social responsibility). Logos help a little with media exposure/branding/new-product reveal only.
- Logos are "tired"/forgettable (static image amid 6,000–10,000 ads/day). Don't hang your hat on logos; include them but make them a tiny part.

### Other anti-patterns
- **Being too salesy** in early meetings/emails — top way to repel prospects short of asking for money outright.
- **Bringing/attaching the proposal too early** — repeatedly cited as a top cause of ghosting & ignored emails.
- **Mass-emailing / e-blasting** prospects.
- **Saying you'll customize** instead of just doing it.
- **Taking rejection personally** (see Mindset).
- **Blanket "Who do you know?" asks to board members** — yields huge cold names (Microsoft, Coca-Cola) with no connection/strategy. Instead, research connections yourself, then ask board members about specific relationships by name.

## 7. Mindset & Principles

- **Sponsorship is marketing, not philanthropy.** Sponsors aren't donors, investors, interested in "awareness," looking to buy levels, or happy with logos on signs. "They are buying marketing assets, and so expect a marketing return on investment. If you can't provide it, they won't continue working with you."
- **Every party must win:** "Sponsorship requires that every party has to win" — sponsor (audience access), audience (great experience), property (better opportunity). Litmus test: "If your attendees do not walk away saying, 'isn't it great the XYZ sponsor was involved in this event?' then you are not practising sponsorship."
- **The audience is the product:** "Sponsorships are, at the end of the day, less about you and more about your audience. It's your audience the company wants to sponsor." "Your audience is your most significant asset." "If your assets are a crown, your audience data is the crown jewel."
- **Sponsorship is a verb / it requires action.** "Companies use sponsorship as a means to a specific, measurable end. Brands want your audience to do something, think something or buy something."
- **You're not selling a product:** "You are not a product, you are not selling a product. You are offering access to your prospect's target market in unique and engaging ways."
- **Sell business partnership, not inventory:** "They stop selling sponsorship inventory and start positioning themselves as business partners." Strong proposals feel "collaborative, strategic, and customized rather than transactional."
- **The money is in custom activations:** "The money is not in the easy benefits. It is found in the custom activations built just for your prospect based on their unique needs and desires."
- **All sponsorship prospects are liars (conflict avoidance):** inspired by Seth Godin's *All Marketers Are Liars*. ~60% who feign interest are avoiding conflict (cites *International Review of Law and Economics*, 2019). "In sales, one of the worst responses you can hear is 'maybe.' Just as bad if not more so is a 'yes' from a sponsorship prospect who doesn't mean it." Be skeptical until the contract is signed.
- **Redefine the unit of progress as "contacts made," not "yeses":** "A no clears the runway." "A specific person telling you they are not a fit is the most useful piece of information your sponsorship program can receive." Distinction: "trying to grow sponsorship" (anxious) vs. "running a sponsorship program" (operational).
- **Direct outreach is the only step that can fail usefully** — substitutes (warm-intro hunting, content drafting, proposal polishing, networking, LinkedIn posting) let you defer the moment of clarity and can stay "in motion forever."
- **Don't take rejection personally (Ira Glass "taste gap"):** Early work disappoints because your taste exceeds your skill — "the most important thing you can do is do a lot of work… You've just gotta fight your way through." Sponsorship is a sales job; "the best thing I ever did was get a thick skin from working in sales" (Joel Feldman). "If they say no to you, it's often because they have a connection to another cause—or your timing is just bad."
- **It's all about relationships:** "Like any other relationship in life, corporate giving is a relationship. You need to get to know what people care about and find common ground" (Heather McGinness). "Do your homework… find out what they care about and see where you align on goals." It's "not necessarily about getting the most requests out" (Kirk Laughlin) — build personal relationships like you would with major donors.
- **Get out and do it:** "There's no replacement for experience… get out of the office, make some asks, take a few rejections, and score a few wins."
- **Solution-minded, treat prospects like friends** (no-budget post): listen to the problem, recommend what works, admit when you can't help.
- **Skepticism over happy ears:** "In the very, very early stages… don't even be cautiously optimistic. Be skeptical."

## 8. Industry-Specific Nuances

### Events
- Sponsorship adds far more than money: (1) **more location options** (a sponsor once offered Chris the top two floors of their office building); (2) **better value via immersive/interactive experiences**; (3) **more attendee conveniences**; (4) **larger audience → more leads.**
- Immersive examples (engagement > free samples): Optimum Nutrition VR workout booth (The Arnold 2019); Lush museum-like Instagrammable installations w/ voice+visual search (2017 Creative Showcase); Sleep Number nap stations (CES 2018); branded phone charging stations; couches/chairs for rest.
- Capture leads via attendee contact info (name/email/phone) at signup; follow up with a post-event survey + nurture sequence.

### Festivals (12 KPIs sponsors want — from data post)
1. **Audience data** (most important; survey demographics/geographics/psychographics; 25+ data points per segment).
2. **Attendance data** (scan QR/ticket codes; tickets SOLD ≠ attendance; only excuse to lack it is a first-year event).
3. **Sales** (sponsor's pre vs. post sales; track your own ticket/merch/F&B sales too).
4. **Conversions** (attendees = leads to the sponsor).
5. **Website traffic** (spikes are short-lived → use opt-in forms).
6. **Online searches** (proves brand interest generated).
7. **Social media mentions** (tools: HootSuite, Talkwalker, Social Searcher).
8. **Email list signups** (offer a lead magnet — checklist, eBook chapter).
9. **Brand reputation** (monitor via social-mention tools; useful when prospecting too).
10. **NPS** (single 0–10 question; see scoring bands above).
11. **Email open rate.**
12. **Email click-through rate** (pair with open rate; an open alone is weak signal). Weave KPIs into the **fulfillment report** + future case studies to become a "sponsorship magnet."
- Festival audience-survey topics: why they attend; favorite/least-favorite parts; other events attended in last 6–12 months; brands they engage with across travel/retail/insurance/automotive/entertainment/telecom/financial services.

### Sports
- Most high-profile, lucrative type of cash sponsorship; effects trickle down to other areas.
- Big-name sponsors (e.g., Pepsi Super Bowl Halftime) draw bigger audiences than weak brands ("Pibb Xtra Halftime"). Aim for ≥1 well-known sponsor.
- Frees athletes/promoters to train and plan instead of chasing donations (esp. NASCAR/motorsports where athletes self-source sponsors).
- Long relationships built on shared interest: Coca-Cola–FIFA World Cup (since 1978; advertised at stadiums since 1950); Red Bull–F1 (created Red Bull Racing Team); Samsung–IOC/Olympic Partner program (+ Samsung Sports division across many sports). Olympic Partner members include GE, Coca-Cola, Panasonic, Toyota, Visa, Intel, Airbnb.
- NFL specifics: see Numbers section (Global Markets Program lets teams in states banning betting — SF 49ers, Minnesota Vikings, Atlanta Falcons — pursue international gambling sponsors from 2024).

### Nonprofits / Corporate Giving
- Sponsorship (public recognition, give-take) is EASIER to lock down than donations (private recognition, org keeps control).
- In-kind sponsorship examples for nonprofits: free/discounted meals, travel, vehicle giveaways, discounted services — "what makes it a sponsorship is recognition of the donation" (name on flyer/banner/T-shirt back/social or TV ad).
- Donations suit relief efforts, feeding/sheltering, offloading excess inventory, community-pillar goals; or putting skilled employee volunteers to use (e.g., tutoring in after-school programs).
- Gala caution: "Companies don't really like going to your gala" — they view seats as work; engage new contacts via volunteering first (see $175/plate note).

### Conferences / Associations
- Real activation examples Chris used at his Corporate Partnership Conference: **Deloitte Graffiti Wall, Keurig Unlimited Coffee Station, Funnel Cake and Networking.** A coffee bar is a perfect (obvious) activation for a business conference where attendees fly in tired.
- In large/complex orgs, naming "levels" wastes weeks/months — avoid it.

- **Banks** (Wells Fargo top at 2.89%; also US Bank, BofA, PNC, Union, TD) → marketing/branding dept; be clear it's not a loan. Wells Fargo: 501c3s only; focus areas = environmental/civics, culture/art, health/human services, K-12 education.

### Why companies sponsor (7 sponsor goals — from "Why Companies Sponsor")
Lead generation; better brand awareness; embrace a new role (corporate citizen); stand out from competition; gain media exposure; greater audience reach (audience intersection — both sides feed off each other's audience); drive more sales. Do due diligence both ways (a wrong-fit or scandal-tainted partner damages the smaller party more).

### Sponsorship vs. Advertising
- Advertising is a SUBSET of sponsorship services. Advertising = one-time, short-term, transactional (TV, radio, billboards, print, web ads). Sponsorship = deep, lasting, two-sided relationship; everyone wins.
- Ad metrics: conversions, ROI, viewers, click-through rate, impressions.
- Sponsorship metrics: email click-through rates, customer feedback, donations/sales, event attendees (badge scan/mobile check-in/ticket access), new customers.
- Sponsorship can ALSO deliver advertising; a billboard/TV vendor can't deliver logos, social, or event funding.

### 2024 Trends (mid-year report)
- Category-specific (specialist) deals stay big — don't be jack-of-all-trades.
- Deal values at record highs (driven by sports).
- Sports betting still huge; women's sports surging (media coverage → viewership → brand interest).
- VR/AR shaping activations (AR scavenger hunts, virtual try-on, behind-the-scenes; e.g., VR FedExCup Playoffs w/ Golf+ & Mastercard, 100,000+ players).
- **AI in sponsorship — what it CAN do:** asset/activation ideation, original image generation, email writing, drafting proposals/wrap-up reports, audience segmentation, parsing raw data, generating discovery icebreakers/questions. **What it CAN'T do:** negotiate or renegotiate deals, run live discovery sessions face-to-face, collect audience data via survey. Use AI as an augmentative tool, not a crutch.
- Procurement growing in sponsorship (compliance, deal management, negotiation).
- Growing sponsorship types: influencer, podcast, online-media deals; cash sponsorships remain perennial.
- Starting in 2024: "know thyself" (your goals) → "know thy audience" → build prospect list.
