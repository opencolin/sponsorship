# Sponsorship Playbook — Consolidated Sales Playbook (Merged Digest B)

Consolidated from 4 source digests (blog chunks 03, 04, 05 + transcript chunk 01) covering ~146 articles/talks on how to GET corporate sponsorships. Deduplicated; recurring points stated once and flagged "(recurring)."

---

## 1. Core Philosophy & Mindset

**The foundational reframes (all recurring across dozens of posts/videos):**
- **"Sponsorship is nothing but audience and access to audience."** Companies don't want to sponsor *you* — they want to sponsor *your audience*. Your audience is the crown jewel / #1 asset. You are a **"curator of audience,"** not a place to stick logos/commercials.
- **The hierarchy of needs: sponsor > audience > you.** You sit at the *bottom*; serving the other two is how you get what you want.
- **Sponsorship is a marketing investment / marketing discipline / B2B sale — NOT philanthropy, NOT a donation, NOT charity, NOT an ATM, NOT advertising-only.** "Sponsors are not investors — they're marketers" (credited to "my friend Larry"). "Sponsors are customers." Success is measured through marketing ROI. (Often "erroneously referred to as corporate philanthropy.")
- **Think like a MARKETER, not a salesperson.** "It's even more critical to think about sponsorship from a marketing perspective than a sales perspective."
- **"The sponsorship proposal does not make the sale — YOU do."** (Signature sign-off, in nearly every video.) Résumé/job-interview analogy: a résumé doesn't get you the job; the proposal is "a key to nothing… not even mandatory."
- **"Fall in love with the [sponsor's] problem,"** not your package. The customer rarely buys what you think you're selling (Peter Drucker) — so you must ASK.
- **Diagnose before you prescribe.** Sending a package = prescribing before diagnosing. Assuming you know what a sponsor wants is "the cardinal sin of sponsorship" (birthday-gift-for-a-stranger analogy).
- **"The riches are in the niches"** (recurring). Niche down relentlessly; "people are more than one thing"; that's where the money is.
- **Curiosity is the secret weapon — the opposite of selling.** "The person who cares more about the prospect's goals is the person who wins the deal." "In every sales relationship a purchase is made — either you sell them, or they sell you on why they won't work with you."
- **You should NOT think of yourself as "selling."** "Stop that immediately… have real conversations with people you want to work with, and if they don't want to talk to you, move on." Discovery calls are "way more fun than sales calls."
- **A sponsor does not care about your cause.** Cure cancer, stop world hunger — irrelevant. A cause is only a jumping-off point. Nonprofits must stop filling decks with mission/vision/values.

**Mindset for endurance & execution:**
- **"He who fails the most wins."** "Those who are successful in life have the biggest discard piles" (Seth Godin). "If you don't hear 'no' 10 times every day, you're doing something wrong."
- **Always be positive** (not happy — *positive*; self-fulfilling prophecy). Reframe rejections: "no time" → reschedule; "no budget" → next budget / split payments across fiscal years. "This is not a positive thinking exercise per se; this is a mental health survival technique."
- **Front-load the work; the ask should be effortless.** House-painting analogy: ~95% of the job is prep (audience, activations, discovery, customization, valuation); only ~5% is the actual ask. "30 hours of prep makes the actual job incredibly simple."
- **There are no shortcuts.** "Sponsorship is not a part-time job. It is a profession and a distinct discipline." Only two outcomes: a fast NO (shortcut) or a slow YES (full process) — no happy middle. "90% effort does not equal 90% results; it may equal no results at all."
- **Sponsorship is non-linear** — "more like a squiggly or zig-zaggy line." Pivot when something fails, but give it weeks/months, not two days.
- **A sponsorship program is never static** — "constantly changing and evolving after each deal." "The job is never done."
- **Present as an equal / professional** — not a supplicant — to avoid "social dismissal" (sponsors ignore amateurs).
- **Patience + start early:** sponsorship is multi-person, multi-layered; your contact usually can't approve money alone. Start ≥6 months out.
- **Treat every sponsor "like they're the million-dollar dream sponsor"** (full research regardless of size).
- **Leave ego at the door** when receiving feedback ("think about it like someone else's program").
- **The human element reigns:** "If you can't speak about your sponsorship opportunity with a spark in your heart and fire on your breath, how can you expect a sponsor to get excited about it?"
- **"You can't sell sponsorship from behind a desk. Fact."**
- **Results-driven, not relationship-driven:** "The days of smooching clients on the 18th green are long gone." Connections "count for nothing in the face of a poor sponsorship proposal."
- **Sponsors talk to each other and move between companies** — burning one relationship damages your org's and your professional reputation.

## 2. The End-to-End Process (overall sequence / funnel)

**The Master Sponsorship Sales Process** (the canonical spine repeated in virtually every post/video; flagged recurring). Slightly different enumerations appear, unified here:

1. **Set goals** — know what you need (cash, promotion, or both) and an exact dollar figure. ("Give me money so I'll have it" is not a goal and not a property.)
2. **Identify your properties** (opportunities).
3. **Collect & segment audience data** — survey; niche down into 3+ avatars ("Russian nesting dolls").
4. **Build inventory of assets** — catalog everything sellable (≥100 assets); add experiential/activation ideas to widen the sponsor pool.
5. **Get internal buy-in** on assets/activations BEFORE going to market (so you're not stuck in a 6-week internal approval when a sponsor is ready to move).
6. **Value (valuate) assets** — market-value benchmarking + honest gut-check.
7. **Write the proposal/business case — then SIT ON IT** (do not send unsolicited; "lock it in a safe and give the key to someone else").
8. **Prospect** — build a long list of companies that want *this* audience (warmest → coldest; Concentric Circles).
9. **Find the named decision-maker** and a warm "in."
10. **Direct outreach** — short email/call requesting a *meeting*, never a sale, never the proposal.
11. **Discovery session** — ask questions, listen, diagnose; no pitch, no proposal.
12. **Follow-up meeting(s) — BAMFAM** (book the next meeting before leaving the current one).
13. **Send proposal ONLY when genuinely asked** (review it together, don't just send) → negotiate → **lawyer** → contract.
14. **Deliver / activate** (over-deliver by ≥10%).
15. **Fulfillment report** → renewal/upsell meeting → referral (within ~1 week of wrap).

**The Five Stages of Sponsorship Sales** (transcript core framework, recurs):
1. Inventory building + audience identification + valuation. ("If your strategy doesn't start with inventory, valuation and audience data you are set up for failure.")
2. Prospecting (ask "what companies want to get in front of this audience?").
3. The prospecting/discovery meeting ("the only goal for the first meeting is to get the second meeting").
4. Building the (tailored) sponsorship package — only AFTER discovery.
5. Activation + Fulfillment (fulfillment is *inside* the sales stages because "the fulfillment report is the best prospecting tool out there").

**"Direct outreach is the only work that closes sponsorship deals."** Steps 1-9 and 13 feel like "the work" but produce no meetings on their own.

**The 7-step sales funnel stages:** Leads → Prospects → Intro email sent → Phone call → Discovery session → Proposal submitted → Follow-ups. (Alt naming: Cold prospects → Cold calls → Discovery meeting → Proposal → In/out.)

**"3-a-day" daily method:** every single day, move 3 people at EACH stage (research 3 leads, qualify 3 prospects, send 3 intro emails, call 3, hold 3 discovery sessions, submit 3 custom proposals, follow up with 3). "You are only moving three people at a time, but if you do this every single day, you will make budget. Then you will exceed budget!"

**Switch your metric from REVENUE to VOLUME.** Track count of prospects at each stage + activity levels, not dollars. "The key is movement; every day you want to move prospects up one rung of the sales ladder." The pipeline is a "living, breathing entity" — when a gap opens in a column, add a new prospect.

## 3. Audience Research & Data

**Audience is the heart of everything (recurring).** "Sponsors buy access to your audience." A festival owner is "a curator of audience."

**Depth target:**
- **At least 25 data points per audience segment** (recurring baseline). Events/conferences: **30+**. Step-5 framing: be able to "rattle off at least 30 different data points."
- **Segment into 3+ avatars / buyer personas / niches** (minimum 3; 3–5 typical).

**Granularity:**
- Income by **$5,000 increments**; age by year; geography by borough/neighborhood; plus job title, industry, buying behavior, # events attended.
- B2B/conferences also need: decision-making authority level, titles, industries, whether you can influence a buying decision.
- Niche down to groups of "only 20 or 30 people" — that's the *goal* (Russian-nesting-doll / onion metaphor: keep subdividing until the smallest doll is empty).
- **Psychographics** = "behavioral motivators and the most telling part of the audience survey": beliefs, motivations, opinions, brand/consumption behavior, interests outside your org.

**Survey method:**
- Run the survey **yourself** — do NOT use AI (75% of consumers worry about AI misinformation; an AI-led survey breeds mistrust).
- Send **3 emails total:** (1) heads-up, (2) the survey, (3) reminder. Tell them it's short (**under 10 minutes**) and easy (multiple-choice).
- Best send window: **10 a.m.–1 p.m. local, Tuesday/Wednesday/Thursday.**
- **Incentivize** (discounts, exclusive codes, freebies, contests/giveaways). For tight-knit communities, the **honest/humble appeal** works ("help us better serve the community; free, 10 minutes").
- Give the audience **2–4 weeks** to respond; full process can take "weeks if not longer."
- **Re-survey if it's been >6 months** since the last one.

**Define audience correctly:**
- "Everybody is the target market of nobody." Avoid "general public / general population / middle-class families." (Babies are part of the general population but don't buy groceries.) "Do you know which sponsors are targeting the 'general population'? None."
- Don't define audience *only* by "people who like [your topic]" — too narrow; limits you to gear/topic sponsors.

**Supplementary:** use secondary/third-party research. Tools for digital audiences: Google Analytics audience reports, Twitch Analytics (baseline demos), platform analytics.

## 4. Properties & Inventory (Assets)

**Property / rights holder** = the sponsorship seeker (event, podcast, team, festival, program, gala, marathon, conference, naming rights, concert series, VIP/donor function). Each property has its own audience segments.

**Inventory targets:**
- Build **at least 100 individual assets** per property. "Dozens, maybe even 100+, assets to choose from."
- Create an *exhaustive* inventory (include low-value items like logos, but have many high-value ones too).

**Asset = "something you are willing to give a company in exchange for money"** — tangible or intangible: logo placement, signage, naming rights, social posts, speaking slots, emails/eblasts, sampling rights, exclusivity, branded programs, captive audience, broadcast rights, race-car/uniform/helmet real estate, database/email marketing, mic mentions.

**Logos are explicitly the LOWEST-value asset (recurring, emphatic):**
- "Sponsors Don't Want Logos." "Logos do not convert customers or make sales." "Nobody cares about logo placement." A logo is "a very, very small part."
- A logo-on-sign is "at best worth **three cents per view**." "Logo soup" near registration is near-worthless without huge volume.
- Even brands' own ads aren't "just a logo" — they tell a story.
- The few places logos *genuinely* work: Twitch/streaming **logo overlays**, and (limited) motorsport car/suit/helmet real estate.

**Sampling rights are among the most valuable assets.** Giving a sponsor's product into the audience's hands = sampling (a test drive *is* a product sample). "Sampling rights are lucrative — charge for them."

**Lead gen = "a license to print money."** Leads = emails, phone numbers, quote requests, free-trial signups, booked meetings.

**Don't offer assets outside your control** (e.g., naming rights to a track/venue you don't control) — the deal collapses on delivery.

**Avoid thin / "push-only" inventory** (only logos/signage/e-blasts). "Nothing more disappointing to a sponsor than… only two or three types of assets."

## 5. Valuation & Pricing

**Core valuation question (recurring):** "What would my sponsor pay on the open market to get this *exact same outcome*?" — NOT "what are others charging for the same bundle / their gold tier."

**Market-valuation process (per asset/activation):**
1. Research market value (what a pro/firm/radio/newspaper/online ad charges for the same thing).
2. Decide if yours is worth same / higher / lower based on an honest quality assessment.
3. Discard low-value assets that don't fulfill a sponsor need.
4. Sum the assets = property value.
5. **Document the entire process immediately** (sponsors ask "How did you come up with this price?" and can tell if you're making it up).
- Involve **2–3 stakeholders** (not more — "too many cooks") to temper the urge to inflate every price.
- **Market value is a baseline, not the final price.**

**Methods:**
- **Market valuation** = asset-by-asset custom pricing (generally recommended).
- **CPM** (cost per mille / per 1,000 impressions/downloads/views) and **CPA** (cost per acquisition) dominate for influencer/social/podcast.
- **Podcast ROI value formula:** (downloads ÷ total spent on the podcast) × 100 — then add the value of assets/activations on top.

**Pricing philosophy (recurring):**
- "Pricing is more art than science." "Every property is unique"; no magic formula. Communicate your **"Sponsorship DNA"** (unique value proposition) confidently.
- Most assets should sit **at market value**; a few above, a few below. Don't inflate everything "like overinflating a bike tire."
- **People far more often UNDERvalue than overvalue.**
- **Don't put prices in the proposal** — leave wiggle room. An asset worth $500 to you may be worth $2,000 if it solves a sponsor's problem ("that's $1,500 you're leaving on the table").
- Be ready to state your **ROI ratio** — sponsors expect 2:1, 3:1, even 4:1 ("they want to pay less than market rate," which is *why* you must know market value).
- For deals under ~a couple thousand dollars, a back-of-napkin number is fine; for **$100K+** you need a real market valuation.

**Quick valuation hack:** open a Facebook/Twitter/LinkedIn ad campaign, target your exact audience, and read the platform's quoted CPM/CPC bid right before paying — that's what brands pay to reach your audience. Charging less = leaving money on the table.

**Bad (avoid) valuation techniques:** guessing; doing what you did last year; copying competitors verbatim ("when you copy a guess, you perpetuate that wrongness"); starting from "how much I need" and applying it arbitrarily.

## 6. Activation

**Activation = an experiential-marketing opportunity that connects audience + sponsor in meaningful ways the brand AND the audience both love — "switching on" a sponsorship.** It is NOT a tier, NOT a logo, NOT necessarily expensive/cool/complex. (Example: branded water to a thirsty crowd for brand awareness.)

**The activation formula (recurring):** (a) sponsor pain point + (b) audience pain point/need, married within (c) the marketing budget. "The magical moment where the needs of your sponsor and the needs of your audience overlap."

**Selling the package is "just the beginning of your responsibilities."** It's your DUTY to ensure sponsors actually USE what they bought.

**Internally, call your offering "activation" or "experiential marketing"** so you think "where can I create more experiences?" not "where can I stuff more logos?"

**Generating ideas:**
- Have a discovery session → take it **one sponsor goal at a time** → brainstorm WITH the sponsor → remember your audience (the 3rd party).
- **Column A = sponsor goals, Column B = audience goals; connect one from each** in a way that makes sense.
- **Ask the audience** what they loved/hated, want more/less of — then use sponsorship to give them more. "Requires no creativity on your part at all" because the ideas come from attendees.

**The Four-Step Activation Plan (named framework):**
1. **Sponsor Onboarding Meeting** — uncover each sponsor's specific objective (exposure, product launch, leads, overcoming bad PR). Discuss which audience segment each activation targets, the problem it solves for the audience, and for the sponsor. Assign team leaders on both sides.
2. **Clarify Sponsorship Agreements** — a detailed signed-off checklist functioning as a schedule (every step, responsibility, deliverable, deadline; logo placement, equipment ownership, production schedules, collateral deadlines, speaker schedules, venue setup/access, delivery schedules).
3. **Confirmation for Proper Execution** — pre-event sign-off on all collateral; day-of, station team reps at each activation; take photos as proof; push attendees to post with event hashtags; screen-capture social proof in case posts get deleted.
4. **Event Postmortem & Fulfillment Reporting** — gather feedback (General; Sponsor-Specific via rating survey; Attendee via incentivized survey; per-Activation review).

## 7. Prospecting & Pipeline Building

**Build the prospect list FROM the audience survey (recurring core technique, ~15 posts)** — NOT from association directories, industry conferences, or past-sponsor contact lists (those brands "have no real reason to engage with this property specifically"). "The hardest part of prospecting you already took care of."

**The Concentric Circles Method (4 nested rings, warmest center → coldest outer; recurs):**
- **Circle 1 / Center (hottest):** brands your audience named liking/using in the survey — weight heavily the ones mentioned by multiple people. (Best version: brands the audience said they **intend to purchase within the next 18 months** — yields **20–30+ brands alone**.)
- **Circle 2:** brands that **advertise to** your audience. Find them by asking the audience what media they consume, OR build an avatar and ask "who markets to these people?" then consume that media (radio/TV/magazines) and note who's spending. Keep only the ones that keep coming up.
- **Circle 3:** brands that **should** be interested but don't currently advertise to them (your own research, no data). "This is the class of brands that most people start and end with — and this is probably why you're struggling."
- **Circle 4 (coldest but viable):** direct **competitors** of every brand in circles 1–3.
- Then research each warm prospect individually before outreach; discard mismatches.
- Result: never fewer than ~100 prospects; usually capped at **300–500**. (Smaller method yields 50–60+.)

**The Nine Paths to a Sponsor** — stop thinking "companies"; companies are full of **people**. Reach each department, at C-level / VP / Director / Manager / Coordinator / Admin:
1. **Marketing** (primary path — sponsorship is a marketing discipline)
2. **Sales**
3. **Sponsorship** (if in their title)
4. **Communications**
5. **Brand**
6. **Product Development**
7. **HR / Employee Engagement** (if you have employee-engagement assets)
8. **Customer Retention** (if you have VIP/retention assets)
9. **(Nonprofits only):** Community Investment / CSR / Philanthropy / Foundation — they do philanthropy, NOT sponsorship, but ask them "who should I talk to about your marketing/sales/sponsorship goals?" for a warm internal intro.

**List sizing & hygiene:**
- Keep the list LONG ("the longer, the better") because companies flake/ghost. Recommended 30–60 deep (smaller properties); ≥a dozen hot prospects to start; podcasts start with 20–30, expand past 50. In a recession, push deeper.
- Expand with: competitors' sponsors (as examples — don't poach the same brand), your vendors/suppliers, donor database, past volunteers, board members, past event attendees.

**Don't chase mega-brands for size/wealth alone (recurring).** Coca-Cola, Nike, Arm & Hammer get "thousands, maybe millions" of requests; they're the coldest leads, ignore small fish, only work with premium-caliber properties, and even a yes would under-monetize a non-aligned audience. Pick on **audience alignment** instead.

**Current sponsors/exhibitors are your HOTTEST prospects** (top of funnel) — it's far easier to grow an existing sponsor than find a new one.

## 8. Outreach (cold email/call, follow-up cadences)

**Goal of outreach = book a MEETING. Never to make the sale, never to send a proposal/package/one-pager/attachment.** (Recurring.)

**Email rules:**
- **2 sentences (max 3).** "Anything over three sentences you could pretty much just delete the email yourself." A 3-sentence email takes <1 minute to read.
- **The first sentence must explicitly state that your audience is interested in their product.** "Asking a company for money because they have money and you'd like some is a path to no sponsorship."
- **No attachments** (spam filters flag them; signals you want a sale on first contact).
- Use a **professional email handle**, not a personal one.
- Frame the ask as picking their brain / a **15-minute discovery call** about an opportunity.

**Find the right contact** via LinkedIn — search titles containing **Brand, Marketing, Business/Product Development, Communications, Sales** (media sponsors: Community Affairs/Relations, Sales Manager, Brand/Community Partnership, Marketing Manager).

**Always seek a warm "in" (recurring)** — a mutual connection ("friend of a friend" is fine) to get past the gatekeeper.

**DON'T:** email info@/hello@/customerservice@; call the number on the back of the credit card hoping to reach the CMO; call the front desk and get redirected endlessly; "go around the gatekeeper."

**Follow-up cadence:**
- Per person: ~**3 emails → phone call → 2 emails → call.** No answer → move the *entire* sequence to the next contact, then the next, then the next vertical.
- Alt: after sending materials, wait ≥1 day / a few business days up to a week → then **alternate phone + email daily for ~7 days** → if still silent, switch contacts or write off the prospect for this event and move on.
- Alternate email + phone across follow-ups; have a call script; leave a voicemail; follow up in a few days.

**"A lack of response IS a response — and it's still a no" (recurring).** "Silence is its own answer." After ~1–2 weeks and 2+ follow-ups with no reply, treat it as "no" and move on — but don't pester incessantly.

**Subject lines:** straightforward, curiosity-tapping; **≤70 characters**; use the sponsor's name; no emojis; minimal punctuation (≤3 types); no all-caps (1 capitalized word max); subject must match body (never dishonest); don't overthink it. (See §16 for formulas.)

## 9. Discovery Meetings

**The discovery session is NOT a sales meeting (recurring, emphatic in nearly every post).** It's a "fact-finding mission," "interview like a journalist," "purely exploratory." **The only goal of the first meeting is to get the second meeting** (and to build trust + learn). NOT to close, NOT to submit a proposal.

**Three goals of discovery:** (1) Build trust, (2) Learn (discover needs/goals/wants), (3) Book the second meeting.

**Bring NOTHING to the first meeting (recurring, emphatic).** No proposal, package, one-pager, pamphlet, leave-behind, case for support — and in the strictest version, **not even a pen and paper or business card.** Writing things down the instant a prospect speaks makes them "feel preyed upon" (like a car salesperson noting what you want). After the meeting, step outside and **dictate notes into your phone / email them to yourself.** (Softer version allows a blank notebook + a short jotted question list + a rough meeting outline.)

**Questions:**
- Ask **5–7 (up to 10 max)** from the canonical **37-question** bank — never all 37 ("a business meeting, not an interrogation"). Pepper them in naturally; wait a couple minutes between each.
- **Memorize** them (reviewing ~1 hr before is fine); give yourself permission to depart from the script so you don't sound robotic.
- **Talk ~20% of the time; ~90% of what you say should end in a question mark.**
- Start in the **audience** space → then **business goals** → then **success metrics**. Hang on audience until fully understood.
- Walk in already knowing the Googleable basics (years in business, what they sell, values, past sponsors) — don't waste time there.

**Technique:**
- **"What do you mean by that?"** is the single most powerful question — use it even when the answer seems obvious. "The first-level answer you get is almost never the real answer, in sales and marriage." Other probes: "What else? Why do you think that is? How do you know that's a problem? How would you know if you solved it? What have you tried that worked/didn't?"
- **"Can you tell me more about that?"** to peel the onion (e.g., "sales fell" → "sales dropped 32%, down 15% YoY").
- **Diagnose before prescribe.** For "brand awareness": don't ask "how can we generate awareness?" (if you reached out, they're already aware of you). Instead: "What **image** are you trying to put into the world, and how can I help reinforce it? How do you know if your brand is growing or shrinking?"
- **Record meetings (with permission)** — replay to digest jargon and harvest the sponsor's exact words to inject into proposals (makes them feel heard).
- **Confirm the avatar back to them** ("Your ideal customer is the 40-year-old high-net-worth mom in the suburbs, three kids, drives a Lexus — do I have that right?").
- **Summarize + acknowledge** the diagnosis before offering a solution.
- When asking permission to share your approach, **go silent and wait** — "when you ask someone's permission, you don't keep talking."

**Length:** 30–45 minutes.

**Soft skills / pre-meeting ritual** (in-person OR video): icebreaker/one joke (non-sexual/non-religious/non-political; a sports team or big TV show beats the weather; two jokes absolute max), firm handshake, eye contact, skip the sales spiel. Eat something first (not a new food), use the bathroom, comb hair, review questions. **No elevator pitch — ever, at any stage** ("save it for your networking events").

**Don't say the word "sponsorship" in the first meeting** — and don't substitute "partnership" (pointless); just frame it as wanting to understand their goals and see if there's a chance to collaborate.

**BAMFAM before leaving** (book the next meeting). **Don't expect to close in meeting #1** ("absolutely the exception, not the rule"); negotiation usually starts meeting #2 or after 3+.

**Close discovery** with the fit + permission question (see §11/§16).

## 10. Proposals & Business Cases

**Two distinct documents (key distinction):**
- **Business case** = sent *before* a meeting. **No pricing, no grids.** ≥50% audience data, plus activation ideas, media footprint, case studies. Looks like a rate card. Its ONLY job is to **earn a discovery meeting** (NOT money). (A festival "proposal" sent cold is really a business case.)
- **Proposal** = built *after* a meeting. Has values/market-tested numbers. **"If you put dollar figures in the document, it's a proposal."**

**The proposal supports YOU; it is not the sales tool (recurring).** "I have sold millions in sponsorship without ever creating a sponsorship package." "The proposal does not make the sale. You do."

**Timing:**
- **Write it only after discovery; send it ONLY when the sponsor genuinely asks** (may take 1–5 meetings). Sending early/unsolicited "is a way to kill the deal, not cinch it." "Lock it in a safe and give the key to someone else."
- When asked, **review it together** ("I'll put together a *draft* for you to review") — don't just fire it over. Resistance to a review meeting = they're not really interested / you missed something in discovery.

**Composition (recurring "50% Rule"):**
- **No less than 50% of the deck = audience data.** "If you only have one page of audience data, you can only have a two-page proposal."
- **About-Us section: max 1 page, ideally 1 paragraph**, mission/values condensed to a single sentence. "Before discovery, the sponsor doesn't care about your company. At all."
- Present audience data attractively (graphs/tables/charts, not paragraphs).
- Short, snappy paragraphs (sponsors skim).
- **Don't list prices** until you and the sponsor start "talking dollars."
- Never label the document "sponsorship proposal" / "sponsorship package" in the text (instant turn-off).
- Use a **menu, not levels** — let sponsors pick assets. Customization should be invisible: "Does a luxury car need to tell you it's high-end?" (omit the word "customization" entirely; just deliver custom assets — "Actions speak louder than words" / "Your actions speak so loudly I cannot hear what you are saying," Emerson).

**Standard 6-page proposal structure** (sports / athlete / motorsport; "don't make it longer OR shorter"):
- **Page 1 — Title:** name/tagline/logo/date/location/website. NEVER label it "sponsorship proposal."
- **Page 2 — Audience data:** the single most important page; ALL demographics incl. salary + buying power + psychographics; charts not paragraphs. Up to 50%.
- **Page 3 — Event/opportunity intro:** ONE paragraph on your org (≈6 sentences max), ONE paragraph on the event/game. (Some versions: 3 paragraphs — cause, event, attendees.)
- **Page 4 — Package/menu:** priced assets; no gold/silver/bronze; never say "sponsorship package" in text.
- **Page 5 — Activation ideas:** on their own page to browse at leisure.
- **Page 6 — Contact + titled CTA** (e.g., "We want to hear from you!").

**Brochure** (a visual document tying proposal + package together; distinct from the written proposal/prospectus): **6–10 pages**, one package per page. Build in Canva / MS Word / Adobe Spark; proofread; print once on basic paper to check folding even if sending digitally; save/send as **PDF** (never .docx or TIFF), or print on high-quality presentation paper.

**A proposal also documents what was discussed in discovery** so both parties are aligned.

## 11. Negotiation & Closing

**Talk money only AFTER the proposal**, and only once interest is confirmed — "there's a time and place for talking about money, and it comes after the proposal." Talking money too soon kills the second meeting.

**Never advance a step without explicit (not implied) permission.** The asking-permission close: *"I think it sounds like we have a fit here, would you agree? Is this a good time for me to put together a draft proposal for your feedback?"* — only after discovery confirms you can genuinely link their target market to your audience. A "yes" to "are we a fit?" only means *could be a fit* — "a minor win at best," not a yes to working together.

**Negotiate by adjusting the package, not arbitrarily:**
- "Too expensive?" → ask what they'd prefer to pay, then **remove assets** to lower cost.
- Want more of an outcome? → **add assets** and raise the price.
- "It's your business, it's your rules — negotiate. Valuation is a guide to help you make business decisions."

**Pin down budget when they say "I don't have one":** walk the cap down — "If I put a $500K proposal in front of you, could you say yes? No? Stop me when I hit the cap — $400K, $300K, $200K, $50K, $25K…" Or reassure: "I'll share our valuation so you know I didn't invent the number — I just need to know what range to aim for." (If truly no budget: "Great — that means we can do some really cool stuff, as long as we can show ROI.")

**Find the other decision-makers without asking "who's the decision maker"** (people lie / dislike it): "So I can invite the right people on my end — who else on **your** end should be part of this conversation so we really get it right?"

**Be upfront about the cash amount you need;** don't under-ask to be "polite." Sometimes you never have to ask — the sponsor brings up pricing/contract themselves.

**Objection handling (core principle):** Unless the sponsor says "no" outright, it is NOT a rejection. "I don't have budget right now" = no budget *right now* (circle back). "I'm too busy right now" = busy *right now*. Ask clarifying follow-ups instead of retreating: "When will you have more time? Should I call back in a week? Two weeks?"; "Roughly how much is left in your sponsorship budget?"; "When can we circle back to budget — a month or several?" **But genuinely take a clear "no" for an answer** — "No means no." Don't pester after a direct refusal. If budget truly won't exist until after the event, **tell the truth** — both sides must accept they likely won't sponsor this one.

**After negotiation,** expect to wait several days to a week while the sponsor clears figures with people above them; follow up but be patient.

**Always have a lawyer review the contract before signing (recurring).**

(See §16 for verbatim "just send me a proposal," "let me think about it," and other rebuttals.)

## 12. Fulfillment & Reporting

**The fulfillment report (a.k.a. post-event / wrap-up report; the "thank-you letter") is "the best prospecting tool out there" / "the most valuable tool in the sponsorship game" (recurring).** It opens renewal, provides proof-of-track-record for *new* sponsors, protects you legally, and serves as your own record.

**Over-deliver by ≥10%** on every promise.

**Contents (~6 pages):**
- A chart of **every promised deliverable** + whether/how it was met + areas you **over-delivered.**
- **Original value vs. revised/delivered value** (separate column/tab).
- An honest rating: **over-delivered / delivered / under-delivered.**
- Attendance stats; photos of a packed hall; audience data; engagement metrics (social, web, reviews); press coverage/media clips; calculated ROI per deliverable; attendee testimonials; pictures of the sponsor engaging the audience; screenshots of social/web (in case posts get deleted).

**Be honest (recurring).** Never inflate numbers (don't claim $50K in sales when it was $10K — sponsors can trace their own sales). "Don't hide the mistakes under the rug"; the sponsor (or their rep) was there and saw it. You can only improve what you acknowledge.

**Measure what's IN YOUR CONTROL, not the sponsor's conversion failures.** If you delivered 1,000 leads and they converted only 506 due to a weak opt-in/site, that's not your miss. Measurable-in-your-control items: logo placements (photograph + number them), speaking slots (prove time/attendance), attendance, emails sent, social posts made, activation existence/goals.

**Timing (deliver FAST, while fresh and before next year's budget is allocated):**
- Within **~1–2 weeks** of the event (most common); some say "the day after… maybe two days, tops"; renewal posts say talk renewal "in a matter of days."
- The **two-week post-event window is the most important timeframe.** Know your renewals/gap within ~1–2 weeks.
- Quarterly reports if it's a yearlong relationship.

(The fulfillment report flows directly into renewal — see §13.)

## 13. Renewals & Upgrades

**Renewing usually earns MORE money but requires offering more** (new activations/assets from a fresh discovery session). Retention > acquisition, especially in a downturn.

**The renewal loop (recurring):** Fulfillment report → book a **feedback meeting (NOT a pitch meeting)** → gather feedback (just listen; don't plan your defense) → implement it BEFORE pitching → make the renewal offer → ask for a referral. In the fulfillment/sponsor-summit meeting, ask: how did it go? **"What do I have to do RIGHT NOW to make this your #1 marketing priority next year?"** → get a *draft-proposal* yes → **BAMFAM.**

**Renewal offer framing:** *"If I make the required changes and double down on what you liked, would you be interested in looking at a draft proposal about renewal?"* — you're asking to send a proposal, not asking for money yet.

**Two deal structures:** a package worth **+50%**, OR a **multi-year deal at the same flat rate.** (Value the new assets first to decide more-money vs. longer-deal.)

**If they say no:** ask **"why not?"** (they may have softened earlier feedback). **Always ask for a referral** ("anyone else in your network who'd benefit?") and make the intro yourself **the same day.** A "no" sponsor is still a path — a geography-mismatch "no" turned into intros worth tens of thousands (see §19).

**Don't torch the bridge** even if they decline — stay cordial, check in a few times a year; they may refer you or return. "Burning bridges is foolish when you might need the sponsor down the line."

**Maintain the relationship year-round** (festive greetings, share relevant articles, occasional check-ins) so you're not "that friend who only calls when they need something." **But invest in the sponsorship ASSET itself** (exclusive benefits, networking events, research, cross-promotions), not just the personal relationship — because marketing/brand staff turn over and may be gone at renewal.

**Renewal mechanism (contractual):** Right of First Refusal lets a party buy something the moment it goes on the market.

**Upgrade exhibitors → sponsors (recurring):** a sponsor is **200x** the marketing value of an exhibitor and carries ~90% margin. Run a **discovery session with every exhibitor** (they're your hottest prospects — already gave you money), give them fulfillment reports + meetings, then move them up. (See §16 for the exhibitor-upgrade script; §19 for the $6K booth → title sponsor case.)

## 14. Scaling, Team & Operations

**Scale up the program once you have 3–5 successful sponsorships** under the same approach.

**Get one sponsor first, keep them, then scale.** "More isn't always better" — only take on as many sponsors as you have bandwidth/creativity for.

**Don't scale mediocrity.** "Scaling mediocrity only leads to bigger piles of mediocrity" (Keith Cunningham, *The Road Less Stupid*). Working longer hours instead of fixing the leaky bucket = "alchemy" (see §17/§18).

**Five Habits for productivity:**
1. Reset mid-day / take breaks (treat the afternoon as a distinct day).
2. Do the things you hate first (make a to-do list before checking email).
3. Small daily goals (the 3-a-day funnel).
4. Get out of the office — block 3 days/week out; decline meetings unless with a prospect or intro-maker.
5. Always be positive.

**Diagnose your own program (Bottleneck / Blueprint), fix the bottleneck — don't try to do everything (avoid burnout).** (See the diagnostics in §18.)

**Hiring sponsorship sales staff:**
- **Prereqs before hiring:** a crystal-clear opportunity (goals/outcomes defined with stakeholders) + current audience research + customer avatars.
- **Pay market rate** for the experience level, or don't hire yet. Post on **specialty sales boards** (not just Indeed/Monster).
- Look for: innate knowledge of the sponsorship process, critical/analytical thinking, relationship-building, sales savvy, communication, problem-solving.
- **NEVER hire for a borrowed Rolodex/contacts** — those came from past employers and leave with the hire (you "paid them to build contacts for your competitors"); Rolodex claims are often lies. Hire someone who builds **your** network and leaves it with you. "Every current employer is a future past employer."
- Ask candidates about their **first 90 days**; don't expect closes in the first weeks (a cold pipeline can't perform like a year-stewarded one).
- **Build a sales culture:** healthy competition/gamification; track activity (prospecting, eagerness) not just closes; boost morale continuously (gift cards, lunch). **Never publicly shame poor numbers** (handle 1-on-1). **Don't make incentives "lame like a pizza party."**
- Don't let non-experts dictate to sponsorship experts how to sell ("hire a roofer, let them roof"). "Sales reps want to succeed far more than you want them to" — trust and support them.

**Convince/teach your boss to change strategy (8 steps):** Know your stuff → learn boss's goals slowly across conversations (not a formal meeting) → come up with suggestions + a valued plan → pick the right time → choose language gently (validate, never patronize; don't say "your approach sucks") → pitch benefits-focused (let valuation do the selling) → be flexible → check in (monthly if uninvolved, weekly if involved). Don't be a know-it-all, don't take all the credit (make it "their idea"), always give the boss final say + lawyer review before signing.

## 15. Funnel Math & Benchmarks (ALL numbers/metrics/$/rates/timeframes/%)

### Headline funnel models

**The Quarter-Million Funnel (mid-sized property):**
- **200 contacts → 45 discovery meetings → 15 proposals → 5 sponsors.** "To close five, you talk to about two hundred."
- 5 sponsors × $50,000 = **$250,000/year.**
- Ratios: ~1 in 4 qualified contacts → discovery; ~1/3 of discoveries → proposal; ~1/3 of proposals → close.
- **200 contacts ÷ 50 working weeks = 4 direct contacts/week (~1/day).** Time cost: **under 3 hours/week** (most properties spend more than that just reformatting the proposal).
- Diagnostics: below 4 contacts/week = funnel starved; below 1/week = funnel doesn't exist. Meeting rate below 1 in 20 = the prospect list (most common), email (second), or contact ID (third) is wrong.

**The "Know Your Numbers" backwards model (to land 10 new sponsors):**
- Proposal→sale close: **50%** → send **20 proposals.**
- Discovery→proposal-request: **~33%** → run **60 discovery sessions.**
- Outreach→meeting: **30%** → reach **180 engaged prospects.**
- Response rate: **20%** → need **900 total prospects in pipeline.**
- Net: **900 prospects → 10 sales** (note these rates are "higher than average," so you may need more).
- The 5 metrics to track: # prospects needed, # replies, # who agree to meet, # proposals *requested* (not sent), # sales closed.

**Generic sizing:** Sponsors needed = **Revenue goal ÷ average package cost.** ($500,000 ÷ $10,000 = **50 packages**.) To close ~25 new sponsors: "a few hundred prospects."

### Pipeline / list sizing
- Prospect pool: ≥100 via Concentric Circles; usually capped **300–500.** Smaller method: 50–60+. Recommended depth 30–60 (deeper in a recession). Podcasts: start 20–30, expand past 50.
- Survey "intend to purchase in 18 months" question alone → **20–30+ hot brands.**

### Audience data
- **≥25 data points per segment** (events/conferences **30+**). Minimum **3 avatars** (3–5 typical). Income granularity to **$5,000 increments.** Re-survey if **>6 months** old.
- Survey participation: **>25% reply = "onto something"; more than a quarter = great shape.** Don't expect 100%.

### Outreach / conversion
- **Cold-email reply target: 10–20%** ("very, very well"); some say **15–20%.** **5–10%** agreeing to a call = "outstanding / phenomenal." Ignoring best practices → **1–2%** (if lucky). Mass-blasting → **~0%.** Expecting 50–100% is why people get frustrated.
- Long designed proposal sent cold to ~200 companies: **<1% reply.** Cold-emailed proposal open rate ≈ **0% (maybe 0.1%).**
- Direct-mail conversion: **2% on a good day.**
- Email open-rate target: **~18%** (Campaign Monitor) — below that, suspect the subject line.
- Healthy property split: **~40% reply rate, ~25% meeting rate** ("reply rate is conversation; meeting rate is pipeline" — don't quote one as both).
- Changing only the email (same list) usually **triples reply rate within ~4 weeks**; first new discovery meeting typically lands within ~3 weeks of adopting the weekly rhythm.

### Closing / ratios
- **Closing rate: start with a 30% goal**, increase as you gain sponsors; bands 30–50%, 50–75%.
- Discovery-close ~33%; proposal-close ~33–50%.
- **97% of sponsors dislike tiered (gold/silver/bronze) packages; only ~3% accept them** ("count the brands that accept them on one hand and still have fingers left over").
- **ROI ratios sponsors expect: 2:1, 3:1, 4:1** (worth $1 → sponsor wants to pay ~33¢ at 3:1, 25¢ at 4:1).

### Failure / iteration thresholds
- 10 emails with no reply = enough signal to change course ("even one email and no reply is enough"); don't send 90 more. ~1% (or under) open rate from 10 emails = wrong approach; volume won't fix it.
- ≥2 weeks of dead cold-calling with no other pipeline = move on (recession post).

### Digital/advertising rates (valuation lens, from "Digital Marketing and Sponsorship")
- **CPM (≈ logo placement): $5–$10 per 1,000 impressions** (pennies/fractions of a penny per view). A logo-on-sign = "at best **three cents per view**."
- **Cost per click (≈ web traffic): $2–$10/click** (a hyperlinked logo may get ~1 click per 10,000 visits).
- **Cost per send (≈ eblast): $0.50–$1.00/send** (LinkedIn-style DM). A logo at the bottom of someone else's email = a fraction of this.
- **Cost per lead (most valuable): $50 to $2,000+/lead** (email / free-trial / booked meeting).
- Advertising (logos + commercials) = only **~5–10%** of total sponsorship dollars; **90–95%** of the **$60B+/yr** market goes to everything but logos/ads.
- Sponsorship margin "sometimes 90%" (vs. exhibit booths). A sponsor is **200x** an exhibitor in marketing value.

### Channel-specific pricing
- **Podcast:** host-read ads **$18–$30 CPM**; Dynamic Ad Insertion (DAI) **$12–$18 CPM**; legacy 1,000–9,999 listeners/episode ≈ **$25 for a 60-sec ad** (AdvertiseCast 2021). Downloads: **1,000–5,000/episode** (niche/expert), **10,000+/episode** (mid-tier packages); "no magic number" (some sponsors OK with 5,000, others want 50,000+); need ≥several hundred (better: several thousand) listeners before pursuing. 70–80% avg episode completion target. Fast-forward buttons are 15/30s — "exactly the length of your commercials."
- **YouTube CPM: $10–$50 per 1,000 views** (1M views → $10K–$50K). CPM = before YouTube's cut; RPM = after.
- **Twitch:** sponsored social post 2024 = **$200/post** (still valuate). 140M+ MAU (2024).
- **Influencer tiers:** micro 10K–50K; macro 150K+ (also cited "macro 150K+, mega 500K+"); social-media post market avg **$100** (set $50/$100/$200/$400 by quality).

### Naming rights
- Most assets at market; logos low-value. Regional venues = low-to-mid **six figures/yr**; major venues = high six figures to **eight figures/yr.** Deal lengths: **10–30 yrs** (major), **3–7 yrs** (regional). Small buildings/wings ≈ **3 yrs**; arenas/malls/high-rises ≈ **10–20 yrs.** Annual escalators **2–5%.**
- 26 of 32 NFL stadiums branded; NFL naming-rights deals avg **$8.09M/yr**; MetLife paid **$425–500M over 25 yrs.** Most expensive: Scotiabank × Maple Leaf Sports — **CAD $800M / 20 yrs (US$635.84M)**, 2017. GoldenPalace.com paid **$650,000** to name a monkey.

### Media partnership leverage
- Properties valued title sponsorship at "low five figures," added a national media partner, jumped to "mid six figures." Waiting until after a title sponsor would have cost **>$1M over three years.** Ask media partners for **3:1 or 4:1 ROI** on your media cash investment.

### First-year / discounts / renewal
- First-year discount: **20–50% off** (a rate you can afford while hitting goals); full price on renewal. Recession: bonuses/discounts for multiyear deals.
- Renewal uplift: **+50%** OR multi-year flat. Over-deliver target: **≥10%.** Renewal/upsell conversion (real story): **~30%** of partners agreed to **double** their investment when shown over-delivery and asked.

### Event/cost baselines (for sizing the ask)
- Gala ≈ **$10,000–$15,000**; convention ≈ **$175,000–$325,000.** Golf fundraiser nets ≥**$5,000** (below = "not worthwhile"), up to **$100,000+.** Wine: pay $500 for wine, find a sponsor to pay **$3,000** to be the wine sponsor.
- Virtual event: webinar-scale **$2,500–$10,000**; single-day w/ panels+breakouts **$10,000–$20,000**; multi-day **$20,000–$50,000.**
- Venue rental **$100–$2,000+/hour** (8-hr conference = $800–$16,000 in space alone). Local 30-sec TV ad up to **$1,000.** Radio **$200–$5,000/week.** Social ads **$15–$200/day**, up to **$6,000/month.**
- Marketing-budget rules of thumb: **2–5% of sales revenue** on advertising (podcast); a $500M-revenue company spends **8.6%** of revenue on marketing.
- Festival: PRS fees = **3% of ticket revenue**; VAT **≥20%.** Festivals can be funded by multiple small sponsors (five at $4k = $20k).

### Timeframes / cadences
- Corporate next-year decisions made by **end of October / early November** — start outreach in the fall (~30 days out) to get on the radar.
- Decision typically takes **2–3 meetings** before a sponsor sees the proposal (corporate event).
- Cold-letter → discovery → proposal → close needs **≥6 months** of runway; don't cold-send if the event is 1–3 months out.
- Follow-up: give a few business days up to a week of silence after sending materials; "communication cadence" = ~7 days of emails + calls.
- Fulfillment report: within **1–2 weeks** (within **7 days** to lock renewal).
- Conference planning **≥12 months out.** Festival promotion **≥8 weeks out, certainly within 6** (avg person sees **6,000–10,000 ads/day**). Festival contest/giveaway: **~4 months out.** Podcast trailer: 5 sec–2 min, aim ~1 min.
- Best email send times (Mailmunch): Tuesday #1 (top slot **10 a.m. Tue**), then Thu/Wed; (Thu 8 p.m., Wed 2 p.m., Thu 6 a.m., Thu 10 a.m.).
- Social cadence (Hootsuite): LinkedIn 1–5×/day, Twitter 1–5×/day, Facebook 1–2×/day, Instagram 3–7×/week. Content: ≥2×/week building to 4×/week (podcast); ≥1×/week (festival).

### Cited industry / market stats
- Sponsorship market **$60B+/yr** ($68B invested — Forbes); US CAGR "reaching $220M in 2027" (Statista, as written). Motorsports industry **$5.8B** (2022). Highest-grossing festival ≈ **$30M** (2019). Burning Man cost **$35.8M** (2015). US hosts **1.8M events/yr**, **270,000** conferences/conventions (2017). ~92,000 food trucks in US (2026).
- **Recession:** global sponsorship spending *increased* in 2008 and was slightly higher in 2009 (RTR Sports/Statista) — "sponsorship didn't stop." Resilient industries: utilities, telecom, healthcare, consumer staples.
- Nielsen 2024: sponsorship → avg **10%+ lift in purchase intent** (with exposure). Kantar: integrated-campaign sponsorship → **22% stronger brand recall.** Velocity 2025: digital-touchpoint activations capture **3–5× more first-party data.** Bizzabo 2024: **72%** of sponsors rank lead quality #1 ROI driver. Experiential layering → **~30%** better recall; event-driven content → **~3×** engagement of standard posts. CNN: **>75%** of sports sponsorships promote junk food.
- Conscious consumerism (Porch Media) avg spend/transaction: Gen Z **$58.30**, millennials **$54.91** (lowest), Gen X **$60.36**, boomers 55–75 **$61.69**, 76+ **$60.38.** Gen Z: 77% act on causes they feel strongly about; 65% bought after an influencer; 23% have boycotted a brand.
- Video boosts email conversions up to **80%** (VWO). Cautionary: logos "increased a sponsor's sales by only 1.5%" vs. a promised 25%.

### Small-audience proof points
- One practitioner personally sold **$80K–$150K** individual deals to an audience of **~150 people** (sold dozens); a **350-person** first-year conference beat mega-conferences on total sponsorship revenue; multiple six- and seven-figure deals on audiences of "a couple hundred."

### The structural 5 sections of a sponsorship LETTER
1. Intro to you + your opportunity (1–2 sentences; "less is more"). 2. The reason you got in touch (lead with the *connection*, not the money). 3. Audience info (real data, not "middle-class families"). 4. Your activation + sponsorship opportunities (give **3 activation examples** your sponsors/audience love). 5. A mention of when you'll follow up (specific date/time).

### Sponsorship-letter FAQ guidance
- Start a letter: "A simple 'Hello, my name is…' suffices." Address it to **the decision-makers** — never "Dear Sir/Madam," "To Whom It May Concern," "Dear friend," "Dear supporter," "I hope this note finds you well" (sound like spam; lower response). If they agreed to meet but set no time: "There is no meeting happening if you don't have a date or time set" — confirm or propose one.

### The 5 core discovery questions (canonical, recurring) + secret close
1. "Who is your target audience / target customers / target market?" (Start here — signals you understand the new rules.)
2. "What action do your customers take right before they make a purchase, and how do you typically get them to take that action?" (Walk me through your sales funnel.)
3. "Tell me about a sponsorship agreement that went badly, and why — what made it not produce ROI?"
4. "What can you tell me about your sales goals for the coming year?"
5. "What would you consider the most important elements of a sponsorship package, and why — and what should I avoid?"
- **Secret close:** "This sounds like we have a fit — would you agree? Is this a good time for me to put together a **draft** proposal to submit for your feedback?" (Demand explicit, not implied, consent. If "no," honor it.)
- Gain-agreement tags to end a sentence with: **"…am I on the right track?"** / **"Does that sound fair?"**

### Additional discovery questions (from the 37-question bank)
- "Tell me about a sponsorship that went badly and why." / "What was the last marketing campaign your company did, and how did it go? What next time?" / "What do you consider the ideal sponsorship partner?" / "What are your short-term and long-term business goals?" / "How does a sponsorship with you typically go?" / "What was the most recent feedback you've received from your audience?" / "What are your company's core values?" / "How do you measure sponsorship success?" / "Who is your ideal customer and why?" / "Can I tell you a bit about my audience to see if we have common goals?" / "How do people move through your company's sales funnel?" / "How do you define brand awareness?" / "How much does new customer onboarding cost your company?"

### The full Discovery Sales-Meeting Script (verbatim flow)
1. **Build rapport.** "Hey, how are you? I live in Canada, the weather's always terrible — you must live somewhere amazing." (Don't over-stay it.)
2. **Set the stage / tie-down.** "I want to be respectful of our time — we only have 30 minutes, so let's dive in. Quick check-in: are you still okay to go for the full 30 minutes?" (Ask up front so they can't escape an intense moment later.)
3. **Frame the meeting.** "The reason for the call is to see whether we share common goals or a common audience and whether there's potential to work together in the future. If it's okay with you, I'd love to learn about your organization, what you're trying to achieve, what's working and what isn't, and some of your goals next quarter. Does that work for you?" (Use "Does that sound **fair**?")
4. **Audience first.** "I'd love to learn more about your target market and your ideal customer. Who's your ideal customer? What do they value? What's important to them? What challenges are your customers facing? What problems do you solve for them? What do you want your customers to walk away thinking and doing?" — Dig: "Interesting — what do you mean by that? What else? Why do you think that is?"
5. **Confirm the avatar.** "I have a good sense of who your customer is — am I right? Your ideal customer is the 40-year-old high-net-worth mom in the suburbs, three kids, they play soccer, drives a Lexus — do I have that right?"
6. **Identify challenges.** "What metrics are you responsible for achieving, and what are your biggest problems in achieving them? You want to sell 50 cars this quarter — how are you doing? What happens if you don't get 50 test drives?" (Ask hard questions casually.)
7. **Summarize + acknowledge (diagnose).** "Do you mind if I check in to make sure I understand? You want to sell 50 cars by April 1, your radio ad budget isn't delivering because people aren't commuting/listening, so you can't tell people about your promotions and they're not coming for test drives. Do I have that right?"
8. **Offer a solution (prescribe).** "Perfect — I can definitely help you connect with these people, run a cool contest to get them down to the lot. Does it make sense for me to share a bit about how we work with [their avatar]?" → **then go silent and wait.**
9. **Explain your approach, not a pitch.** "We focus on a sponsor's goal outcomes first, then match it to their target audience. We create an experience that engages the audience in meaningful ways — we don't do stock packages with stuff you don't want. We worked with a sponsor last year who wanted X, we did Y, it worked pretty well. What else can I share? Where should we go from here?" → silence until they speak.
10. **Handle "just send me a proposal" (the death-kiss / "cold garden response").** "Absolutely, no problem — I'll put together a **draft** for you to review and you can tell me if I'm on the right track. I know you mentioned X — is there anything else you want me to add? … So you want a proposal designed to achieve these three outcomes, via these assets/activations, measured by these ROI measures — anything else? I'm free all day Thursday and Friday; what works best for a quick call to review?"
11. **Budget pushback** — walk the cap down (see §11).
12. **Find other stakeholders** — "so I can invite the right people on my end, who else on your end should be part of this?"

### "Just send me a proposal" rebuttal (verbatim, when said BEFORE discovery)
Say **"No,"** then: "Everything we do is custom and I couldn't possibly put together a proposal without first learning from you what you are trying to accomplish, who you are trying to connect with and whether or not we can help you achieve your business goals."
- Alt framing: tell them you're "taking a stand against bad sponsorship sales practices and that you only create packages based on the needs of your sponsors," then ask for a 5-minute call about their goals, audience, and what they prefer to invest in.
- **Ignore-the-request litmus test:** pretend you didn't hear / "meant to send but got busy." Asked once = brush-off; asked again = genuine interest. Never admit you ignored them.
- **Honest one-liner:** "I don't have any materials to share right now because I'd like to get a better sense of what you're trying to achieve first."

### "Let me think about it" response (verbatim, two-part)
> "Sure! Take your time. These are big decisions and potentially a big investment as well as a huge part of your upcoming marketing strategy. You should think about it."
Then: "Is there anything I can provide you to help you make that decision? Are there any questions you have for me? Would it be helpful if I put you in touch with an existing partner of ours so you can discuss how they're seeing value in this partnership?"

### "Are we a fit?" close at end of first meeting (verbatim)
> "It sounds like our companies could work really well together. Do you agree?" (A "yes" only means *could be a fit* — a "minor win at best.")

### Checking with your boss mid-deal (verbatim)
> "Let me just confirm this with my boss and I'll get back to you today." (Then actually follow up the same day.)

### Discovery questions to fundraising-event prospects (Gail Perry, verbatim)
> "Who is your target market?" / "Our audience is comprised of… Is that of interest to your company? If yes, why? If no, then tell me who is?" / "Tell me about your ideal customer." / "What are ways that you engage with your customers?" / "What sponsorships have you engaged in recently that you thought went well? Why?" / "Was there a time when a sponsorship opportunity didn't produce results? What could have been different?"

### Exhibitor-upgrade script (verbatim)
Caller: "We want to buy an exhibit space." Instead of "That'll be $6,000": "What are you hoping to accomplish with an exhibit space?" → [they list goals a booth can't deliver] → "It sounds like you're really interested in branding and thought leadership — an exhibit booth really isn't the best place for that. You'll get some benefits and I'm happy to set you up with a booth, but would you be interested in hearing about some other options that would help you achieve these goals?" → "I'll put a booth on hold for you, great location, no concern" → full discovery → custom proposal.

### "No package" model script (the Goldilocks "just right" property)
- "We don't have one. Everything we do is custom based on your needs. If we don't have a fit, then there's no need to waste your time with a package."
- That property's buyer-side questions: Who is your target market? / What action do you want them to take? / If we work together, what is the one thing you have to have to consider this a success? / How can we help you look good in front of your client? / Are there people your client wants to meet? Can we help make an introduction? / What do you hate about sponsorship packages? / What was the coolest thing you saw at a recent event that you wish we could do together?
- Close: "Does it sound like we have a fit? Can I put something together and send it over for your feedback before it goes to the client?"

### CTA language for proposals (verbatim)
> "We don't do gold, silver, and bronze packages because we know they don't work. We want to build something just for you to help you connect with your target market."

### Renewal ask (verbatim)
> "If I make the required changes and double down on what you liked, would you be interested in looking at a draft proposal about renewal?" — plus "What do I have to do RIGHT NOW to make this your #1 marketing priority next year?"

### AI-policy question to ask a sponsor (verbatim)
> "Is it okay if I use AI for ideation, graphics, or proposals?"

### Pipeline-building scripts
- **Business Breakfast:** host introduces you as "a friend and colleague"; you tell the crowd you want two things: (1) to buy each of them a coffee in the next two weeks; (2) for someone to host an identical breakfast for you next month. Over coffee ask: who should I be talking to about my sponsorship opportunity? what do you value in a sponsorship offering? how can the two of us work together?
- **Friday Five:** Friday at 1:00 PM, email 5 cold prospects to your referral network asking ONE question: "Do you know anyone on this list, or at these companies, that you can introduce me to?" Pre-write the intro email so they only forward it and CC you. (Confirm each of the 5 is connected to ≥1 person on your referral list via LinkedIn first.)

### Phrases to AVOID
- "To whom it may concern," "Dear friend/supporter," "I hope this note finds you well." • "We don't want sponsors, we want partners" (define "partner" and you'll just describe good sponsorship). • Anything opening with founding year, team size, CEO bio, 27-year history. • The words "gold/silver/bronze" anywhere ("sponsor repellent"). • "Sponsorship proposal" / "sponsorship package" in the document text.

## 17. Named Concepts & Glossary

- **BAMFAM** — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting." Before the current meeting ends, schedule the next one (sponsors are busy — "60 other tasks"; if you leave the ball in their court, momentum dies). The antidote to "send me a proposal" → ghosting. (Taught in the Sponsorship Accelerator.)
- **Property / rights holder** — the sponsorship seeker (event, show, team, festival, program, gala, etc.) and the full collection of assets + activations offered. "Give me money so I'll have it is not a property."
- **Brand / sponsor** — the company buying sponsorship (provides funding/products/services).
- **Asset** — "something you are willing to give a company in exchange for money"; tangible or intangible. Logos are explicitly **low-value.**
- **Activation** — experiential marketing; "switching on" a sponsorship; connects audience + sponsor in a way both love; must satisfy an **audience need AND a sponsor need simultaneously.** NOT a tier, logo, or necessarily expensive.
- **Fulfillment report (a.k.a. post-event / wrap-up report; "thank-you letter")** — proof-of-delivery document (promised vs. delivered, revised values, photos, metrics, ROI). "The best prospecting tool out there." Basis for renewal, case studies, legal protection.
- **Fulfillment meeting / sponsor summit** — the renewal meeting where you present the report and ask for next year/upsell/multi-year.
- **Discovery session** — exploratory first meeting to surface the sponsor's challenges/goals; NOT sales, NOT asking for money. Goal = get the second meeting.
- **Business case** — sent BEFORE a meeting; no pricing; ≥50% audience data; job = earn a meeting. Looks like a rate card.
- **Proposal** — sent AFTER a meeting; has dollar figures. "If you put dollar figures in the document, it's a proposal."
- **Sponsorship package / menu** — priced list of assets; analogous to (but not literally) a restaurant menu; **never tiered.** Page should be called "Our Opportunities"; use "menu" internally only.
- **Avatar / buyer persona / niche** — a named, semi-fictional composite of a real audience segment, based on data ("a semi-fictional representation of your *sponsor's* ideal customers based on research of *your* audience" — twist on HubSpot). Everything but the name is real (amalgamated) data. Examples: "Event Planner Eve" (25, event planner, ~$73k/yr, time-starved); "Busy Bob" (40, $80k, married, 2 kids, suburban, travels for work). Include ≥3.
- **Macro audience** — the whole audience of your property; drill down to 3–5 avatars.
- **Psychographics** — behavioral motivators (beliefs, motivations, opinions, brand/consumption behavior, outside interests); "the most telling part of the audience survey."
- **Market value / market valuation** — base price a service commands on the open market, used as the *baseline* (not final price) for asset-by-asset custom pricing. Generally recommended method.
- **CPM** (cost per mille) — $/1,000 impressions/downloads/views. **CPA** (cost per acquisition) — commission per achieved sponsor goal. **CPC** (cost per click) and **cost per send / cost per lead** — digital-funnel valuation analogues. **RPM** — YouTube revenue per 1,000 after the platform's share.
- **DAI (Dynamic Ad Insertion)** — podcast ads inserted on-demand, targetable, swappable without re-uploading. **Baked-in / live-read ads** — recorded live, permanent, untargeted (pre/mid/post-roll).
- **In-kind / contra sponsorship** — sponsor provides free goods/services instead of cash. **NOT a donation** — still requires assets/activations in exchange ("An in-kind donation is NOT a sponsorship!"). Reframe: giving away free product IS **sampling** (lucrative — charge for it).
- **Sampling rights** — putting a sponsor's product into the audience's hands (a test drive is a sample); one of the most valuable assets.
- **Media sponsor** — an outlet/contact securing earned airtime/press (guaranteed exposure on specific channels). **Promotional sponsor** — amplifies via advertising + web/social (influencer, podcaster). Both differ from organic media. **Cash sponsor** — most sought. (The **four sponsorship types:** in-kind/contra, cash, promotional, media.)
- **Lead gen** — emails, phone numbers, quote requests, free-trial signups, booked meetings. "A license to print money."
- **Naming-rights variants:** **Title-sponsored** (you actually name the property after the sponsor; original name erased, e.g., Camping World Bowl) vs. **Presenting rights** ("[Festival] presented by [Brand]"). Naming alone is "technically" title sponsorship but "not sufficient."
- **Exclusivity provision** — blocks competitors from sponsoring/advertising; commands a premium. **Prominence/dominance provision** — guarantees the sponsor's signage appears more often, larger, brighter, more visible than all others. **Right of First Refusal** — buy something the moment it goes on the market (renewal mechanism).
- **Exhibitor vs. Sponsor** — an exhibitor **pays you** for booth space, is in it for themselves, not invested in your turnout; a sponsor **gives you** cash/promo/in-kind to solve their goals and IS invested. A sponsor is ~**200x** an exhibitor in marketing value. An exhibit booth is "an asset, a commodity — not sponsorship."
- **"Happy ears"** — uncritically believing everything a sponsor says (e.g., that "just send me the proposal" is genuine). A trap; take early platitudes "with a grain of salt."
- **"Sponsor speak / code"** — sponsors avoid conflict by saying nice things they don't mean ("send me the proposal" = "no thanks"; = "let's grab coffee sometime").
- **Spaghetti approach / "spaghetti mode"** — throwing every fact/asset/offer/level at the wall hoping something sticks. The #1 thing to avoid in emails and meetings. "Less is more."
- **Sponsorship alchemy** — the false belief that mixing low-value ingredients (bad prospecting, no audience data, gold/silver/bronze, no fulfillment) and applying "heat/pressure" (longer hours, more emails/calls) turns it to gold. It only makes "bigger piles of mediocrity."
- **Qualified lead vs. regular lead** — a regular lead stumbles in knowing nothing; a qualified lead has researched, knows products + pricing, is readier to buy. Your audience = qualified leads handed to the sponsor "on a silver platter." Lead-quality (depth in funnel) > lead-quantity for projecting spend.
- **Push marketing assets** — logo-on-everything / e-blasts / signage; a too-narrow inventory.
- **Common benefits list / pool & Hybrid sponsorship package** — a non-customized stock set offered to all sponsors ("customized light"). Boilerplate allowed: "all our partners get the following benefits…" then offer a custom package to anyone who wants more. A time-saver but caps revenue and kills collaboration.
- **Media kit** (influencer context) — like a proposal but includes a bio/"about" section, social stats, past collabs, testimonials.
- **NPS (Net Promoter Score)** — 0–10 recommend scale; 0–6 detractors, 7–8 passives, 9–10 promoters. Used to gauge festival-sponsor impact YoY.
- **Social dismissal** — sponsors ignoring/dismissing people who present as amateurs; present as an **equal** to avoid it.
- **CTA in a business case** — what you want the sponsor to DO = pick up the phone and call you (so you can build something custom), NOT "sign up online" or "send a check."
- **Russian-nesting-doll / onion niching** — keep subdividing a segment until the smallest doll is empty.
- **Sponsorship Accelerator** — a paid training program (scripts, forms, full lifecycle).

## 18. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

**The #1 recurring villain — Gold/Silver/Bronze tiers** ("the cardinal sin," possibly *the* worst mistake; "so dead they make dead horses look lively"; "tear it up and throw it in the garbage"):
- Force sponsors to buy assets they don't want to reach the one they want — the "combo meal," "fries you can't buy without the cheeseburger," "store forcing cologne + shoes + gift card to get the shirt" analogies.
- Zero customization; focus on price tiers instead of goals; ~97% of sponsors dislike them (~3% accept); leave money on the table; make sponsors shop around to other charities.
- **Renaming the tiers doesn't fix it** (Platinum, Ruby, "adamantium" — "a rose is still a rose; a dud is still a dud"). The problem is having grids *at all*; they make you look like an amateur.
- Brands that *seem* to make G/S/B work close **in spite of** the grid (via unseen custom assets / trade-deal leverage you may not have) — don't copy them.
- **NOTABLE INCONSISTENCY:** two guest-contributor posts contradict the house line — one ("Present Different Sponsorship Levels") and one film-festival post ("offering different levels of sponsorship") recommend tiers. Treat these as outliers; the dominant, heavily-repeated position is **anti-tier / pro-custom-menu.**

**Proposal / outreach anti-patterns:**
- **Going "proposal-first" / leading with or emailing the proposal** (recurring). Turns sponsorship into a direct-mail campaign (2% conversion); the sponsor "has no idea who you are, no agreed-upon problem, no reason to read fourteen pages today." Proposal lands in a "for budget season" folder, never reopened. "'Send me a proposal' is code for 'no thanks,' and when you go in proposal-first you go right to the no." (Real client "Dave" lost a Fortune-500 deal by emailing bullets about his 1.5M impressions — the sponsor replied it wasn't impressive and declined to meet.)
- **Mass-emailing/eblasting proposals/packages** to strangers — "carpet bombing people with proposals is not prospecting." A 0%-success activity at scale still yields 0% (lawn-care analogy: right things, wrong order/quantity/time).
- **Putting pricing/grids/menus in the document** at all (backs you into a corner; removes negotiation room). **Including a mail-in cut-out + check** ("absolutely brazen").
- **Being me-centric** — the proposal "is not a biography." You get **one paragraph** about yourself. "The sponsor doesn't care about your company. At all."
- **Long paragraphs / burying audience data deep** — sponsors skim and never read it.
- **Labeling the doc "sponsorship proposal/package"** in the text.
- **Copying competitors' proposals/pricing** — a posted proposal didn't necessarily win anything; copying perpetuates mistakes (especially overpricing, "dead in the water"); and you can't answer "How did you come up with this price?"

**Pricing anti-patterns:**
- **Overpricing** — sponsors "always notice" / "sniff it out"; they rarely say no outright, they **ghost** (eBay-Cheeto / $3,500-SpongeBob analogy).
- **Underpricing** — sponsors work with you but pay far less, "no one will tell you," and they'll expect cheaper assets next time.
- **Letting emotion/ego inflate asset values.**

**Meeting / process anti-patterns:**
- **Treating discovery as a sales meeting / bringing the proposal to it / hard-pitching for 30–45 min** (recurring).
- **Talking money too soon** (kills the second meeting). **Waiting for the sponsor to set up the next meeting** (use BAMFAM).
- **Bringing materials / visibly taking notes** in the first meeting ("preyed-upon" feeling).
- **Having "happy ears" / falling for sponsor "code."** Firing a proposal on "just send me a proposal," then getting ghosted.
- **Taking answers at face value** instead of asking "what do you mean by that?" 2–3 times.
- **No elevator pitch — ever.** **Don't try to "get around the gatekeeper"** ("a very angry person who has a gatekeeper for a reason"). **Don't email info@/customer-service or call generic numbers** to reach the CMO.
- **Assuming you know what the sponsor wants** ("the cardinal sin").
- **Hearing a deferral as a rejection** (self-fulfilling prophecy; crush-on-a-date analogy) — but also **pestering after a clear "no."**

**Audience / targeting anti-patterns:**
- **"Everyone"/general-public/middle-class-families audience.** **Defining audience only by "people who like [topic]."** **Chasing big brands for size/money alone** (coldest leads).

**In-kind anti-patterns:**
- **Letting in-kind value = $0.** Once you give away wine/AV/lunch "free," you've "created a market value of $0" — then you can't prove your brand/audience is worth more than $0 (restaurant "free dessert then surprise bill" parable).
- **Double-dipping** — accepting free product from an in-kind sponsor AND a cash sponsor who outranks them → a very unhappy in-kind sponsor. Fix: **buy the product yourself** and fold its cost into the valuation.
- **Selling wine sponsorship to wine producers** — sell it to professional-services firms who want your demographic.
- **Treating contra as "less work"; accepting items that cost you to receive/distribute; aligning with a controversial brand or one that offends paying sponsors; offloaded "crappy tech."**
- **Using sponsorship to cover hard costs / letting ticket sales drive revenue** — "the hard costs of your event have nothing to do with the market value of your assets." (In-kind is only justified to offset genuine budgeted hard costs, e.g., $10k legal services for $10k sponsorship.)

**Measurement anti-patterns:**
- **Measuring what you can't control** (don't accept blame for the sponsor's own conversion failures). **Overpromising / underdelivering** (give conservative estimates — expect 1,500 attendees → tell the sponsor 800–1,000; promised 5,000, 2,000 show "doesn't look good"). "Overpromise/underdeliver is one of the worst things you can do in sponsorship" (risks breach, financial + reputational death). **Lying about value/attendance** "always comes back to bite you." **Inflating fulfillment numbers** (sponsors trace their own sales). **Skipping the fulfillment report** (fastest way to lose sponsors after year one).

**Structural / strategic anti-patterns:**
- **Relying on your cause / sob story.** **Leading with logos as a top asset.** **Thin/push-only inventory.**
- **The 5 direct-outreach substitutes (avoidance behaviors):** (1) polishing/sending long proposals cold; (2) sponsorship industry networking events (decision-makers aren't there — you network with competitors/vendors); (3) LinkedIn posting as a strategy; (4) waiting on warm intros (usually land on a non-decision-maker 3 rungs from budget); (5) content marketing as a lead engine. Common thread: "None require a specific named human at a specific company to tell you no. They protect you from rejection… The hours were never the constraint. The willingness to be told no by a specific person was."
- **Building a strategy around online forms** ("very efficient at an incredibly inefficient process").
- **Selling sponsorship from your desk.** **Treating relationships as the only thing needed** ("sponsors are results-driven, not relationship-driven").
- **Confusing brand with logo, and "brand awareness" with "logo awareness."**
- **Setting goals too low** ("just cover costs" / "make as much money as possible" — not real goals; leaves five-/six-figure title deals on the table; "play to win, not not-to-lose").
- **Small-audience trap:** chasing low-ticket consumer products and vanity metrics ($2-coffee sponsors, logo-on-a-sign) instead of high-ticket sampling/lead-gen/speaking/networking.
- **Calling sponsorship "easy"** (waiting until 1–2 months out, emailing strangers, "that company has money"). **"I don't have time / 90% effort"** ("90% effort may equal no results").
- **Working longer hours instead of fixing the leaky bucket** (alchemy / scaling mediocrity).
- **Verbal-only agreements** (he-said/she-said; recordings may be inadmissible). **Skipping the lawyer.**
- **Booking irrelevant sponsors/exhibitors** just because they'll pay. **Forcing exhibit space on a sponsor.** **Calling exhibitors "sponsors."**
- **Treating sponsors as investors or donors** (banks/VCs are for seed money; charity is for donors).

**AI anti-patterns:** using AI to run discovery, collect audience data, or negotiate (it can't, and shouldn't — NY lawyer fired for fake AI-generated cases). Always fact-check AI; put your own spin on graphics (no AI copyright law yet; reverse-image-search-detectable); AI copy "tells" = ultra-short sentences + repeated phrases.

**Other:** trusting a sponsorship committee with no expertise (Property A filled out a bank's online form on committee advice, waited 180 days, was declined, missed budget). Board overreach in nonprofits (boards make intros/host events — NOT send packages/sell/dictate tactics). Hashtag gaffes — read aloud / have several people vet ("#CLitFest" Chester Literary Festival; "#susanalbumparty"-type risk). Teaching-your-boss errors (being a know-it-all, "your approach sucks," taking all the credit, no final say / lawyer review). Hiring rushing (1–3 days back-to-back interviews), not calling references, cookie-cutter questions, fuzzy onboarding, lame incentives, public shaming, expecting a cold pipeline to perform like a stewarded one, non-experts dictating to experts.

## 19. Case Studies (named client/guest stories, industry + outcome + tactic)

- **CJ Scarlet (Harmony NC):** Landed a **$30,000 multi-year partnership within 3 days** — and more in the first 90 days than her org had raised in 30 years. Tactic: the full audience-first process.
- **Chris Norwood (DUSK Festival):** Went "from sponsorship student to master"; **doubled revenue.** Built a **300-company prospect list** ("just right") spanning ~50 companies across 10 industries; branched contacts across marketing/sales/experiential/lead-gen/HR; researched prospects' interests on LinkedIn.
- **Anissa Cooke (SynBioBeta, conferences):** Left old mistakes behind. Tactics: **record jargon-heavy calls** and harvest the sponsor's exact words for proposals; social/mixer activations sell first ("sponsors eat them up"); recreate serendipity virtually (Speakeasy 3D platform) when remote.
- **Practitioner track record:** Tripled a charity's revenue in 18 months; held a **$3M portfolio in 2008**; sold **$80K–$150K** deals to a **~150-person** audience (dozens of deals); a **350-person** first-year conference beat mega-conferences on total sponsorship revenue (no booths; custom experiential tied to brand goals).
- **The "cross out the package" friend** (senior sponsorship leader): In week one, handed each inherited sponsor the predecessor's package + a pen: "cross out everything you don't care about." **Every** sponsor crossed out **>half** (didn't care about free tickets, social posts, stage thank-yous, logo soup). She tore up the package, delivered only what they wanted, produced fulfillment reports, then asked them to double spend — **~30% agreed to double their investment.**
- **Martial-arts podcast:** Struck out pitching martial-arts clubs/gear. Audience research revealed listeners skewed **white-collar professionals**; pivoted to **HR companies + recruiting agencies** (workshops/experiences with a famous figure in the sport). Result: **six figures.** (Lesson: audience-knowledge shift.)
- **Drag racer + barbecue (motorsport):** Survey showed his database was overwhelmingly **construction business owners** who loved barbecue. Built a VIP experience (pellet-grill + whiskey/bourbon) for entrepreneurs, but had an **insurance company pay for the whole thing** (insurance/accounting/legal are real needs for business owners). Charged a major premium for serving the exact audience the insurer wanted in a high-trust environment.
- **Female motorsport enthusiast:** A client's survey showed they over-indexed for women (**~15%** of database) with specific hobbies/income. Instead of "logo on the car," pitched brands on connecting with "the female motorsport enthusiast who makes this much, prefers these brands, buys these cars, travels this often." It worked — differentiation via audience knowledge.
- **Hospital / x-ray company trade deal (2008):** A sponsor mid-layoffs declined a six-figure ask but noted they "must be the gold sponsor at a hospital gala" — because that sponsorship was tied to the hospital **buying 5 years of x-ray machines.** Outsiders saw "gold/silver/bronze working"; reality was a **trade deal** (success *in spite of* the grid).
- **"Boss-approved $50K custom" job:** Forced to sell gold/silver/bronze in a junior role, one seller used the package only as a **conversation starter**, did discovery, told leadership "I can sell silver for $20K, but they want 4–5 custom assets and have **$50K**." Leadership always agreed (2–3x the rights fee).
- **Exhibitor → title sponsor:** A caller wanted to buy a **$6,000 exhibit booth.** The seller ran discovery instead ("what are you hoping to accomplish?"), put the booth on hold, did full discovery with leadership, built a custom proposal. They **pulled budget from several other conferences**, took the **title sponsorship**, and renewed year after year.
- **Client A vs Client B (online forms):** Client A hired high-schoolers to copy-paste boilerplate into hundreds of online forms → months later got the polite "more requests than we can fund" rejection. Client B used audience data + prospecting to find the right contact, ran discovery, spent ~3 months co-building a custom proposal, met in person, got a handshake yes — then filled out the same form as a **rubber-stamp.** Both filled out the form; only the order differed.
- **Geography "no" → referral → deal (Hidden Value):** After thorough discovery, a prospect said "you're not a fit, wrong geography." The client asked "Is there anyone in your network we should speak to?" → warm intros → a deal worth **tens of thousands.**
- **The bank — work-the-verticals (Nine Paths):** Reaching the CMO via customer service failed (Fortune 100). Working **marketing/sales/sponsorship/comms/brand/product** at C/VP/Director/Manager/Coordinator/Admin levels: a cold-prospected bank became the **presenting sponsor for two years** (directly from a recorded over-the-shoulder training video). Separately, at a nonprofit a fundraiser had a bank's **CSR/Foundation at $10K**, told her "I bet we can help you recruit," got a warm intro to the **VP of HR (Dave)**, who added **$35K to recruit** while keeping the $10K = **$45K/yr for three years.** Another bank: a warm intro to a **Coordinator** on the staff-engagement committee → an outdoor-festival play-and-engage deal.
- **The VIP bathroom activation (national fundraising event):** Portable washrooms ran out of paper towels/soap by 9:30am; post-event survey confirmed "the bathrooms sucked" was the #1 complaint nationwide. Instead of begging a porta-potty company for free units, they got a **consumer-goods company** to build a **VIP bathroom experience** (plush towels, sunscreen, bug spray, kept stocked) + AC trailers for VIPs/top fundraisers. Follow-up survey: the bathroom experience was the #1 thing people **loved.**
- **Dave Stewart / Wet Fly Swing (podcast):** "Downsizing aspirations isn't failing" — start with smaller sponsors; "the bigger sponsors usually follow not too long after."

## 20. Industry-Specific Playbooks

### Events / Conferences / Exhibit Halls
- **Name packages by GOAL, not tier:** "Lead Generation," "Brand Recognition," "Experiential Marketing," "Brand Building," "Product Placement," "Sampling," "Contests," "Growing their database," "Thought Leadership." Call the page "Our Opportunities."
- **Upgrade exhibitors → sponsors** (200x value, ~90% margin) with exclusive exhibitor-only packages; run discovery with every exhibitor (your hottest prospects); give them fulfillment reports.
- Decision typically takes 2–3 meetings before the proposal. Lock the second meeting before leaving the first; produce a fulfillment report to renew.
- **Advanced activations/exhibiting:** projection mapping, social-media walls (real-time posts), AR/VR, unconventional venues (museums, galleries, a whole movie multiplex), authentic props/photo ops, entertainers (acrobats, magicians, comedians), mini stores & eateries / sponsored cocktail service, sponsored charging stations (free or contact-gated), scavenger hunts with hashtags + lead capture.
- **Exhibit-hall traffic boosters:** rework marketing (target the *right* demo; go digital/trackable; have a budget); event app (vendor list, map, live schedule, data collection); giveaways/scavenger hunts/raffles (easy entry; end-of-day to retain people); door prizes gated behind a mini-game; varied non-messy refreshments; celebrity/industry-star signings; all-day free live sessions listed in the app.
- **Displaying sponsors visibly:** floral wall (luxury/fashion/beauty), branded pool floats/floating signs (outdoor), neon signs bent into logos, projections, photo backdrops, event badges/lanyards, printed furniture, charging stations ("this charging station is brought to you by [sponsor]"). Avoid "another boring sign."
- **Conference planning:** ≥12 months out; name patterns — company-name (Oracle OpenWorld), acronym (SXSW, TNW), single dramatic word (Collision, Rise); estimate first-year attendance by averaging 3–5 similar conferences; over-rent space; build a **separate conference website** + app (if 50+ booths / multi-day); price tickets after most spend is known (break even, then build up); early-bird tiers spaced out.
- **8 conference-marketing tactics:** conference-specific hashtag, retargeting, dedicated social handle, email marketing, **get listed on industry event calendars** (free; email/call them), influencer marketing, host a contest, livestream part of a multi-day event (FOMO).

### Virtual Conferences / Selling Virtual to a Skeptic
- 8 steps incl. **demo + request quotes** before buying. Platforms named: Eventzilla, GoTo Webinar, Communique, Webex Events/Socio, Demio, Swoogo, Livestorm (also Zoom, Remo, InEvent, Attendify, Evenium, Cadence). Mix prerecorded + live (too much live = tech risk; too much prerecorded = "feels like YouTube"). Plan time zones (record + replays). **Build in breaks.** Demand **24/7 customer service.** Engagement: Q&A, virtual scavenger hunt, trivia, karaoke.
- **Selling to a skeptic (5 tactics):** back claims with hard data (understand *why* skeptical — past burn? ROI fear?); **expand what you sell** (non-tangible assets — written/video sponsor profiles, advertising, recommendation placement, streaming demos, virtual booths — price by sophistication); **demo your platform** flawlessly; unique virtual activations (virtual mall, freemium offers, mailed physical swag bags, virtual scavenger hunt, branded waiting rooms, sponsored pre-recorded entertainment); strong analytics that integrate with the platform.

### Festivals / Film Festivals
- Cost = thousands to millions. Budget line items: bars, catering, traders, artist merch, PRS (3%), VAT (≥20%), staffing, AV/PA, security, insurance, permits, ticketing app, waste, utilities, stage(s), performers. Funding: **sponsorship (multiple small sponsors can replace one big one — five at $4k = $20k)**, grants, loans, investors, ticket sales. Theme ≠ genre (EDC = light/LEDs; Lilith Fair = women artists). Ticket providers: Eventbrite, Ticketmaster/Live Nation, AXS, ShowClix. Price at multiple points (regular/VIP/single/multi-day, group >4, early-bird). Outdoor = weather contingency + refund/postponement plan; stop the show in a thunderstorm.
- **Festival proposal = a "business case"** to earn a discovery meeting: **50% audience data**, ≥25 data points/segment, **≥3 avatars**, short **2-sentence intro**, media footprint/impressions/partners, case studies + sponsor quotes, vanity metrics (attendance, email subs, social, web), strong CTA ("call me, we build custom"). **No pricing, no tiers.**
- Match festival theme to sponsor identity (tech co ↔ sci-fi fest; sustainability brand ↔ environmental docs → "reach out to all local solar companies"). Local/small businesses = "a treasure trove."
- **Activation ideas:** sponsor-themed screenings (coffee sponsor → coffee-tasting / exclusive drink), immersive "tech lounge," interactive/VR booths, movie-prop photo booths, contests/giveaways (sponsor provides prize + gets signup data), scavenger hunt, interactive thematic mini-museum displays.
- **Festival timeline:** **1 yr out** (review metrics, survey attendees, secure site, fulfillment report + renegotiate, prospect new sponsors, budget); **6 months out** (finalize sponsors/media, order supplies, confirm performers, vendor contracts); **1 month out** (day-of timeline, ticket push, site tour, AV test, infra, finalize map, test app).
- **12 festival-sponsor success metrics:** tickets sold, attendance (RFID/scannable), NPS, customer-feedback survey, social interactions, website visits, email open rate, email CTR, onsite purchases, new leads, lead quality, brand impressions. Compare YoY, same period; honestly attribute how much the *sponsor* (vs. your own marketing) drove the lift.
- **Promotion:** start ≥8 weeks out; free media (accept every interview), influencers, sponsors, contests (4 months out). Branding: name, logo, brand story, **color palette** (White=balance/calm, Green=health/growth, Blue=strength/trust, Pink=wisdom/imagination, Red=boldness/passion, Orange=confidence/friendliness, Yellow=warmth/optimism), slogan, typography, consistent voice. Catering: food trucks, small bites, handheld, artisanal, sustainable.

### Nonprofits / Charities / Associations
- **Sponsorship ≠ corporate philanthropy** — a separate marketing discipline. **Your audience is rarely the people you help** (homeless, food-bank users — companies rarely want to market to them); it's **"the people who care about who you're helping."** Exception: students at a college/university (companies want students).
- **Partnership, not philanthropy** — pitch marketing/BD/HR people held to budgets; "use their money to achieve yours." The donor pyramid does NOT apply; the sales process does. "Proving you're a good cause is neither necessary nor sufficient."
- **Nonprofit Sponsorship Program Build (10 steps):** distinguish sponsorship vs. philanthropy → understand why companies sponsor → "audience, not mission" → define audience (≥25 pts/segment) → identify properties → in-kind vs. cash up front → experience not logos → build a package (no levels/grids/prices) → get the board on board (right role only) → know your value.
- **Secret extra pathway:** Community Investment / CSR / Foundation — they do philanthropy, not sponsorship; use them for **warm internal intros** to marketing/sales/HR.
- **Board role:** make intros, identify interested companies, host networking events, attend a sponsor summit — NOT send packages or sell.
- Properties: galas, marathons/races, festivals, peer-to-peer fundraisers, naming rights (building/room/event/program), conferences, golf tournaments, networking events, concert series/theatre, VIP/donor functions, skilled-volunteer training events.
- **Fundraising vs. individual giving (donor vs. corporate distinction):** an *individual major donor* is motivated by **impact / social ROI** (research net worth/salary/prior giving; meet in their home with family; hear personal stories; talk mission/vision; never lead with logos/gala passes). Shift to **philanthropy mode** when meeting in-home with family, deep personal stories, requests for more impact, never pivoting to "how can I help your company." Shift to **cause-marketing mode** when title contains brand/comms/HR/CSR, or they ask about your database/events/stakeholders/market research. Report-back differs: donors → mission goals, # people/animals/projects, site tours, board thanks; corporate → website traffic, product distribution, speaking ops, employee-volunteer numbers, press hits, formal fulfillment package. Four shared truths (Maeve Strathy): relationships matter; giving is an investment (both want ROI); accountability counts; both give because they see **value.**

### Fundraising Galas / Mega-Sponsorships (Gail Perry guest)
- "Your audience is the most important aspect… NOT your cause." "The most important tool in your tool belt is NOT the proposal but the **question mark.**" Handing over a package = "asking for money before you've earned the right."
- Sponsors hate logo signs, 30-sec donor speeches, coupon-stuffed delegate bags ("incredibly outdated"). Provide **≥25 audience data points** (incl. brand preferences, buying habits, coming purchases, whether you can impact a buying decision).
- **Real activation examples:** superhero photo booth; Hummer test drive atop a mountain (not for Hummer's customers!); free chocolate milk for kids in the face-painting line; VIP bathroom experience with a bathroom concierge. All ideas came from asking the audience.
- Fulfillment report within **2 weeks**; ask what they liked / could improve / whether goals were met / whether they'll renew if you fix issues — if yes, **send an updated agreement and lock them in now**; if no, you have a few months to win them back.
- **Gala planning:** venue/food/drink/entertainment first → cost it → decide funding gap → choose financial vs media/promotional sponsor (best = both); don't chase small companies if you need deep pockets.

### Sports / Athletes / Soccer Teams
- Largest, most popular sponsorship type; multi-million-dollar deals. **Org benefits:** goals (uniforms, training facility, travel/lodging), web/social traffic, attendance, sales, name recognition (must convert the traffic spike with lead magnets/opt-ins before it drops). **Sponsor benefits:** access to new/qualified audience, free advertising, ↑sales, web/social traffic, community-care perception, brand awareness.
- **Assets:** target market, venue signage (more valuable than expo signage), branded seat sections/booths, naming rights, supplier contracts, exclusivity, digital marketing. Walk the venue and photograph it for signage ideas.
- Select sponsors by **sports-sponsorship background**, not just name. **Promise attainable attendance from real data** (last game 600 → expect 800–900, not 2,000). Athlete value hinges on **performance** (winners attract more sponsors). Athlete audience = the affiliated sports org's audience.
- **Gamification activations:** trivia (RFID/Jumbotron, ≤10 questions), gamification app (badges, location tasks, rewards), VIP experiences (credit-card-gated, spin-to-win), competitions (basket-throw, field-goal, thumb wars, races, rock climbing, digital horse betting — easy + free + RFID-scan), leaderboards, scavenger hunts, live polls, social-media wall (hashtag-gated), Jumbotron shoutouts tied to a sponsor-relevant action. Prizes = sponsor products / sponsor-named cash prize.
- **Health & wellness** can offset junk-food sports excess (>75% of sports sponsorships promote junk food) — harder sell, unique positive-perception advantage. Verticals: supplements, wearables, saunas, red-light therapy, sleep mats, coaching, apps (many small brands: Hims, Keeps, Roman, Truvani, Joy Organics).

### Motorsport
- "There's no such thing as motorsport sponsorship — there's only sponsorship." The motorsport *fan* is one of the most-studied audiences; what matters is **YOUR** fans/subset and how they differ.
- **Logo placement on cars/fire suits/helmets/toolboxes = "a race to the bottom"** (high supply, static demand → pennies); premium spots (front/side of car) cost more. Analyze email DB, social, web, YouTube, your own VIP events.
- **Pitch the subset** ("17% of our audience fits this buyer persona — want to connect with them?"), build a tailored activation, attach a sponsor whose customer = that audience (insurance sponsoring a business-owner BBQ). Charge a premium for low-sales-resistance, high-trust access.
- **Don't offer assets outside your control** (naming rights to a track you don't control). Proposal = ~6 pages incl. cover; sit on it until requested. Motorsport is inherently interesting → easier activations. Use **case studies** (problem → how you helped → rich data) to fill the "talk about yourself" space valuably.

### Naming Rights
- **Title-sponsored** (name erased) vs. **Presenting** ("presented by"). 2025/26 entrants: tech, fintech, EV makers, airlines, renewable-energy brands ("long-term brand infrastructure"). Beyond sports: airports, universities, transit (Penn Medicine Station, NRG Station; Tampa names streetcars), healthcare campuses, mixed-use developments, libraries, museums, performing-arts centers.
- **Building Naming Rights Agreement — required clauses:** new name (exact stylization) → duration → where signage is displayed → rules on changing the name (+ who pays, 30/60-day approval window) → payment terms (lump vs. incremental) → promotional benefits (expectations not guarantees, based on prior-tenant data) → termination rights (both sides). Run the first draft past a lawyer; involve the landlord on signage limits.
- **Naming-Rights Success (5 steps):** know your budget (now + future) → choose the building smartly (confirm no demolition/redevelopment in the contract window) → recognize a name is more than a name (community fabric, local retail tie-ins) → right provisions (exclusivity + prominence/dominance) → understand payment structures (installments + step-ups + escalators 2–5% + equity + community-investment commitments).
- ROI drivers (Intuit Dome mini-case, 23 yrs): repeated broadcast mentions, community/STEM programs, unaided recall via repetition, positive association, plus SEO/discoverability (Google Maps, ticketing, rideshare apps). Evaluation — **properties:** structural stability/no demolition, consistent attendance + media, guaranteed prominence + category exclusivity, positive community sentiment, fallback/co-branded options. **Sponsors:** audience alignment, competitive cost-per-impression, digital extensions (AR signage, app placements, broadcast mentions, social), strong multi-use calendar, CSR/sustainability alignment.

### Podcasts / Influencers / YouTube / Twitch
- "You are a curator of audience," not a place for ad reads. **Downloads ≠ value; quality of audience is the asset.** A B2B cybersecurity podcast with 2,000 targeted listeners can out-perform a comedy show with 20,000. "Audience quality, trust, and relevance now outweigh sheer size."
- **Pricing models:** CPM, CPA, market valuation (CPM/CPA more common for social/influencer). Host-read **$18–$30 CPM**, DAI **$12–$18 CPM**, YouTube **$10–$50/1,000 views**, Twitch **$200/social post**. 60-sec ad read = low value (fast-forward buttons are 15/30s).
- **Two docs:** business case (before, no price) vs. proposal (after, with price). **TL;DR 6 steps:** audit listener data → define audience-aligned sponsors (lookalikes + competitors) → discovery call (goals/outcomes/budget) → choose ad model → value via CPM + activation → deliver and measure (listener lift, conversions, KPIs).
- **Higher-value assets beyond ads:** interview the sponsor as a guest, promotional social posts, product placement (must feel natural), show naming rights, VIP-experience contest prizes, contests/giveaways, branded apps, sponsored segments, branded episodes.
- **Ad models:** live-read (pre/mid/post-roll), DAI (swappable), branded episode, sponsored segment. Modern stack: programmatic (Spotify Audience Network, Acast), branded mini-series, podcast+newsletter bundles, ad-free membership tiers, short-form video repurposing (TikTok/Shorts) with sponsor tags.
- **Marketplaces:** Gumball, Acast Marketplace, Spotify Audience Network, Podcorn, AdvertiseCast, Megaphone, Substack. **Hosting:** Transistor, Buzzsprout (+ Affiliate Marketplace). **Directories:** Spotify, Apple, Google Podcasts (top 3, slow approval); TuneIn, Pandora, iHeartRadio, Podchaser, Stitcher.
- **Podcast growth (10 ways):** valuable (not self-promo) social posts; link in email signature; directories; advertising (with a budget); transcribe + repurpose (Rev.com, Castos, Happy Scribe, Sonix, GoTranscript) into blog/video + lead magnet; ~1-min trailer at top of feed; tag guests; invite high-caliber guests; incorporate feedback; 15 min/day on DMs/comments. SEO: "page 2 = might as well not exist." 7 marketing channels: advertising, social, content marketing, email (lead magnet = transcripts/eBook), contests, website (3-click rule, mobile), SEO. Get one sponsor first, then scale.
- **Influencer tiers:** micro 10K–50K, macro 150K+, mega 500K+. **Media kit** = proposal + bio + social stats + past collabs + testimonials. Watch cancel-culture risk from mismatched sponsors; verify platform ToS / three-strike rules; get a lawyer.
- **Twitch:** define niche (e.g., fighting games), build engaged (not bot) audience. Activations: contests/giveaways (sponsor product, easy entry), **logo overlays (one of the few places logos work)**, sales banners, product placement + discount codes, affiliate links, channel panels, unboxings, sponsored gameplay. 140M+ MAU; survey for ≥25 data points/segment beyond Twitch Analytics.
- **YouTube/influencers:** mostly intangible assets (X minutes of a video, cross-posts). 26 of 32 NFL stadiums branded (naming-rights reference). **Podcast agreement must include:** effective/start/end dates; full names + addresses; cash amount; services + how the sponsor can use them; delivery dates; right of first refusal; refund/dispute instructions; non-compete; applicable law; exclusivity; payment schedule (lump vs installments, fees/taxes); IP ownership; termination terms + penalties + notice. Always a lawyer; never verbal.

### Apps
- No existing audience? Use your *target* audience (the hypothetical designed-for buyer) to seed concentric-circle prospecting. Asset menu can be beautifully designed (you're a developer/designer). Activation example: a sponsor scavenger hunt that captures attendee contact info for the sponsor's lead-gen.

### Small / Local Business
- Small audience is fine — niching makes any audience look valuable; sponsors "eat up segmented data like candy." Local sponsors have shallower pockets → plan for **multiple** sponsors (2–5); start early (each needs its own assets/proposal/meetings). Use local relationships as the warm "in"; keep the first meeting in an office, not a casual lunch.
- Grow a small business via sponsorship: team up with a media sponsor (trust + reach; expect exclusivity), leverage social media creatively (Twitter threads, IG polls, FB stories — not just logos), partner with a nonprofit (CSR credibility), partner only with value-aligned sponsors.

### In-Kind / Contra
- Examples: free TV/radio/digital/print ads, free travel/accommodation (staff, speakers, VIPs, or prizes), celebrities/keynote speakers, printing, electronics/tech, food/drink, branded swag.
- **You can charge a paying sponsor for the "benefit" of giving you an in-kind gift / volunteers** — don't give a "wine sponsor" slot for free. Use contra to **raise the value of cash sponsors** (free media mentioning a title sponsor → add to that sponsor's assets and value it). Reframe giving away product as **sampling** (charge for it). To upgrade in-kind → cash: **buy the product yourself** and fold the cost into the valuation. Vet in-kind sponsors (don't offend paying sponsors / align with controversial brands / accept items that cost you to receive/distribute). Best in-kind brings: sellable assets (cool tech, free airtime), attendee-impressing services (event app, media wall), prizes, VIP-package fillers (hotel rooms, alcohol, spa), staff-life items.

### Media Sponsors
- **Secure media partners BEFORE cash sponsors (recurring)** — raises impressions on title/presenting sponsorships, dramatically increasing value (doing it backwards makes the program underperform).
- **Process:** clarify audience → survey/build niches → review the last 12 months of organic media for trends/outlets/top stories → shortlist most-frequent outlets → match outlets to each segment → plan a media budget → build an audience-only package → pitch the **top 3 outlets** → use results to re-value all opportunities.
- **Organic (free)** vs. **Paid:** organic has no control over placement/timing/narrative, can't be valued/packaged, only measurable after the fact, vanishes if a bigger story breaks; paid lets you control story/timing/location, charge inside packages, bundle website + podcast + YouTube + newsletter + social clips.
- Media partners demand **exclusivity across all their channels** — best practice: pick one partner owning all desired channels. Bring a budget (even small) for leverage; ask **3:1–4:1 ROI**; pitch the sales team first ("I have a budget, want to negotiate a media sponsorship"). Modern partners want **content integrations** (interviews, segments, co-created stories, livestreams), not logos; formats: pre-rolls, mid-rolls, segment sponsorships, livestream takeovers. Modern discovery Qs: video view-through rates, listener retention, newsletter open/click rates. Tools: Google Alerts, Mention, social-listening, Google Analytics. Benefits: ↑impressions/reach (younger digital-native audiences), ↑brand value ("proof of legitimacy" attracts more sponsors), ↑ticket sales, beefier fulfillment report.

### Food Trucks / Food-Truck Festivals
- Distinguish a food-truck **business** seeking growth capital (that's an investor/bank) from a food-truck **festival/rally** seeking marketing sponsors (this is sponsorship).
- **4 sponsor types:** cash (expansion/staffing/events), contra/in-kind (ingredients/packaging/uniforms/merch), media partners, promotional.
- Prospecting: audience surveys (collect emails via cards/QR/Wi-Fi/loyalty), competitor inspiration, local research (coldest), social listening (TikTok/IG geotags, foodie FB groups, Reddit, neighborhood newsletters). Audience data points beyond "food" → unlocks auto, insurance, travel, banks.
- **Activation ideas:** print sponsor name/logo on food (cupcake chocolate, donut, burger bun), contests/giveaways with sponsor products, sponsor coupons (gate behind email signup), name a menu item after the sponsor, branded eco-friendly drinkware/plates/napkins, doorbusters, "Fast Lane Payment" (tap-to-pay sponsor), QR-ordering (tech partner), eco packaging (sustainability brand), photo backdrops, digital scavenger hunts, SMS sign-ups, free samples.
- **Metrics sponsors expect:** foot traffic, orders/hour pre-vs-post activation, QR/coupon scan rates, email/SMS signups, social reach + UGC, clicks to sponsor landing pages. Fulfillment report within **7 days**; get a signed contract for next year (payment can come later).

### Golf Tournaments
- A golf fundraiser nets ≥$5,000 (below = "not worthwhile"), up to $100,000+. **Revenue tactics:** (1) multiple sponsors per hole (separate tee-off + green sponsors — be transparent, avoid conflicting companies); (2) in-kind ≠ sponsorship (pay $500 for wine, find a $3,000 wine sponsor); (3) negotiate the right to add prizes to the prize table as activation (iPads, trips); (4) sponsor-only play (no foursomes sold directly; sponsors theme a hole + enter teams); (5) sell a VIP pass at registration (covers all on-course games + first raffle round); (6) strategic logo placement — beware a "name-tag sponsor" out-shining the title sponsor (people literally mistook the name-tag sponsor for the title sponsor). Typical assets: title, hole/tee/green, golf cart, food/drink/wine/water, branding on tickets/tables/invites/prizes, speaking ops, custom emails, signage.

### Contracts / Agreements (cross-industry "5 things" + more)
- Two parties: **sponsor** + **rights holder/property.** Always customize per sponsor; **consult a lawyer** (posts are "information only"). Include: agreement date; names + addresses; start/end dates; right of renewal/first refusal; fee + what rights it buys; how the property promotes the sponsor (logo placement, # emails/social posts, materials); logo-usage/trademark rules; how the sponsor may promote + use the property's logo; products/services + quantities; key delivery/approval dates; statement that parties are **independent contractors**; dispute + refund handling; **noncompete clause**; governing law; an **"entire agreement"** clause (oral reps excluded); amend in writing.
- **The 5 emphasized clauses:** **Exclusivity** (define competitors precisely; commands a premium), **Sponsorship Payments** (installments vs. lump sum, dates, taxes), **Sponsor Benefits** (match the purchased package exactly), **Termination** (notice required, cost, refunds — often a % up to full refund if the event cancels/under-delivers), **Intellectual Property** (usage rights both ways, retain ownership, define limits).

### Cross-Industry: Conscious Consumerism & Recession-Proofing
- **Conscious consumerism:** affordable price points; go green (sustainable venues, reusable/biodegradable materials, eco-aligned sponsors); **scale DOWN** (excess no longer impresses; flashy spend contradicts green messaging); transparent supply chain; connect with influencers; give back genuinely (Canadian distillery: 1 tree/purchase, ~6,000 planted). Gen Z drives this; lavish spending can trigger boycotts/negative press.
- **Recession-proofing (9 moves):** sharpen audience data (25 pts/segment) → activations "worth paying for" (third-party gut-check) → know your value (don't inflate; table low-value assets) → prove ROI (project X attendees × Y% signup from history; use case studies even without sponsorship history) → pivot fast (≥2 weeks dead cold-calling with no pipeline = move on) → ask for sponsor feedback weekly → prospect aggressively (deeper than 30–60) → produce fulfillment reports (retention > acquisition) → offer bonuses/discounts for multiyear deals ("reliable money worth its weight in gold").

## Appendix: Cross-Cutting "Always / Never" Quick Reference
- **ALWAYS:** audience-first; warm intro; request a meeting (not a sale); discovery before proposal; bring nothing to the first meeting; BAMFAM; customize; valuate honestly; conservative promises (under-promise, over-deliver ≥10%); fulfillment report fast; pursue renewal + referral; use a lawyer for contracts; track volume not revenue.
- **NEVER:** proposal-first / unsolicited; gold-silver-bronze tiers (house line; 2 outlier guest posts disagree); discovery-as-sales-pitch; lead with logos; talk money too soon; copy competitors' pricing; overpromise; mass-eblast packages; rely on verbal agreements; hire for a borrowed Rolodex; chase mega-brands for size alone; build a strategy on online forms; sell from your desk.
