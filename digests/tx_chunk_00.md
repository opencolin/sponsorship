## 1. Frameworks & Processes

### The Sales Funnel / Pipeline (Moves Management) — "How to Build a Sponsorship Sales Funnel"
Don't track revenue as your primary metric; track **activity volume at each stage**. "When done properly revenue comes in almost on its own." Build the pipeline backwards from a revenue goal. Stages (list in Excel):
1. Cold prospects
2. Cold outreach (cold call / cold email)
3. Discovery meeting
4. Invited to submit a proposal/package
5. Yes/No (closed)

**Worked example (numbers chosen for easy math):**
- Revenue goal: $350,000
- Average sponsorship: $10,000 → need **35 sponsors**
- Timeframe: 12 weeks
- Assumed conversion rate at each stage: **50%** (one in two)
- Therefore: 35 closed ← 70 proposals submitted ← 140 discovery meetings ← 280 cold outreaches
- **An average week = 6 proposals submitted (~1+/day), 12 discovery sessions (~2/day), 24 cold calls (~5/day).**
- "The secret to good prospecting is volume, knowing your numbers, and every single day sitting down in front of your computer and moving through your moves management cycle."
- If you don't know your real conversion rates, guess, start now, and learn your actual rates over time.

### The Sponsorship Blueprint (overall journey) — "My Opportunity is Different"
1. **Define your offering** — audience, assets, build activations.
2. **Stand out** — know your value, use bundling strategies (ditch gold/silver/bronze), build sales material sponsors actually want to read.
3. **Prospect** based on your audience; build real connections; prove ROI.
4. **Launch partnerships and renew them.**

### Five Steps to Selling Sponsorship (full webinar framework)
1. **Change your mindset** (the hardest and arguably only step). Sponsorship ≠ philanthropy. Sponsorship is a *verb* (an activity companies do to hit goals). "There's no such thing as sponsors" — there are companies using sponsorship as one tool among sales/marketing/advertising.
2. **Know your value** (valuation). Don't make up numbers or price off your costs.
3. **Always provide a fulfillment report** — regardless of sponsor size ($500 or $1M).
4. **Stop using the sponsorship package to make the sale.** Do your own market research; reach out targeted.
5. **Know your audience** — "No audience, no sponsorship. The end." 50% of a proposal must be audience data; 30+ data points (his older standard; 25+ in newer videos).

### The Core Sponsorship Model (recurring across videos)
Audience → Value/Valuation → Activations → Delivery → Fulfillment report/ROI. Stated as: "How can I get 25+ data points on my audience? How can I give sponsors access to that audience? What is my value? How do we activate / create cool experiences? How do we deliver and prove it through fulfillment reports?"

### The Full Sponsorship Sales Process (sequence, recurs in many videos)
Audience research/data → Prospect research (suspecting) → Outreach (email to warm up → phone call) → Discovery meeting(s) → Custom proposal (or straight to contract) → Negotiation → Contract/invoice → Activation/fulfillment → Fulfillment report + wrap-up meeting → Renewal/upgrade.

## 2. Tactics & Techniques

### Prospecting / Outreach
- **Prospecting = two phases:** (1) research/"suspecting" — identify categories & businesses that fit your audience, then find the right contacts inside them; (2) outreach to find who has interest. (Kevin's framing.) Chris calls phase 1 "suspecting more than prospecting."
- **Who to target inside a company:** people in marketing, sales, product, brand, experiential marketing, or business owners. NOT a generic "sponsorship" inbox.
- **There is no magic database of "decision makers."** No one knows every sponsor; no list of direct phone numbers to "the sponsor." You have 100% accountability for finding and connecting with them via old-fashioned prospect research.
- **Email's only job is to warm up the call** — NOT to sell. "If you're sending things by email expecting someone to make a purchase, you are not prospecting, you are spamming."
- **The 7×7 method** — a structured cadence (7 touches) to warm a lead before / around the call so the call is "lukewarm" not cold. (Templates provided inside the Sponsorship Accelerator.)
- **First email: 3–4 sentences.** (1) Who I am / my property; (2) I see you serve niche X, we have audience Y (shared audience); (3) Can we connect? Keep it short, copy/paste a template, tweak per recipient. Short emails still don't guarantee replies — that's fine.
- **Local vs. national:** prospect them identically; give everyone the same effort. Your *audience data* decides whether local or national brands fit, not your guess. Common advice: start local, prove value, then climb the brand's corporate ladder to regional/national.
- **"Always believe the prospect"** (mentor "Travis"): "Not right now" means not right now (not no). "No budget" means no budget (not no). Don't go away until they say "please go away."
- **P&P / "Pleasant but Persistent"** (Candace's "courting"): a "no" this year ≠ no next year. She courted one org 8–10 years before landing them as a stage sponsor. Each cycle, go back to the drawing board: "What I proposed didn't work — what else are you looking for?"
- **When a sponsor says no, immediately call their competitor** (Kevin). Use it as motivation; he grew a competitor from local → regional → corporate to 10× the lost sponsor's spend; when the original sponsor wanted back in, the answer was "No."

### Discovery
- **Just send a proposal? Don't.** Go straight into discovery the moment they answer the phone, so you avoid the scheduling back-and-forth (and avoid them asking for your pitch deck = "a fancy way of saying I am definitely going to cancel this call").
- Ask what problems they're having (selling product, connecting with target market, building brand). Let them talk. "Listen to discover, not listen to respond."
- **Co-create activations** with the sponsor in the discovery meeting — "the burden is not on you to come up with all the great creative activation ideas… co-create them together." Your sponsor knows better than you how they get customers.
- **Ask budget directly** — "What's your budget?" (not "Do you have a budget?"). Then go silent (the "game of chicken / break first"). Drill: "What's your budget to solve this / to achieve these sales goals?" If they won't answer after ~8 attempts, that's feedback your discovery was weak.
- Notes: Chris brings nothing to meetings (no phone/iPad/notepad/proposal); keeps notes in his head and summarizes back. If you must take notes, ask permission: "Do you mind if I take notes? I just don't want to forget anything." A notepad is acceptable; sales material is not.

### Proposals
- A proposal is a **sales aid, not a sales tool.** "The sponsorship proposal doesn't make the sale, you do" (the channel tagline, ends nearly every video).
- **Send the proposal only AFTER at least one discovery call** — ideally deliver a *draft* proposal live in a second meeting and shape it together.
- **No pricing in a proposal/pitch deck** sent before meetings. Pricing goes in only after you've met (at least twice to negotiate). **No grids, no gold/silver/bronze, no tiers.**
- **No "cutout"/order form** for a check or credit-card info. The CTA is "get in touch," not "send money."
- Contact page: **one real person's email + one phone number that you will answer** — never info@, hello@, or sponsorship@.
- **If a sponsor refuses to meet until they see something, send a "business case"** (not a priced proposal): audience data, activation ideas, media footprint, case studies — no pricing/values — just enough to decide whether to meet.
- You can skip the proposal entirely: Chris has sold "tens of millions" with no proposal — discovery → agree on outcome/activations → send a **sponsorship agreement (contract)** which triggers an invoice. "Proposal and contract are not the same thing. You should always have a contract."

### Closing / Negotiation
- **BAMFAM — Book A Meeting From A Meeting.** Never leave a sales meeting (or end a call/Zoom) without the next step on the calendar. "Oh you want me to send a proposal? Absolutely — how does Wednesday at two o'clock look?" If they resist booking the follow-up, you have "happy ears" (hidden objection).
- **Actively try to hear "no."** Reinterpret everything other than "where do I put my credit card" as a no. A firm "no thank you" is worth as much as a firm yes; everything in between wastes your time.
- **Actively invite objections** in the meeting: "Why might this not work? What concerns do you have? What will your decision-maker say? If in 6 months we work together and it didn't work out, what would have gone wrong?" Objections happen either in the meeting (where you handle them) or after (where they look like ghosting).
- **Tiers/options are acceptable ONLY after discovery**, presented live as "door #1 / door #2 / door #3" anchored to the problem they told you to solve (e.g., #1 solves it at their budget; #2 = "twice as much for only 60% more"; #3 = "sign for 3 years, get 50% off").

### Activation, Fulfillment & Renewal
- **Track EVERYTHING you promised:** email open rate, click-through, downloads, coupon use, social engagement, attendance, web visitors and which pages. So you can produce a fulfillment report.
- **Fulfillment report contents:** a photo of every single thing you promised (logos, program ads, samples lined up on a table, people/crowd), screenshots of web traffic + logo placements + earned/purchased social media, plus event/program stats. Best practice: a **two-column chart — "everything we promised + its value" vs. "everything we delivered + its actual value"** (values come from your valuation) to prove ROI/over-delivery.
- **Cadence:** deliver the fulfillment report **within ~2 weeks of the event**, call a meeting, ask for feedback, then **renew/upgrade on the spot** (multi-year if possible). For title sponsorships, send fulfillment reports at least yearly — Chris prefers **quarterly** (or every 6 months); never wait until the end of a multi-year deal.
- **Renewal is the cheapest revenue.** "Shiny object syndrome is lethal." Talk to existing/past sponsors, upgrade and renew them, sign multi-year. After fulfillment-report meetings you can open a fiscal year with 60–80% of revenue already booked.

## 3. Scripts, Templates & Exact Language

### Objection-handling scripts (from "Common Sponsorship Sales Objections" + others)

**First principle:** Distinguish a *complaint* from an *objection.* "That's expensive," "that's a lot of money," "I'm too busy" are **complaints**, not objections. "In sales we don't respond to complaints, we respond to objections." Convert complaint → objection by asking: *"What do you mean by that?"*

**"Just send me a proposal":**
> "I actually don't have a proposal — we don't have a stock package we just give to people. Frankly I'm not even sure what I'd propose yet because we haven't had a chance to talk. So why don't we jump on a quick call, you tell me what you're trying to achieve, and I'll create a proposal based on that."

If pushed: *"Absolutely — what would you like me to include in that proposal? Tell me what you typically look for when you open a sponsorship package."* (People who won't give 20 minutes to discuss the proposal won't read a 30-page proposal from a stranger.) "Buyers are liars / prospects are liars — 'just send me a proposal' means 'no thanks.'"

**"Budget is already spent"** (the "Cole Gordon response" — "No problem"):
> "No problem, I totally get that. Let me just ask you a question — when do you typically assign your budget? … When does your budget cycle renew?"
If they don't know → you're not talking to a decision maker. Then ("the F word — *fair*"):
> "Cash flow is finance's problem. If we want to work together we'll just invoice you whenever you need — even after the event/campaign. This way you still get all the benefits but we don't have to worry about budget cycles. Sound fair?" → "How can we make this happen knowing your needs and budget cycle are out of sync?" Get a small deposit: *"What do you think is fair — 10%? We'll do 10% up front."*

**"We don't do sponsorship":**
> "Yeah, neither do we. The gold-silver-bronze logo-soup thing? What a waste of everyone's time. What we do is help brands connect to their audience in meaningful ways — some people call that sponsorship, I don't care what it's called. So let's drop the word 'sponsorship' — tell me what your marketing/sales goals are." (A logo on a sign "is barely advertising… the worst advertising.")

**"We just want a booth / signage / samples":**
> "Can I ask an honest question — why do you want a booth? What are you hoping to accomplish? How will you measure success? … I frankly don't think a booth is the best way to do that. What if we set up a situation where you're talking to people *with your product in their hands*, getting them to sample it while you're there, by giving them an incentive like winning something cool — do you think that'd get more samples consumed?" ("I have yet to meet a business that sees value in a booth for a booth's sake.")

**"We prefer in-kind only / free product":**
> "Oh, why?" *(then stop talking).* "It sounds like what you're hoping to do is get people to try your product — why is that valuable to you? … In my experience the best way is for *both* of us to put effort into getting product into the right hands, and allocating staff time has hard costs. So what's a reasonable budget to make sure we get the outcome you're after?" (Reframe: in-kind = **sampling**, and "sampling is expensive — you pay me per sample." When a sponsor wants to give out product, "I think jackpot, I just made tens of thousands in cash.")

**"Let me think about it":**
> "Absolutely — but what *specifically* is it that you want to think about? Let's discuss it right now. … Between now and 24 hours from now, what will happen that tells you you've thought about it enough? Score 1 to 10 — 1 being 'I wish I hadn't picked up the phone,' 10 being 'I want to buy a thousand of these' — where are you? … 8? Great, what would it take to get to a 10? The truth is, all the info about our opportunity comes from me right now on this call — I don't have a package to send you — so let's just have the conversation now." ("You cannot overcome an objection you believe — stop believing it.")

**"Marketing isn't part of our local operations / it's all done at HQ":**
> "Do you mean you have a zero-dollar marketing budget, or that you go through an internal approval process from another office? … What's the process to get them involved? What can I give you to advocate internally? When can we jump on a call with them? Which departments have a budget we can tap into — sales, marketing, HR? We can get creative."

**"I don't have time for a discovery call, just email me":**
> "No problem — I'm happy to jump on a call while you multitask, like on your way to meetings. In my experience a 5-minute discovery call is the best way to save time so I don't write a 100-page proposal you now have to read. I'm also happy to meet someone else on your team — just tell me who, so we put together something that serves your needs. What's a better time?"

### Prospecting call open (from the cold-call webinar)
> "Hey Kevin, my name's Chris. We're connected on LinkedIn — you mentioned you're open to a quick call. We share a common audience. I run a [property] and work with audience A/B/C — recently did a survey, and they love to buy your stuff. Would you be open to a quick discussion about ways we can help you sell more of that?" → If yes, **schedule a discovery call and get off the phone** to do your homework.

### Phone-call follow-up (after 7×7 emails)
> "Yep, that's me — I sent you three emails, the 7×7 method says I have to phone now." → Prospect: "Oh yeah, I've been meaning to get back to you, thanks for following up."

### When they ask for your "levels" on the phone (Candace, live)
> "Hey look, we don't do levels — but I'd love to chat if you have time today to talk about the different opportunities and how we can customize based on you." (Then she had the call and sent a proposal *after* it.)

### "Stop getting ghosted" two-part tactic (from "Why Sponsors Ghost You")
1. **Ask three more questions for every statement.** To "just send me a proposal": *"Amazing — what would you like to see in the proposal? What budget range should I be thinking about? Who else do we need to talk to before I put something together?"*
2. **BAMFAM** at the end of every meeting.
> "If they resist [booking, shaping the proposal, or connecting you to decision makers], you have happy ears." "Everything other than 'where do I put my credit card' is a no."

### The "is it fair?" close ("F word"): "It's really hard for people to say something is not fair… unless they are being unfair." If your terms truly are fair, "fair enough" gets a yes; if they're lying, the real objection surfaces.

### Auditioning the prospect (Dermot, gym owner)
> "Let me stop you right there — I'm auditioning *you*, because I care about my community. Yes we offer a service, but we're not beggars." (Mindset reframe; "service sales is a service.")

## 4. Numbers & Benchmarks

- **Audience data standard:** **25+ data points** on your macro audience (older webinar says **30+**) AND **3 distinct avatars/buyer personas/niches**, each with its own ~25 data points. In a 10-page proposal, **at least 5 pages (50%) must be audience data.**
- **Funnel math example:** $350K goal ÷ $10K avg = 35 sponsors; 50% conversion per stage → 70 proposals / 140 discovery meetings / 280 cold outreaches over 12 weeks → 6 proposals, 12 discovery, 24 calls per week.
- **Proposal close rate target:** "If your sponsorship proposals are not getting at least a **60% close rate**, you're doing something wrong — usually you're not customizing." (Stated as "at least 60%" / "a 60% close rate.")
- **Time to land a sponsor:** Good = **60 days** (only OK; for ~$10K–$75K, established property, leaves money on the table). Better = **4–6 months** (for ~$100K–$200K, presenting-level). Best = **12 months** (full year; allows negotiation, ignores budget-cycle pressure, full-price). Worst = **≤30 days / 2–5 weeks** (starting from scratch = aggressive discounting, high stress). Effort scales non-linearly: ~1,000 hrs over 12 months for ~$500K becomes ~1,500 hrs if compressed to 6 months. "60 days before you need it, sponsorship is your full-time job times two."
- **Sponsorship type vs. timeline:** exhibit space / sampling / delegate-bag inserts → sellable up to day-of. **5-figure ($10K+) incl. presenting rights → 4–6 months.** **Naming/title rights / multi-year → 12–18 months** (then legal/compliance, plus multi-year measurement).
- **Payment terms reality:** all big-company contracts go to legal; expect **net 30** if lucky, up to **net 120**.
- **Title/naming rights term:** **3–5 years minimum**, **10+ years better** (one client even renamed the street their building was on → 10+ years).
- **Effort split:** "You'll put in 10× to 100× more work *after* you get the cash" (activation, delivery, ROI proof, fulfillment) than to win it.
- **"$50K+ total" threshold:** if seeking over **$50,000** total for a festival, you *must* do a formal valuation.
- **Industry size:** Worldwide sponsorship ≈ **$60 billion/yr** (also cited "$60B with a B"); North America ≈ **$20B/yr, growing ~4%** (Sponsorship Landscape Study / ESP Properties); Canada ≈ **~$2B/yr, growing ~13%**.
- **Effort myth:** "90% effort does NOT equal 90% outcome." Put in 70–90% of the effort needed for $1M and you get **$0**, not $900K.
- **Survey mechanics (Chris's exact method):** **Pro SurveyMonkey** account ≈ **$350/yr**; send **2 e-blasts** (e.g., Tue 10am + Thu 10am); incentive = chance to win **one of three $25 gift cards** → yields **30+ data points at 95% confidence / 5% margin of error.** Across millions surveyed in Canada/US, "if we survey 50,000 people we might get **2 complaints**."
- **Client survey results (webinar):** Candace's festival got **2,400 survey responses** (expected 300). Mallory got **100+ replies within 30 minutes** of emailing her database.
- **"Record" close times:** Chris closed a sponsor **24 hrs before** his conference — but it was only **~4%** of total sponsorship revenue. Kevin closed one **at registration** (0 seconds before) — but **<0.5%** of total. "Exceptions prove the rule."
- **Renewal leverage:** providing a fulfillment report lets you book **60–80% of next year's revenue** before the fiscal year opens.

## 5. Named Concepts & Terminology

- **BAMFAM** — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting." Never leave a meeting/call without the next meeting on the calendar; otherwise you chase, look desperate, and the power shifts to them. (Adopted, not coined, by the Collective.)
- **Property** — any sponsorship opportunity/rights-holder: festival, podcast, athlete, sports team, conference, building, esport, nonprofit, etc. "Property here just refers to any sponsorship opportunity — not a physical property."
- **Asset** — an individual sellable unit (logo placement, e-blast, speaking slot, sampling rights, tickets, social posts, naming, VIP/hospitality).
- **Activation** — an experiential marketing opportunity / "cool, interesting, unique, effective ways to engage your audience" that produces a measurable sponsor outcome (contest, sampling, social campaign, email collection, test-drive experience). "Activations are what you sell to sponsors, not logos."
- **Fulfillment report** — "the most valuable tool every sponsorship property has." Photo/screenshot proof of everything promised + delivered, with values, proving ROI. Drives renewal.
- **Avatars / buyer personas / niches** — distinct audience segments (minimum 3) within your macro audience, each profiled with ~25 data points.
- **Valuation** — determining the *market rate* of your assets by asking "How would my sponsor get this exposure/benefit/asset *without me*?" (e.g., equivalent small ad, social-ad rates the platforms reveal). Protects you in negotiation and underpins ROI proof.
- **Happy ears** — (taught to Chris by "Mike Mark") hearing what you *want* to hear; assuming "just send me a proposal" = "we'll work together" when it means "please leave."
- **Title / naming / presenting rights** — Title/naming = the property is *named after* the sponsor (Company Name Festival) + name-in-wordmark; "presented by" alone = presenting rights, NOT title. Comes with exclusivity, the most assets/activations, highest value, multi-year.
- **Name in wordmark** — actually embedding the sponsor's company name into your property's brand name; "necessary but not sufficient" for title sponsorship.
- **Exclusivity** — category exclusivity (no competing brands at all) vs. direct-competitor-only. Must be explicitly defined and lawyer-vetted (cautionary tale: a "bank" that also meant *insurance*).
- **In-kind / contra sponsorship** — free product/services/media; "really just a fancy word for sampling." Treat as a real sponsorship (activations, fulfillment report) and as the *opening of the sales pipeline* (upgrade to in-kind + cash → mostly cash).
- **Media partner/sponsor** — a media company promotes you (you help them get readers/listeners/viewers). Spikes brand value and all asset values; must be secured *before* cash sponsors to monetize it.
- **Pass-through rights** — your audience connects with one of the media partner's advertisers/partners.
- **Vanity metrics** — shallow numbers ("1,000 people come to our conference," follower counts) that are NOT real audience data.
- **Business case** — a no-price outline (audience, activations, media footprint, case studies) sent when a sponsor demands something before agreeing to meet.
- **Macro audience** — your entire audience (vs. the avatar-level breakdowns).
- **"The prize"** — mindset that *you* hold the power because you own the audience; the brand needs your audience.
- **P&P / Pleasant but Persistent** (Candace) and **"courting"** — long-game relationship persistence over years.
- **7×7 method** — structured 7-touch cadence to warm a lead before the call.
- **The "F word" (fair)** and **"the Cole Gordon response" ("No problem")** — named objection-handling moves.
- **Run rate** — what Chris cares about ("I worry about contracts and booked deals… run rate") over cash flow.

## 6. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

- **Sending a proposal/package before any discovery call** (the #1 self-sabotage). "Sending a proposal too soon" → thrown in the garbage. First email = far too early.
- **Gold/Silver/Bronze (any tiers/levels), renamed or not** — "the mark of the amateur"; a 50-year-old (some say 40-year-old) tactic; "sponsors hate them"; renaming to Platinum/Ruby/Diamond/Adamantium changes nothing — *the existence of levels is the problem.*
- **Making up your value / pricing off your costs.** "Food & beverage is expensive this year" → sponsor: "We don't care, that's your problem." Sponsors hire firms (like the Collective) to value deals — they can tell you guessed. Cautionary case: **Marathon B** (below).
- **E-blasting packages to strangers** ("dialing for dollars," spamming) — "very efficient but not effective." 100% output, 0% outcome = "better off not doing it at all."
- **Vanity metrics as audience data** ("people who like what we do," "1,000 attendees"). "People are more than just one thing" (Coach Mark). Endemic-only thinking (a 5K → only Nike/Brooks/Adidas) ignores that attendees buy cars, watch TV, pay taxes.
- **Overestimating delivery** ("once people hear how great we are, it'll be a success"). "Sponsors are not investors… they want guaranteed ROI" and will go elsewhere.
- **Assuming what sponsors want** (awareness/logos/e-blasts bundled your way) instead of asking.
- **Leaving sponsors in the dark** — no fulfillment report, no comms about timing/needs/registration; ignoring them for 9–10 months then sending a renewal proposal.
- **Taking a partner controversial *to your audience*** (e.g., a soda company at a health-conscious event) — alignment with audience values matters, not whether the brand is "inherently" controversial.
- **Failure to deliver / no tracking system** — making promises with no way to prove fulfillment.
- **Ignoring/disrespecting "small" sponsors** (the $500–$10K givers) — they're the highest-likelihood upgrades up the ladder; "the world is big… small to you may be huge to them" (some 2–3 person firms spend $100K+/mo on marketing).
- **No-response is worse than "no."** A "no" lets you remove them from the pipeline and move on; no-response makes you follow up forever (and risk spam blacklists). "No responses is the kiss of death."
- **Believing "all I need is the right contact"** — getting in front of the right person *at the wrong time* (unprepared) burns the relationship. Decision makers are *hard* to convince (the Sarah case).
- **Believing "my opportunity is different"** — internal focus; a non-sequitur. "Toyota's goals don't change based on your property." Restaurant/menu analogy: "we're not the kind of restaurant that gives food for money — just give us money." Not knowing audience/value/activations/ROI "doesn't mean you're different, it means your skill set needs an upgrade."
- **Saying "we don't need ROI because we're a good cause / a startup with no audience data."** Sponsors don't care about your cause/mission ("the cause is completely irrelevant" — a brand told Chris directly).
- **For charities — looking for "mission alignment"** with a for-profit. "If they shared your mission they'd be a nonprofit (your competitor)." You're looking for a **shared audience**, not mission alignment.
- **Treating sponsorship as "easy money" / philanthropy / major gifts.** "It has as much in common with philanthropy as baseball does with spaghetti." Boards wrongly think companies are cash cows.
- **A sponsorship proposal that's a "case for support."** A proposal is a **marketing document** (audience, activations, case studies, media partnerships), not mission/vision pages. (One real proposal: 23 of 36 pages were company history — "stuff nobody cares about.")
- **Including pricing, grids, a check/credit-card cutout, or "don't see what you like? we'll customize!" line in a pitch deck.** That last line signals you *won't* customize and didn't care about their needs.
- **Putting all activation-idea burden on yourself** instead of co-creating with the sponsor.
- **Assuming "if a brand sponsors a property like mine, they'll sponsor me."** "Sponsors don't sponsor properties, they sponsor audiences."
- **Saving sponsorship for last / assuming it's easy.** At the Collective, "I go get sponsors before I even book a venue… if there are no sponsors there's no event."
- **Charging too little to make a "yes" easy** — makes you look low-value. "More is not better; better is better." Undervaluing = needing more sponsors = more obligations to fulfill.
- **Believing a good meeting that ends in ghosting "went well."** "If the meeting went well, they wouldn't be ghosting you." A real discovery meeting "shouldn't feel like fun — it should feel like work."
- **Sending organic-media-only as a monetizable asset** — no guaranteed placement, can't control the story, advertisers can veto your sponsor's mention. Never sign a media-partner contract without a **guaranteed minimum number of impressions.**
- **Switching to fully-custom for *new* sponsors while leaving legacy sponsors on stock packages** (Kelly's plan) — risks alienating loyal sponsors who'd have loved a custom experience. Bring them along via feedback meetings.

## 7. Mindset & Principles

- **"The sponsorship proposal does not make the sale — you do."** (Signature line; ends ~every video. Variants: "the sponsorship *package* doesn't make the sale.")
- **"Fall in love with the problem you solve, not your solution."** Plus Keith Cunningham: *"Shoes that don't fit are not a bargain at any price."* Rigid packages = jamming a size-7 shoe onto every sponsor's foot.
- **"You are the prize."** "There's only one thing brands will spend money into debt to get — customers. Customer is just a fancy word for audience." Begging flips the power: "If you're begging, you've already lost."
- **Efficient vs. effective** (Drucker: "efficiency is doing things right; effectiveness is doing the right thing"). The industry obsesses over efficiency (pretty decks, big prospect lists, online forms) and isn't effective. Effective work = audience data, activations, discovery, customization/valuation, fulfillment + renewal.
- **Sponsorship is a discipline** "like accounting, like law" with its own rules you must learn. "Respect the process… sponsorship is a full-contact sport — done face to face."
- **Sponsorship is a verb** and **"there's no such thing as sponsors"** — only companies using sponsorship to hit goals (sell products). Ask "What are your marketing/sales goals?" not "what do sponsors want?"
- **Sponsorship is B2B marketing**, not fundraising/donations/investment. "If you want donations, ask a rich person; an investment, ask an angel; don't call it sponsorship."
- **Start before you're ready / "build the plane as you fly."** "The best time to plant a tree was 20 years ago; the second best time is right now." Waiting is "the most expensive choice." (Mallory: waiting would have cost her ~$20K.) James Russell Lowell: "All the beautiful sentiments in the world weigh less than a single lovely action." "Fortune favors the bold."
- **A quick "no" vs. a slow "yes."** You can get a fast no (e-blast a stock package, skip discovery) or a slow yes (discovery, custom, in-person). To bring in dollars, be ready for the slow yes.
- **Anxiety is the bridge.** Warren: champions "reinterpret anxiety as excitement" ("I'm not stressed, I'm amped"). "Before the cold call the answer was already no — you've only shifted a guaranteed no to a maybe yes; the bridge that crosses you is anxiety." Combat-sports framing: "if you're not a little nervous before the walk, something's wrong."
- **Fear is your barometer** (cold-call webinar): don't know what to say when they answer → you lack audience data; can't offer ideas → weak activations; can't prove ROI → do valuation. "None of those should stop you from picking up the phone."
- **"The only thing worse than prospecting not working is when it works"** (Chris) — the scariest moment is "Sure, I've got a few minutes — how can I help you?" Prospecting "is like the gym": getting started is the scariest part; it gets easier with reps, never goes away ("keep your funnel full").
- **"If you can remember the name of the person who told you no, you're not doing enough prospecting."** Rejection should be so common it doesn't sting; it's not personal.
- **Get paid to show up, not to close** (Emanuel's reframe): compute your $/hour from total sales revenue ÷ selling hours; you earn that *whether or not* a given call closes. "Go be of service, there's no pressure." Shift from "selling people" to "serving people."
- **You should be in asking/listening mode, not telling mode** (Car Sales parable — see §8).
- **Customization is an evolution, not binary.** Move legacy sponsors over by asking how you can serve them better; "everyone deserves the right to choose how they engage with you."
- **Systematize the systematizable, protect the human work** (Warren): automate email templates/calendars ("send the template on the day the calendar says — takes 10 minutes"); the irreplaceable part is "getting on the phone and connecting with humans" and tailoring your business case. "If your output is 100% but your outcomes are 0%, you'd be better off not doing it at all."
- **Sponsorship is hard and not a cure-all.** "Sponsors are demanding… if they don't get ROI they'll ask for a refund." Sometimes sponsors create more problems than they solve.

Toronto). Got a **2020 George-Floyd "sponsorship windfall"** of box-checking corporate cash with no ROI ask → "false sense of comfort"; when it dried up they realized they didn't know/value/leverage their audience. After completing a sponsorship-sales training program: built wireframes, used valuation calculators, **landed 2 new contracts across 2 of 3 properties**, and made the **"we are the prize" mindset shift** ("transformative this year"). Runs experiential activations (NOT logos): **"kicks & sips"** (new shoe line paired with cocktails), **"cars & coffees,"** food-truck festivals / block parties with **NASCAR** (Bubba Wallace; making the sport approachable). Signature event: a **grand tasting at a major NBA arena — 750 attendees, 25 caterers**, team mascot/cheerleaders; the winning caterer won a **stadium contract → over $1M in sales over 3 years** (now its own property/wireframe). Two B2B conversations: brands targeting restaurateurs (food processors, liquor, POS) vs. brands targeting diners. Tactics he models: discovery by "letting them talk," asking budget then going silent ("game of chicken"), test-drive activation math ("how much is 300 test drives worth? $30K → I'll get you 300"). Feed the Soul gives **$10K grants**. URL: blackrestaurantweeks.com.
- **Brendan / Elite Feats** — represents running events across the US; each race might generate $5–10K alone, but **wrapped in a campaign with a media-buy strategy** it's "1+1=10"; "scalable from day one," replicable on a national footprint.

Result: **$0 raised over 2 years** — no time to prospect daily, follow up, value, run moves management. "If you're too busy to do sponsorship properly, you're too busy."
- **Mallory** — full-time job + family, passion project, "no time," refused to do a survey ("no one will reply"). - **Jamie (Stop Guessing)** — emailed a package; prospect replied "I'll take Bronze at half price." Instead of discounting by email, Jamie called, ran discovery ("How do you get people to try your product? - **Sarah (All I Need is the Right Contact)** — real-estate sales background; used the Collective's templates to land a meeting with a **Fortune 500 CMO**; her CEO insisted on attending. CMO refused the pitch and asked: tell me about your audience (Sarah: "people who come to our conference") → "really tell us — decision-making authority, past/coming purchases, not vanity metrics" (Sarah froze); activation ideas? - **Candace Goar (webinar panelist)** — Sponsorship & Development Director, Festival International de Louisiane (Lafayette; largest international music & arts festival in the US; **non-ticketed/free**, **300K+ over 5 days**, ~39 yrs, her 10th). After the Collective, overhauled 37 years of protocols; ran a **post-event survey (Mon/Tue after) → 2,400 responses** (expected 300), validating demographics and giving richer data to pivot pitches. **Courted one org 8–10 years → finally landed them as a stage sponsor** ("Pleasant but Persistent"). Uses the 7×7 method, 3–4-sentence emails, meticulous notes on who replied. A long-ghosting sponsor finally replied; she asked "why now?" → "Our marketing strategies have changed."
- **Marathon A vs. Marathon B (both charity events, same sponsor "Dave," same shoe brand giving 1,000 free shoes)** — Marathon A did a valuation, sold Dave **the *right* to give away the shoes for $50K/yr over 5 years = $250K** *plus* the free shoes. Marathon B felt it'd be "overstepping" to ask for money on top of Dave's "kindness" → got only the shoes. Net: Marathon B "raised **negative $250K**" (left a quarter-million on the table that went into Dave's pocket/bonus). "You must, must, must do valuation."
- **Dave & Sally (the $25K ballot-collection deal / fulfillment-report story)** — sponsor sent rep "Sally" to collect ballots (insurance/professional-services quotes) at a ~3,000-person conference. Expected ~10% (≈3,000 ballots); got **3 ballots = ~$8,000 per ballot** vs. a $3K starting package → a loss even at 100% conversion. Sally told her boss the event was "poorly attended"; Chris's **fulfillment report (page 7)** showed Sally texting behind her booth surrounded by thousands. Chris took accountability: "Don't send Sally — send me **two iPads**, I'll auction them for ballots with my team." Next year they **overflowed the ballot box**, and Chris closed a **multi-year deal (≈$125K)** — all proven with a Word-doc + portable-camera fulfillment report. Lesson: without the report, "Dave would lie to save face" at renewal.
- **Chris's "10,000 packages/month" awakening (webinar)** — early career, measured success by packages mailed (~387/week; boss: "make it 400"); 6 months, $0, phone never rang. A "Dave" finally called inviting him to *pitch* (not buy) — and revealed: "We haven't read a sponsorship package in 10 years; we get **10,000 unsolicited packages a month (~500/business day)**; a whole department/online form just says 'no thank you.'" They invited Chris because **market research** showed their target (40-ish high-net-worth suburban hockey moms who prefer Lexus/BMW — a toy company's audience) matched his property. - **The Ski-Hill Truck Test-Drive (Measuring ROI)** — a client set up a test-drive area atop a ski hill; people drove a truck through a frozen winter landscape to see winter handling. All attendees were in-market for a vehicle within 12–18 months. **Measure what's in your control (test drives delivered), not cars sold.**
- **Chris & wife buying a car (Sponsorship & Car Sales parable)** — every time they asked about roof racks / ground clearance / dog space, the salesperson answered "if you buy this car I'll send my kids to college / take a vacation / go to Disney World." They left. Moral: don't sell sponsorship by talking about the good *you'll* do with the money or your mission/CEO bio — be in **asking/listening mode** about the buyer's needs.

## 9. Industry-Specific Nuances

### Festivals & Events
- Proposal structure (Ultimate Festival Proposal): **Title page** = great photo + festival name + dates + location only (high-risk to add history/bios — "the more you add, the more likely they delete it"). Then describe the opportunity (what makes it loved, not your founding year) → **≥50% audience data** (25+ macro points + 3 avatars @25 each) → **activation ideas** (audience first) → vanity/attendance/media footprint → case studies/testimonials (aim for 3 distinct activation types: contest, sampling, email collection, social campaign) → CTA + one-person contact. **No grids, no pricing.** Must do a valuation if seeking **$50K+** total.
- Communication schedule before AND after the event (registration updates, media opportunities, over-delivery); don't go dark 9–10 months then ask for renewal money.
- Respect "small" sponsors — they're your upgrade pipeline.

### Food Festivals (Warren interview)
- Two distinct B2B conversations: brands wanting access to **restaurateurs/vendors** (food processors, liquor, POS) vs. brands wanting **diners/consumers**. Vendors and consumers are *both* monetizable audiences.
- Find "real estate" on-site (posters, table tents) AND on your website (directory by zip/dietary filter) to give sponsors continuous touchpoints; microactivations (samplings/tastings) > static logos.
- Beware windfall money with no ROI ask — it dries up with economic/political/cultural shifts (feast/famine "5–10 years"). Shift to an ROI model and a different suite of sponsors opens up. Think outside endemic brands (NASCAR for a restaurant week).
- Charging $10K "because food & beverage is expensive" gets dismissed — must show ROI.

### Charities / Nonprofits
- **Sponsorship is NOT philanthropy / major gifts / a case for support.** Don't seek "mission alignment" — seek **shared audience**.
- Your **audience is your donors, volunteers, board, and those who care about the cause — NOT the people you serve** (a food bank's audience is its donors/volunteers, not its food recipients).
- Sponsorship can be **totally unrestricted funding** (rare and gold — "a dollar unrestricted is worth $100 of restricted"); worth the difficulty. But it's harder than donations: lots of work *after* the cash (activate, deliver, fulfillment report, multiple meetings).
- Fundraisers (or the NGO revenue specialist) usually own it, but it must obey **marketing** rules, not fundraising rules. "The cause is *irrelevant* to sponsors" (a brand said so to Chris's face). The vast majority of global sponsorship goes to **sport/motorsport**, not charities (though charities can do well).

### Sports / Motorsport
- Receives the bulk of sponsorship dollars. Title/naming deals often require switching *your* products/services (bank, official credit card, drinks served, even corporate-travel airline) and create exclusivity/ambush-marketing risks (Shoe Co. A title sponsor vs. team preferring Shoe Co. B). Always involve a lawyer.

### Podcasts
- Don't just sell low-CPM ad reads ("you'll never make money unless you have ~100M downloads/month"). Build **custom assets/activations**, value by audience + brand value + deliverables.
- Do: discovery first, customize (no gold/silver/bronze), highlight audience data (not just downloads — 25+ points + 3 avatars), act like a professional regardless of how laid-back the brand is, get everything in writing + a post-close confirmation meeting. Don't: send a rate card/proposal/pricing first; be "too salesy" (it's a long negotiation, not a hard sell).

### Title / Naming Rights (advanced)
- Must include: name-in-wordmark; multi-year (3–5+ yrs, 10+ better); **exclusivity** (define category vs. direct-competitor; lawyer-vet); **regular fulfillment reports** (quarterly/semi-annual, never end-only); custom activations run through valuation; plus commonly VIP/hospitality, digital marketing (content/blogs/contests, not just logos on posts), tickets/free passes, giveaways/contests, sampling rights, employee engagement. Not a one-call close — multiple meetings, both lawyers, 12–18 month process.

### Media Partnerships
- Two models: **organic** ("no money") — good for fulfillment-report optics & partner goodwill but unguaranteed, can't control story, advertisers can veto, **can't be monetized**; **cash-leveraged** — you *pay* the media partner (e.g., bring $5K, get $30K of value) to get a **contract with guaranteed (prime-time) minimum impressions** you can control and resell to cash sponsors. **Media partnership must precede cash sponsorship** to lift your value. Build activations for the media partner (they already own awareness — they want a funnel step / pass-through rights). Hit-list source: review all organic media you earned in the last 12 months — those outlets already believe your story fits their audience. Never sign without a **guaranteed minimum impressions** clause.

### In-Kind / Contra
- Treat as a real sponsorship (knowing audience, discovery, negotiation, activation, fulfillment report) — it's "sampling," and you still recognize the partner (logo placement, promotion, delivery to their target customer). Use it as the **opening of the sales pipeline**: prove value year 1, upgrade to in-kind+cash year 2, move toward mostly cash. Can also leverage in-kind media to drive foot traffic/eyeballs you then monetize via cash sponsors.

### Conferences / Associations / B2B
- Sponsors want B2B-specific audience data: job titles, industries, **decision-making capacity/authority** of attendees/members.
- For a brand-new event with no data: research the **interest audience** (e.g., running magazines/orgs for a race; sponsorship pros for a sponsorship conference) and base the business case on **how you'll guarantee a sellout** (the *theme* determines *who* comes; your job is proving *how many*). De-risk year 1 with a **discount + price-lock** ("half price, never increases if you renew") — Chris's first conference sponsors stayed even after he sold the conference. "Sponsors are not investors" (per "Larry the sponsorship guy") — they need a return, so spend year-1 energy/budget guaranteeing a full house.

### Athletes / Influencers
- Don't pitch on "I'm deserving / following my passion / making a difference" — that's mission-based language that won't move marketers. Same rules: audience, activations, valuation, ROI.
