## 1. Frameworks & Processes

### The Five Stages of Sponsorship Sales (core framework, recurs everywhere)
1. **Inventory building + audience identification + valuation.** Build an inventory of **at least 100 individual assets** per property (logo placement, speaking, sampling, etc. — the things people normally cram into gold/silver/bronze). Identify your audience (25+ data points). Do valuation. "If your sponsorship sales strategies do not start with inventory, valuation and audience data you are set up for failure."
2. **Prospecting.** Once you know inventory, value, and audience, prospecting is simple: ask "what companies want to get in front of this audience?"
3. **The prospecting/discovery meeting (first meeting).** No sponsorship packages allowed. "The only goal for the first meeting is to get the second meeting."
4. **Building the sponsorship package** — tailored to that prospect, only AFTER discovery.
5. **Activation + Fulfillment (sandwiched).** Activation = connect sponsor to target market in meaningful ways the brand and audience both love. Then fulfillment = a report proving you did (and over-delivered on) what you promised. Fulfillment is in the sales stages because **"the fulfillment report is the best prospecting tool out there"** (renewal + referral).

### The correct order (from "Sponsorship Shortcuts" — anti-alchemy sequence)
1. Identify your **properties** (opportunities). "Give me money so I'll have it is not a property."
2. Identify your **audience** — 25+ data points.
3. Segment audience into **three avatars / buyer personas / niches**.
4. Build **assets** — catalog logo placement, eblast, sampling, speaking, experiential/activation ideas (giant list).
5. Get **internal buy-in** on assets/activations BEFORE going to market (so you're not stuck in 6-week internal approval when a sponsor makes an offer and then moves on).
6. **Valuation** — know what your opportunity is worth and justify pricing.
7. **Prospect research** — which companies want to sell to your audience (not which are big enough to have money).
8. **Meeting** with prospects — ask questions; do NOT bring or send a proposal first.
9. Build a **custom package** based on the meeting.
10. **BAMFAM** — book a follow-up meeting, go over the custom package, close.

### Full podcast/general sales pipeline (from "Complete Guide to Podcast Sponsorship," mirrors motorsport/event videos)
1. Build inventory (everywhere you place a logo / read a message — but that's the small part).
2. Add experiential/contests/sampling to widen the sponsor pool.
3. Know your audience: macro audience (25+ data points) → identify **3 unique avatars** via survey data.
4. Build activations (overlap of audience desires and sponsor goals).
5. Know your value = "what would my sponsor pay to get this exact same outcome on the free market" (NOT what others pay for logo placement).
6. Build two documents: **business case** (before meeting, no price) and **proposal** (after meeting, with price).
7. Outreach: short 2-sentence email to get a meeting.
8. Discovery (30–45 min, investigative journalist; diagnose problems).
9. **BAMFAM** before leaving.
10. Sign contract → deliver (over-deliver by ≥10%).
11. **Fulfillment report** → renewal/upsell meeting → BAMFAM again to close next term within a week of wrap.

### The Discovery Meeting flow (from "Sponsorship Sales Meeting Strategy" / Script)
Build rapport (5–7 min) → Audience (their target market — hang here until fully understood) → Their goals (what they want the audience to DO; how they get customers; how they measure success) → Diagnose problem ("diagnose before prescribe"; identify roadblocks) → Brainstorm solutions together → Ask for **next steps** (not the sale). End: "Where do you think we should go from here?" If they say "proposal," respond "Perfect — tell me exactly what I should include." Then BAMFAM.

### The Sponsorship Blueprint / Checklist (diagnostic, from "Launching Your Sponsorship Program")
Don't try to do everything. Diagnose the bottleneck, then fix that stage. The flow (each stage depends on the one before it):
**Audience → Offer (clarify) → Sell → Deliver/Activate → Renew (fulfillment report + sponsor summit).**
Diagnostic logic:
- Losing sponsors every year → focus on **renewal** (fulfillment reports, sponsor summits).
- Can't get meetings / nobody responds → your **audience data is no good** or your **product/offer** is weak (NOT your templates).
- Get meetings but they say no → work on **sales chops/valuation** OR your **offer** (activations).
- Sponsor asks "who's your audience?" and you don't know → start at **audience**.
- Note: "sales material" is NOT a leverage point on the blueprint; it is not where to focus.

### The Five Questions framework (first-meeting, from "How to Ask for Sponsorship")
See Scripts section for full wording. Structure: (1) target audience, (2) pre-purchase action + how they drive it, (3) a sponsorship that went badly + why, (4) sales goals for the year, (5) most important elements / what to avoid in a package — plus a secret closing "fit + permission" question.

### The 37 Discovery Questions framework
Three goals of discovery: **(1) Build trust, (2) Learn (discover needs/goals/wants), (3) Book the second meeting** — NOT submit a proposal. Sequence: Prospecting → Discovery (trust → discover) → follow-up meeting → proposal → negotiation. Start every discovery in the **audience** space, then **business goals**, then **success metrics**. ## 2. Tactics & Techniques

### Prospecting — Concentric Circles theory (from "Sponsorship Prospecting Made Easy")
Start with warmest in the center, work outward:
- **Center (hottest):** Brands your audience said (via survey) they intend to purchase within the next 18 months — yields **20–30+ brands alone**.
- **Ring 2 (advertising):** Brands actively advertising/marketing to your audience. Find them by asking your audience what media they consume, OR build an avatar and ask "who markets to these people?" then consume that media (radio, TV, etc.) and see who's spending to reach them.
- **Ring 3 (possible interest):** Brands you *think* should be interested, based on what you know — no research. "This is the class of brands that most people start and end with, and this is probably why you're struggling."
- **Outer ring (coldest):** Direct competitors of every brand already identified in the inner rings. (Identify 2–3 auto brands → their competitors enter here.)
Result: clients following this never have fewer than 100 prospects; usually have to cap at **300–500**.

### The Nine Paths to a Sponsor (prospecting pathways)
Stop thinking "companies"; companies are full of **people**. Reach these departments/titles, each at C-level / VP / Director / Manager / Coordinator / Admin level:
1. **Marketing** (the primary path — sponsorship is a marketing discipline)
2. **Sales** (director/VP/coordinator/manager)
3. **Sponsorship** (if it's in their title)
4. **Communications**
5. **Brand**
6. **Product development**
7. **HR / employee engagement** (if you have employee-engagement assets)
8. **Customer retention** (if you have VIP/retention assets)
9. **(Nonprofits only — secret 7th/9th path): Community investment / CSR / philanthropy / Foundation.** These folks don't do sponsorship — they do philanthropy — but ask them "who should I talk to about your marketing/sales/sponsorship goals?" for a warm internal intro.
- **DO NOT** email info@/hello@/customerservice@ or call the number on the back of the credit card hoping to reach the CMO. **DO NOT** try to "get around the gatekeeper" — embrace the gatekeeper.
- **DO NOT** go straight to "Hey Nike, give me money." Work each vertical: start where you have a warm intro; if none, run the email cadence; if no answer, move the whole sequence to the next person, then next, then next vertical.

### Outreach cadence
Per person: ~3 emails → phone call → 2 emails → call. No answer → move the *entire* sequence to the next contact, and so on. (From "Nine Paths.")

### Email tactic — keep it 2 sentences
Never send long emails or sales material to get a meeting. (Scripts below.)

### Discovery technique — be an investigative journalist
In discovery you should talk ~20% of the time; **~90% of what you say should end in a question mark.** Ask about target market, marketing spend, ROI/ROAS, how they measure positive outcomes, what they wish they could do more/less of. Diagnose, then prescribe.

### Bring NOTHING to the first meeting (from "How to Run a Sponsorship Sales Meeting")
Bring "nothing" literally — no package, one-pager, pamphlet, leave-behind, business card, or pen and paper. Writing things down the instant a prospect speaks makes them "feel preyed upon" (like a car salesperson noting what you want). After the meeting, step outside and **dictate notes into your phone, email them to yourself.** Memorize 10–12 questions; give yourself permission to depart from them so you don't sound robotic.

### Don't say the word "sponsorship" in the first meeting
Don't replace it with "partnership" either (pointless) — just frame the meeting as wanting to understand their goals and see if there's a chance to collaborate.

### Negotiation — adjust the package, not arbitrarily
"Too expensive?" → ask what they'd prefer to pay, then **remove assets** to lower the cost. They want more of an outcome? → **add assets** and raise the price. "It's your business, it's your rules — negotiate. Valuation is a guide to help you make business decisions."

### Closing draft-proposal technique
Never just "send a proposal." Say: "I'll put together a **draft** for you to review." Then extract what they want in it + budget range, then BAMFAM a meeting to go over the draft together. Resistance to that meeting = they're not really interested / you missed something in discovery.

### Pinning down budget when they say "I don't have one"
Walk the cap down: "If I put a $500K proposal in front of you, could you say yes? No? OK, stop me when I hit the cap — $400K, $300K, $200K, $50K, $25K…" Or reassure: "Don't worry, I'll share our valuation so you know I didn't invent the number — I just need to know what range to aim for." (If truly no budget: "Great — that means we can do some really cool stuff, as long as we can show ROI.")

### Find the other decision-makers without asking "who's the decision maker"
Never ask "who's the decision maker" (people lie / dislike it). Instead: "So I can invite the right people on my end — who else on **your** end should be part of this conversation so we really get it right?"

### Online sponsorship forms — fill out LAST, not first
The form is the **last** step (a rubber-stamp after a yes), never the entry point. Doing forms at scale = "very efficient at an incredibly inefficient process." (Client A vs Client B case below.)

### Fulfillment & renewal
Over-deliver by **≥10%**. Document everything: photos, screenshots (social, web), media clips, attendee testimonials, pictures of the sponsor engaging the audience. Deliver the report **within ~1–2 weeks of the event** (event videos) or **within 7 days** (food truck). In the fulfillment meeting: ask how it went → ask "what do I have to do RIGHT NOW to make this your #1 marketing priority next year?" → get a draft-proposal yes → BAMFAM. Goal: know your renewals (and budget gap) within a week of wrap.

### Get referrals from a "no"
A sponsor who's not a fit is still a path. Ask: "I appreciate the honesty — is there anyone in your network you think we should be speaking to?" (Hidden-value case below.)

## 3. Scripts, Templates & Exact Language

### The 2-sentence outreach email (generic, podcast video)
> "Hey, I see that you work with X company. We just surveyed our audience — they mentioned your product. Do you want to jump on a call and see how we can work together?"

### The cold email (motorsport video — fuller version)
Subject: **"Hey Dave"** (their first name)
> "Hi Dave, I saw on LinkedIn that you're involved in the marketing side of [company name]. We recently surveyed our database and your company had a strong showing. Wondering if you have a few minutes to talk about what we found and whether or not our audience is one of your priorities. Any chance you're free this week for a quick call? Let me know. [Your name]"

Principle: the **first sentence must explicitly state that your audience is interested in their product.** "Asking a company for money because they have money and you'd like some is a path to no sponsorship."

### The Five First-Meeting Questions (verbatim)
1. "Who is your target audience / target customers / target market?" (Start here — it signals you understand the new rules of sponsorship.)
2. "What action do your customers take right before they make a purchase, and how do you typically get them to take that action?" (Walk me through your sales funnel.)
3. "Tell me about a sponsorship agreement that went badly, and why — what made it not produce return on investment?"
4. "What can you tell me about your sales goals for the coming year?"
5. "What would you consider to be the most important elements of a sponsorship package, and why — and what should I avoid?"
Secret close: "This sounds like we have a fit — would you agree? Is this a good time for me to put together a **draft** proposal to submit for your feedback?" (Demand explicit, not implied, consent. If "no," honor it — don't send a package.)

### The Discovery Sales-Meeting Script (verbatim flow, from "Sponsorship Sales Meeting Script")
1. **Build rapport.** "Hey, how are you? I live in Canada, the weather's always terrible — you must live somewhere amazing." (Step 1 is "build rapport," not "ask three questions." Don't over-stay it or get too friendly.)
2. **Set the stage / tie-down.** "I want to be respectful of our time — we only have 30 minutes, so let's dive in. Quick check-in: are you still okay to go for the full 30 minutes?" (Ask this UP FRONT so they can't later use "oh, I have to bounce" to escape an intense moment.)
3. **Frame the meeting.** "The reason for the call is to see whether or not we share common goals or a common audience and whether there's potential to work together in the future. If it's okay with you, I'd love to learn about your organization, what you're trying to achieve, what's working and what isn't, and some of your goals next quarter. Does that work for you?" (Use "Does that sound **fair**?" — "nobody likes to be accused of being unfair.")
4. **Audience first.** "Just to get things going, I'd love to learn more about your target market and your ideal customer. Who's your ideal customer? What do they value? What's important to them? What challenges are your customers facing? What problems do you solve for your customers? What do you want your customers to walk away thinking and doing?"
   - Dig: "Interesting — what do you mean by that? What else? Why do you think that is?" ("The first-level answer you get is almost never the real answer, in sales and marriage.")
5. **Confirm the avatar.** "I have a good sense of who your customer is — am I right? Your ideal customer is the 40-year-old high-net-worth mom who lives in the suburbs, three kids, they play soccer, drives a Lexus — do I have that right?"
6. **Identify challenges.** "What metrics are you responsible for achieving, and what are your biggest problems in achieving those metrics? So you want to sell 50 cars this quarter — how are you doing, are you on track? What's going to happen if you don't get 50 test drives?" (Ask hard questions casually — "if you ask a question like it's normal, people will answer it.")
7. **Summarize + acknowledge (diagnose).** "Do you mind if I check in really quick to make sure I fully understand? You want to sell 50 cars by April 1, your radio ad budget isn't delivering because people aren't commuting/listening anymore, so you can't tell people about your promotions and they're not coming for test drives. Do I have that right?"
8. **Offer a solution (prescribe).** "Perfect — I can definitely help you connect with these people, run a cool contest to get them down to the lot. Does it make sense for me to share a bit about how we work with [their avatar]?" → **then go silent and wait for yes/no.** "When you ask someone's permission, you don't keep talking."
9. **Explain your approach, not a pitch.** "We focus on a sponsor's goal outcomes first, then match it to their target audience. We create an experience that engages the audience in meaningful ways — we don't do stock packages with stuff you don't want. We worked with a sponsor last year who wanted X, we did Y, it worked pretty well. What else can I share with you? Where should we go from here?" → silence until they speak.
10. **Handle "just send me a proposal" (the death-kiss / "the cold garden response").** "Absolutely, no problem — here's what I'll do, I'll put together a **draft** for you to review and you can tell me if I'm on the right track. I know you mentioned X — is there anything else you want me to add? … So you want a proposal designed to achieve these three outcomes, via these assets/activations, measured by these ROI measures — anything else? I'm free all day Thursday and Friday; what works best for a quick call to review?" (You go OVER the draft together; you don't send it.)
11. **Budget pushback** — walk the cap down (see Tactics).
12. **Find other stakeholders** — "so I can invite the right people on my end, who else on your end should be part of this?"

### Five questions plus secret close (recap, identical intent across videos)
Always end every meeting with explicit permission-seeking: "Do you agree there's a fit here? Can I submit a draft proposal for your feedback?"

### Exhibitor-upgrade script (from "Upgrade Your Exhibitors")
Phone rings, "We want to buy an exhibit space." Instead of "That'll be $6,000":
> "What are you hoping to accomplish with an exhibit space?" → [they list goals a booth can't deliver] → "It sounds like you're really interested in branding and thought leadership — an exhibit booth really isn't the best place for that. You'll get some benefits and I'm happy to set you up with a booth, but would you be interested in hearing about some other options that would help you achieve these goals?" → "I'll put a booth on hold for you, great location, no concern" → full discovery → custom proposal.

### "What do you mean by that?" (from "The Power of Curiosity")
The single most powerful question. Use it even when the answer seems obvious. When a prospect says "we just want brand awareness": "What do you mean by that? What is brand awareness to you? How do you measure whether someone is more or less aware of your brand? Aware for what purpose?" Other probes: "How do you know that's a problem? How would you know if you've solved it? What have you tried that worked / didn't work? What assumptions are you making about your sales goals?"

### Diagnose-don't-prescribe for "brand" (from "Sponsors Don't Want Logos")
Don't ask "how can we generate brand awareness?" (if you're reaching out to them, they're already aware of you, so awareness isn't their problem). Instead: "What **image** are you trying to put out into the world, and how can I help you reinforce it? How do you know if your brand is growing or shrinking in the marketplace?"

### Closing line used across nearly every video (mindset mantra)
> "The sponsorship proposal does not make the sale — **you** do."

### Phrases to AVOID
- "To whom it may concern," "Dear friend," "Dear supporter," "I hope this note finds you well." (Sound like spam; lower response.)
- "We don't want sponsors, we want partners." (Define "partner" and you'll just describe good sponsorship.)
- Anything that opens with your founding year, team size, CEO bio, or 27-year history.
- "Just send me a proposal" is the prospect's polite code for "no, thank you."

## 4. Numbers & Benchmarks

- **Audience data points:** 25+ recommended (events: 30+). Recurs constantly.
- **Avatars/personas:** minimum **3** per audience.
- **Inventory:** at least **100 individual assets** per property.
- **Prospect pool:** ≥100 following the method; usually capped at **300–500**.
- **Survey-sourced hot prospects:** 20–30+ brands from the "intend to purchase in 18 months" question alone.
- **Business case / proposal composition:** **≥50% audience data.** (e.g., 36-page doc → 18 pages audience data — though 36 pages is too long.)
- **Email length:** 2 sentences / ≤3 sentences. "Anything over three sentences you could pretty much just delete the email yourself."
- **Cold-outreach response benchmark:** **5–10%** agreeing to a call is "outstanding"; "a 100% response rate is unrealistic; 10% is phenomenal." (vs. ~0% from blasting.)
- **Discovery meeting length:** 30–45 min. Salesperson talks ~20%; ~90% of statements end in a question mark.
- **Over-delivery target:** ≥10%.
- **Fulfillment report timing:** within 1–2 weeks of event; within **7 days** to lock renewal.
- **Renewal/upsell conversion (friend's story):** ~**30%** of partners agreed to **double** their investment when shown over-delivery and asked.
- **Advertising/digital benchmark rates (from "Digital Marketing and Sponsorship," used for valuation):**
  - **CPM (≈ logo placement):** $5–$10 per 1,000 impressions (i.e., pennies/fractions of a penny per view).
  - **Cost per click (≈ driving web traffic):** $2–$10 per click. (A hyperlinked logo may get ~1 click per 10,000 visits.)
  - **Cost per send (≈ eblast):** $0.50–$1.00 per send (LinkedIn-style direct message). A logo at the bottom of someone else's email is worth a fraction of this.
  - **Cost per lead (most valuable):** $50 to **$2,000+** per lead, depending on definition (email / free-trial signup / booked meeting).
- **Logo-on-sign valuation:** "at best worth **three cents per view**." A "logo soup" near registration is near-worthless without huge volume.
- **ROI ratios sponsors expect:** 2:1, 3:1, even 4:1. (Worth $1 → sponsor wants to pay ~33¢ at 3:1 or 25¢ at 4:1. "Sponsors want a deal — they want to pay less than market rate," which is why you must know market value.)
- **Sponsorship spend split:** advertising (logos + commercials) is only **~5–10%** of total sponsorship dollars; **90–95%** of the **$60B+/yr** market goes to everything but logos/ads.
- **Sponsorship margin:** "sometimes 90%" (vs. exhibit booths).
- **Sponsor vs. exhibitor value:** "a sponsor is **200x** what an exhibitor is" in marketing value.
- **Unsolicited proposals a single Fortune 500 marketer receives:** stated as a large monthly volume (caption garbled the figure, framed as "every single month, it's a crowded marketplace").
- **Timeline to attempt cold-letter → discovery → proposal → close:** need **at least 6 months**; don't send a cold sponsorship letter if your event is 1–3 months out.
- **Podcast fast-forward buttons** are 15 or 30 seconds — "exactly the length of your commercials." (Why ad reads add no value.)
- **Small-audience proof points:** One practitioner personally sold **$80K–$150K individual deals** to an audience of **~150 people** (sold "dozens"); a **350-person** first-year conference beat mega-conferences for total sponsorship revenue; multiple six- and seven-figure deals on audiences of "a couple hundred."

## 5. Named Concepts & Terminology

- **Property / opportunity:** the thing you're seeking sponsorship for (event, podcast, team, festival, program). "Give me money so I'll have it is not a property."
- **Asset:** an individual sellable element (logo placement, eblast, sampling rights, speaking slot, exhibit space, broadcast rights). An exhibit booth is "an asset, a commodity — not sponsorship."
- **Activation:** the overlap between what your audience wants more of and what your sponsor wants — "a cool experience for your audience that lets them engage with a sponsor." Think of it as "switching on" your sponsorship; the antidote to gold/silver/bronze. Internally, refer to your sponsorship as "activation" or "experiential marketing" so you think "where can I create more experiences?" not "where can I stuff more logos?"
- **Fulfillment report:** post-delivery proof (photos, screenshots, media clips, testimonials) that you did and over-delivered on every promise. "The best prospecting tool out there" / "the most valuable tool in the sponsorship game."
- **Fulfillment meeting / sponsor summit:** the renewal meeting where you present the report and ask for next year/upsell/multi-year.
- **Renewal:** locking the sponsor for the next term, ideally within a week of wrap via fulfillment report + BAMFAM.
- **BAMFAM — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting":** never end a discovery/call without the next meeting confirmed on the calendar. Antidote to "send me a proposal" → ghosting.
- **Business case:** the document sent **before** a meeting. No pricing, no grids. ≥50% audience data, plus activation ideas, media footprint, case studies. Its ONLY job is to get a discovery meeting. Looks like a rate card.
- **Proposal:** the document **after** a meeting. Has pricing/values and market-tested numbers. "If you put dollar figures in the document, it's a proposal."
- **Buyer persona / avatar / niche (niche):** "a semi-fictional representation of your **sponsor's** ideal customers based on data and research of **your** audience." (Twist on HubSpot's definition.) Used to think like the audience and guide activations. Example below.
- **Macro audience:** the whole audience of your property; you then drill down to 3–5 avatars.
- **Title / naming rights:** you actually **name the property after the sponsor** (Company-Name Festival/Podcast). If you only say "presented by," that's **presenting rights**, not title sponsorship. (Naming alone is "technically" title sponsorship but "not sufficient.")
- **Presenting rights:** "[Festival] presented by [Brand]" — distinct from title.
- **In-kind / contra:** free stuff/product from a company. Reframe: giving away free product IS **sampling**, and **sampling rights are lucrative** — charge for them.
- **Sampling rights:** giving a sponsor's product into the audience's hands (test drive = a product sample). One of the most valuable assets.
- **Lead gen:** emails, phone numbers, quote requests, free-trial signups, booked meetings. "If you can deliver leads to a sponsor you have a license to print money."
- **ROI / ROAS:** return on investment / return on ad spend; sponsors expect 2:1–4:1.
- **Social dismissal:** sponsors dismiss/ignore people who present as amateurs; you must present and act as an **equal** (a professional, not a supplicant).
- **Sponsorship alchemy (anti-pattern):** belief that mixing low-value ingredients (bad prospecting, no audience data, gold/silver/bronze, no fulfillment) and applying "heat/pressure" (longer hours, more emails, more cold calls) turns it to gold. It only makes "bigger piles of mediocrity."
- **Sponsor repellent:** the words "gold/silver/bronze" on any sales material.
- **CTA (call to action) in a business case:** the thing you want the sponsor to DO = pick up the phone and call you (so you can build something custom), NOT "sign up online" or "send a check."

## 6. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

- **Gold/silver/bronze tiered grids.** The recurring #1 villain across every video. "Tear it up and throw it in the garbage." Grids make you look like an amateur **regardless of the name** (Platinum, Ruby, "adamantium" — the problem is having grids at all). "Sponsors don't want to work with amateurs."
- **Sending packages/proposals to strangers** (and at scale). "Carpet bombing people with proposals is not prospecting." Doing a 0%-success activity at scale still yields 0%. (Lawn-care analogy: right things, wrong order/quantity/time.)
- **Putting pricing in a document before a meeting.** You don't know their budget, goals, or audience yet.
- **Leading with logos / "logo soup."** Logos are the least valuable, least memorable asset. Even brands' own ads aren't "just a logo" — they tell a story. "That feeling that makes you hope they don't ask how you got the values in your pitch deck" exists because logos don't drive ROI.
- **Defining your audience as "everybody / general public / middle-class families."** "Everybody is the target market of nobody." Not audience data.
- **Defining audience only by "people who like [your topic]."** Too narrow; limits you to gear/topic sponsors. People are "more than just one thing."
- **Treating sponsors as investors or donors.** "Sponsors are not investors — they're marketers." Don't make a case for seed money (that's what banks/VCs are for) or run sponsorship as charity/philanthropy. Nonprofits: stop treating sponsors like donors; "we do good work, companies should give us money" misses audience + activation entirely.
- **Long emails (2,000 words).** Nobody reads them; they trigger social dismissal.
- **Hard-pitching / selling for 30–45 min** in the first meeting instead of doing discovery.
- **Taking answers at face value** instead of asking "what do you mean by that?" 2–3 times.
- **Bringing materials / visibly taking notes** in the first meeting (preyed-upon feeling).
- **Happy ears:** hearing "send me a proposal" as interest, firing one over, then getting ghosted for months. Always BAMFAM.
- **Skipping the fulfillment report.** The fastest way to lose sponsors after year one. ("Event wraps → ignore sponsor for 9.5 months → they hear from you only when the unsolicited package arrives → budget's been reassigned.")
- **Calling exhibitors "sponsors."** They get radically different value; not the same thing.
- **Forcing exhibit space on a sponsor.** (A sponsor won't attend if forced to take a booth they don't want.)
- **Confusing brand with logo, and "brand awareness" with "logo awareness."**
- **Calling sponsorship "easy."** Actions reveal the belief: waiting until 1–2 months pre-event, emailing strangers, "that company has money, let's send them a package." "Sponsorship is really hard work — it's worth it, but it's hard."
- **Working longer hours instead of fixing the leaky bucket** (alchemy). "Scaling mediocrity only leads to bigger piles of mediocrity" (Keith Cunningham, *The Road Less Stupid*).
- **Trying to "get around the gatekeeper."** You meet "a very angry person who has a gatekeeper for a reason."
- **Emailing info@/customer-service lines or calling generic numbers** hoping to reach the CMO.
- **Copying big players' public gold/silver/bronze packages.** They succeed *in spite of* grids, not because of them — they have leverage (audience, trade-deal leverage) you may not. (Hospital/x-ray and "boss approved $50K custom" cases below.)
- **Building a strategy around filling out online forms.** Efficient at an inefficient process.
- **Setting goals too low** ("I just want to cover costs" / "make as much money as possible"). Not a real goal; leaves five-/six-figure title deals on the table. Play to win, not "not to lose."
- **Small-audience trap:** chasing low-ticket consumer products and vanity metrics. With a few hundred people, forget $2-coffee sponsors and "logo on a sign"; go for high-ticket sampling, lead gen, speaking, targeted networking.

## 7. Mindset & Principles

- **"The sponsorship proposal does not make the sale — you do."** (Signature sign-off, nearly every video.)
- **"Sponsorship is nothing but audience and access to audience."** (Recurs constantly.)
- **You are a "curator of audience,"** not a place for companies to put commercials/logos.
- **Sponsorship is a marketing discipline / B2B sale** — use B2B tactics. "Sponsors are marketers, not investors" / "Sponsors are not investors, they're marketers" (credited to "my friend Larry").
- **The customer rarely buys what you think you're selling.** (Peter Drucker, quoted in "What Do Your Sponsors Actually Want.") You must ASK the sponsor what they're really after.
- **Diagnose before you prescribe.** Sending a package = prescribing before diagnosing.
- **"The riches are in the niches."** Niche down; people are more than one thing; that's where the money is.
- **Curiosity is the secret weapon** — the opposite of selling. "The person who cares more about the prospect's goals is the person who wins the deal." "In every sales relationship a purchase is made — either you sell them, or they sell you on why they won't work with you."
- **You should NOT think of yourself as "selling."** "Stop that immediately… have real conversations with people you want to work with, and if they don't want to talk to you, move on." Discovery calls are "way more fun than sales calls" — prospects say "no one approaches us this way; this was actually enjoyable."
- **Present as an equal / professional** to avoid social dismissal.
- **Audience size is "the least interesting quality of your sponsorship opportunity."** Small audiences are a **superpower** (easier to survey/focus-group, easier to know what they want, higher value when niched). We teach big properties to "act like small properties."
- **Front-load the work; the ask should be effortless.** (House-painting analogy: ~95% of the job is prep — audience, activations, discovery, customization, valuation — only ~5% is the actual ask. "30 hours of prep makes the actual job incredibly simple.")
- **There's no such thing as "sponsors" — only companies (who must make money and hit marketing goals) and the people accountable for those goals.** You can't force people to open emails or respond; you can only "make your offer so attractive they can't wait to engage."
- **Add value to the audience.** A sponsor message/commercial "irritates your audience"; sponsorship done well gives the audience more of what they want. Reframe every relationship toward what people normally (mis)call "partnership" — but call it whatever; what matters is HOW you engage.
- **Sponsors can offer more than cash** (referrals, introductions, in-kind). Seeing them only as a "cash machine" loses money.
- **It's far easier to grow an existing sponsor (or exhibitor) than to find a new one.** Treat current sponsors/exhibitors as your hottest prospects (top of funnel).
- **What gets measured gets done** — great opportunities have clearly defined goals (how much money, by when, what ROI for sponsors, what activations, attendee targets, marketing/growth plan). "If you build it, they will come" does NOT apply.
- **Diagnose your own program** (blueprint), fix the bottleneck, don't try to do everything (avoid burnout).

## 8. Case Studies (named tactics, industries, outcomes)

- **The "cross out the package" friend** (senior sponsorship leader): In week one, sat with each inherited sponsor, handed them the predecessor's package + a pen, said "cross out everything you don't care about." **Every** sponsor crossed out **>half** (didn't care about free tickets, social posts, stage thank-yous, logo soup). They'd signed only for "the three or four benefits left + two others no one asked about." She tore up the package, delivered only what they wanted, produced fulfillment reports, then asked them to double spend — **~30% agreed to double their investment.** Lesson: ask what they're actually buying.

- **Martial-arts podcast** (podcast video): Kept striking out pitching martial-arts clubs/gear. Dug into audience → it skewed **white-collar professionals**. Pivoted to **HR companies and recruiting agencies** (B2B), who arranged workshops/experiences with a famous figure in the sport to reach these C-suite/future-C-suite pros. Result: **six figures** in sponsorship — driven by an audience-knowledge shift.

- **Drag racer + barbecue** (motorsport video): Survey revealed his database was overwhelmingly **construction business owners** who loved **barbecue**. Built a VIP experience (pellet-grill provider + whiskey/bourbon company) for entrepreneurs only — but had an **insurance company sponsor/pay for the whole thing** (insurance, accounting, legal are real needs for business owners). Charged a major premium by serving up the exact audience the insurer wanted, in a high-trust environment.

- **Female motorsport enthusiast** (motorsport video): A client's survey showed they were **over-indexed for women (~15%** of database) with specific hobbies/income. Instead of "put a logo on our car like everyone else," pitched brands on connecting with "the female motorsport enthusiast who makes this much, prefers these brands, buys these cars, travels this often." It worked — differentiation via audience knowledge.

- **Hospital / x-ray company trade deal** (Problem With Stock Packages): In 2008, a seller went to a sponsor mid-layoffs; the sponsor declined a six-figure ask but noted they "must be the gold sponsor at a hospital gala" — because that gala sponsorship was tied to the hospital **buying 5 years of x-ray machines.** Outsiders saw "gold/silver/bronze working"; reality was a **trade deal**. They succeeded *in spite of* the grid, not because of it.

- **"Boss-approved $50K custom" job** (same video): In a junior role forced to sell gold/silver/bronze, one seller used the package only as a **conversation starter**, did discovery, then told leadership "I can sell silver for $20K, but they want 4–5 custom assets and have **$50K**." Leadership always agreed (2–3x the rights fee). Outsiders saw grids; the real deal was custom.

- **Exhibitor → title sponsor** (Upgrade Exhibitors): As a conference committee's sponsorship lead, a caller wanted to **buy a $6,000 exhibit booth.** Instead of taking the sale, the seller ran discovery ("what are you hoping to accomplish?"), put a booth on hold, did full discovery with their leadership, built a custom proposal. They **pulled budget from several other conferences**, took the **title sponsorship**, and renewed **year after year.**

- **Client A vs Client B / online forms** (Mastering the Online Form): Client A hired high-schoolers to copy-paste boilerplate into hundreds of online forms → months later got the polite "more requests than we can fund" rejection. Client B used audience data + prospecting to find the right contact, ran discovery, spent ~3 months co-building a custom proposal, met in person, got a handshake yes — then was asked to fill out the same online form as a **rubber-stamp** formality. Both filled out the form; only the order differed.

- **Geography "no" → referral → deal** (Hidden Value): After thorough discovery a prospect said "you're not a fit, wrong geography." The client asked, "Is there anyone in your network we should speak to?" → "Of course, let me introduce you." Those intros led to a sponsorship deal worth **tens of thousands.**

- **The bank — work-the-verticals** (Nine Paths): Reaching the CMO via customer service failed (Fortune 100). The seller instead worked **marketing/sales/sponsorship/comms/brand/product** at C/VP/Director/Manager/Coordinator/Admin levels. A bank he cold-prospected in a recorded "over-the-shoulder" training video became the **presenting sponsor for two years** — directly from that video's outreach. Separately, at a nonprofit he had a bank's **CSR/Foundation at $10K**; he told her "I bet we can help you recruit," got a warm intro to the **VP of HR (Dave)**, who added **$35K to recruit** while keeping the original $10K = **$45K/yr for three years.** Another bank: a warm intro to a **Coordinator** who happened to sit on the staff-engagement committee → an outdoor-festival play-and-engage sponsorship deal.

- **The VIP bathroom activation** (Activations Sponsors Love): At a national fundraising event one organizer found a "crowd of angry moms" — portable washrooms were out of paper towels/soap by 9:30am; post-event survey confirmed "the bathrooms sucked" was the #1 complaint nationwide. Instead of begging a porta-potty company for free units (bad idea), they got a **consumer-goods company** to build a **VIP bathroom experience** (sampling plush towels, sunscreen, bug spray, keeping everything stocked), plus air-conditioned trailers for VIPs/top fundraisers (raised $X). Follow-up survey: the bathroom experience was the #1 thing people **loved.** Sponsor happy, audience happy.

- **Practitioner conferences:** A **350-person** first-year conference **beat mega-conferences** on total sponsorship revenue (no exhibit booths, custom experiential tied to brand goals — not logos). And selling **$80K–$150K** deals to a **~150-person** audience (dozens of deals).

## 9. Industry-Specific Nuances

### Podcasts
- "You are a curator of audience," not a place for ad reads. Downloads ≠ value; **quality of audience** is the asset.
- Fast-forward buttons (15/30s) exist because ads irritate the audience. Ad reads are 5–10% of value at best.
- Macro audience (25+ data points) → **3 avatars**. Activations = contests, meet-a-famous-guest you interviewed, be-a-guest opportunities.
- Two docs: business case (before, no price) vs proposal (after, with price).
- Whole pipeline mirrors the general framework (above), ending in fulfillment report + renewal within a week.

### Events / Festivals (incl. food truck festivals)
- Reframe: companies sponsor events for customers/profit/staff/brand — if they only wanted logos they'd buy ads. Events = engaging niche, captive audiences in ways they love.
- Types of event sponsors: **cash, experiential, promotional partners, media sponsors, in-kind/contra.** How you activate/leverage cash sponsorship determines how much cash you get.
- **30+ data points** for events; B2B events/conferences also need: decision-making authority level, titles, industries, whether you can influence a buying decision.
- **The two-week post-event window is the most important time frame** — deliver the fulfillment report, invite feedback, ask "what do I have to do right now to make this your #1 priority next year?" Know your renewals/gap within ~2 weeks.
- **Festival proposal = business case:** ≥50% audience data, then assets/activations (replace gold/silver/bronze), then **media footprint** (outlets, impressions — boosts sponsor value too), short 2-sentence intro, case studies with sponsor quotes, vanity metrics (attendees, social, web) included but not the whole story, and a **strong CTA = "call me, we build custom."**
- Food trucks: distinguish a food-truck **business** seeking growth capital (that's an investor/bank) from a food-truck **festival/rally** seeking marketing sponsors (this is sponsorship). Audience data points beyond "food" → unlocks auto, insurance, travel, banks. Negotiate by adding/removing assets. Fulfillment report within **7 days**; get a signed contract for next year (payment can come later).

### Nonprofits / Charities / Associations
- **Your audience is usually NOT the people you help** (homeless, food-bank users — companies rarely want to market to them). It's **"the people who care about who you're helping"** — a powerful relationship to broker. Exceptions where recipients ARE a target market: students at a college/university (qualified donees; companies want students).
- Stop treating sponsors as donors / sponsorship as philanthropy. "Proving you're a good cause is neither necessary nor sufficient."
- Nonprofits have a **secret extra pathway**: Community Investment / CSR / Foundation — but those teams do philanthropy, not sponsorship; use them for **warm internal intros** to marketing/sales/HR.
- Don't default to filling out the online charity form.

### Sports / Motorsport
- "There's no such thing as motorsport sponsorship — there's only sponsorship." The motorsport *fan* is already one of the most-studied audiences; what matters is **YOUR** fans/subset and how they differ from the macro audience.
- Logo placement on cars/fire suits/helmets/toolboxes = "a race to the bottom" (high supply, static demand → driven to pennies). Analyze email DB, social, web traffic, YouTube, your own VIP events.
- Pitch the **subset** ("17% of our audience fits this buyer persona — want to connect with them?"), build a tailored activation, attach a sponsor whose customer = that audience (e.g., insurance sponsoring a business-owner BBQ). Charge a premium because the audience trusts you and arrives with low sales-resistance.

### Conferences / Exhibitors
- Exhibitor ≠ sponsor (200x value gap). Exhibit booths can be a good profit center (and you may run a hall), but the strategy is to **upgrade exhibitors into sponsors** (90% margin).
- Run a **discovery session with every exhibitor**; they're the **hottest prospects** (top of your funnel — already gave you money). Give exhibitors fulfillment reports + meetings too, then move them up.
- Show a prospect you have their **highest-quality, most valuable niche**, then co-create ways to connect them to just that segment.

### Digital marketing (as a valuation lens)
- Map sponsorship assets onto the digital funnel to value them: logo ≈ CPM, web traffic ≈ CPC, eblast ≈ cost-per-send, lead gen ≈ cost-per-lead (rates in §4). The deeper down the funnel (closer to a purchase/lead), the more valuable and the more customization/negotiation required.
- Quick valuation hack: open a Facebook/Twitter/LinkedIn ad campaign, target your exact audience, and read the platform's quoted CPM/CPC bid right before paying — that's what brands pay to reach your audience. If you're charging less, you're leaving money on the table.

### Valuation (cross-industry)
- The core question: **"What would my sponsors/these brands pay on the open market to get the exact same outcome?"** — NOT "what are others charging for the same bundle of assets / their gold tier."
- Compare logos to small targeted print ads; samples to coupon/sample inserts in publications hitting the same audience; etc.
- A back-of-napkin number is fine under ~a couple thousand dollars; for **$100K+** you need a real market valuation. Be ready to state your **ROI ratio** (sponsors expect 2:1–4:1).

## Cross-cutting recurring rule (stated many times)
**Order matters and the proposal is never the entry point.** Audience data → 3 avatars → inventory/assets → internal buy-in → valuation → prospecting → discovery meeting (bring nothing, ask questions, diagnose) → BAMFAM → custom proposal reviewed together → contract → over-deliver → fulfillment report → renewal/referral within a week. Skip steps or reorder them and you "do the right things in the wrong order/quantity/time" — guaranteeing the seed won't germinate.
