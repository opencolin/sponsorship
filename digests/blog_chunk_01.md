## 1. FRAMEWORKS & PROCESSES (named, step-by-step)

### The Core Sponsorship Sales Process (the master sequence, recurs across nearly every post)
The canonical order — doing it OUT of order is the #1 cause of failure:
1. **Know your audience** (audience research / survey FIRST, before anything else).
2. **Prospect** (build a prospect list from audience data — see "4 circles" below).
3. **Research** each prospect (public info: brand values, products, partners).
4. **Outreach** → goal is ONLY to book a discovery session (not to sell).
5. **Discovery session** (ask questions, learn pain points; NO selling).
6. **Create/customize assets & activations** tailored to what you learned (only AFTER discovery).
7. **Valuation** of those assets (research market value, adjust up/down).
8. **Negotiate** (money comes up here, not before) — proposal only if/when asked.
9. **Contract** (have a lawyer review; only sign deliverables you can fulfill).
10. **Deliver** (track/quantify data meticulously during the event).
11. **Fulfillment report** (within 3 days of event, ≤1 week) → leads to renewal.
12. **Renew** (meet to review report, renegotiate for next year/multiyear).

> "Find a sponsor first, then create assets tailored to their needs based on what you learned during the discovery session." Doing assets first is "backward."

### 9-Step "Sponsorship Magnet" Framework (Become a Sponsorship Magnet)
1. **Stop chasing** (chasing = desperate; repels sponsors).
2. **Stop copying everyone else** (gold/silver/bronze imitators get majority = low-value results).
3. **Know your audience** (niche down: neighborhood not city; job title/income bracket not "occupation").
4. **Grow your audience** (lead magnets, opt-in forms, mobile-optimized site, nurture funnel).
5. **Use case studies** (real-life proof, rife with numbers; social proof > grids).
6. **Find media partners** (single most powerful multiplier — see benchmark below).
7. **Do what you say** (deliver in full; honesty over false promises).
8. **Produce a fulfillment report** (reopen dialogue for renewal).
9. **Use social media wisely** (post assets/activations/audience data, NOT "we need money").

### The "7 Ways to Offer Real Value" Framework
1. Provide detailed audience research. 2. Learn their needs in discovery. 3. Customize assets. 4. Co-create activations with the sponsor. 5. Skip the proposal (unless asked). 6. Track data during the event. 7. Make a fulfillment report.

### The "Sponsorship Cookbook" Framework (amateurs have a recipe; masters have a cookbook)
Steps: Customize everything → Know your audience (25+ macro data points, ≥3 micro-niches) → Build amazing experiences (activations after discovery) → Know your value (valuation) → Mix and match assets/activations per sponsor. Plus the **Hybrid Proposal** (below).

### Free-Training Blueprint (referenced)
Chris's free training = "my model of sponsorship acquisition and my nine-part blueprint for growing a sponsorship program." (Nine-part blueprint not fully enumerated in these posts.)

## 2. TACTICS & TECHNIQUES

### Prospecting
- **Source prospects from an audience survey.** Ask what brands the audience uses/consumes/likes and which brands advertise to them. The survey responses = your hottest prospects.
- **Expansion math from the survey** (recurs): For each brand the audience names → add brands that advertise to that brand's audience → add brands that *should* market to your audience → add direct competitors of every brand listed. This easily reaches 50–60+ prospects (podcast post) or "394 warm prospects" (referenced repeatedly).
- **"4 circles of sponsorship prospects"** (referenced method): built entirely from audience data — brands they use, competitors of those brands, brands that might be interested, competitors of those.
- **Use competitors as inspiration**, not to copy: for each competitor's sponsor, brainstorm 1–2 similar brands → "two dozen potential prospects." Then filter through YOUR audience as litmus test.
- **Look locally first** — easier to connect (shared community), audience overlap, less time wasted than prospecting cross-country. Recommend local sponsors for first few deals even if audience isn't hyperlocal.
- **Don't chase the big fish.** Fortune-500/household brands are inundated and can be infinitely picky; smaller/mid-sized sponsors have greater NEED and respond more. (Recurs heavily.)
- **Cast a wide net** — "thousands [of prospects] are waiting to be discovered"; no magic number. Smaller budgets just mean contacting more.
- **Look beyond obvious sponsors** to business/marketing media (Ad Age, Mediapost, Strategy, Forbes, Inc.) and niche trade magazines (e.g., fashion → Glamour, but also Happi, GCI) whose readership matches your audience.

### Outreach
- **Never go in cold if avoidable. Find a common contact** — the single most-repeated outreach tactic. "Find someone who knows someone." Ask colleagues, vendors, business partners, past guests, staff. A shared connection is "your foot in the door" — gets consideration, NOT a guaranteed deal.
- **Get to the decision-maker** (not necessarily top of totem pole, but fewer layers above them). Skip the receptionist / red tape.
- **The only goal of outreach is to book the discovery session.** Not to pitch, not to attach a proposal.
- **Propose a specific date/time yourself** — don't ask "what works for you?" (causes endless email ping-pong). Sponsor either accepts or counters; agree to their counter unless impossible.
- **Prefer email over phone** if you're not a smooth talker (easier to fine-tune the message).
- **Cold-call cadence / follow-up cadence** (the BENCHMARK cadence — see §4):
  - Day 1: Email #1 / Day 2: Email #2 / Day 3: Email #3 / Day 4: Phone call #1 / Day 5: Email #4 / Day 6: Email #5 / Day 7: Phone call #2.
  - Run cadence on ONE contact at a time. If no response, restart with a 2nd, then 3rd contact. **Max 3 contacts** before moving on (≈3 weeks total). No response after full cadence = silent rejection.
- **PP Approach (Persistent but Polite)** — Kevin Grothe: after initial contact, reach out by email or phone "at least every couple of weeks." Keeps you top-of-mind without intruding. Multiple follow-ups with no response = the answer is no.

### Discovery Meeting
- **Discovery is NOT a sales meeting.** Bring only a list of questions and a notepad. "There's no need to close the deal today." (Recurs in nearly every post — this is a central doctrine.)
- **Be an investigative journalist.** Goal: learn what they're trying to achieve, what they've tried, why it's not working, what it'll take to work.
- **Ask, then listen** — "listen more than you speak."
- Discovery can be in person, Zoom, or phone — format doesn't matter, substance does.
- **Discovery can span multiple calls** — Dave Stewart (podcaster) runs discovery over 3–4 calls. What matters is staying on discovery.
- Ask **~10 questions max** from the 37-question list, **tailored** to what the prospect says (go off-script); don't just read the first 10.
- After discovery, ask yourself per asset/activation: **"Would my sponsor find this valuable?"** (Forget whether YOU think it's valuable.)

### Proposals
- **Skip the proposal unless asked.** Many deals close with no formal written proposal. If they want it, they'll ask — could be after 2–3 meetings, 4–5, or never.
- **Never attach a proposal to a cold email** — guarantees the virtual trash bin. ("Like throwing a pot of limp spaghetti at a wall, hoping something will stick. Typically, nothing does." — Mark Sabourin.)
- **Standard proposal = ~6 pages**, content-allocated: brief title page → ONE paragraph on your org → ONE paragraph on your cause → straight into audience data → case studies. May exceed 6 pages for data/case studies; must NOT exceed 6 pages because you told your life story.
- **No pricing in the proposal.** No cuttable coupons, no check request, no asset/activation pricing, no order form. Including pricing gives the sponsor only "take it or leave it" (they usually leave it; or you undervalue).
- **Don't use the word "custom"/"customized"** in the proposal — let the customization speak for itself.
- **Hybrid Proposal model**: standardize the parts that rarely change (audience data) and customize the rest (assets/activations after discovery). Saves hours vs. building from scratch, without recycling stock packages. Best of all: build the proposal WITH the sponsor.

### Negotiation & Closing
- **Money comes up only after valuation and during negotiation** — never upfront. "You can't talk about money with your sponsor until you know your value, and you can't know your value until after you do a proper valuation."
- **Many meetings happen between discovery and negotiation** (assets, activations, audience data).
- **Set minimum AND maximum** (cause marketing / sponsorship): minimum recognizes your brand's intrinsic value (you get paid even if zero sales); maximum protects the sponsor (expect them to ask for one).
- **No means no — walk away.** "Unless the sponsor says no, then they're not outright rejecting you." But once you hear a true no, stop.

### Activation
- **A good activation fulfills ONE need of your audience AND ONE need of your sponsor simultaneously** (the single most-repeated activation rule).
- **Substance over flashiness.** Add "pizzazz" only after the activation achieves the required goals. Low-cost activations routinely beat sponsors' expectations; "free" activations have worked more than once.
- **Co-create with the sponsor** — "who knows a sponsor's pain points better than the sponsor themselves?" Sponsors love getting involved in event planning if you ask.
- **Worked example (email-list activation):** Sponsor struggles to build email list + audience wants interactive/participatory moments → run a contest where the prize is the sponsor's product and entry requires an email signup. Sponsor gets list + product trial; audience gets engagement.
- **FOMO / scarcity** is a reliable activation lever (limit tickets/samples, exclusive merch, limited-time pop-ups) — but don't limit so hard the audience can't experience it.
- **Reward engagement proportionally** (Amex/Coachella: rewards for opening a credit card were commensurate with the ask).
- **Harness the event's atmosphere** (Aperol at hot Coachella → hydration + Italian theming).

### Fulfillment & Renewal
- **Fulfillment / wrap-up report = ~6 pages** (incl. title page): event overview, benefits promised + delivery status, photos/samples (esp. sponsor's activation), attendance data, other metrics (e.g., booth visits), thank-you page.
- **Timing is critical: have it on the sponsor's desk within ~3 days, no more than 1 week.** If you wait, the sponsor forgets details and may allocate/spend next period's budget elsewhere.
- **Process:** finish report → contact sponsor to set a meeting → send report → give a few days to digest → meet → ask candid feedback on delivery → discuss renewal (you can request more money if you've improved year over year).
- **Podcasts: send quarterly (or monthly/biquarterly) reports**, not annual — keeps sponsor reassured and keeps reports readable. Dave Stewart almost lost a sponsor who'd declined renewal; after he sent a report showing 2× value per episode, the sponsor re-signed.
- **Express interest in extending the deal EARLY**, before the sponsor becomes a "past sponsor" and reallocates budget — once their budget is gone, even a willing sponsor can't help.
- **Implement sponsor feedback** → builds a track record that justifies longer deals and higher fees.

## 3. SCRIPTS, TEMPLATES & EXACT LANGUAGE

### Warm-intro outreach email (verbatim, Building a Winning Prospecting Strategy)
> Hi there, [name],
> It's [your name]. I'm a colleague of [your connection's name], who mentioned you when I brought up my latest opportunity.
> My event, [name], is hosting its annual [event name] soon, and I'd love to chat with you to see if we have any common ground between our plans and goals for the year.
> Are you available on Thursday at 2:45 for a quick chat? Please let me know.
> Thanks, and have a great day,
> [Your name]

Notes on this template: don't ramble about your event ("your contact doesn't care much… yet"), don't ask for sponsorship upfront, don't attach the proposal, keep it short.

### Cold email structure for podcast sponsorship (Cold Email Strategies)
Order: **Subject line → Introduction → Mention mutual connection → Flattery → Meeting date/time → Sign-off.**
- **Subject line** examples (verbatim): "Hi Sam, do you have time for a quick meeting this week?" / "Can I pick your brain about something, Sam?" Personalize; ask a question; can name the mutual connection. Avoid jokes, over-creativity, and emojis (may render as blank rectangles; less professional).
- **Introduction** (verbatim model): "Hi, Sam. My name is Adam, and I've been hosting the Living Your Best Life podcast for the past three years. The show is about strategies for finding your bliss and maximizing your potential." (A few sentences only.)
- **Flattery** (verbatim model): "The reason I'm writing you is that my next episode is on mindfulness, and I've heard you're one of the best experts out there. I'd love to chat more outside of the show to hear your insights." (Don't lay it on too thick → reads inauthentic.)
- **Sign-off**: "many thanks" or "hope to talk to you soon!" is fine.
- **Length: 50 words can suffice; max ~125 words.** Follow-up emails shorter (50–75 words).
- **Recommend a specific date/time** (give advance notice; same week if possible). If they counter, agree to their time unless you truly can't.

### Objection-handling scripts (Common Sponsorship Sales Objectives) — what the objection MEANS + how to flip it
- **"Just send me a proposal"** (after only ~5 min) = brush-off. Flip: "My process usually entails a discovery session first where I learn about your needs, then I produce a customized report." Or ask: "What would you want to find in a sponsorship package?"
- **"I don't have time for a discovery call"** = usually a complaint, not an objection. Flip: ask "When WILL you have time?" If they name a slot → real; if "not sure" → brush-off. ("If they don't have 20 minutes for a discovery call, what makes you think they'll spend 40 minutes reading your proposal or weeks on your event?")
- **"I already spent my budget"** = maybe true, maybe smokescreen. Flip: ask "When do you determine your budget for the year/quarter?" If it's January and they claim the year's budget is gone → they're lying (and a company that burns a year's budget in 2 months is too financially irresponsible to work with). Ask when the budget cycle renews; if May, write them off for now and check back in May. They may not know the budget → find a decision-maker.
- **"We don't do sponsorship"** (yet they have past sponsors) = they don't do YOUR version (logos/signage). Flip — redefine the CONTENTS not the name: "We help brands connect to their audience in meaningful ways. Maybe that's sponsorship to some people, but I don't care what you call it. I want to help your company achieve more of its goals." Renaming it "partnership" won't help; customizing assets will.
- **"We just want a booth / [asset]"** = they think that asset alone meets their goal. Flip: ask "Why? What do you think you'll accomplish with just a booth? How would you measure success?" Then upgrade it into an interactive/elevated version. "It's what you do with it and the outcomes."
- **"We prefer in-kind only"** = often they really want to put product in attendees' hands. Flip: ask why; then design an activation that gets the audience trying samples more engagingly/profitably than dumping branded pens.
- **"Let me think about it"** = hardest to overcome. Flip: ask "What's giving you pause?" Surface and assuage the hidden concern.
- **"Marketing is not part of our local operations"** = often a lie. Flip: ask outright who handles marketing and propose a joint call to allocate budget. If they hem and haw, it was a brush-off.

### Board-member discovery questions (Get Your Board on Board)
Hand board members these questions (NOT a proposal):
- "Can you tell me more about your business goals?"
- "What activities do you find work really well to help you build your business?"
- "What markets are you predominantly looking to reach?"
- "What does a successful sponsorship arrangement look like to you? What does it help you to achieve?"

### Internal "culture of sponsorship" questions (Building a Culture of Sponsorship)
Ask colleagues/departments (bring coffee + cookies):
- "What about our current sponsorship program do you feel is working and we should do more of?"
- "What do you feel is not working that we need to improve or stop altogether?"
- "Have you seen other organizations doing something you feel could work for us?"

### Festival sponsor — politely declining a sponsor's idea (verbatim)
> I appreciate you sharing your idea, and while I'll consider it for another event, based on [X data], I think my audience might like this more…

### Get a sponsor talking (festival relationships)
> What do you think about that?

### Phrases / terms to AVOID
- "Custom" / "customized" in a proposal.
- "General public" / "families" / "the middle class" as audience descriptors (too vague).
- Gold / silver / bronze (or any renamed tiers) — "the mark of an amateur."
- Logos / "shoutouts from the podium" as your primary asset ("as low-value as assets get").
- Calling sponsorship a "partnership" to dodge the "we don't do sponsorship" objection (doesn't work).

## 4. NUMBERS & BENCHMARKS

### Funnel / outreach math
- **Cold email response rate: ~8.5%** (2023, Klenty) — "eight responses for every 100 emails."
- **Cold call success rate: ~3–10%** (3% the more common figure).
- **Cold email reply timing (Cience 2021):** 19% reply within 1 hour; 77% within 12 hours.
- **Follow-up cadence:** 7-day cadence (5 emails + 2 calls) per contact; **max 3 contacts ≈ 3 weeks** before abandoning.
- **Podcast cold-email follow-up:** wait 2–3 days between emails; up to 5 emails over 14 days (Day 1, follow-ups ~Day 3, 7, 10, 14), then stop.
- **"The ask" is ~5% of the total sponsorship sales process** (so a broker doing the ask handles ~5–10%, you handle ~90–95%).

### Audience data
- **Have at least 25 data points** on your audience to present to a sponsor (recurs). Beyond basics (age, gender, geography, marital status, occupation, income) add: customer longevity, spend per year/quarter/month, brands consumed, # of your events attended, decision-making capacity.
- **Niche to the smallest unit:** neighborhood/borough not city; job title/income bracket not "occupation."
- **Survey cadence:** issue audience surveys ~twice a year / once or twice a quarter (podcasters: twice a year).
- Worked audience example: not "40-something suburban moms," but "women 40–45 in Chicago's five most popular neighborhoods, making $90k/yr in the pharmaceutical industry, with two kids."

### Pricing / valuation
- **Sponsorship seekers can generate close to 100 assets**; some "generate close to 100 assets."
- **Value each asset by researching market value**, then adjust up/down for your quality/expertise. Never copy a valuation found online.
- **Value EACH sponsor's property separately** — 5 sponsors = 5 valuations (properties differ per prospect).
- **Media partner can be a multi-million-dollar multiplier:** Chris's client's title-sponsorship property valued at **< $80,000**; after securing a media partner (plus good audience data + case studies), re-valued as a **multi-million-dollar property**, and sponsors started calling HIM.

### Timing
- **Best window to prospect/budget: ~September 15 – December 15** (companies set next-year budgets Sept–mid-Dec; second half of December is "a wash"). January is too late — budgets are set.
- **In that window, every discovery call is worth ~5×** (sometimes more) any other time of year.
- **Surgical timing rule (Mark Sabourin):** engage ≥3 months before the SPONSOR's fiscal year begins. Fiscal years vary: many nonprofits Jul 1–Jun 30; retailers often Feb 1–Jan 31; seasonal businesses align to busy periods; check public companies' annual/quarterly reports. Missed the window? Companies often have **discretionary marketing funds year-round.**
- **In the Sept–Dec window you'll often get a "loose agreement" now with cash transferring in January.**
- **You can't get a sponsor in 2–3 weeks.** So much depends on others' response times (e.g., audience survey alone can take ~2 weeks). Treat sponsorship as a **3–5 year commitment / long-term investment.**

### Dollar figures / market sizes
- **Average sports sponsorship value: $326,000 (2022, KORE/SBJ), +12%.**
- **~160 million Americans watched live sports monthly (2023); 54M+ watched the 2023 NFL draft; F1 viewership 87.4M (2020).**
- **US online gambling revenue: $5B (2022)** — NJ $1.66B, MI $1.58B, PA $1.36B, CT $233.1M, WV $109.51M, DE $13.53M.
- **Alcohol ad spend dropped ~$2B (2021→2022); 48% of ~2,400 alcohol advertisers (2021) stopped advertising in 2022.**
- **AR devices: 1.7B projected (2024) vs. 200M (2015).**
- **AR app cost: $5,000 minimum, up to $10,000+ for a basic app; ~$250,000 for something involved.** VR is even pricier (needs headsets/equipment).
- **Brokers charge on average ~25% of sponsorship** (range commonly cited 20–35%), plus expenses; sometimes flat monthly fee. Example: 25% of a $100K campaign = $25K removed immediately.
- **Broker incentive math (why commission hurts you):** Scenario 1 — broker spends 700 hrs to sell at $100K (true value) → $25K = ~$36/hr. Scenario 2 — broker undervalues, spends 25 hrs, sells at $30K → $7.5K = ~$300/hr. Commission rewards fast undervaluing, against your interest.
- **Cautionary tale (Linda):** paid a "connector" **$25,000**, lost it plus **8 months**, business went under.
- **Unsolicited proposals:** a sponsor friend's company receives **10,000 unsolicited proposals/month** — all trashed unread (recurs ~4×). They have no sponsorship email/form and don't want to be found.
- **Podcast value leap (Dave Stewart):** ~$150/episode (CPM-based, "a few hundred dollars") → **$15,000** sponsorship after moving to lead-gen + live events/activations; doubled revenue 3 years running.
- **CPM example:** a 60-second podcast ad ≈ **$25 CPM** (cost per 1,000 listeners).
- **Podcast network commission:** ad-revenue share commonly **70/30 (i.e., 30% commission)**, or per-download (e.g., every 1,000–5,000 downloads).
- **Cause-marketing case studies (numbers):**
  - Two identical national pinup campaigns, same charity, two retailers: **Company X (organic/optional, 3× foot traffic) raised ~$300,000; Company Y (top-down, metrics-driven, CEO-to-cashier accountability) raised $1.2M** — a **$1M difference** from approach alone. Company Y still runs it yearly; X moved on.
  - Small-shop product+sponsorship: $14 item, $1 to charity; needed 10,000 units for $10K. By adding low-level sponsorship on product/displays, **raised $50,000 (5×)** — "more than half" of what Chris's national campaigns raised.
  - Small-shop social campaign: $1 per Facebook like, max $2,500 → company got 5,000+ likes (paid $2,500); charity also gained 1,000 likes → converted to event attendees; 5 yrs later that company is a major supporter.
- **Tax: UBIT** — Form 990-T required if **>$1,000 gross income** from unrelated business; volunteer-labor exemption can avoid UBIT.

## 5. NAMED CONCEPTS & TERMINOLOGY

- **BAMFAM** = **B**ook **A** **M**eeting **F**rom **A** **M**eeting. Set the next meeting before leaving the current one. Both parties are busy; without a booked next step, momentum dies "like a slippery fish." (Recurs ~4×; a signature concept.)
- **PP Approach** = Persistent but Polite follow-up (~every couple of weeks).
- **Discovery session** = initial exploratory meeting to uncover the sponsor's pain points, what they've tried, what hasn't worked, and future plans. NOT a sales meeting.
- **Asset** = tangible or intangible thing you offer a sponsor (naming rights, promotions, contests, giveaways, interviews, employee benefits, paid/traditional media, venue usage, pass-through benefits, speaking/exhibiting, etc.). Logos/podium shoutouts = lowest value.
- **Property / sponsorship property** = a collection of related assets (e.g., your event, podcast, festival) that you sell. Title-sponsorship example valued </>$80k→multi-million.
- **Inventory development** = the process of canvassing your event/venue to list all asset opportunities (booths, speaking slots, signage spots, etc.).
- **Activation** = experiential marketing moment/opportunity connecting sponsor + audience; must satisfy one audience need + one sponsor need.
- **Fulfillment report / wrap-up / post-event report** = ~6-page document delivered ≤1 week post-event proving what you delivered vs. promised; the on-ramp to renewal.
- **Valuation** = determining the worth of your assets/activations via market research, then adjusting for quality/expertise.
- **Avatars / buyer personas / audience personas** = naming + personifying a niched-down audience segment (e.g., "Financial Frank": a Brooklyn tax specialist, $75k/yr, two kids, time-poor, likes tech brands). Makes matching sponsors easier. Not a real person — an amalgamation.
- **Brand value** = your audience's worth as perceived by the sponsor; the basis of your price. Intrinsic value justifies a campaign minimum.
- **Qualified sponsorship payment** (IRS IRC §513(i)) = payment with no expectation of substantial return benefit beyond use/acknowledgement of name/logo/product lines; NOT advertising. Likely tax-deductible.
- **UBIT** = Unrelated Business Income Tax — owed on taxable (advertising-like) sponsorship activities.
- **CPM** = cost per mille (per 1,000 listeners) — the podcast analogue of logos (low-value, what beginners default to).
- **Brand sponsorship / branded sponsorship** = a brand acting as sponsor (vs. promotional, in-kind, media, contra/cash sponsorship).
- **In-kind / contra sponsorship** = free goods/services instead of cash.
- **Cause marketing / cause-related marketing** = a for-profit + nonprofit partnership exchanging marketing assets for money/services ("buy product X, $Y to charity"; POS pinups; social campaigns). NOT the same as sponsorship (cause marketing leverages the brand for profit on both sides; sponsorship is a contribution to a specific event/program the nonprofit leverages for marketing).
- **Cause sponsorship** = charities/nonprofits/associations (incl. hospitals, education) engaging in sponsorship.
- **Sponsorship broker** = sells your inventory for commission. **Consultant** = builds systems/teaches strategy. **Contractor** = executes within your process. (Important distinction.)
- **Hybrid proposal** = standardized parts (audience data) + customized parts (assets/activations).
- **Pass-through benefits** = an asset type (rights/benefits you can pass to the sponsor).
- **"The riches are in the niches"** = marketing maxim driving the niche-down-your-audience doctrine.
- **Gnothi Seauton / "Know Thyself"** = invoked for cause sponsorship: know your audience, what they value, why they support you, how they decide.

## 6. MISTAKES & ANTI-PATTERNS (what NOT to do, and why)

- **Creating assets before finding a sponsor** — backward; assets end up generic/low-value.
- **Asking for money upfront** — premature; you don't know your value yet. Remove all money mentions from the proposal draft.
- **Skipping the discovery session** — you end up offering stock packages that don't fit; sponsor assumes you pitch everyone the same.
- **Treating discovery as a sales meeting** — kills the deal (the Sarah cautionary tale: pitched logos to a Fortune-500 CMO who wanted lead gen; couldn't answer audience questions; meeting died, got a warning at work, bridge burnt).
- **Sending an unsolicited proposal / attaching it to a cold email** — straight to trash.
- **Gold/silver/bronze tiers (or any tiers)** — "the mark of an amateur"; hides high-value assets to force higher payment (deceptive); shows your hand; "take it or leave it" → they leave it or you undervalue.
- **Relying on logos / podium shoutouts / CPM ad spots** — only deliver brand awareness; can't do lead gen, sales, conversion. ("A sign doesn't do lead gen.")
- **No audience data / vague audience data** — "kills sponsorship programs dead." Targeting the "general public" (≈570M in North America) is a fatal flaw → expect a form-letter "no."
- **Guessing your value / copying someone else's valuation** — can't defend the number; you leave money on the table (pawnshop/Faberge-egg analogies recur).
- **Over-relying on the proposal** — it's a crutch; the sale is made by you, not the document.
- **Chasing sponsors** — desperate, off-putting (2,000-word intro emails, LinkedIn money-asks, unsolicited packages, unsolicited sales material).
- **Copying other sponsorship seekers** — gets you the majority result (low/no value).
- **Charities treating sponsorship like philanthropy** — sponsors are marketers, not donors. Self-check: "Am I treating this like philanthropy?"
- **Charities seeking MISSION alignment** — won't exist (sponsors are corporations); seek AUDIENCE alignment instead.
- **Choosing prospects by how much money they (supposedly) have / by brand name** — wrong criteria. Companies don't always have money; budgets are finite. Prospect by audience data.
- **Assuming sponsorship is easy / will come to you because your cause is good** — both false; treat it like a full-time job.
- **Hiring a sponsorship broker** — see §8; high commission, asset devaluation, short-term focus, you don't learn, they may sell your sponsors to competitors, evergreen fees, reputational/ethical risk (esp. charities).
- **Believing in a sponsorship marketplace/app or a "magic Rolodex" connector** — doesn't exist; sponsors are "hard to find because they want to be hard to find." Shortcuts = scams.
- **Getting in front of the right contact at the wrong time / unprepared** — worse than not getting the meeting (you burn the bridge).
- **Waiting too long to renew** — sponsor forgets and reallocates budget.
- **Letting board members carry a proposal / make offers** — keep process control with the sponsorship lead; board's job is to open doors and have a conversation only.
- **Conference over-expectations (Expectation vs. Reality):** renting a venue for lofty ideals vs. projected attendance; assuming a sponsor = automatic promotion (the news spike is short-lived — keep promoting); assuming the sponsor plans/funds everything; expecting only cash (sponsors offer customers, traffic, in-kind, introductions to other sponsors).
- **Proposal mistakes (Common Proposal Mistakes):** skipping prospect research; targeting wrong sponsors; not communicating value proposition (beyond brand promotion → sales/leads/engagement); unprofessional attitude (typos, lateness); ignoring legal/ethical issues; failing to nurture the relationship; no follow-up step; one-size-fits-all tiers; not tracking campaign success; underselling your event.
- **Cause marketing pitfalls:** misaligned brand partnerships (women's shelter ≠ beer company; human-rights org ≠ child-labor company) → PR backfire; under-budgeting assets; assuming "big shop only"; selling products yourself (walk away); going to your database too many times (natural cap on campaigns).

## 7. MINDSET & PRINCIPLES (verbatim where memorable)

- **"Your audience is your Faberge egg. It's your most valuable asset and should be treated as such."** Audience is the #1 thing you offer a sponsor (recurs constantly).
- **"Companies want to sponsor your audience, not you."** Don't take it personally — it means you have something valuable.
- **"Amateurs have a recipe; masters have a cookbook."** Sponsors want professionals.
- **"Sponsorship is marketing."** "Marketing is the language of sponsorship." Your cause is not marketing data; your company history is not a marketing solution. "A sponsor doesn't care" about your cause because it doesn't drive their goals.
- **"Sponsorship is not philanthropy."** Philanthropy meets social goals; sponsorship meets business goals (recurs heavily for charities/boards).
- **"In sponsorship, there are no shortcuts. There is no silver bullet."** "Sponsors are hard to find because they want to be hard to find."
- **Prepare for a 3–5 year commitment.** "Just like you don't ask someone to marry you after the first date… you don't just jump into sponsorship."
- **Stop chasing** (dating analogy): chasing repels; being chill attracts. "Sponsors can smell you coming from a mile away."
- **Don't under-promise-and-overdeliver OR over-promise-and-underdeliver — promise what you can reasonably do, then deliver it.**
- **"Next year's shade will come from a tree planted today."** / Amelia Earhart: **"Always think with your stick forward"** (build forward momentum) — used to justify acting NOW / starting before you're ready.
- **Failure isn't personal** — "this is business… it has nothing to do with you as an individual." Use losses to refine, not to self-flagellate. ("Don't ever take a rejection personally — use it to learn, grow, and evolve.")
- **Sell your SIZE as an advantage** (small shop): full customization, decision-makers at every meeting, no bureaucracy, deep knowledge of your audience. "Working in a small shop is only a disadvantage if you decide it is."
- **Nobody cares more about your sales, brand, and sponsors than you and your staff** — the core argument for in-house over brokerage.
- **Invest in yourself** (Dave Stewart): don't settle for "a few thousand dollars"; five- and six-figure deals are attainable without a huge property or brand.
- **Be informative but not snooty** (internal teaching): best teachers aren't arrogant; "there are no stupid questions."
- **Self-evaluate quantitatively AND qualitatively** (Kevin Grothe) — see the diagnostic ladder in §8.

## 8. INDUSTRY-SPECIFIC NUANCES

### Podcasts
- 8 ways to find podcast sponsors: advertising networks (Acast, Art19, Midroll, Earwolf, Wondery, PodcastOne, Gimlet, Radiotopia — but you're 1 of thousands, and they take 30% or per-download cuts + may force cross-promo); network; check competitors; look locally; contact past sponsors; social media; ask contacts; **use audience data (the best — save for last).**
- **Have a 20–30 second elevator pitch** ready (what the show is about + who listens).
- CPM is the podcast equivalent of logos — low-value beginner default. Move to lead gen + live events/activations for 5-figure deals.
- **Connections (guests) raise show value** — interviewing big names in your niche.
- **Fulfillment reports matter MORE for podcasts** (ongoing, not annual) → send quarterly/monthly; pack with listenership + advertising data (no event photos).
- **Deal length:** shortest Dave offers = 6 months; also yearlong. A **1-year contract is the sweet spot** (long enough to prove value, short enough to revisit).
- Discovery often takes **3–4 calls** for podcasts.

### Festivals / Events
- 9 relationship tactics: be friendly (friendly ≠ friends); take interest in their problems; stay in touch (≥weekly in first half of planning, ramp to daily as event nears); keep them posted on progress; ask their opinions/help; implement their feedback; say thanks (don't wait until after); deliver objectives (the #1 thing — niceties never compensate for weak data/low-value offerings/poor delivery); provide additional value during lulls (share partners, tools, industry reports, audience data).
- Relationship-building is "a double-edged sword" — valuable but must not usurp data/value/delivery.
- FOMO/scarcity, VIP experiences, and atmosphere-matched activations dominate festival activations (Coachella examples: limited tickets, exclusive merch, branded lounges/piazzas, hydration in the heat).

### Conferences
- **Size the venue to PROJECTED attendance, not ideals** (estimate via last event's invited-vs-attended ratio, interest questionnaire, or competitors' event sizes). A conference is "a giant business meeting" — too many attendees hinders its purpose.
- Sponsors offer more than cash: new customers, web/social growth, in-kind, and **introductions to other sponsors** ("worth its weight in gold").
- Keep promoting even with a big-name sponsor (the announcement spike fades fast).
- Plan the event yourself with sponsor INPUT — they meet you halfway, they don't plan it for you. (Activation ideas: giveaways, branded VR booths, games tied to the purpose.)

### Charities / Nonprofits / Causes (cause sponsorship)
- **It is NOT harder to get a sponsor as a nonprofit** — the only difficulty is over-focusing on your cause or assuming the cause lets you skip the process.
- Audience = donors, board, volunteers, members, program users, attendees, investors, partners. Survey and niche them down just like a company.
- Three perceived barriers (debunked): (1) "No brand awareness" — good! You don't want the general public; sponsors want YOUR specific audience. (2) "Can't give away database access" — sponsors don't want to buy your list (privacy-law violation + bad practice); they want you to communicate to your stakeholders to drive a measurable action. (3) "We're too small" — nobody has advantages in sponsorship; sell size as an advantage.
- **Be explicit you're seeking SPONSORSHIP, not a donation** — contacts will try to redirect you to the donations department.
- **Corporate sponsorship is viable for nonprofits** — set clear boundaries/policies; pursue corporations aligned with your values; don't fear "selling out."
- **CEO/decision-maker at meetings = a small-shop superpower** (instant approvals; "shout across the office").
- AR has little obvious benefit for nonprofits/charities (best for sports + tech).

### Cause Marketing (distinct from sponsorship)
- 3 pillars of successful cause marketing: a plan/goal; strategic planning; engaging audience + sponsor to hit objectives.
- **Start with the prospect** (both winning case studies began by asking prospects what they valued — NOT by building a campaign and shopping it). Grab a coffee, no proposal.
- Best practices: select a cause carefully (noble/feel-good causes help; authenticity is core); team with relevant companies (start from audience); know your value (set minimum + maximum); know when to say no; mobilize your database (natural cap on # of campaigns); measure and report back.
- Strategy basics: set a revenue goal → research audience → consider your cause from the partner's CSR lens → list assets + apply values → build a sales funnel (Cold prospects → Cold calls → Discovery meeting → Partnership proposal → In or out) → consider ideal partners.
- Top-down, metrics-accountable execution dramatically outperforms organic/optional (the $1.2M vs $300k case).
- **Pro bono** is a legit asset source (designers/SEO/marketers in exchange for name+logo).
- Blending cause marketing + sponsorship beats either alone (the "showdown" ends in a tie; sponsorship "saved the day" because the cause campaign surfaced valuable assets).

### Sports / Motorsport
- Most profitable sponsorship area; average sports deal $326k (2022). Start small/local (community racing leagues) → add naming rights as you grow.
- Motorsport asset hierarchy (high→low value): **naming rights to races/championships** (immortalized in history books; e.g., Nextel/Sprint/Monster Cup Series) > **branded logo on race vehicle** (huge merch/video-game value) > **logo on race uniforms** > **commercials** (targeted airings) > **logo on track/other signage** (lost in the shuffle; few seconds of TV).
- Sponsors needn't be racing-related (NASCAR has 50+ sponsors incl. AWS, Built protein bars, iRacing; F1 incl. Workday, Zoom, Rolex, Crypto.com).
- **Splitting one title sponsor into multiple "premier partners"** (NASCAR 2020: Coca-Cola, Xfinity, Geico, Busch) reduces each sponsor's financial burden → appealing.
- Tobacco used sponsorship as an advertising loophole (Winston/NASCAR 1971–2003 after the Public Health Cigarette Smoking Act banned TV ads).

### B2B / Global / Trends (2024)
- B2B sponsorship is growing (cloud/data/supply-chain/fintech/consulting) but runs the same process; confirm B2B goals (not all want brand-building) in discovery.
- Global expansion via sponsorship: market research → market-entry strategy (sponsorship is the partnership piece) → expect more rejections, re-research the new audience, re-niche per culture, navigate language/time-zone/cultural barriers. Don't treat sponsors as stepping stones.
- 2024 trends to watch: sports↑, tobacco (better-for-you products, e.g., Mission Winnow) possibly returning, alcohol↓ (regulation/youth concerns), digital/VR/AR↑, sustainability↑ (practice what you preach), B2B↑, crypto (volatile but persisting), online gambling↑ (possible future bans), AI (use for drafting/ideation, NOT for prospect research—hallucinations—and not to fully write proposals), VIP experiences↑.

### Tax (US/IRS; disclaimer: not legal/tax advice)
- Qualified sponsorship payment (acknowledgement only, no substantial return benefit) → likely deductible. Advertising-style benefits → taxable (UBIT).
- **Nontaxable:** sponsor name/location/phone/logo on site & print; ONE static link to the sponsor's homepage only; product display/free samples (no endorsement).
- **Taxable:** corporate sponsorship framed as advertising; acknowledgements in regularly scheduled publications/newsletters; payment contingent on attendance; preferential treatment (better tickets/reception); link to a product/service page; listing order phone number, pricing, or endorsements; exclusivity (assumed "substantial return benefit").
- UBIT: Form 990-T if >$1,000 gross unrelated-business income; volunteer-labor exemption can avoid it. See a tax professional once sponsorship complicates returns.

### AR / Tech in sponsorship
- Uses: virtual fan experiences (kiosk meet-and-greets), virtual merch try-ons (clothing, beauty, even household goods like blenders on a counter), AR scavenger hunts (cheaper to run; real prizes), live enhancements (player stats/fun facts at games), behind-the-scenes/virtual tours.
- Pros: more engaging events, great for data collection (user behavior/preferences), relatively easy once you have the know-how. Cons: expensive ($5k–$250k), less immersive than VR, better fit for sports/tech than nonprofits.
- Tools named: Vossle, Streem, ARKit, Scope AR, Cluetivity (AR games), Hololink (education).

### Brokerage (deep-dive — My Beef With Sponsorship Brokerage)
- **Verdict: avoid full-cycle brokerage.** "Nobody cares more about your sales, your brand and your sponsors than you."
- Distinguish broker (sells inventory for commission) vs. consultant (builds systems/teaches) vs. contractor (executes in your process). "The ask is ~5% of the process" yet brokers take ~25% of value.
- Commission-only incentivizes speed/undervaluation, not your interest (the $36/hr vs $300/hr math).
- **Charities:** AFP discourages commission-based fundraising; CRA (Canada) flags results-based compensation as risking charitable status/public trust; US nonprofits avoid % fundraising over IRS private-benefit scrutiny. Even though sponsorship is "marketing not fundraising," public perception blurs the line.
- Red flags in a broker: ghosted social presence, no documented process, no case studies/references, promises without discovery, asks for an upfront retainer (you'll get scammed), they contact YOU (good brokers have waitlists and are selective).
- The "magic Rolodex" is a myth — brokers get contacts from their LAST client and will sell YOUR sponsors to YOUR competitors when they move on.
- Brokers only take "sure thing, golden goose" properties; if your goal is ~$50k, any broker willing to take you should be avoided; ~$500k could fund a full-time in-house salesperson with money to spare.
- Narrow legit uses: short-term/seasonal campaign with immovable deadline; a large brand that already has strategy/valuation/assets and only needs outbound; temporary overflow; purely transactional logo-only deals; mature property with CRM/pricing/fulfillment already built. Safest contracting model: defined scope, flat fees, NO ownership of sponsor relationships, full transparency.

### Self-evaluation diagnostic ladder (Kevin Grothe — Beyond the Basics)
- Cold outreach NOT producing discovery calls? → re-examine WHO you approach and your email content.
- Booking calls but few proposal requests? → change what you say on calls / redirect the conversation to increase interest.
- Presenting proposals but no commitments? → fix the packet to meet prospect needs; check formatting/graphics; switch blocky text to easy-to-read bullets.

### Building internal buy-in (Culture of Sponsorship)
- Buy coffee + cheap carbs to get meeting acceptance; invite colleagues to discovery + fulfillment meetings; learn colleagues' expertise; host a Sponsor Summit (internal reps hear directly from sponsors); treat colleagues like sponsors (uncover their goals, provide value, listen more than you speak); ideate + value as a team (but limit valuation participants — "too many cooks"); find a protégé so the culture survives your departure.
