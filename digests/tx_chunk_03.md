## 1. Frameworks & Processes

### The end-to-end sponsorship process ("How to Get Sponsorship for Anything")
1. **Collect audience data** — get crystal clear on who your audience is (attendees/listeners/viewers/fans/social followers/donors/volunteers — source doesn't matter). Without it you can't pick prospects, can't build activations, can't prove ROI.
2. **Identify prospective sponsors** from the audience data (brands the audience prefers + whose consumers they are).
3. **Build a prospect pipeline** — aim for ≥300 prospective companies before outreach. (Hard part is limiting to 300, not finding them.)
4. **Outreach** — short 1–2 sentence email pointing to the shared audience, asking for a quick call (NOT a proposal).
5. **Discovery** — sit down, ask about their business, target market, ROI measurement. Purpose is to learn, not sell.
6. **Custom proposal** — built after discovery, based on their goals; presented/walked through live.
7. **Agreement** — engage a lawyer, write the contract.
8. **Deliver** — do everything you promised (and a bit more).
9. **Fulfillment report** — prove delivery, then ask them to renew in that same meeting.

### The valuation process ("Five Trends" webinar)
Property identification → inventory building → audience identification → tangible values → brand value → activation ideas → **prospects fall out of this as the last output**. Key insight: prospecting is an OUTPUT of valuation, not a starting activity. "By the time you get to prospecting after evaluation it's incredibly obvious who you should be talking to."

### The "Sponsors on Demand" 3-system model
Goal: **leads every day, meetings every week, sponsors every month** → "3 to 5 cash sponsorship within 90 days."
- **Leads every day**: start with audience (25+ data points, 3+ avatars) → prospect research yields hundreds/1000+ prospects → log in a simple CRM/moves-management worksheet (department, company, prospect name, email/LinkedIn).
- **Meetings every week**: use the 7×7 outreach method (below) across 7 departments.
- **Sponsors every month**: needed for cash flow; avoid last-minute scramble; close via discovery + BAMFAM.

### Discovery meeting flow / "staircase" (Sponsors on Demand)
Bottom→top: (1) Build **rapport** / connect as equal → (2) get to know **their audience** → (3) **their marketing goals** → (4) **their roadblocks** → (5) **diagnose** ("your problem is X") → (6) **prescribe** (your sponsorship as solution) → top of the staircase = **BAMFAM** (book a meeting from a meeting). Questions categorized by: audience, business goals, success measures, ROI (37 discovery questions referenced).

### Sales-rep "advocate" framework ("Sales Secrets From the Frontline")
When you can't get internal buy-in, flip roles: ask the prospect (Gary) for his must-haves, nice-to-haves, and an honest best price (explicitly NOT a negotiation tactic). Then say: "Don't think of me as the salesperson — think of me as your advocate/ambassador, an extension of your team. I'll negotiate on your behalf." Present it internally as the sponsor's "final take-it-or-leave-it offer." Result: boss says "make it so." Author sold ~$1.5M this way.

### Refund / crisis decision framework ("Refunds, Cancellations")
Three options, all real decisions (moving forward is NOT "no decision"): **Cancel / Postpone / Move forward**. Process: (1) talk to lawyer → (2) accountant → (3) venue (then loop repeatedly) → (4) talk to sponsors one-on-one BEFORE any e-blast. Once you accept every option costs money, decide on principle by asking: What's best for the **sector/industry**? Best for our **attendees** (and loved ones)? In **5 years** which choice best serves the event's long-term success?

## 2. Tactics & Techniques

### Prospecting
- **Start with your own audience data**, then map to: (1) brands your audience already prefers (hottest prospects); (2) magazines/websites your audience consumes → read them → note who ADVERTISES there (proof they pay to reach this audience); (3) **endemic** brands (aligned to property type — note: NOT warmest, since they already reach the niche); (4) **competitors based on your audience** (other properties targeting your audience → see who sponsors them); (5) verticals/categories of every other audience interest.
- **Niche/over-index angle** (Motorsport video): a client over-indexed on female motorsport fans (only 15% of DB but far above norm) → pivoted to brands selling typical products to women, gaining a competitive advantage no competitor had.
- **Find contacts via free LinkedIn**: go to the company → People → filter for job titles containing marketing / sponsorship / sales / communications / product / brand. Connect, then reach out. Free LinkedIn is enough; skip Sales Navigator ("save your money… go up for a $1,000 dinner"). Apollo.io, Hunter.io, plain Google also mentioned. Follow the company so their posts roll up (usually posted by marketing).
- **Ambient/always-be-opening prospecting** (Kevin stories): got a Comcast/Xfinity sponsor from a rep at a table in Best Buy (referral chain) while shopping; got a law-firm sponsor by hearing their radio ad on a station targeting the same audience ("if they're spending money to get in front of my target audience…") — called and offered to beat their ~$30k/month spend.
- Watch the news for rebrands, new products, new market entrants, new companies — all signal available marketing dollars.
- "Hunt where the buffalo are / fish where the fish are."

### Outreach
- **"Hey Dave" templates**: very short 1–2 line emails, all start "Hey [name]," point to shared audience, ask for a call. Copy-paste-able.
- **Direct outreach is THE conversion event** ("The real work"): a specific email to a *specific named person* (NOT sponsorship@/contact@) at a specific company, with a real reason, asking for a 15-minute discovery meeting.
- **7×7 method** (see Scripts §3 for cadence): 7 outreaches × 7 departments × ~3 contacts each.
- **Mix channels**: LinkedIn for research + first connection, then move to professional work email (more reliably checked than LinkedIn DMs); phone still works ("people think the phone doesn't exist — it's still part of the process"). Warm the cold call with 2–3 emails first.
- **Timing**: prospect Monday–Friday, "sunrise to sunset"; the answer to "when is the best time to prospect?" is "yes."

### Discovery meetings
- Show up with **nothing in hand** — no deck, no pitch ("don't bring stuff to sales meetings"). The Travis story: walked into a pitch with literally nothing; asked the brand "we'll write you a check for a million — how do you want us to spend it?"; refused to cash it until they understood goals → walked out with $2.5M.
- Be a **journalist/researcher** (Kevin's framing): "tell me about it… tell me about your goals… tell me about your brand." Use **parroting** — repeat their last few words to show listening.
- **Surface objections on purpose** ("Nine Things"): invite "we have no budget / not interested / only do branding." If the meeting ends with "just send a proposal" and you haven't heard the objections, you'll be ghosted.
- **"Your consumer is everyone" rebuttal** (Brendan): say it back passively — "Your consumer is everyone?" — and they'll narrow it. E.g., laundry-tab brand → "anyone who does laundry" → probed → "people in small apartments / older people" (lighter). Chris's sarcastic version: "When was the last time you sold a product to a four-year-old?"
- **Budget extraction** (Kevin & Brendan): never ask "what's your budget?" Frame it casually/non-chalant ("I'm not holding you to anything"). If "we have no budget": "If I came up with the ideal package and it was $250,000, would you consider it?" → "no" → "OK so that amount's wrong — but how wrong? Wrong by $10k or by $200k?" → get to a range. Brendan: "We have people spending a couple thousand to people in six figures — what would more accurately reflect your budget?" Once the guard drops, pivot to "we can spend it on event marketing emails, post-race digital goodie bags, speaker fees, logo placement — which lever matters most?"

### Proposals
- **Proposal comes AFTER discovery**, presented live (call/Zoom/in person), never sent cold/unsolicited.
- If a prospect insists "just send me a proposal" before meeting → send a **business case** (audience-only, no pricing) instead, designed to get them excited to talk but not enough to decide without you.
- Keep proposals **bullet-pointed and short** (Kevin: "they're going to flip and peruse — you're painting a picture"). Start by restating their objectives (verbatim from discovery), then strategy/plan.
- Ana case (biotech conferences): **recorded discovery calls** (with permission) and used the sponsor's exact language in the cover letter + proposal → sponsor said "this is exactly what we need" → closed a six-figure deal in 2 weeks, skipping the full proposal process.

### Negotiation / closing
- **BAMFAM** every time — never leave a meeting without the next meeting booked.
- **Multi-year ask**: simply ask "Do you want to do this for three years or five?" — that tells you what to put in the proposal. For name/word-mark (title) deals, insist on ≥5 years.
- **Re: "I can get this cheaper elsewhere"** (Kevin): "Great — now what would they have to do for it to be worth twice as much? Cool, what if I just did those things for you?" (They negotiate down; you add value.)
- For first-time/new properties: offer a **20–40% discount** framed as "I want to share the burden/risk with you"; lock sponsors into multi-year at that price in year one.

### Activation
- Definition + method (see §5). Build activations at the **overlap of audience-wants and sponsor-wants**.
- **Activation brainstorm after signing** (see §1/§2 "Do This After Signing").
- Budget for activation inside the deal (see Numbers §4).

### Fulfillment & renewal
- Pre-build the fulfillment/wrap-up report; turn it around within **24 hours** of the event.
- Hold a wrap-up meeting (already booked at contract signing). Ask: "everything you hated, everything you loved, everything you wish we'd done differently"; then walk the report; then ask the renewal question.
- **Fulfillment report → next-year proposal**: ask "Can I put together a draft proposal with all the things you said you want more of and none you didn't?" then BAMFAM to walk it through.

## 3. Scripts, Templates & Exact Language

### BAMFAM script (recurs across many videos)
> "Absolutely, I'll put together something just for you. Are you free Tuesday at 3:00 so we can go over the proposal together?"

And when asked for a proposal in discovery:
> "Sure, let me put something together — and just so I know what budget range should I stay within?" … then … "Great, what day next week would you like to meet to go over the proposal?"

### Motorsport outreach/discovery flow (verbatim shape)
> "I just use the free version of LinkedIn… search for people who work for that company with the word marketing or sponsorship or sales or communications or product directly in their job title… connect… introduce myself and ask them if they want to have a meeting."

### The "Do the Opposite" cold-call line
> "I have 40-year-old high-net-worth moms who live in the suburbs, have two or more kids, drive a Lexus, and their kids play soccer or hockey. Does that line up with your target market?"

### Keynote "lock the check in my desk" close (Travis/Chris)
> "I'm going to take your check and lock it in my desk, and I'm going to call a meeting with you. Until I know exactly what you're trying to achieve and whether I can deliver, I haven't earned the right to cash the check. My job is to make sure that at the end of a year together you beg me to work with you again."

### Gold-silver-bronze opener (Chris's self-deprecating joke, used to make a point)
> "Before I show up — can you tell me, was it gold, silver, or bronze that caught your eye?" (Answer: they hadn't read a sponsorship package in 10 years.)

### Bank rebuttal on logo placement (recurs)
> "Do you think there's anyone left in this country who hasn't heard of our bank? We don't need you to build our brand. What we need is for your audience to take one action and one action only: to bank with us." / "We spent $50 million last year on media — we don't need your help to get our logo in front of a couple thousand people."

### 7×7 outreach cadence (Sponsors on Demand) — exact sequence
Per department, per company: **Mon email → Tue email → Wed email → Thu phone call → Fri email → Mon email → Tue phone call** = 3 emails + 1 call + 2 emails + 1 call = 7 outreaches. If no response, move to the NEXT department (don't assume one person speaks for the company). The 7 departments to hit: **Sales, Sponsorship, Marketing, Brand, Communications, Product, HR/Recruiting** — ~2–3 named people each ≈ 21 contacts per company.

### Budget-objection script (Kevin/Brendan, verbatim shape)
> "If I could come up with the ideal package for you and it was $250,000, would you consider it?" → "No." → "OK, obviously that amount isn't right — but how wrong is it? Is it wrong by $10,000 or by $200,000?"

### Endurance intake-form data hook (Elite Feats)
> "83% of our attendees said they would change a purchase based upon sponsorship of one of our events. So get ready to make an impact."

### "Sponsorship is a bad word" challenge
When a prospect says "sponsorship" or "partnership," react as if hearing it for the first time:
> "What do you mean by sponsorship?"
Then for 10 meetings in 10 business days, don't try to sell anything; just ask: "How does your business work? What are you trying to achieve? How do you achieve those goals? How can I help you achieve them? What can I do for you to make your job easier?"

### Discovery openers (Brendan)
> "Tell me about it." / "Tell me about your goals." / "Tell me about your brand." → then parrot the last few words.

### Renewal question (Fulfillment Report video)
> "If we make all the changes you just said, do you want to work together again? Yes or no." (Maybe is not acceptable.)

### Turn-a-no-into-referrals line (recurs)
> "Who should I be talking to in your network that you think would be interested?"

### Phrases to AVOID saying aloud
- "Support," "donation," "tax receipt," "give back to the community," "invest"/"investor," "ground floor."
- Don't pitch "partnership" as a euphemism for sponsorship (sponsors don't think of themselves as "partners"; "partner" means something specific in business).
- "Just send me a proposal" = code for "no thank you" (when YOU hear it from a prospect).
- A logo on a sign is an **output**, not an **outcome** — don't sell outputs.

## 4. Numbers & Benchmarks

### Funnel math (recurs, the canonical example)
- **200 contacts/year → 45 discovery meetings → 15 proposals → 5 sponsors @ $50,000 = $4M/year program.** ("The real work," "How to Fail")
- 200 contacts/year = **4 outreaches/week** (the test: if you made <4 qualified named-person contacts this week, you didn't do sponsorship).
- **Reply rate = a conversation; meeting rate = the pipeline.** The funnel runs on meeting rate, not unsolicited proposals.

### Conversion / close rates
- **Close rate on proposals submitted: 30–50%** ("standard"); Chris's is ~40–50%. Meaning **50–70% of prospects who say "yes/send a proposal" are lying** ("Your Sponsors Are Lying"). For every 10 who ask for a proposal, ~7 ghost you.
- Discovery → sale: **1 in 4** discovery meetings → sale (Sponsors on Demand). To get 10 sponsors you need ~40 meetings.
- Brendan (endurance, after qualifying budget): **~50% close rate** ("not 10% — more like 50%").
- Amber testimonial: **18 emails on a Sunday → 11 meetings booked** (using the template).

### Pipeline / volume
- Keep **≥300 prospects** before starting outreach; some clients end up with **1,000+**.
- Kevin's database ~1,000 contacts; reaches out to ~half (500–600) per year on an established property. In active prospecting mode: "hundreds and hundreds of emails and phone calls over a quarter."
- A Fortune-500 sponsorship head receives **~10,000 unsolicited packages/month** (recurs in 4+ videos; also stated as "10,000+ every single month, read none").

### Pricing rules / valuation
- **Aim for ≥25 audience data points** (sometimes stated 20+), and **≥3 avatars/personas** per property (10 data points minimum in one older framing; 25 is the standard).
- **Impression-value math (sponsor's view, "Dave"):** value 1 impression at $0.10 → a sign seen by 500,000 = "$50,000" face value. But sponsor's brand strategy demands **3:1 ROI**, so they'd pay ~**$16,600** for that $50k sign. "Spending $16K on a $50K sign is average job performance" — good sponsors push lower; you must find the brand that values it higher.
- **Logo placement ≈ a quarter of one penny** per person who sees it. Logo placement = least valuable asset.
- B2B/VIP audiences worth **200%–1000%+ more** than "general public." 50 attendees controlling corporate budgets > a room of high-net-worth individuals ("a rich person can only buy so many BMWs; someone who controls a $5M legal budget = thousands of Mercedes").
- **City A vs City B marathon case**: identical asset (Nike shoe giveaway), same sponsor/individual. City A gave it free for 6 years (loved free shoes) = **–$300,000 left on the table**; City B (after valuation) charged **$50,000/yr** = +$300k + free shoes. "Dave" (the brand) knowingly let City A undercharge.

### Timeframes / cadences
- **Best window to reach out: October / November / early December** regardless of when your event is (budget-planning season). Kevin: "my entire next year was made in Oct/Nov/Dec." 5:1 ROI on time this period (20:1 for Kevin).
- Don't take the **Dec 24–Jan 3** week fully off prospecting — "a very expensive week to take off." Monitor email; take calls.
- Lead time by deal size (Festivals): **title/six-figure or multi-year → 12–18 months; high-five-figure ($30k–$60k) → 6–9 months; $2k–$5k → 4–6 months.** 3 months is too short for cash/title sponsors.
- **Renew 80% of sponsors within 30 days** of the event/opportunity (recurs). Stonyfield-conference example: closed **80% of next year's sponsorship within 24 hours** of the event; **46% bought tickets before the agenda was even released**; **87% said it exceeded expectations.**
- Don't nickel-and-dime: if an activation extra is within **5–10%** of total package value, eat the cost.

### Dollar outcomes (named, see §8 Case Studies)
- Memphis in May: >$30M in sponsorship over Kevin's ~18-yr tenure; $200k+ in 4 contracts in one half-week; one **3-yr × $200k/yr = $600k** deal closed pre-budget-close.
- Elite Feats: grew one sponsor **$12k → $120k** (3-yr multi-year); >$300k total in a year; $45k multi-year off one event.
- CJ: $30,000 multi-year secured in first 3 days of the program.

### Broker math (Should I Hire a Broker)
- Brokers charge **$3k–$5k/month retainer, 6-mo minimum (~$18k)** PLUS **~20% commission** (lower retainer → higher commission, and vice versa; **no-retainer is not an option**). Commissions are **EVERGREEN** (∞ — apply year over year, even after you part ways, even on sponsors you grow yourself).
- On a **$500k** opportunity: ~**$118k** in year-one fees (≈24% effective yr1, then 20% yr2+, ~80% max margin). DIY (e.g., ~$10k one-time to build internal capacity) = **~98% margin yr1, ~100% yr2** (~2% effective commission).
- Brokers won't take you unless your opportunity is **≥$500k** AND they're ~100% sure of success — "the very fact a broker will work with you proves you'd have succeeded anyway."
- Brokers do ~10% of the workload; you still do ~90%.

## 5. Named Concepts & Terminology

- **BAMFAM** — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting." Never leave a meeting without the next one booked, or you'll get ghosted/chase forever.
- **Property** — your sponsorship opportunity / the thing you sell sponsorship against (gala, event, team, festival, podcast, building, VIP boxes, each individual game). "A fancy word for sponsorship opportunity."
- **Asset / inventory** — sellable components (logo placement, mentions, speaking, booths, sampling rights, e-blasts, experiences). Each has a market value. "Inventory = your ingredients."
- **Activation** — "switching on" your sponsorship; coming up with an idea WITH the sponsor to engage their target audience in new ways. Lives at the **overlap of (what your audience wants) and (what your sponsor wants)**. Delivers value to the audience, not just exposure to the sponsor.
- **Fulfillment report / wrap-up report** — post-event document proving you delivered every promised asset (with photos, quantities, and updated values showing ROI vs. what they paid). The renewal tool. Used interchangeably.
- **Avatar / persona** — a specific buyer profile (a sub-segment of your macro audience): age, gender, income, kids, banking/auto/telecom preferences, values, motivations, frustrations, planned purchases. Need ≥3 per property.
- **Macro audience** — the broad/high-level audience (vs. avatars). Plus "concentric circles": attendees + database + social followers + speakers'/media partners' reach.
- **Output vs. Outcome** — Output = logo/sample/booth/speaking (a means to an end). Outcome = what the sponsor actually wants (customers, leads, brand shift, sales). "No sponsor ever woke up needing a booth or a gold package."
- **Brand value** — a component of valuation (alongside tangible/market values).
- **Tangible value / market valuation** — what a brand would pay on the open market to get the *exact same outcome without you*. The valuation north-star question: **"What would a brand pay for this exact same outcome without me?"**
- **Discovery** — pre-proposal meeting to learn the sponsor's goals/audience/ROI measures; "not a sales call."
- **Social dismissal** (Sponsors on Demand) — when you show up proposal-first / talking logos & donations & "investment" and are instantly deemed an **amateur**; "sponsors don't want to work with amateurs."
- **Happy ears** — believing you have the sale because the prospect sounds interested, when there's no money in hand. Antidote: BAMFAM.
- **Endemic sponsors** — brands whose products naturally align with your property type (ski brands for a ski event). Aligned but NOT your warmest prospects (they already reach the niche).
- **Media sponsorship / In-kind (Contra) sponsorship** — non-cash types: media assets, or free stuff, in exchange for sponsorship recognition. Securing a **media partner** can multiply valuation (see §8).
- **Always Be Opening (ABO)** — replaces "Always Be Closing." Open relationships daily; "the second you make a sale you lose your warmest prospect." (Credit: Greg Hickman for ABO; Seth Godin "all marketers are liars" reframed to "all prospects are liars.")
- **Motion vs. Action** (James Clear / Atomic Habits) — Motion = busywork that feels productive (building decks, debating page 37, e-blasting cold). Action = discovery calls, surveying audience, fulfillment reports.
- **The hybrid model** — common pool of baseline assets ("all sponsors get logos + e-blasts") + a library of pre-priced activations you slot in after discovery. Avoids both rigid gold-silver-bronze AND building every proposal from scratch.
- **Hey Dave templates** — the short copy-paste outreach emails (all start "Hey Dave").
- **The 50% rule** (Five Components) — ≥50% of your proposal must be audience data.
- **The 1,000 true fans** principle — you don't need millions; a niche of loyal fans is high-value ("the riches are in the niches" — credited to Dave Rizzoli).
- **Curator of audience** — the correct self-identity for a sponsorship seeker: "think of yourself as a curator of audience, not a bowl of logo soup." (Coach Mark: "the prize is your audience"; "people are more than just one thing.")

## 6. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

- **Sending unsolicited proposals / gold-silver-bronze packages cold.** It's the #1 sin; lands in trash; ~0% reply. "What you write on page 62 doesn't matter — no one reads it."
- **Gold-silver-bronze / tiered grids at all.** Signals you don't know your audience, don't know your value, will focus on logos, and are an amateur. "Out of favour for two decades." Came from the **finance department** (easier to forecast), not from what sponsors want.
- **Copying competitors' packages** ("Most Common Mistake" — Andrew): you don't know if theirs worked, their audience, their goals, or whether the rep succeeded *in spite of* the package. Like following someone's map without checking the destination.
- **Treating sponsorship as philanthropy/donation** (charities). Sponsorship is a marketing technique, not a grant. Mission belongs in ONE paragraph; most charity decks waste 6–10 pages on mission/vision/CEO bio/board.
- **Blending sponsorship + CSR/philanthropy** ("the Fox chasing two birds catches none"): the proposal bounces between marketing (sees philanthropy) and CSR/foundation (sees marketing) until someone trashes it.
- **Leading with the cause/property** ("your cause/team/festival is irrelevant" — to the sponsor; it's only relevant to your audience). Sequence is always **property → audience → prospect**, never property → prospect.
- **Selling logo placement as the main value.** Worth ~¼ penny/impression; puts you in competition with every other logo. Necessary but not sufficient.
- **No valuation / making up numbers** ("shoulder-shrug method," starting from "how much money I need"). Sponsors know market rates (return on ad spend); made-up values alienate them and leave money on the table (both over- and under-pricing kill deals).
- **Skipping/weak audience data** ("people who like ribs and beer" / "everyone" / "general public" / "all of Canada"). "Everyone" = code for "I have no idea who my audience is." General public = lowest value, needs millions of eyeballs.
- **Treating sponsors as banks/investors/donors.** They're marketers spending marketing dollars; they demand ROI and are accountable to stakeholders.
- **"I do partnerships, not sponsorship" as a get-out-of-delivery-free card.** Renaming changes nothing; they still want marketing ROI.
- **Skipping follow-up.** Most sales close on the 5th–10th follow-up. "If you won't follow up, you probably shouldn't do the original outreach."
- **Showing up to a "pitch" walking the deck page-by-page** instead of doing discovery.
- **Fear of objections.** If you don't surface them, you get ghosted post-meeting.
- **Scaling up mediocrity** ("He Who Fails the Most"): if 10 emails get 0 replies, sending 1,000 gets 0 (just bigger). 1% open rate stays 1% at scale (Keith Cunningham). Test/change variables instead. Bradley internalized non-responses as rejection (was actually a spam filter) and quit.
- **No renewal strategy / waiting 9–11 months then sending a package.** That's an *anti*-renewal strategy; the sponsor has already moved on.
- **Waiting too long to start (festivals).** 1 week/1 month/3 months is too short for real sponsors.
- **Getting free stuff and calling it sponsorship.** Free stuff = sampling; you should CHARGE a brand to give it away, not get it free (Chris's photobooth story: boss wanted a free $300 photo booth → got a $25,000 cash sponsor instead).
- **Letting your event be "ambushed" by free raffle giveaways** (Brendan): stopping the event to raffle a $150 donated product (below your supporter level) *lowers* the tangible value of your assets.
- **Putting "sponsorship" on your website as a passive afterthought** ("download this package & send a check"). Nobody buys a $50k car because a website told them to. Instead: "Want to talk sponsorship? Let's have a conversation."
- **Trying to get *around* the gatekeeper** (Getting Around the Gatekeeper). If you bypass the process you meet a frustrated decision maker who already passed.
- **Only chasing Fortune 500s** — everyone does, so they're flooded; tens of thousands of lesser-known firms can sponsor at 5–7 figures.
- **Risk-based decision making** (broker video) — outsourcing because you secretly don't believe you'll succeed; you pay a huge premium for that fear.
- **Only capturing audience on one platform** (influencers) — if you only have a social following, brands can just run their own ads at your followers and pay you nothing. Capture email; diversify platforms.

## 7. Mindset & Principles

- **"The sponsorship proposal does not make the sale. You do."** (The signature sign-off — appears in ~20+ videos.)
- **"Sponsorship is not about putting logos on stuff. You can do that if you want, but that's not sponsorship."**
- **"Think like a marketer, sell like a (discovery-based) salesperson, fulfill like an account manager."** (Stonyfield video — what makes sponsorship hard AND awesome; rebuts "you just have to be good at relationships" → "you do, and then 20 other things.")
- **"You are the prize."** The audience is the prize; businesses can't exist without acquiring customers, so an audience-holder has leverage (without arrogance). (Coach Mark Saberin: "the prize is your audience.")
- **"More is not better. Better is better."** (Audience quality > size.) Multi-million-dollar deals sold in front of 150 people AND in front of tens of millions — both work.
- **"The riches are in the niches."** Smallest, best-defined audience wins.
- **"He who fails the most wins" / "the person with the biggest discard pile wins."** (Seth Godin.) Lean INTO failure like leaning forward on skis — counterintuitive but the only way to stay in control.
- **"Always be opening," not "always be closing."** Create conversations daily; "which relationships can I open today?"
- **"The person who cares the most wins — and that's you."** Basis for building internal capacity over outsourcing.
- **"All prospects are liars."** 50–70% who say "yes/send a proposal" don't mean it; the worst answer is "maybe," next-worst is an insincere "yes." Don't gauge pipeline weight on what prospects say.
- **Prospecting is psychological & hard on purpose** (Kevin): "if it's hard for you it's probably hard for everyone — so it's my moat." "Feel the fear and do it anyway." "It sucks; it's always going to suck — but do it anyway." "Prospecting is an Olympic-level sport."
- **"Motion feels like progress; action produces outcomes."**
- **Sponsorship is genuinely hard** ("Eleven Realities" #11): a salesperson + marketer + account exec + SDR + BDR all at once; "if it feels like it's not working most of the time, that usually means you're doing it right."
- **The win-win-win**: solve the participant/audience's problem, deliver ROI to the sponsor, and serve yourself ("this isn't charity work for the company").
- **Discovery is a respect-based, honest, two-way conversation** ("no trickery, no sales — just good discovery; either you can deliver or you can't").
- **Don't take rejection personally** (Brendan): "If you remember the names of the people who rejected you, you're not talking to enough people." There are near-unlimited leads. "There are two types of people: those who work in sales and those who haven't realized they work in sales."
- **Growth is parabolic, not linear** (Brendan): doubling $50k→$100k feels great; $100k→$120k feels like "only 20%" but is still +$20k (and ~40% of yr1). Talk in dollars; interest/residuals/annual contracts compound.
- **"The water's fine — you have to dive in."** No proposal? No problem. "You know your property better than any stranger-prospect."

## 9. Industry-Specific Nuances

### Charities / nonprofits
- **Sponsorship ≠ philanthropy** (recurs heavily). Your sponsors are NOT the Foundation/CSR/community-engagement/corporate-philanthropy people — those are not your sponsors and not who to talk to. Talk to marketing/brand/BD/sales/product/communications.
- For every $1 a company might donate, there's ~$100 in marketing that could be spent on you. Corporate philanthropy is "a nice idea [that] doesn't exist" (tiny, hyper-competitive). "I have no idea how to get a capitalist company to just give me its profit."
- Mission/vision → ONE paragraph. Spend the rest on audience + activations.
- If a company explicitly says "this is a donation, no recognition," honor it (and: cash the check, THEN ask for a marketing meeting and do something free for them that year to start a sponsorship relationship — Brendan).
- Board "warm intros" usually fizzle ("just connecting, look forward to seeing what comes of it" → nothing). Don't rely on them.
- Legal exposure: calling it sponsorship but delivering like philanthropy (or vice versa) creates real risk.

### Festivals (film/music/food — process is the same regardless of type)
- Be a **curator of audience**; ask "will this make my festival better for my target audience?" If no, walk away from the sponsorship.
- 7 mistakes: waiting too long; making beautiful decks instead of selling; focusing only on money (sponsors also save you costs + give referrals); skipping audience data; skipping discovery; no valuation; no fulfillment report.
- Activation method (on iPad): list what the audience wants (fun, food, drinks, VIP, family time / time away, A/C or — in the "coldest capital city" [Ottawa] in February — warming stations, fires, hot adult beverages while skating) × what the sponsor wants (leads, customers, samples, emails) → overlap = activation (e.g., give email to enter the warming activation).

### Sports / motorsport
- Shift from **billboard thinking** ("logo on my car") to **fan/audience thinking**. Your competitive advantage is your fan base + how you interact with attendees (you have access to fans whether at the track or not).
- Prospect on **attendees AND your own fans**; niche/over-index angles (female motorsport fans example) open non-traditional prospects.
- NASCAR comp: write into contracts that "your logo will show up in others' stuff and vice versa — it lifts all boats." Big-name sponsors amplify everyone (so evaluators ask "do you have big-name sponsors?").

### Title sponsorship (specific risks)
- Title sponsors may **require you to switch products/services**: change your bank, official credit card (if you do card offers to fans), drinks served, even shoes the team wears. Conflicts can be accidental — or deliberate **ambush marketing** by a competitor. Get it explicitly clear; always involve a lawyer. For name/word-mark changes, do ≥5-year deals.

### Endurance events (Elite Feats specifics)
- Aggregating many small local events unlocks national/regional sponsors who'd otherwise auto-reject the online form. The decision maker (or someone who knows one) able to cut a $20k–$30k check is at essentially every event (experiential angle — Chris Norwood).
- Trackable digital activations (post-race digital goodie bags, codes) beat "slashy but unmeasurable" billboard-style assets (bus wraps, mile markers). "Someone running by sweaty doesn't note a mile-marker sponsor for later."
- A "$150 donated raffle item" mindset reveals a property that doesn't know its audience's value.

### Podcasts / influencers
- A 20-sec ad-read / logo / thank-you = **advertising, not sponsorship**. Real sponsorship spans the whole marketing spectrum (experiential, branding, lead-gen, contests) → higher fees + better ROI.
- Build a **media-kit-style business case** (total audience, web traffic, social, impressions, interviews, DB size, open rate) → break into 3 avatars → case studies/activation ideas → strong CTA, NO pricing/grids.
- **Capture audience on multiple platforms + get emails** (don't let a brand just ad-target your followers for free). ≥20 data points; psychographics matter (what makes them buy, values, value-alignment).
- Contests/incentives must be anchored to what the audience genuinely wants, or actions won't convert.

### Conferences / associations (B2B)
- Use **B2B audience metrics** in proposals/fulfillment: decision-making authority levels (% VPs/C-suite vs. mid-level vs. consultants), industries, % from brands/Fortune-500 — instead of B2C "planned purchases/brand preferences."
- Survey questions come in **B2C and B2B sets** (Sponsors on Demand workbook).

### First-time / brand-new opportunities
- A new opportunity is an **advantage**: you can ask sponsors what they want and build the event around them (venue, agenda, exhibit space) — you only get this chance once.
- Treat it like a business: real plan, budget, marketing strategy (paid/organic), media strategy, conservative attendance forecasts. "If you build it they will come" (Field of Dreams) is the WRONG strategy. You DO know your audience (your marketing channels define it: an accounting conference attracts accountants).
- Offer a year-one **discount (20–40%)** framed as risk-sharing; sign **multi-year at that locked price** in year one. Think in 3–5-year timelines.

### Crisis / refunds (any industry)
- Gold-silver-bronze sellers will likely owe **full refunds** (no valuation = can't prove partial delivery; assets are mostly at-the-event vanity metrics). Custom/valued packages let you argue partial (e.g., "social + webinar + e-blast = 60% delivered → refund the rest").
- Legal entitlement ≠ right call: keeping money on a transactional basis can cost the relationship + future revenue. Alternatives to refunds: credit for next year; another property same fiscal year/same audience/same timeframe/same outcomes (if sales-goal-tied).
- Over-communicate with sponsors; "you cannot over-communicate." Sponsors who bought custom (knowing-the-goals) packages are far easier to work with in a crisis. (Chris & Brad postponed their conference; offered out-of-scope digital activations free; lost zero sponsors, none asked for refunds.)

### Brokers / outsourcing (any industry)
- "Commission-only at 1–2%" doesn't exist; anyone offering it lacks the experience to know it's nonsensical → run. Real brokers = high retainer + ~20% **evergreen** commission, ≥$500k minimum, highly selective. Math overwhelmingly favors building internal capacity. Brokers don't bring magic contacts (CMOs don't await broker calls); if they "have contacts," they got them from past clients — and you're their future past client (your contacts then at risk).

### Gatekeepers (any industry)
- Don't try to get *around* them — turn the gatekeeper into your **advocate**. Their job is to bring GOOD opportunities to the decision maker. Do good discovery on the gatekeeper (their goals, the decision maker's priorities, target market, what activations work). Bypassing = meeting an angry decision maker who already passed.
