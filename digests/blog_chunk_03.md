## 1. Frameworks & Processes (with actual steps)

### The 5-Step Sponsorship System (the core spine of the methodology)
1. **Audience data** (survey-driven)
2. **Prospect selection** (built FROM audience data)
3. **Contact identification** (find the named decision-maker)
4. **Direct outreach** (the only step that closes deals)
5. **Discovery-led proposal** (proposal only after a discovery conversation)
"Direct outreach is the only work that closes sponsorship deals." Steps 1-3 and 5 feel like "the work" but produce no meetings on their own.

### Full Sponsorship Lifecycle (Chris Norwood / general process)
Prospecting → Discovery session → Valuation → Negotiation → Event/program/opportunity (deliver) → Post-event (fulfillment) reporting → Renegotiation.

### The 7-Step Sales Funnel + "3-a-day" daily method (Five Habits)
Funnel stages: Leads → Prospects → Intro email sent → Phone call → Discovery session → Proposal submitted → Follow-ups.
Daily discipline: every single day move 3 people at EACH stage — research 3 new leads, qualify 3 prospects, send 3 intro emails, call 3, hold 3 discovery sessions, submit 3 custom proposals, follow up with 3. "You are only moving three people at a time, but if you do this every single day, you will make budget. Then you will exceed budget!"

### Five Steps to Selling Sponsorship That Actually Work
1. Stop thinking of sponsorship as philanthropy.
2. Know your value (do a valuation).
3. Always provide a fulfillment report.
4. Stop using the sponsorship proposal to make the sale.
5. Know your audience (be able to rattle off 30+ data points).

### From Cold Calls to Closing — 9 Prospecting Techniques
1. Audience surveying. 2. Selecting the hottest prospects (brands audience uses most). 3. Augmenting the rest of the list (advertisers to that audience → companies that should advertise → competitors of all of the above). 4. Segmenting your audience. 5. Cold calling prospects. 6. Discovery session. 7. Brainstorming assets/activations. 8. Value. 9. Propose sponsorship.

### How to Ask for Corporate Sponsorship — 6-step ask
Know what you need → Determine what you can provide (ROI) → Generate prospect list → Have a discovery session → Keep following up (BAMFAM) → Ask and negotiate.
Plus 4 pre-ask questions: Do I have audience data (25+ points/segment)? Do I know my value (via valuation)? Have I created custom assets/activations? Am I asking too early?

### How to Ask for Sponsorship for an Event — 5 steps
Be sure what you need → Find the right people (LinkedIn + network) → Decide call vs. email → Set up a meeting → Know how/when to follow up.

### Generating Activation Ideas — process
Have a discovery session to learn sponsor goals → Take it one goal at a time → Brainstorm with your sponsor → Remember your audience (3rd party). Column A = sponsor goals, Column B = audience goals; connect one item from each in a way that makes sense ("the magical moment where the needs of your sponsor and the needs of your audience overlap").

### Valuation Process (recurring across many posts — state once)
For EVERY asset/activation: (1) research market value (what a professional/firm/radio/newspaper/online ad charges for the same thing); (2) decide if yours is worth same / higher / lower based on honest quality assessment; (3) discard low-value assets that don't fulfill a sponsor need. Then sum = property value. Document the entire process immediately after (sponsors ask how you got the number and can tell if you're making it up). Involve 2-3 stakeholders (not more — "too many cooks") to temper the urge to inflate every price.

### Convince Your Boss to Change Strategy — 8 steps
Know your stuff → Learn boss's goals (slowly, across conversations, not a formal meeting) → Come up with suggestions + valued plan → Pick the right time → Choose language gently (validate, never patronize) → Pitch your plan (benefits-focused; let valuation do the selling) → Be flexible → Check in (monthly if uninvolved, weekly if involved).

### Building Naming Rights Agreement — required clauses
New name (exact stylization) → Duration → Where signage displayed → Rules on changing the name (+ who pays for changes, 30/60-day approval window) → Payment terms (lump sum vs. incremental schedule) → Promotional benefits (expectations not guarantees, based on prior tenant data) → Termination rights (both sides). Always run first draft past a lawyer; involve landlord on signage limits.

## 2. Tactics & Techniques

### Prospecting
- **Build the prospect list FROM the audience survey**, not from association directories, industry conferences, or past-sponsor contacts (those brands "have no real reason to engage with this property specifically").
- Prospect list tiers (warmth order): (1) hottest = brands audience named in survey; (2) warm = advertisers who already work with those brands; (3) cooler = companies that *should* advertise to your audience; (4) coldest = competitors of every brand on the list.
- This expansion produces 60, 70, 100, sometimes 200 brands. Chris Norwood had a 300-company list ("just right"). Recommended minimum: 50-60 (smaller properties), at least a dozen hot prospects to start.
- Don't chase brands purely on size/wealth (Fortune 500, household names) — they're the coldest leads and ignore small fish. Pick on audience alignment instead.
- Research every prospect before contact (website, LinkedIn, press, news) to confirm value alignment; disqualify mismatches.

### Outreach (email)
- Goal of email = build rapport + get a phone call. NOT to make the sale, NOT to send a package/one-pager/attachment.
- Keep body to **3 sentences max** ("if your email is longer than three sentences… don't send one"). A 3-sentence email takes <1 minute to read.
- No attachments (spam filters flag them; signals you want a sale on first contact).
- Use a professional email handle, not personal.
- Alternate email + phone across follow-ups.

### Discovery Meeting / First Meeting
- "Go to the meeting empty handed!" No proposal, no package, no leave-behind, no case for support.
- Goal of first meeting = gather info + get the second meeting (NOT close). "End every sentence with a question mark." Ask + listen far more than you talk.
- Treat it as info-gathering to determine fit and build a custom proposal.
- Bring a blank notebook + pen; memorize discovery questions or bring them on paper.
- First-meeting soft skills: icebreaker/joke to open, firm handshake, eye contact, skip the sales spiel.
- Ask only ~5-10 questions, not all 37 available.
- Use "Can you tell me more about that?" to peel the onion and extract specifics (e.g., "sales fell" → "sales dropped 32%, down 15% YoY").
- **Record meetings (with permission)** — replay to digest jargon, and harvest the sponsor's exact words to inject into proposals (makes them feel heard; reacts favorably). (Anissa Cooke tactic.)

### Gatekeeper Handling
- You CAN'T (and shouldn't want to) get past the gatekeeper. Reframe: **turn the gatekeeper into your advocate.**
- Even if you reach the decision-maker directly (found their number, charmed past), they don't know you — dropping "sponsorship $$$" out of the gate blows the opportunity.
- Treat the gatekeeper call as a *mini* discovery session: do company research first, ask a few questions about their current challenges + target audience, be respectful (even if pushed back on), explain your opportunity thoroughly but concisely, no sales talk.
- Goal: get the gatekeeper excited enough to describe your opportunity to their boss "with vigor."
- Different secretaries have different personalities — call back, you may get a more amenable one.

### Online Sponsorship Request Forms
- DO fill them out — but ONLY after you've already closed the sale through normal prospecting/discovery. The form is for internal "rubber stamp"/policy compliance to release money, NOT a sales tool. (Property B closed via network + discovery, then was told "go fill out that form so we can release the money.")

### Proposal / Packaging
- Proposal supports YOU; it is not the sales tool. "I have sold millions in sponsorship without ever creating a sponsorship package."
- Don't write the proposal until the prospect asks for it (writing early = rework as you learn more).
- Use a **menu, not levels** — let sponsors pick the assets they want.
- When proposing, present audience data attractively (graph/table) + assets/activations + brief company description, but **don't list prices** until you and the sponsor start "talking dollars."
- Customization should be invisible/self-evident: "If your sponsorship package is truly customized, you don't need to mention it anywhere… Does a luxury car need to tell you it's high-end?"

### Negotiation & Closing
- Never submit a proposal or advance a step "until you have your prospect's explicit permission to do so."
- The asking-permission close: *"I think it sounds like we have a fit here, would you agree? Is this a good time for me to put together a draft proposal for your feedback?"* — only after the 5 discovery questions AND only if you can genuinely link their target market to your audience.
- Be upfront about the cash amount you need; don't under-ask to be "polite."
- Sometimes you never have to ask — the sponsor brings up pricing/contract themselves.

### Fulfillment & Renewal
- Always deliver a fulfillment / post-event / wrap-up report — recurring across many posts.
- BAMFAM at every stage; produce the report fast; use it to open renegotiation.
- Renewal tactics: "dip into the well twice" (re-approach last year's sponsor), use the fulfillment report as a renewal springboard and as proof-of-track-record for *new* sponsors.
- Maintain the relationship year-round (festive greetings, share relevant articles, occasional check-ins) so you're not "that friend who only calls when they need something."
- Invest in the sponsorship asset itself (exclusive benefits, networking events, research, cross-promotions), not just the personal relationship — because marketing/brand staff turn over and may be gone at renewal.

## 3. Scripts, Templates & Exact Language (verbatim)

### Cold email — "ask for a referral contact" version
Subject: *Had an idea for [insert product or service].*
Body:
> Hey [name],
> Any chance you could suggest the best contact for those in the product X side at your company?
> Thanks,
> [your name]

### Cold email — "flatter the expert" version (no internal contact)
> Hey [name],
> I saw on LinkedIn that you're involved in [insert product or service] and I would love to connect and ask your thoughts about a new project I'm working on. Are you free tomorrow at [time]?
> [your name]

### Subject-line patterns
- *[Mutual connection name] recommended I reach out*
- *Met at [place/event], let's connect!*
- *Had an idea for [insert product or service].*
- Keep straightforward; no emojis, no trying to be funny/mysterious.

### The 5 core discovery questions (recurring canonical set — appears in multiple posts)
1. Who is your target audience?
2. How do you like to engage and market to your customers? / What action do customers take right before purchase, and how do you get them to take it?
3. What does your target market value?
4. What can you tell me about your marketing and sales goals for the coming year?
5. What would you consider the most important elements of a partnership/sponsorship package (and what should I avoid)?

### Additional discovery questions (from the "Questions for the First Meeting" expanded list)
- Tell me about a sponsorship agreement that went badly and why? What made it not produce ROI?
- What was the last marketing campaign your company did, and how did it go? What would you like to do next time?
- Can you tell me what you consider to be the ideal sponsorship partner?
- What are your short-term and long-term business goals?
- How does a sponsorship with you typically go?
- What was the most recent feedback you've received from your audience?
- What are your company's core values?
- How do you measure sponsorship success?

### Food-truck-specific discovery 5
Who is your target audience? / How do you participate in sponsorship? / What does your target audience value? / What are your sales goals for the year? / What do you think are the most important parts of a sponsorship package?

### The "no package" sponsorship-seeker script (Goldilocks "just right" property — model behavior)
- "We don't have one. Everything we do is custom based on your needs. If we don't have a fit, then there's no need to waste your time with a package."
- Questions that property asked the (buyer-side) Chris: Who is your target market? / What action do you want them to take? / If we work together, what is the one thing you have to have to consider this a success? / How can we help you look good in front of your client? / Are there people your client wants to meet? Can we help make an introduction? / What do you hate about sponsorship packages? / What was the coolest thing you saw at a recent event that you wish we could do together?
- Closing line: "Does it sounds like we have a fit? Can I put something together and send it over for your feedback before it goes to the client?"

### AI-policy question to ask a sponsor (verbatim)
"Is it okay if I use AI for ideation, graphics, or proposals?"

### Business-Breakfast event script (pipeline-building)
At the event, host introduces you as "a friend and colleague"; you tell the crowd you want two things: (1) to buy each of them a cup of coffee in the next two weeks; (2) for someone to agree to host an identical breakfast for you next month. Over coffee you ask: who should I be talking to about my sponsorship opportunity? what do you value in a sponsorship offering? how can the two of us work together?

### Friday Five script
Friday at 1:00 PM, email 5 cold prospects to your referral network asking ONE question: "Do you know anyone on this list, or at these companies, that you can introduce me to?" Pre-write the intro email so they only have to forward it and CC you. (Use LinkedIn first to confirm each of the 5 is connected to ≥1 person on your referral list.)

## 4. Numbers & Benchmarks

### The Quarter-Million Funnel (key, load-bearing math)
- **200 contacts → 45 discovery meetings → 15 proposals → 5 sponsors.** ("To close five, you talk to about two hundred.")
- 5 sponsors × $50,000 each = $250,000/year (mid-sized property).
- Conversion ratios: ~1 in 4 qualified contacts → discovery meeting (the other 3 pass for reasons unrelated to skill). ~1/3 of discovery meetings → proposal (2/3 reveal a fit problem). ~1/3 of proposals → close (2/3 don't convert).
- **200 contacts ÷ 50 working weeks = 4 direct contacts per week** (~1/day).
- Time cost: 4 real contacts/week (prep + email/call + follow-up logging) = **under 3 hours/week**. Most properties spend more than 3 hrs/week just reformatting the proposal.
- Diagnostic: below 4 contacts/week = funnel starved; below 1/week = funnel doesn't exist.
- Meeting rate below 1 in 20 = prospect list, contact ID, or email is wrong (list problem most common, email second, contact ID third).
- "Reply rate is conversation. Meeting rate is pipeline." Healthy property: ~40% reply rate, ~25% meeting rate. Don't quote one number as both.
- Changing only the email (same list) usually **triples reply rate within ~4 weeks**. First new discovery meeting typically lands within ~3 weeks of adopting the weekly rhythm.

### Email / conversion benchmarks
- Cold email response rate (best practices): **10-20% is doing "very, very well."** Ignoring best practices → **1-2%** response (if lucky). Expecting 50-100% is why people are frustrated.
- Direct mail conversion: **2% on a good day.**
- Long designed proposal sent cold to ~200 companies: **<1% reply rate.** Two-sentence audience-overlap emails asking for 15 min: "multi-percent range."
- Email open rate target: **~18%** (Campaign Monitor); below that, suspect the subject line.
- "If you don't hear 'no' 10 times every day, you're doing something wrong."
- Audience survey: don't expect 100% participation; **>25% reply = "onto something," more than a quarter = great shape.** Give 2-4 weeks to respond.

### Audience data depth
- **At least 25 data points per audience segment** (recurring). Step 5 framing: be able to "rattle off at least 30 different data points."
- Segment granularity: income by **$5,000 increments**, age by year, geography by borough/neighborhood, plus job title/industry/buying behavior/# events attended. Niche down to groups of "only 20 or 30 people" — that's the goal.

### Failure / iteration thresholds (He Who Fails the Most)
- 10 emails with no reply = enough signal to change course ("I'd even say sending one email and not hearing back is enough"). Don't write off as a fluke; don't send 90 more.
- ~1% (or under) open rate out of 10 emails = wrong approach; more volume won't fix it.
- Scale up the program once you have **3-5 successful sponsorships** under the same approach.

### Pricing examples / dollar figures
- Podcast: 1,000-9,999 listeners/episode ≈ **$25 for a 60-second ad** (AdvertiseCast 2021). CPM = cost per mille (per 1,000 downloads). A 60-sec read = low value (≈ a logo at an event).
- Social media post market-value example: avg **$100**; you might set $50, $100, $200, or $400 depending on quality.
- Golf tournament: sponsorship cost ranges four-five figures up to six figures; a golf fundraiser usually nets **at least $5,000** (below that, "sponsorship isn't necessarily worthwhile"), can hit **$100,000+**. Wine sponsor example: pay $500 for wine, then find a sponsor to pay **$3,000** to be the wine sponsor.
- Event cost baselines (for sizing the ask): gala ≈ **$10,000-$15,000**; convention ≈ **$175,000-$325,000**.
- Building naming rights duration: small buildings/wings ≈ **3 years**; arenas/malls/high-rises ≈ **10-20 years**.
- Case studies: CJ Scarlet = **$30,000 multi-year** within 3 days (and more in first 90 days than her org raised in 30 years). Chris Norwood doubled revenue; 300-company list → ~50 companies across 10 industries. Anissa Cooke (SynBioBeta). One practitioner tripled a charity's revenue in 18 months and held a $3M portfolio in 2008.
- Hospital gala anecdote: $250K "Gold Sponsor" level purchased only to qualify to sell x-ray machines (sponsorship "in spite of" the package).

### Pipeline-building math (394 free warm prospects)
- Business Breakfast: 1/month × 12 prospects = **144 warm prospects/year.**
- Friday Five: 5 prospects × 50 weeks = **250 warm prospects/year.**
- Total = **394 warm prospects in 12 months** (excluding ongoing referrals/competitor research/networking).

### Conscious-consumerism spend data (Porch Media)
Avg spend per transaction: Gen Z **$58.30**, millennials $54.91 (lowest), Gen X $60.36, boomers (55-75) $61.69, 76+ $60.38. Gen Z: 77% act on causes they feel strongly about; 65% bought after an influencer; 23% have boycotted a brand.

### Cited industry stats
Nielsen 2024: sponsorship → avg 10%+ lift in purchase intent (paired with exposure). Kantar: integrated-campaign sponsorship → 22% stronger brand recall. Velocity 2025: activations w/ digital touchpoints capture 3-5× more first-party data. Bizzabo 2024: 72% of sponsors rank lead quality as #1 ROI driver. Experiential layering → ~30% better brand recall than exposure alone. Event-driven content → ~3× engagement of standard brand posts. CNN: >75% of sports sponsorships promote junk food. ~92,000 food trucks in US (2026).

## 5. Named Concepts & Terminology

- **BAMFAM** — "Book A Meeting From A Meeting." Always schedule the next meeting before the current one ends, so momentum never stalls. (Taught in the Sponsorship Accelerator.)
- **Property / rights holder** — the sponsorship seeker (the org/event selling sponsorship).
- **Brand / sponsor** — the company buying sponsorship.
- **Asset** — tangible or intangible goods/services offered (logos, signage, naming rights, social posts, speaking slots, emails). A logo is a "very, very small part" / low-value asset.
- **Activation** — an experiential marketing opportunity that "connects your audience and your sponsor in meaningful ways," creating an audience-loved experience that also delivers sponsor outcomes. NOT a tier, NOT a logo, NOT necessarily expensive/cool/complex. (Example: branded water to thirsty crowd for brand awareness.)
- **Fulfillment report / post-event report / wrap-up report** — detailed end-of-deal report (metrics, deliverables delivered vs. over-delivered, photos, screenshots). Opens renewal, protects legally, serves as your own record.
- **Discovery session** — info-gathering meeting (not a sales meeting, not asking for money) to learn sponsor goals/challenges and determine fit.
- **Market value** — base price for a service used as the *baseline* for valuation (not the final price). "It's meant to serve as a baseline."
- **CPM** — cost per mille (per 1,000 podcast downloads / impressions / views); fixed pricing.
- **CPA** — cost per *acquisition* (cost to acquire a new customer), NOT cost per action; indicates sponsor ROI.
- **Market valuation** — asset-by-asset custom pricing; Chris's generally recommended method (less common for influencer/social, where CPA/CPM dominate).
- **Spaghetti approach / "spaghetti mode"** — throwing every fact/asset/offer/level at the wall hoping something sticks. The #1 thing to avoid in emails and meetings. "Less is more."
- **Qualified lead vs. regular lead** — regular lead stumbles in (saw an ad), knows nothing; qualified lead has researched, knows products + pricing, is readier to buy. Your audience = qualified leads handed to the sponsor "on a silver platter."
- **Push marketing assets** — logo-on-everything, e-blasts, signage in many formats; a too-narrow inventory.
- **Contra / in-kind sponsor** — provides tangible goods (ingredients, merch, packaging) rather than cash. NOT a true sponsorship in the cash sense ("An in kind donation is NOT a sponsorship!").
- **Media partner** — press/TV/radio/influencers who amplify reach (but DO expect business outcomes in return, not pure goodwill).
- **Promotional sponsor** — provides any form of promotion (social posts, billboards, distribution).
- **Avatars / audience segments / niches** — "the riches are in the niches"; small, specific audience groups that map to a sponsor's target market.
- **Sponsorship Accelerator** — a paid training program (scripts, forms, full lifecycle).

## 6. Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

- **Gold/Silver/Bronze tiers** — the cardinal sin (possibly "*the* worst" mistake). Brands "throw thousands of these packages out every month, ignoring them completely." Forces sponsors to buy assets they don't want; "screams lack of creativity." Use a menu instead. Seeing a respected property use G/S/B doesn't mean it works — they may close *in spite of* it, with unseen custom assets.
- **Leading with / emailing the proposal** — turns sponsorship into a direct-mail campaign (2% conversion). Sponsor "has no idea who you are, no agreed-upon problem to solve, no reason to read fourteen pages today." Proposal goes in a "for budget season" folder = the destination, never reopened.
- **Trying to make the sale on the first call/email.** "You do not want to make the sale on the first call. Sponsorship is a process, not direct mail!" Doing so leaves money on the table.
- **Spaghetti / too much info** — 1,000-word standardized emails, links, graphics, misspelled name, mismatched fonts from copy-paste.
- **Attachments / one-pagers in cold outreach** — signals you want a first-meeting sale + makes wrong assumptions about their interests.
- **Guessing your valuation** — standard (bad) techniques: guessing, doing what you did last year, copying competitors, or starting from "how much I need" and applying it arbitrarily. People far more often *undervalue* than overvalue.
- **Copying competitors' numbers verbatim** — "When you copy a guess, you're perpetuating that wrongness," and you can't explain your math.
- **Trying to inflate prices above market** — sponsors notice ("they always notice"); they rarely say no outright, they ghost you. (eBay-Cheeto / $3,500-SpongeBob analogy.)
- **Undervaluing** — sponsors will work with you but pay far less, and "no one will tell you"; they'll also expect cheaper assets next time.
- **Relying on your cause / sob story** — "A sponsor does not care about your cause." Cure cancer, stop world hunger — irrelevant. A cause is only a jumping-off point.
- **Thin/push-only inventory** — only logos/signage/e-blasts; "nothing more disappointing to a sponsor than… only two or three types of assets."
- **Broad/"everyone" audience** — "Do you know which sponsors are targeting the 'general population'? None." Babies are part of the general population but don't buy groceries.
- **Selling only to cover costs / in-kind to lower costs** — see golf wine example; you make more by paying for wine AND landing a wine sponsor.
- **The five direct-outreach substitutes (avoidance behaviors):** (1) polishing/sending long proposals cold; (2) sponsorship industry networking events (decision-makers aren't there; you network with competitors/vendors); (3) LinkedIn posting as a strategy; (4) waiting on warm intros (usually land on a non-decision-maker 3 rungs from budget); (5) content marketing as a lead engine (brands don't research sponsorship like a buyer's journey). Common thread: "None of them require a specific named human at a specific company to tell you no. They protect you from rejection… The hours were never the constraint. The willingness to be told no by a specific person was."
- **Chasing big brands for size/money alone** — coldest leads; ignore small fish; even if they say yes you'd under-monetize a non-aligned audience.
- **Pestering after silence** — "Silence is its own answer." After ~1-2 weeks and 2+ follow-ups with no reply, treat it as "no" and move on. People avoid conflict and won't tell you they're disinterested.
- **Writing the proposal too early / forcing it down their throat** — putting "the cart before the horse" without a discovery session.
- **5 pages about your cause/company history** — you get "a couple of sentences"; the bulk of a good proposal is rich audience data.
- **Using AI to negotiate, run discovery, or collect audience data** — it can't, and shouldn't (NY lawyer cited fake AI-generated cases and got fired). Always fact-check AI; reverse-image-search-detectable graphics; AI copy "tells" = ultra-short sentences + repeated phrases.
- **Trusting a sponsorship committee with no sponsorship expertise** (Property A filled out the bank's online form on committee advice, waited 180 days, was declined, missed budget).
- **Reporting only "we put logos on signs"** — "Sponsors don't want 'awareness'" (or not JUST awareness); they want awareness that leads to a specific action.
- **Selling sponsorship from your desk** — "You can't sell sponsorship from behind a desk. Fact."
- **Treating relationships as the only thing needed** — "sponsors are results-driven and not relationship-driven." Connections "count for nothing in the face of a poor sponsorship proposal." "The days of smooching clients on the 18th green are long gone."

## 7. Mindset & Principles (verbatim where memorable)

- "Your sponsorship proposal is not the sales tool, **you are**!" / "the sponsorship proposal doesn't make the sale, you do!"
- "Sponsorship is a process, not direct mail!"
- "Sponsorship is never philanthropy. In fact, sponsorship is a marketing technique and success is measured through marketing ROI."
- "Asking for sponsorship is easy! As long as you don't ask for money (yet!)."
- "The riches are in the niches."
- "He who fails the most wins." / "Those who are successful in life have the biggest discard piles." (Seth Godin)
- "If you aren't asking questions, you are losing the sale." / "End every sentence with a question mark."
- "The goal of the first meeting is not to make the sale but to gather information and determine whether or not you have a fit." (Recurring across many posts.)
- "Sponsors buy access to your audience" — your audience is your #1 asset. "It's not so much you that they're interested in, but your audience."
- "Change isn't easy, but it's almost always good." / "If you continue to do what you've always done, you'll get the same results you always have."
- Always be **positive** (not happy — positive). Self-fulfilling prophecy. When turned down, thank them; reframe "no time" as "reschedule," "no budget" as "ask me about next budget / let me split payments across fiscal years." "This is not a positive thinking exercise per se; this is a mental health survival technique."
- Treat every sponsor "like they're the million-dollar dream sponsor" (put in the research regardless of size).
- Patience: sponsorship is multi-person and multi-layered; your contact usually can't approve money alone. Start early (≥6 months before the event). Don't rush; don't wait forever.
- "Fall in love with the problem" (the sponsor's problem), not your package.
- The human element reigns supreme: "If you can't speak about your sponsorship opportunity with a spark in your heart and fire on your breath, how can you expect a sponsor to get excited about it?"
- Five Habits for productivity: (1) reset mid-day / take breaks (treat afternoon as a distinct day); (2) do the things you hate first (make to-do list before checking email); (3) small daily goals (the 3-a-day funnel); (4) get out of the office — block 3 days/week out, decline meetings unless with a prospect or intro-maker; (5) always be positive.

## 8. Industry-Specific Nuances

### Fundraising / Nonprofits / Individual Giving vs. Sponsorship
- Individual major donor: motivated by **impact / social ROI**; you research net worth, salary, prior giving, common influencers; meet (often in home with family), hear personal stories, talk about your mission/vision. Never lead with logo placement, email addresses collected, gala passes, or branded tweets.
- Corporate / cause-marketing prospect: it's a **marketing spend**; target people in marketing, business development, brand, communications, HR, or CSR. Talk business goals, profitability, the metrics *that professional* is measured on (product sales, market share). Sponsorship "often erroneously referred to as corporate philanthropy."
- Shift into philanthropy mode when: meeting in their home with family, they share deep personal stories, they ask for more impact stories, they ask about social impact and never pivot to how you can help their company.
- Shift into cause-marketing mode when: title contains brand/communications/HR/CSR (CSR can go either way and "often acts like grant givers"), or they immediately ask about your database/events/stakeholders/market research, or they talk products/market research/proposals/corporate volunteering.
- Report-back differs: donors → mission goals met, budget honored, # people/animals/projects, site tours, board members thanking them. Corporate → website traffic, product distribution, speaking opps, employee volunteer numbers, press pickup/media hits, formal fulfillment package w/ photos.
- Four shared truths (Maeve Strathy): relationships matter; giving is an investment (both want ROI); accountability counts (report back); both give because they see **value**.
- "Sponsorship is NOT fundraising"; the Business Breakfast tactic is borrowed from the charitable sector but is "the single best tool for growing your pipeline."

### Sports
- Largest, most popular sponsorship type; multi-million-dollar deals; every industry wants in.
- Org benefits: achieve goals (uniforms, training facility, travel/lodging), web/social traffic, attendance, sales, name recognition. Must be ready to convert the traffic spike (lead magnets, opt-ins) before it drops off.
- Sponsor benefits: access to new/qualified target audience, free advertising, increased sales, web/social traffic, community-care perception, brand awareness.
- Naming rights = "premier assets" (arenas, hospital wings, office towers). Examples: Chrysler Building, BofA Plaza, MetLife Stadium, FedExField.
- Health & wellness can offset junk-food sports sponsorship excess; harder sell but unique positive-perception advantage. Verticals listed: supplements, wearables, saunas, red-light therapy, sleep mats, coaching, apps, etc. Many wellness brands are small (Hims, Keeps, Roman, Truvani, Joy Organics, etc.).

### Gamification activations (sports)
Engages audience, creates unforgettable experiences, enables personalization, elevates ROI, spreads word-of-mouth. Ideas: trivia (RFID/Jumbotron, ≤10 questions), gamification app (badges, location tasks, rewards), VIP experiences (credit-card-gated, spin-to-win), competitions (basket-throw, field-goal, thumb wars, races, rock climbing, digital horse betting — keep easy + free + RFID-scan), leaderboards, scavenger hunts (IRL or digital), live polls (lower value), social media wall (hashtag-gated), shoutouts (Jumbotron, tied to sponsor-relevant action). Prizes should be sponsor products (or a sponsor-named cash prize).

### Podcasts / Influencers
- 3 pricing models: CPM, CPA, market valuation (CPM/CPA more common for social/influencer; market valuation generally recommended elsewhere).
- 60-second ad read = low value. Higher-value opportunities: guest speakers (plan talking points, send questions ahead, don't be salesy), product placement (must feel natural), contests (valuable sponsor-provided prize), VIP experiences.
- Add a menu provision inviting the sponsor to co-customize assets; correct them if they undervalue an asset (you did the market research, they didn't).

### Events / Festivals / Conferences
- Convention cost $175K-$325K; gala $10K-$15K (sizing the ask).
- Customize everything; lock the second meeting before leaving the first; produce a fulfillment report to renew.
- SynBioBeta (conferences): social/mixer activations are sponsors' first-to-sell ("eat up"); record jargon-heavy calls; recreate serendipity virtually (Speakeasy 3D platform) when forced remote.
- DUSK Festival: build a deep prospect list, know your value, branch across marketing/sales/experiential/lead-gen/HR contacts; research prospects' interests on LinkedIn.

### Golf Tournaments (revenue tactics)
1) Multiple sponsors per hole (separate tee-off + green sponsors, even multiple of each) — low cost each, adds up; be transparent + avoid conflicting companies. 2) In-kind ≠ sponsorship (pay $500 for wine, find a $3,000 wine sponsor). 3) Negotiate the right to add prizes to the prize table as activation (iPads, free trips). 4) Sponsor-only play (no foursomes sold directly; sponsors theme a hole + enter teams). 5) Sell a VIP pass at registration (covers all on-course games + first raffle round, enables credit card). 6) Strategic branding/logo placement — beware a "name tag sponsor" out-shining the title sponsor (people literally mistook the name-tag sponsor for the title sponsor). Typical golf assets: title, hole/tee/green, golf cart, food/drink/wine/water, branding on tickets/tables/invites/prizes, speaking ops, custom emails, signage.

### Food Trucks
- 4 sponsor types: cash (expansion/staffing/events), contra/in-kind (ingredients/packaging/uniforms/merch), media partners, promotional.
- Prospecting: audience surveys (collect emails via cards/QR/Wi-Fi/loyalty), competitor inspiration, local research (coldest), social listening (TikTok/IG geotags, foodie FB groups, Reddit, neighborhood newsletters).
- Activation ideas: print sponsor name/logo on food (cupcake chocolate, donut, burger bun), contests/giveaways with sponsor products, sponsor coupons (gate behind email signup), name a menu item after the sponsor, branded eco-friendly drinkware/plates/napkins, doorbusters, "Fast Lane Payment" (tap-to-pay sponsor), QR-ordering (tech partner), eco packaging (sustainability brand), photo backdrops, digital scavenger hunts, SMS sign-ups, free samples.
- Metrics sponsors expect: foot traffic, orders/hour pre-vs-post activation, QR/coupon scan rates, email/SMS signups, social reach + UGC, clicks to sponsor landing pages.

### Conscious Consumerism (cross-industry activation guidance)
Select affordable price points, go green (sustainable venues, reusable/biodegradable materials, eco-aligned sponsors), scale DOWN (excess no longer impresses; flashy spend contradicts green messaging), be transparent about supply chain, connect with influencers, give back genuinely (Canadian distillery: 1 tree per purchase, ~6,000 planted). Gen Z drives this; lavish spending can trigger boycotts/negative press.

### AI in Sponsorship
- AI CAN: write emails/subject lines (skeleton to retool), ideate assets/activations (vet for feasibility/budget/fit), segment audience (input behaviors/income/location/interests), create images (struggles with hands/otherworldly), help draft proposals/reports (outlines, tables, charts).
- AI CAN'T: run a discovery session, collect audience data (do surveys yourself — 75% of consumers worry about AI misinformation; an AI-led survey breeds mistrust), negotiate/renegotiate deals (use a lawyer).
- Best practices: research every AI suggestion (fact-check), put your own spin on graphics (no AI copyright law yet), ask the sponsor's AI policy upfront.
